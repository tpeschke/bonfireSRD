Introduction

What is a Confrontation?

A Confrontation is any extended battle of wills.

This is mostly of two people in disagreement, who’re trying to convince the other to bend but it can also be the battle in a man’s soul as he tries to decide what to do.

You won’t break out this framework every time someone wants to haggle but this should be a tool in your belt to diversify the types of stories you tell and where you can get conflict.

Flow Of A Confrontation

A Confrontation is like a conversation: it tends to go back and forth but sometimes, it doesn’t.

It has a form but it is like a river; it flows, changes depths and widths; splits and rejoins.

But unlike a river, it can sometimes return to where it once was.

So, in that way, you can have a back-and-forth where people make arguments until one person concedes and moves onto their next set of arguments about a different concern they have, but, realistically, you might have a few back-and-forths that’s interrupted by a line of questions that are only there to get more information in preparation for another argument.

Or you might have people presenting arguments but what these arguments attempt at addressing swaps between the different concerns each party has and so what the last person said is only tangentially related to what they’re responding to.

Thus is the nature of conversations; thus is the nature of Confrontations.

The main goal of this framework is to allow the GM and players to arbitrate how well they’re doing when trying to convince someone without slowing down roleplaying. It will slow you down initially, as you learn the rules, but soon you’ll find that you can easily run small Confrontations all the time, without the players knowing and without you thinking twice.

Translating The Rules

Unless your players are very rules focused or have questions about how they’re doing, most of the time, this framework will ‘run’ in the background of a given scene where the players are talking.

Let’s talk about that.

You’re players want to convince a guild informer that they got their hands on to betray his brothers, acting in his own self interest.

Whenever a player says something, you take what they said and ask yourself “Are they appealing to this person’s emotions or are they presenting evidence?” That translates it into the rules.

And then ask yourself “how would this person respond? Why? Are they making an emotional appeal back or are they countering with something that they consider evidence?” Bam; you have their tact.

If the players Appeal and you Appeal back, that’s when you ask for a Check to resolve how well they did: framing the result within the narrative of what’s going on.

This does have a bit of a learning curve (as all new things do) but, theoretically, in these types of scenes, on some level, you’re already thinking about how the NPC is going to respond so this is just taking it one step further.

Trust me, soon it will become second nature.

When to Start a Confrontation

There are a lot of reasons why a Confrontation will take place, some better than others. Remember: When in doubt, the controling player or GM has the final say.

Can & Can’t Do

The first way to initiative a Confrontation is if both these criteria are met:

  1. The person (or people) can do what you want
  2. They’re on the fence

If the person can do what you want but they’re not on the fence about it, no Confrontation happens, so you can’t convince the holy knight to murder a baby: he can do it but he’s probably not on the fence about baby-killings.

If the person can’t do what you want but they’re not on the fence, in your favor, they’ll help where they can but, ultimately, it’s just not in their power.

So, a peasant you saved would be totally willing to help you take down the evil overlord but he’s unable to do so, so no Confrontation.

If a person can do what you want but isn’t on the fence but this time against you, no Confrontation. You’re not going to be able to convince your blood enemy to help you out by healing your wounds and giving you a hot meal.

Characteristics

All that being said, that’s not the end all be all; you can initiate a Confrontation if you can present it in such a way that it appeals to one of their Goals, Traits, Devotions, Racial Temperament, or Flaws.

Goals

If a person can be convinced that doing what you want them to do would forward one of their Goals, that can put them on the fence, allowing a Confrontation.

So, if you know that your archenemy is looking to expand his power, you can approach them; if you team up, you can both take down the bigger threat.

They might still say ‘no’ but showing them that your Goals align might help pave over a rocky relationship.

Traits

If a person has a Trait (or Racial Temperament) that applies, it might put them on the fence or convince them to attempt the action anyway.

For instance, if the knight had the Trait “Those above me know what is best” and is then tasked with killing a baby by his superior officer, that might initiate a Confrontation as he wrestles with his decision.

Devotions

Invoking Devotions area also a way to get people to consider things they normally wouldn’t.

So, if that peasant had a Devotion to the party, even though he can’t win against the evil overlord, he might still attempt it simply due to his love for the party.

Flaws

Finally, Flaws can cause people do things they normally wouldn’t.

For instance, a normally good knight who has an anger issue, driven mad by what the orc tribe has done to his hometown could enter a Confrontation to resist going on a murderous rampage and kill all the orcs, every single one of them. And not just the men, but the women and the children, too. They’re like animals, and he’ll slaughter them like animals.

Racial Temperament

Races in Bonfire has a natural temperament to which they lean which causes them to behavior different than other species. This is called the "Racial Temperament".

When your Racial Temperament applies in a Confrontation, you add a free roll of your Honor Dice to the Check.

Learning Another's Characteristics

Every character who's going to use these rules should invest in the Intuition Skill.

Because you're not physically there and your GM is probably not the greatest actor of all time, things might get missed that your character would have picked up on: the Intuition Skill is meant to simulate that mastery of reading those subtle (and not so subtle) cues that people drop about who they are when interacting with others.

Playing it Out

Okay, so what does a Confrontation look like?

Essentially, both sides will go back and forth presenting arguments to the other, which either counters it with their own arguments or concedes the point.

This very much resembles a normal conversation punctuated with Checks.

The end goal is to get more points than the opposition.

Time Scale

Because they’re a battle of wills, Confrontations have an extremely elastic time scale.

Sometimes, an entire Confrontation can happen within a second (in the terms of making a snap-decision), while other times, it could take weeks (like a trial or diplomatic summit).

And sometimes, it’ll vary.

Say you’re trying to convince an expert to join your crew.

You meet with him in his office where you do your initial pitch, but you quickly realize that there’s a lot you don’t know.

So, you don’t push the point, tell him to have a good day and then do two weeks of research, after which, you approach him again.

Getting Points

So, during a Confrontation, there are 3 things you can do: the first is to present Evidence, the second is to make an emotional Appeal, and the third is to Concede the Point.

Evidence

Evidence is facts: things that you or the opposition believe to be true, even if it isn’t true in actuality.

However, there is subjectivity in it. The only thing about Evidence that really matters is if the person presenting it actually believes it.

Countering with Evidence

Countering Evidence with Evidence is a stalemate and results in both sides getting no points.

Countering with an Appeal

Countering Evidence with an Appeal also results in a stalemate but the defender gains d4! Stress.

Countering with Reasoning

Countering Evidence with Reasoning also results in a stalemate but the defender gains d4! Stress.

Countering with Multiple Pieces of Evidence

If a person has multiple pieces of Evidence that counter the presented Evidence or Appeal, it can be presented at the same time.

If not addressed and countered later on, each of these additional pieces of Evidence is considered to have been conceded to at the end of the Confrontation.

Appeals

Appeals use a person’s emotions to try and convince them. It is using the power of your belief to sway the other person.

To make an Appeal, you need to have a Characteristic that correlates to the tact you’re taking. If you have none, then you’ll have to use Evidence or Concede the Argument.

Countering with Evidence

Countering an Appeal with Evidence is a stalemate and results in both sides getting no points.

Countering with an Appeal

Countering an Appeal with an Appeal results in the person with the greater force of personality prevailing.

A Check is made with each side choosing a Characteristic that relates to their Appeal and adding the Check Mod that reflects their approach (default is Pre but you could add Str’s if, for instance, you were physically threatening someone).

The winner gets get 2 points + 1 point for every 5 full points above the loser's total. The loser gets nothing.

However, they both get d4! Stress, unless the winner beats the losers total by more than 5, in which case only the loser gets the Stress.

Countering with Reasoning

Countering an Appeal with Reasoning results in the person with the greater force of personality prevailing.

A Check is made with each side choosing a Characteristic or Skill that relates to their Appeal or line of Reasoning and adding the Check Mod that reflects their approach (default is Pre but you could add Str’s if, for instance, you were physically threatening someone).

The winner gets get 2 points + 1 point for every 5 full points above the loser's total if the winner was using a Characteristics. If using a Skill instead, they get 1 point + 1 for every 10 full points of difference. The loser gets nothing.

However, they both get d4! Stress, unless the winner beats the losers total by more than 5, in which case only the loser gets the Stress.

What Stat Should I Use?

Pre is the default stat for Confrontation Checks, however, players can try Appeals based on more than just your charisma.

For instance, a big brute trying to intimidate someone might use Str instead.

Reasoning

Reasoning with someone means trying to convince someone with knowledge that you have and so is based off of Skills.

Unlike Evidence it relies on 1) your Confidence, 2) your actual knowledge of the subject, and 3) your ability to communicate that knowledge effectively to the person you're talking to.

Generally, this is used for knowledge that the player doesn't have but the character might based on their Skills.

Countering with Evidence

Countering Reasoning with Evidence is a stalemate and results in both sides getting no points.

Countering with an Appeal

Countering Reasoning with an Appeal results in the person with the greater force of personality prevailing.

A Check is made with each side choosing a Characteristic or Skill that relates to their Appeal or line of Reasoning and adding the Check Mod that reflects their approach (default is Pre but you could add Str’s if, for instance, you were physically threatening someone).

The winner gets get 2 points + 1 point for every 5 full points above the loser's total. The loser gets nothing.

However, they both get d4! Stress, unless the winner beats the losers total by more than 5, in which case only the loser gets the Stress.

Countering with Reasoning

Countering a line of Reasoning with Reasoning results in the person with greater knowledge, confidence, and ability to explain prevailing.

A Check is made with each side choosing a Skill that relates to their line of Reasoning and adding the Check Mod that reflects their approach (default is Int but you could use Pre if you're relying more on your confidence to win the day).

The winner gets 1 point + 1 for every 10 full points of difference. The loser gets nothing.

However, they both get d4! Stress, unless the winner beats the losers total by more than 5, in which case only the loser gets the Stress.

Conceding the Argument

If a person can’t counter an argument with their own, either through an emotional Appeal or through Evidence, they have to concede the point. This is most often signaled by them changing the subject.

In this case, the other side gets 3 points.

Just Inflicting Stress

You can also use these rules to just inflict Stress on your opponent in psychological warfare, in which case you won't be running a traditional Confrontation.

In this case, you'll need to have done something that would warrant taking Stress (such as killing any enemy, causing them to make a Trauma Check, going down a Wound or Stress Category, mutilating their friends' bodies; any war crime done in sight of them or where they'll come across later will do, really) and then make a Check like it's a normal Appeal.

However, because you're not trying to convince them of anything, you won't be tracking points: instead, the number of points a person would have gotten is instead converted to Stress (including the normal Stress you would get). This does mean that, if you fail, you might be getting the Stress instead.

Tracking Points

The easiest way to track points is to create a quick grid of 2 horizontal lines and 1 vertical. Label 1 column “Players” (or “P” for short) and the other column “Opposition” (or “O”) and then label 1 row “Concessions” (“C”) and the other “Appeals” (“A”).

In the end, you’ll have something that looks like the first image.

Whenever one side has to Concede an Argument, put a slash in the C row under their column and, whenever they win an Appeal, note the number of points they got under the A row.

It should look something like the second image by the end of it.

Messy but the quick notes allow you to easily track points and keep things moving.

At the end of the Confrontation, add up all the numbers in the A row and add them to the number of slashes in the C row, multiplied by 3 (because each slash is worth 3 points).

Other Things to Note

Starting Bias

It will be extremely rare that the two sides will be coming into the Confrontation with a clean slate. Preconconviced bias can come from a number of places with the most common being cultural or racial tensions but they can also be the result of people sharing the same faith (at least, outwardly) or being from the same hometown.

There are 5 levels of starting bias. Preferred, Like, Tolerate, Dislike, Hate. These are based on how the person 'defending' in the Confrontation views the person who's trying to convince them.

Preferred

+2 points to convince

Like

+1 point to convince

Tolerate

Nothing

Dislike

-1 point when trying to convince

Hate

-2 point when trying to convince

These points are awarded when the Confrontation starts.

This bias is replaced by any Devotions that the person has that are more specific. For example, two people from the same kingdom would start as Preferred, however, if they have a history of antagonizing each other and one has a Devotion "Can't Stand Her" at Rank 6 they would use that instead.

Adapting to Your Own Campaign

What the relations are going to be is going to be unique to your campaign world, although you don't have to outline every relationship.

Instead, you should focus on relations that are going to help underline the themes of the campaign or things that your characters are interested in and default the rest to Tolerate.

So, if you have priest in the party, you might flesh out the starting biases that the different faiths would have towards each other. Or, if you're campaign is going to be focused on political intrigue between different nobles and the factions they create, you should flesh out starting biases based on that.

While racial tensions might be thing in either world, they also don't have to be and you can have orcs and elves tolerate each other.

Devotions

Devotions are a more powerful driving force than other Characteristics, meaning that, if any Devotions apply, you automatically subtract or add 1 point for every point you have in them, depending on the circumstances. This is normally at the start of of a Confrontation but it can sometimes apply mid-Confrontation. This also doesn't stop you from using it in an Appeal later on. So, if you approach a blood enemy, trying to convince him to help you, even if you’re being entirely reasonable, it’ll be much harder to do so. On the flip side, convincing a good friend will be much easier.

Reputations

Reputations are powerful when used in a Confrontation as they're truths about your character that the other side can't argue.

As such, Reputation serves as Evidence, however, you always get points equal to your Reputation Rank, accept in the case of countering Reputation with an Appeal: in which case, it behaves like a normal Trait.

Equipment

Equipment can also be useful in a Confrontation. If used as Evidence, the normal rules apply but what if you use it as part of an Appeal or a line of Reasoning? You draw your sword to help let the person you're interrogating know you mean business.

If that's the case, you add an extra d6! + the Rank of any applicable Traits the equipment might have. If there are multiple pieces of equipment, you increase the dice size by 1 per piece and add their Traits as well.

If the equipment doesn't really suite the purpose you're using it for, like threatening someone with a broken chair leg instead of a mace, you apply a d4! instead, although the same rules apply for stacking pieces of equipment.

Helper Characteristics & Skills

It might be reasonable that another Characteristic or Skill you have would help you make a more convincing argument. If that's the case, you can bring them in as a Helper.

If you have another Characteristics or Advanced Skill that you think might help you, you can add it as a bonus, however the GM is going to roll an additional d4! for Stress for each Characteristic or Skill you add but they're only going to take the highest. This can't be reduced for any reason.

Others can also apply a Helper Skills or Characteristic to your roll with the same penalty, assuming they're in contact with you and can give you a helping hand.

Multiple People in a Confrontation

If there are multiple people in a Confrontation, all making arguments, the opposition can choose which argument to respond to.

This doesn’t “waste” the unaddressed arguments – they can be tried again at a later time – but it doesn’t give points for using them at that moment

Helping Out in a Confrontation

Given the above rule, you might, reasonably, wonder how you might contribute in a Confrontation. And, truthfully, it's not as straightforward as Combat or Challenges but it is very possible so let's talk about it.

The first is that you can swap around who is actually making the Appeal. This means that everyone gets the spotlight but it also means that the Stress gain is spread out across the party. Since Stress gets harder to heal the more you get, this has a lot of long-term benefits.

The second is helping with ideas. Remember that you can pipe up in- and out-of-character, giving arguments that might be ignored by the opposition initially but then capitalized later on in the Confrontation.

Getting Points

Okay, so a Confrontation will look very much like a normal conversation and there will be a lot more going on than just people presenting Evidence, making Appeals, or Conceding the Argument.

Sometimes a side will just ask a series of questions to learn more about the other side or the whole Confrontation will just look like a tea party.

If there’s any confusion: ask what the intent is. If the intent is to try and convince the person, it might be time to add points.

If the point is anything else (information gathering, for example), then just keep things rolling.

Ending a Confrontation

In the end, one side will need to decide either way, which will end the Confrontation.

This can either be one side dropping it and walking away or it could be them pushing the point to get the other to make a decision.

Pushing the Point

When one side feels they may have the upper hand and that the other has run out of arguments, they can try to push the point, or end the Confrontation. He simply tries to push the other side to make a decision.

A couple of things can happen here.

If the other person has favorable disposition to the person pushing and they still have more to say, the Confrontation continues, with that person gaining 1 point.

If the other person doesn't disposition to the person pushing and they still have more to say, the otherside is considered to have Conceded against every remaining, unaddressed argument that the person has. The Confrontation ends and points are totalled.

If the argument is based off of a Characteristic, they're only considered to have Conceded once per Characteristic and only if that Characteristic was never used in that Confrontation. Additionally, things that would have used as Evidence is also only counted as being Conceded to once as well and, again, only if it wasn't addressed during that Confrontation.

If, however, it turns out that the pushed person does, in fact, have no more to say, regardless of their dispoition towards the other person, than the Confrontation ends and it’s time to compare point totals.

Victory or Defeat

At the end of the Confrontation, both sides reveal their points. The one with the most points gets their way.
