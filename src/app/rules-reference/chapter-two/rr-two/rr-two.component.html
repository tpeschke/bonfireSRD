Introduction

What is a Confrontation?

A Confrontation is any extended battle of wills.

This is mostly when two people disagree, each trying to convince the other to bend but it can also be the battle in a man’s soul as he tries to decide what to do.

You won’t break out this framework every time someone wants to haggle but this will be a tool in your belt to diversify the types of stories you tell and when you can get conflict.

Flow of A Confrontation

A Confrontation is like a conversation: it tends to go back and forth but sometimes, it doesn’t.

It has a form but it is like a river; it flows, changes depths and widths; splits and rejoins. But unlike a river, it can sometimes return to where it once was.

So, in that way, you can have a back-and-forth where people make arguments until one person concedes and moves onto their next set of arguments about a different concern they have, but, realistically, you might have a few back-and-forths that’s interrupted by a line of questions that are only there to get more information in preparation for another argument.

Or you might have people presenting arguments but what these arguments attempt at addressing swaps between the different concerns each party has and so what the last person said is only tangentially related to what they’re responding to.

Thus is the nature of conversations; thus is the nature of Confrontations.

The main goal of this framework is to allow the GM and players to arbitrate how well each side is doing when trying to convince someone without slowing down roleplaying. It will slow you down initially, as you learn the rules, but you'll soon find that you can easily run small Confrontations all the time, without the players knowing and without you thinking twice about it.

Translating The Rules

Unless your players are very rules focused, or have questions about how they’re doing, most of the time, this framework will ‘run’ in the background of a given scene where the players are talking.

Let’s talk about that.

You’re players want to convince a guild informer, that they got their hands on to betray his brothers, acting in his own self interest.

Whenever a player says something, you take what they said and ask yourself “Are they appealing to this person’s emotions or are they presenting evidence?” That translates it into the rules.

And then ask yourself “how would this person respond? Why? Are they making an emotional appeal back or are they countering with something that they consider evidence?” Bam; you have their tact.

If the players Appeal and you Appeal back, that’s when you ask for a Check to resolve how well they did: framing the result within the narrative of what’s going on.

This does have a bit of a learning curve (as all new things do) but, theoretically, in these types of scenes, on some level, you’re already thinking about how the NPC is going to respond so this is just taking it one step further.

Trust me, soon it will become second nature.

When to Start a Confrontation

There are a lot of reasons why a Confrontation will take place, some better than others. Remember: When in doubt, the controling player or GM has the final say.

Can & Can’t Do

The first way to initiative a Confrontation is if both these criteria are met:

  1. The person (or people) can do what you want
  2. They’re on the fence

If the person can do what you want but they’re not on the fence about it, no Confrontation happens, so you can’t convince the holy knight to murder a baby: he can do it but he’s probably not on the fence about baby-killings.

If the person can’t do what you want but they would be willing to help if they could, they’ll help where they can but, ultimately, it’s just not in their power.

So, a peasant you saved would be totally willing to help you take down the evil overlord but he’s unable to do so, so no Confrontation.

If a person can do what you want but isn't willing to help you, no Confrontation. You’re not going to be able to convince your blood enemy to help you out by healing your wounds and giving you a hot meal.

Characteristics

All that being said, that’s not the end all be all; you can initiate a Confrontation if you can present it in such a way that it appeals to one of their Goals, Convictions, Devotions, Descriptions, Racial Temperament, or Flaws.

Goals

If a person can be convinced that doing what you want them to do would forward one of their Goals, that can put them on the fence, allowing a Confrontation.

So, if you know that your archenemy is looking to expand his power, you can approach them; if you team up, you can both take down the bigger threat.

They might still say ‘no’ but showing them that your Goals align might help pave over a rocky relationship.

Convictions

If a person has a Conviction that applies, it might put them on the fence or convince them to attempt the action anyway.

For instance, if the knight had the Conviction “Those above me know what is best” and is then tasked with killing a baby by his superior officer, that might initiate a Confrontation as he wrestles with his decision.

Descriptions

You might also be able to start a Confrontation via a character's Descriptions however there is a caveat: If the Description describe something physical about a person, you can only use it to start a negative Confrontation.

So, if a person is Patient, you should be able to start a Confrontation to get them to wait. But if they're Handsome, you wouldn't be able to try to convince him to help you by appealing to his vanity (unless he has another Characteristic relating to it) but you might be able to start a Confrontation to get him to pick a fight with you by insulting his looks.

Devotions

Invoking Devotions area also a way to get people to consider things they normally wouldn’t.

So, if that peasant had a Devotion to the party, even though he can’t win against the evil overlord, he might still attempt it simply due to his love for the party.

Flaws

Finally, a character’s Flaws can cause a person to do things they normally wouldn’t. If a situation comes up where a Flaw would apply, the character automatically gives in unless they’re able to win a Confrontation.

For instance, a normally good knight who has an anger issue, driven mad by what the orc tribe has done to his hometown could enter a Confrontation to resist going on a murderous rampage and kill all the orcs, every single one of them. And not just the men, but the women and the children, too. They’re like animals, and he’ll slaughter them like animals.

Racial Temperament

Races in Bonfire have a natural temperament to which they lean which causes them to behave differently than other species. This is called the "Racial Temperament" and can cause them to act against their best judgment.

Learning Another's Characteristics

Every character who's going to use these rules should invest in the Intuition Skill.

Because you're not physically there and your GM is probably not the greatest actor of all time, things might get missed that your character would have picked up on: the Intuition Skill is meant to simulate that mastery of reading those subtle (and not so subtle) cues that people drop about who they are when interacting with others.

Playing it Out

Okay, so what does a Confrontation look like?

Essentially, both sides will go back and forth presenting arguments to the other, which either counters it with their own arguments or concedes the point.

This very much resembles a normal conversation punctuated with Checks.

The end goal is to get more points than the opposition.

Time Scale

Because they’re a battle of wills, Confrontations have an extremely elastic time scale.

Sometimes, an entire Confrontation can happen within a second (in the terms of making a snap-decision), while other times, it could take weeks (like a trial or diplomatic summit).

And sometimes, it’ll vary.

Say you’re trying to convince an expert to join your crew.

You meet with him in his office where you do your initial pitch, but you quickly realize that there’s a lot you don’t know.

So, you don’t push the point, tell him to have a good day and then do two weeks of research, after which, you approach him again.

Scope

Another thing to consider is just the scope of the Confrontation, which is basically how many rounds it's going to be. Full on arguments have a very large scope with multiple back-and-forths and Checks while someone just trying to distract another person would be one or two Checks and that's it.

Concerns

Before starting a Confrontation, list out all the concerns the opposing side will have. When they're addressed, put a checkmark next to them as that will be important when resolving the Confrontation. You can also add to this list during the Confrontation as things occur to you (and the NPC).

Making A Check

Confrontation Checks like normal Checks with a base d20! + Stat Mod + the Applicable Rank, however, the different types of Characteristics have different interactions that are unique to Confrontations.

Both sides gain a base d4! of Stress unless the winner beats the loser’s total by more than 5, in which case the winner removes 1 Stress Dice.

Note that additional Stress dice might be added to a Check: in this case, you roll them together, taking the highest. These additional Stress Dice cannot be reduced for any reason.

Windle is making an impassioned plea for Lord Kilgore to spare the children. He has “Children are our future” as a Conviction at Rank 6 and the Oration Skills at Rank 3. He also has a Reputation as someone you don’t want to mess with (Rank 5).

He rolls a d20! + his Pre Mod (+2) + 6 for the Conviction + 3 for the Skill +5 for the Reputation.

Since he’s adding the Skill, he is going to roll 3d4!, taking the highest, for Stress but, since he beat Lord Kilgore’s roll by more than 5, he’s only going to roll 2.

He rolls a 2 for the first d4! and a 4 and then a 4 and then a 2 for his second for a total of 8 Stress.

Convictions

Convictions add their Rank to the Check.

You can add multiple Convictions but, for each one, you’re going to roll an additional d4! of Stress, taking the highest of all Stress dice rolled.

Devotions

If you're doing a single Check where a Devotion applies, figure out the points and then add or subtract the applicable Devotion Ranks to determine the final outcome.

Descriptions

Descriptions can be tacked onto the Check but they don't increase the number. Instead, you get a number of Boons equal to the total Ranks of all Descriptions you use but each Description gives you 1d4! Stress, taking the highest. These Boons can be spent like Skill Boons but can also be spent on points (on a 1-to-1 basis), inflicting/healing Stress, and/or inflicting Emotional States.

Reputation

Reputations behave just like Descriptions.

Burdens & Injuries

Where applicable, Burdens & Injuries will just use their Rank but they can also be more commonly used as Evidence.

Flaws

In that case, since Flaws have no Rank, whenever the character is trying to resist their Flaw, you can either Concede the Point or take 1d4! Stress. Once a person has brought up your Flaw, doing so again does nothing, unless they circumstances have changed. So, if an alcoholic were offered a drink as a party by a random stranger and declined, being offered another drink by another stranger does nothing but having a friend offering a drink does.

Racial Temperament

Racial Temperaments behave just like Flaws.

Skills

Skills can also help make an Appeal or additional Skills can help with a line of Reasoning.

In these cases, you can add their Rank to the roll, like a normal Check, but you’re going to add an additional d4! of Stress for each one added, taking the highest of all Stress dice rolled.

Penalties To A Check

Your Characteristics can also work against you: a Conviction might convince you to do something that isn’t in your best interest or your Description might make it hard to convince someone you have the best intentions.

In these cases, you’re going to apply your Rank as a penalty.

If the player was the one to suggest it, they can heal an amount of Stress equal to their Rank (or what they rolled on their Rank Dice) or gain 1 Integrity.

If the GM is the one to suggest it, the player only gets the penalty.

What Stat Should I Use?

Pre is the default stat for Confrontation Checks, however, players can try Appeals based on more than just your charisma.

For instance, a big brute trying to intimidate someone might use Str instead.

Arguments

So, during a Confrontation, there are 4 things you can do: the first is to present Evidence, the second is to make an emotional Appeal, the third is to present a Line of Reasoning, and the fourth is to Concede the Point.

Presenting Evidence

Evidence is facts: things that you or the opposition believe to be true, even if it isn’t true in actuality.

However, there is subjectivity in it. The only thing about Evidence that really matters is if the person presenting it actually believes it.

Countering With Evidence

If both sides present Evidence to the other, it just further cements their position and neither gains anything.

Countering with an Appeal

Countering Evidence with an Appeal results in the defender gaining d4! Stress but also getting 1 point for every Characteristic that applies. Each side also inflicts an Emotional State on the other (either at Rank 1 or giving an existing emotion +2) that is determined by the other.

Countering with Reasoning

Patiently explaining why their Evidence is wrong can help but most people need a lot of Reasoning to move a little.

Countering Evidence with Reasoning results in the Reasoning person gaining 1 point and d4! Stress.

Countering with Multiple Pieces of Evidence

If a person has multiple pieces of Evidence that counter the presented Evidence or Appeal, it can be presented at the same time.

If not addressed and countered later on, each of these additional pieces of Evidence is considered to have been conceded to at the end of the Confrontation.

Making an Appeal

Appeals use a person’s emotions to try and convince them. It is using the power of your belief to sway the other person.

To make an Appeal, you need to have a Characteristic that correlates to the tact you’re taking. If you have none, then you’ll have to use Evidence or Concede the Argument.

Countering with Evidence

Countering an Appeal with Evidence is a stalemate and results in both sides getting no points.

Countering With An Appeal

Countering an Appeal with an Appeal results in the person with the greater force of personality prevailing.

A Check is made with each side choosing a Characteristic that relates to their Appeal.

The winner gets 2 points + 1 point for every 5 full points above the loser's total. If they get at least 5 points, they also inflict an Emotional State. They can also decide which side gets to take no Stress from their Check. The loser gets nothing.

Countering With Reasoning

Countering an Appeal with Reasoning results in the person with the greater force of personality prevailing.

A Check is made with each side choosing a Characteristic or Skill that relates to their Appeal or line of Reasoning.

The winner gets 2 points + 1 point for every 5 full points above the loser's total and they get to decide which side takes no Stress if the winner was using a Characteristic. If using a Skill instead, they get 1 point + 1 for every 10 full points of difference. The loser gets nothing.

Making an Appeal without a Characteristic

Even if you don't have a specific Characteristic but you roll a d12! instead of a d20! unless the GM rules otherwise.

They might do so in cases where it would culturally make sense to do so; for instance, if you live in the U.S., you wouldn't need a specific Characteristic like "Eating People Alive is Bad" to make that arguement convincing because that's just part of the cultural milieu. In that case, you could roll on the standard d20!.

Showing Your Reasoning

Reasoning with someone means trying to convince someone with knowledge that you have and so is based off of Skills.

Unlike Evidence it relies on 1) your Confidence, 2) your actual knowledge of the subject, and 3) your ability to communicate that knowledge effectively to the person you're talking to.

Generally, this is used for knowledge that the player doesn't have but the character might based on their Skills.

Countering with Evidence

Countering Reasoning with Evidence is a stalemate and results in both sides getting no points.

Countering With An Appeal

Countering Reasoning with an Appeal results in the person with the greater force of personality prevailing.

A Check is made with each side choosing a Characteristic or Skill that relates to their Appeal or line of Reasoning.

The winner gets 2 points + 1 point for every 5 full points above the loser's total and they get to decide which side takes no Stress if the winner was using a Characteristic. If using a Skill instead, they get 1 point + 1 for every 10 full points of difference. If they get at least 5 points, they also inflict an Emotional State. The loser gets nothing.

Countering With Reasoning

Countering Reasoning with Reasoning results in the person with the greater force of personality prevailing.

A Check is made with each side choosing a Skill that relates to their line of Reasoning.

The winner gets 1 point + 1 for every 10 full points of difference. The loser gets nothing.

Conceding the Argument

If a person can’t counter an argument with their own, either through an emotional Appeal or through Evidence, they have to concede the point. This is most often signaled by them changing the subject.

In this case, the other side gets 3 points.

Just Inflicting Stress

You can also use these rules to just inflict Stress on your opponent in psychological warfare, in which case you won't be running a traditional Confrontation.

In this case, you'll need to have done something that would warrant taking Stress (such as killing any enemy, causing them to make a Trauma Check, going down a Wound or Stress Category, mutilating their friends' bodies; any war crime done in sight of them or where they'll come across later will do, really) and then make a Check like it's a normal Appeal.

However, because you're not trying to convince them of anything, you won't be tracking points: instead, the number of points a person would have gotten is instead converted to d4!s of Stress (including the normal Stress you would get). This does mean that, if you fail, you might be getting the Stress instead.

Tracking Points

The easiest way to track points is to create a quick grid of 4 horizontal lines and 2 vertical. Label the 2nd column "Players" (or "P" for short) and the 3rd column "Opposition" (or "O") and then label 1 row "Evidence" ("E"), the next "Appeals" ("A"), "Reasons" ("R"), and last one "Devotions" ("D").

In the end, you’ll have something that looks like the first image.

At the beginning of the Confrontation, note the Ranks of any application Devotions in the D row and then, whenever one side has to Concede an Argument, put a slash in the E row under their column, whenever they win an Appeal, note the number of points they got under the A row, and whenever they succeed at a Line of Reasoning, put a slash in the R row.

It should look something like the second image by the end of it.

Messy but the quick notes allow you to easily track points and keep things moving.

At the end of the Confrontation, add up all the numbers in the A and D rows, and the number of slashes in the R row, and add that total to the number of slashes in the E row, multiplied by 3 (because each Evidence slash is worth 3 points).

Other Things to Note

Starting Bias

It will be extremely rare that the two sides will be coming into the Confrontation with a clean slate. Preconceived bias can come from a number of places with the most common being cultural or racial tensions but they can also be the result of people sharing the same faith (at least, outwardly) or being from the same hometown.

There are 5 levels of starting bias. Preferred, Like, Tolerate, Dislike, Hate. These are based on how the person 'defending' in the Confrontation views the person who's trying to convince them.

+5

Tolerate

Nothing

-10

At the start of a Confrontation, the persuader gains (or loses) a number of points automatically based on the defender’s basies towards them so they behave exactly like Devotions.

This bias is replaced by any Devotions that the person has that are more specific. For example, two people from the same kingdom would start as Preferred, however, if they have a history of antagonizing each other and one has a Devotion "Can't Stand Her" at Rank 6, they would use that instead.

Adapting to Your Own Campaign

What the relations are going to be is going to be unique to your campaign world, although you don't have to outline every relationship.

Instead, you should focus on relations that are going to help underline the themes of the campaign or things that your characters are interested in and default the rest to Tolerate.

So, if you have priest in the party, you might flesh out the starting biases that the different faiths would have towards each other. Or, if you're campaign is going to be focused on political intrigue between different nobles and the factions they create, you should flesh out starting biases based on that.

While racial tensions might be thing in either world, they also don't have to be and you can have orcs and elves tolerate each other.

Equipment

Equipment can also be useful in a Confrontation. If used as Evidence, the normal rules apply but what if you use it as part of an Appeal or a line of Reasoning? You draw your sword to help let the person you're interrogating know you mean business.

If that's the case, you add an extra d6! + the Rank of any applicable Descriptions the equipment might have. If there are multiple pieces of equipment, you increase the dice size by 1 per piece and add their Descriptions as well.

If the equipment doesn't really suit the purpose you're using it for, like threatening someone with a broken chair leg instead of a mace, you apply a d4! instead, although the same rules apply for stacking pieces of equipment.

Emotional States

Emotional states are temporary Descriptions that describe how your character is feeling. For example, they can be angry, tired, calm, sad, quiet, withdrawn, happy, etc.

How They Work

Emotional states work like Descriptions except that they can be inflicted, increased, decreased, and healed.

Inflicting Emotional States

Emotional states are inflicted by making an appropriate Confrontation Check as per normal. If successful, the person gains that emotional state as a Description starting at Rank 0. If they already have that Description, it is instead increased by 1.

If you beat your opponent’s total by more than 5 for Appeals or 10 for any other type of Check, it starts at Rank 1 or is increased by 2.

If this Check is made as part of another Confrontation, you don’t get points. It’s not really considered part of the Confrontation, more of a sidebar.

Conflicting Emotional States You can have multiple Emotional States going at one time: after all, we can be both happy and sad at the same time, which is why we have the term 'bittersweet’ to describe things.

However, there might be emotional states that are directly contradictory, like being both angry and calm. In those cases, you need to lower one before raising the other.

So, if a person is Tired at Rank 3, to give them Energized Rank 0, you’d need to lower Tired 3 times.

Healing Emotional States

After a certain amount of time, people will return to their baseline. Whenever a person with an emotional state drops a Stress Category, they half all emotional state Ranks, rounded down. When they hit 0 Stress, they remove all of them. If they were already at 0 Stress, they remove all of them the next time they Take a Breather.

Getting Points

Okay, so a Confrontation will look very much like a normal conversation and there will be a lot more going on than just people presenting Evidence, making Appeals, or Conceding the Argument.

Sometimes a side will just ask a series of questions to learn more about the other side or the whole Confrontation will just look like a tea party.

If there’s any confusion: ask what the intent is. If the intent is to try and convince the person, it might be time to add points.

If the point is anything else (information gathering, for example), then just keep things rolling.

Ending a Confrontation

In the end, one side will need to decide either way, which will end the Confrontation.

This can either be one side dropping it and walking away or it could be them pushing the point to get the other to make a decision.

Mental Breaks

If a Mental Break happens as part of a Confrontation, the opposing side gets to decide what form the Mental Break takes. For combat, that might entail the person running for it or throwing down their weapons and attempting to surrender but it might also entail them turning their allies. For non-combat scenarios, it generally results in the person just giving into what you want.

Pushing the Point

When one side feels they may have the upper hand and that the other has run out of arguments, they can try to push the point, or end the Confrontation. He simply tries to push the other side to make a decision.

A couple of things can happen here.

If the other person has favorable disposition to the person pushing and they still have more to say, the Confrontation continues, with that person gaining 1 point.

If the other person doesn't disposition to the person pushing and they still have more to say, the otherside is considered to have Conceded against every remaining, unaddressed argument that the person has. The Confrontation ends and points are totalled.

If the argument is based off of a Characteristic, they're only considered to have Conceded once per Characteristic and only if that Characteristic was never used in that Confrontation. Additionally, things that would have used as Evidence is also only counted as being Conceded to once as well and, again, only if it wasn't addressed during that Confrontation.

If, however, it turns out that the pushed person does, in fact, have no more to say, regardless of their dispoition towards the other person, than the Confrontation ends and it’s time to compare point totals.

Victory or Defeat

At the end of the Confrontation, the GM will look at the the Concern list (and check any they didn't during play) and both sides reveal their points.

If the ones that started the Confrontation got the most points and addressed all the Concerns of the opposition, they get what they want and the other side will offer additional help.

If the ones that started the Confrontation got the most points but didn't address all the Concerns of the opposition, they get what they want but that's it, no more.

If the ones defending in the Confrontation got the most points but all their Concerns were addressed, they effectively resist but will offer something else in exchange.

If the ones defending in the Confrontation got the most points and none of their Concerns were addressed, they effectively resist and will offer an additional penalty or problem based on how much they dislike the other side.

So, the players get in a Confrontation to convince the Baron of Ernsdt, their long-term enemy, to help them fight off an orcish invasion.

If, at the end of the Confrontation, the players get more points and addressed the baron's concerns, he will agree to help them by sending men but also he's willing to council with the players and coordinate their attacks as well as letting the players borrow equipment from his armory.

If, however, they got enough points but don't address his concerns, the baron will mobilize his forces and fight the invasion but on his own terms and refusing to work together.

If they don't get enough points to convince the Baron but do address his concerns, he won't send men to help but will allow the players to borrow equipment and pass through his lands unmolested.

Finally, if the players address none of his concerns and get fewer points, the Baron might seize them and try to ransom them off to the orcs or he might let them go but then join up with the orcs to attack the players.
