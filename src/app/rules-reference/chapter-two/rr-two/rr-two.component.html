Introduction

What is a Confrontation?

A Confrontation is any extended battle of wills where one or more sides are looking to put the others in an emotional state that they can capitalize on to get them to do something.

This is mostly when two people disagree, each trying to convince the other to bend but it can also be the battle in a man’s soul as he tries to decide what to do.

You won’t break out this framework every time someone wants to haggle but this will be a tool in your belt to diversify the types of stories you tell and the types of conflicts that happen at your table.

Flow Of A Confrontation

Confrontations are a conversation: they tend to go back and forth but not always. They’re like a river; they flow, change depths and widths; split and rejoin. But unlike a river, they can sometimes return to where they once were.

So, in that way, you can have a back-and-forth where people make arguments until one person concedes and moves onto their next set of arguments about a different concern they have, but, realistically, you might have a few back-and-forths that’s interrupted by a line of questions that are only there to get more information in preparation for another argument.

Or you might have people presenting arguments but what these arguments attempt at addressing swaps between the different concerns each party has and so what the last person said is only tangentially related to what they’re responding to.

Thus is the nature of conversations; thus is the nature of Confrontations.

The main goal of this framework is to allow the GM to easily judge the effectiveness of player arguments based on the NPC, have the NPC adapt and change through an encounter, and also allow the GM to be surprised.

For the players, it allows them to be rewarded for getting to know NPCs, factions, and the settings. It also allows them to engage consistently strategically with the world.

It will slow you down initially, as you learn the rules, but you'll soon find that you can easily run small Confrontations all the time, without the players knowing and without you thinking twice about it.

Attacker & Defender

We’ll use “Attacker” to mean the person/side who opened up the Confrontation and “Defender” to mean the other person/side.

Time Scale

Because they’re a battle of wills, Confrontations have an extremely elastic time scale.

Sometimes, an entire Confrontation can happen within a second (in the terms of making a snap-decision), while other times, it could take weeks (like a trial or diplomatic summit).

And sometimes, it’ll vary.

Say you’re trying to convince an expert to join your crew.

You meet with him in his office where you do your initial pitch, but you quickly realize that there’s a lot you don’t know.

So, you don’t push the point, tell him to have a good day and then do two weeks of research, after which, you approach him again.

Scope

Another thing to consider is just the scope of the Confrontation, which is basically how many rounds it's going to be. Full on arguments have a very large scope with multiple back-and-forths and Checks while someone just trying to distract another person would be one or two Checks and that's it.

End Goal & Emotional States

As stated, the point of a Confrontation is to get your opponent(s) into an emotional state that leads to them taking an action.

You should define the action first and then work with your GM to define what the emotion should be. Below is a table with some common actions and possible desired emotions but it is by no means comprehensive. Mainly, its goal is to point out that an action can be the result of different emotions, sometimes based on circumstances, and that multiple emotions can result in different actions. You might even have two people, inflicting two different Emotional States that push towards the same action ("good cop, bad cop").

Actions & Emotions
Possible Action Possible Emotions
Agree with You Happy, Open
Do What You Want Happy, Guilty, Stupid
Put Down Their Weapon Guilty if you're in the right; otherwise, Fear
Attack You Confident in battle; Angry, if not
Pull Back Fearful, Cautious, Penned In, Overwhelmed
Give You More Info Open, Anxious
Excuse Themselves From a Party Nauseous, Embarrassed, Self Conscious
Make an Impulsive Decision Anxious, Fearful

When a character is feeling these emotions, it’s called an Emotional State and has a Rank, which represents its strength, like any other Characteristic. We’ll talk more about Emotional States shortly.

Conflicting Emotional States

You can have multiple Emotional States going at one time: after all, we can be both happy and sad at the same time, which is why we have the term 'bittersweet’ to describe it.

However, there might be emotional states that are directly contradictory, like being both angry and calm. In those cases, you need to lower one before raising the other.

So, if a person is Tired at Rank 3, to give them Energized Rank 0, you’d need to lower Tired 3 times.

When to Start a Confrontation

There are a lot of reasons why a Confrontation will take place, some better than others. Remember: When in doubt, the controling player or GM has the final say.

Can & Can’t Do

The first way to initiate a Confrontation is if both these criteria are met:

  1. The person (or people) can do what you want
  2. They’re on the fence

If the person can do what you want but they’re not on the fence about it, no Confrontation happens, so you can’t convince the holy knight to murder a baby: he can do it but he’s probably not on the fence about baby-killings.

If the person can’t do what you want but they would be willing to help if they could, they’ll help where they can but, ultimately, it’s just not in their power.

So, a peasant you saved would be totally willing to help you take down the evil overlord but he’s unable to do so, so no Confrontation.

If a person can do what you want but isn't willing to help you, no Confrontation. You’re not going to be able to convince your blood enemy to help you out by healing your wounds and giving you a hot meal.

Characteristics

All that being said, that’s not the end all be all; you can initiate a Confrontation if you can present it in such a way that it appeals to one of their Goals, Convictions, Relationships, Descriptions, Ancestral, or Flaws.

Goals

If a person can be convinced that doing what you want them to do would forward one of their Goals, that can put them on the fence, allowing a Confrontation.

So, if you know that your archenemy is looking to expand his power, you can approach them; if you team up, you can both take down the bigger threat.

They might still say ‘no’ but showing them that your Goals align might help pave over a rocky relationship.

Convictions

If a person has a Conviction that applies, it might put them on the fence or convince them to attempt the action anyway.

For instance, if the knight had the Conviction “Those above me know what is best” and is then tasked with killing a baby by his superior officer, that might initiate a Confrontation as he wrestles with his decision.

Descriptions

You might also be able to start a Confrontation via a character's Descriptions however there is a caveat: If the Description describe something physical about a person, you can only use it to start a negative Confrontation.

So, if a person is Patient, you should be able to start a Confrontation to get them to wait. But if they're Handsome, you wouldn't be able to try to convince him to help you by appealing to his vanity (unless he has another Characteristic relating to it) but you might be able to start a Confrontation to get him to pick a fight with you by insulting his looks.

Relationships

Invoking Relationships area also a way to get people to consider things they normally wouldn’t.

So, if that peasant had a Relationship to the party, even though he can’t win against the evil overlord, he might still attempt it simply due to his love for the party.

Flaws

Finally, a character’s Flaws can cause a person to do things they normally wouldn’t. If a situation comes up where a Flaw would apply, the character automatically gives in unless they’re able to win a Confrontation.

For instance, a normally good knight who has an anger issue, driven mad by what the orc tribe has done to his hometown could enter a Confrontation to resist going on a murderous rampage and kill all the orcs, every single one of them. And not just the men, but the women and the children, too. They’re like animals, and he’ll slaughter them like animals.

Learning Another's Characteristics

Every character who's going to use these rules should invest in the Intuition Skill.

Because you're not physically there and your GM is probably not the greatest actor of all time, things might get missed that your character would have picked up on: the Intuition Skill is meant to simulate that mastery of reading those subtle (and not so subtle) cues that people drop about who they are when interacting with others.

Running A Confrontation

Desired Emotion & Action

Before you begin a Confrontation, you need to define what action you want the person to take and then work with your GM for the emotion that is going to give you that result.

Concerns

List out as many Concerns for the opposing side as you can think of. When they're addressed, put a checkmark next to them as that will be important when resolving the Confrontation. You can also add to this list during the Confrontation as things occur to you (and the NPC) so it doesn’t need to be a complete list.

Types Of Arguments

During a Confrontation, there are 2 types of arguments you can use: the first is to make an Appeal, the second is to Show Your Reasoning. You can also Concede the Point.

Make An Appeal

Appeals use a person’s emotions to try and convince them. It is using the power of belief to sway the other person.

Countering With An Appeal Countering an Appeal with an Appeal results in the person with the greater force of personality prevailing.

A Confrontation Check is made: The winner inflicts an Emotional State on the loser: either gaining one at Rank 1 + 1 for every 5 points of difference or increasing/decreasing one the loser already has by a like amount.

Countering With Reasoning Countering an Appeal with Reasoning results in the person with the greater force of personality prevailing.

A Confrontation Check is made: If the Appealer wins, it works as if they won an Appeal vs Appeal.

If the Reasoner wins, The winner inflicts an Emotional State on the loser: either gaining one at Rank 1 + 1 for every 10 points of difference or increasing/decreasing one the loser already has by a like amount.

Making an Appeal without a Characteristic

Even if you don't have a specific Characteristic but you roll a d12! instead of a d20! unless the GM rules otherwise.

They might do so in cases where it would culturally make sense to do so; for instance, if you live in the U.S., you wouldn't need a specific Characteristic like "Eating People Alive is Bad" to make that arguement convincing because that's just part of the cultural milieu. In that case, you could roll on the standard d20!.

Show Your Reasoning

Reasoning with someone means trying to convince someone with knowledge that you have and so is based off of Skills. This is used for knowledge that the player doesn't have but the character might based on their Skills.

Countering With An Appeal Countering an Appeal with Reasoning results in the person with the greater force of personality prevailing.

A Confrontation Check is made: If the Appealer wins, it works as if they won an Appeal vs Appeal.

If the Reasoner wins, The winner inflicts an Emotional State on the loser: either gaining one at Rank 1 + 1 for every 10 points of difference or increasing/decreasing one the loser already has by a like amount.

Countering With Reasoning Countering Reason with Reason results in the person with the greater force of personality prevailing.

A Confrontation Check is made: The winner inflicts an Emotional State on the loser: either gaining one at Rank 1 + 1 for every 5 points of difference or increasing/decreasing one the loser already has by a like amount.

Asking Questions & Clarifying

Okay, so a Confrontation will look very much like a normal conversation and there will be a lot more going on than just people making Checks and Conceding the Argument.

Sometimes one side will just ask a series of questions to learn more about the other side or the whole Confrontation will just look like a tea party.

If there’s any confusion: ask what the intent is. If the intent is to try and convince the person, it might be time to make a Check. If the point is anything else (information gathering, for example), then just keep things rolling.

Conceding The Point

If a person can’t counter a point with an argument, they have to Concede, which is most often signaled by them changing the subject. In that case, they gain the winner’s desired Emotional State at Rank 2 or increase it by 2, if they already have it.

Making A Check

Confrontation Checks are like normal Checks with a base d20! + Stat Mod + the Applicable Rank, however, the different types of Characteristics have different interactions that are unique to Confrontations.

The Check generates 1d4! Stress die per person involved with the Check; the winner of the Check determines whether their side takes the Stress (so they divide it as they will between all allies) or if it split evenly.

Note that additional Stress dice might be added to a Check: in this case, you roll them together, taking the highest. These additional Stress Dice cannot be reduced for any reason.

Windle is making an impassioned plea for Lord Kilgore to spare the children. He has “Children are our future” as a Conviction at Rank 6 and the Oration Skills at Rank 3.

He rolls a d20! + his Pre Mod (+2) + 6 for the Conviction + 3 for the Skill.

Since he’s adding the Skill, he is going to roll 2d4!, taking the highest, for Stress but, since he beat Lord Kilgore’s roll by more than 5, he’s only going to roll 1.

He rolls a 4 and then a 4 and then a 2 for his second for a total of 10 Stress.

Convictions

Convictions add their Rank to the Check.

You can add multiple Convictions but, for each one, you’re going to roll an additional d4! of Stress, taking the highest of all Stress dice rolled.

Relationships

Relationships are not added to Check; if a Relationship applies to a Confrontation, as a whole, it adds its Rank to the Emotional State's Rank. A GM can rule that this doesn't make someone Emotionally Ready: you might still need to make at least one Check if the situation is dire enough.

Descriptions

Descriptions can be tacked onto the Check but they don't increase your Check. If they apply, you make the Check as normal and then add or subtract the Description's Rank from the number of Emotional State Ranks that were going to be inflicted, but each Description gives you 1d4! Stress, taking the highest.

Reputation

Reputations behave just like Descriptions.

Burdens & Injuries

Where applicable, Minor Burdens & Injuries gives you a +d6! while Major gives you +d12!. Either way, it also gives you an additional d4! of Stress, taking the highest.

Flaws

In that case, since Flaws have no Rank, whenever the character is trying to resist their Flaw, you can either Concede the Point or take 1d4! Stress. Once a person has brought up your Flaw, doing so again does nothing, unless they circumstances have changed. So, if an alcoholic were offered a drink as a party by a random stranger and declined, being offered another drink by another stranger does nothing but having a friend offering a drink does.

Skills

Skills can also help make an Appeal or additional Skills can help with a line of Reasoning.

In these cases, you can add their Rank to the roll, like a normal Check, but you’re going to add an additional d4! of Stress for each one added, taking the highest of all Stress dice rolled.

Penalties To A Check

Your Characteristics can also work against you: a Conviction might convince you to do something that isn’t in your best interest or your Description might make it hard to convince someone you have the best intentions.

In these cases, you’re going to apply your Rank as a penalty.

If the player was the one to suggest it, they can heal an amount of Stress equal to their Rank (or what they rolled on their Rank Dice) or gain 1 Integrity.

If the GM is the one to suggest it, the player only gets the penalty.

What Stat Should I Use?

Pre is the default stat for Confrontation Checks, however, players can try Appeals based on more than just your force of personality. For instance, a big brute trying to intimidate someone might use Str instead.

This is the way that you handle someone trying to make a logical argument vs an emotional appeal: they would still make a Check but they would use their Int instead.

Other Things to Note

Starting Bias

It will be extremely rare that the two sides will be coming into the Confrontation with a clean slate. Preconceived bias can come from a number of places with the most common being cultural or Ancestry tensions but they can also be the result of people sharing the same faith (at least, outwardly) or being from the same hometown.

There are 5 levels of starting bias. Preferred, Like, Tolerate, Dislike, Hate. These are based on how the person 'defending' in the Confrontation views the person who's trying to convince them.

At the start of a Confrontation, the persuader gains (or loses) a number of Emotional State Ranks automatically based on the defender’s basies towards them so they behave exactly like Relationships.

This bias is replaced by any Relationships that the person has that are more specific. For example, two people from the same kingdom would start as Preferred, however, if they have a history of antagonizing each other and one has a Relationship "Can't Stand Her" at Rank 6, they would use that instead.

Adapting to Your Own Campaign

What the relations are going to be is going to be unique to your campaign world, although you don't have to outline every relationship.

Instead, you should focus on relations that are going to help underline the themes of the campaign or things that your characters are interested in and default the rest to Tolerate.

So, if you have priest in the party, you might flesh out the starting biases that the different faiths would have towards each other. Or, if you're campaign is going to be focused on political intrigue between different nobles and the factions they create, you should flesh out starting biases based on that.

While Ancestry tensions might be thing in either world, they also don't have to be and you can have orcs and elves tolerate each other.

Equipment

Equipment can also be useful in a Confrontation; you draw your sword to help let the person you're interrogating know you mean business.

If that's the case, you add an extra d4! + the Rank of any applicable Descriptions the equipment might have. If there are multiple pieces of equipment, you increase the dice size by 1 per piece and add their Descriptions as well.

Helping Out

A person might be able to back up an Argument with their own knowledge. For Convictions, this is covered by making a separate Check, but, for something like Descriptions, if the person they're helping succeeds, the Description adds +1 Emotional State Rank / 5 Description Ranks, rounded up. If they fail, the helper also suffers from any fallout from that Check.

Concessions

A person with an Emotional State can get rid of a Rank (or more) of it by giving a concession. These are smaller actions that aren't exactly what the attacker wants but are along the same lines.

So, for example, instead of falling back, a scared goblin might give a concession and only use defensive Tactics from that point forward or an informant might not rat out the person the characters are looking for but might accidently drop clues that the players can follow up on.

Essentially, concessions are what you get when you do manage to inflict an Emotional State on someone but not enough to win the Confrontation.

More/Less Stress

Inflicting More Stress

You can inflict additional Stress on your opponent, which is just psychological warfare.

In this case, you'll need to have done something that would warrant taking Stress (such as killing any enemy, causing them to make a Trauma Check, going down a Wound or Stress Category, mutilating their friends' bodies; any war crime done in sight of them or where they'll come across later will do, really, but you can just insult them) and then make a Check like it's a normal Appeal. Or you can do this as part of a normal Check if it feels appropriate.

You can trade in 1 or more of the Ranks you would inflict for a Check; each Rank you trade in gives you a d4!, but you'll take the highest of these dice. This does mean that, if you fail, you might be getting the Stress instead.

Taking Less Stress

On the flip side, you can trade in Ranks, on a 1-to-1 basis, to remove d4!s that you would get or give you 1 Relaxation if there are none left to remove.

Ending A Confrontation

There are 3 ways a Confrontation can end

Emotionally Ready

If the difference between a character’s helpful Emotional States and harmful Emotional States is greater than 6, the Confrontation is over. Compare the Ranks of the applicable Emotional States of that character: if their harmful Emotional States Ranks > their helpful ones, they have lost.

Force A Decision

When one side feels they may have the upper hand and that the other has run out of arguments, they can try to Force a Decision.

If the person has more to say and is friendly to the person forcing, the Confrontation continues.

If the person has more to say and isn’t friendly to the person forcing, the Confrontation ends and the pushing side is considered to have Conceded against every remaining, unaddressed argument that the person has.

If the argument is based off of a Characteristic, they're only considered to have Conceded once per Characteristic and only if that Characteristic was never used in that Confrontation.

Otherwise, if the other side has no more arguments, then the Confrontation ends and it’s time to compare applicable Emotional Rank totals, subtracting ones that help them and adding ones that hurt: the one with the highest has lost.

Mental Breaks

If a Mental Break happens as part of a Confrontation, the opposing side gets to decide what form the Mental Break takes. For combat, that might entail the person running for it or throwing down their weapons and attempting to surrender but it might also entail them turning their allies. For non-combat scenarios, it generally results in the person just giving in to what you want.

Victory Or Defeat

Attacker Won With All Concerns Addressed The Defender performs the action and will do an additional action of the Attacker’s choosing.

Attack Won With Unaddressed Concerns The Defender performs the action but nothing more.

Defender Won All Concerns Addressed The Defender won’t do the action and nothing more, although a friendly Defender might offer something in exchange.

Defender Won With Unaddressed Concerns The Defender won’t do the action and will give an additional complication or problem.

At the end of a Confrontation, the loser halves any harmful Emotional State Ranks.

Healing Emotional States

After a certain amount of time, people will return to their baseline. Whenever a person with an Emotional State could heal a Stress, they can, instead, lower an Emotion’s State by 1.

If they’re at 0 Stress, they remove all of Emotional States (desired and undesired) the next time you heal at least 1 Stress.

Emotional States to Relationships

Emotional States translate to longer lasting Relationships, even after they heal. The rule of thumb is that, to change an NPC’s Relationship towards a character by 1 Rank, the player must inflict an Emotional State on an NPC that is greater than their current Rank in a corresponding Relationship. So, if NPC has no Relationship with you (0), you just need to inflict a Rank 1 Emotional State. This is also true, in reverse, for NPCs inflicting Emotional States on NPCs.

This is a rule of thumb because Relationships can wax and wane. For instance, a Relationship can fundamentally change from one of trust to absolute distrust if one side backstabs the other. Likewise, you might have multiple Relationships with a given NPC, reflecting a complicated history or a Relationship might rise as another falls. If you’re unsure, talk to your GM about the specifics about how things might play out. Finally, this can apply to a whole group of people, monsters, or things. After watching their companions gets slaughtered by goblins, in which a hobgoblin captain inflicts fear on the characters, the characters might get the Relationships “Fears Goblins” as a catch-all for that entire encounter.
