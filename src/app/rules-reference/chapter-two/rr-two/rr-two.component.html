Introduction

What is a Confrontation?

A Confrontation is any extended battle of wills.

This is mostly of two people in disagreement, who’re trying to convince the other to bend but it can also be the battle in a man’s soul as he tries to decide what to do.

You won’t break out this framework every time someone wants to haggle but this should be a tool in your belt to diversify the types of stories you tell and where you can get conflict.

When to Start a Confrontation

There are a lot of reasons why a Confrontation will take place, some better than others. Remember: When in doubt, the controling player or GM has the final say.

Can & Can’t Do

The first way to initiative a Confrontation is if both these criteria are met:

  1. The person (or people) can do what you want
  2. They’re on the fence

If the person can do what you want but they’re not on the fence about it, no Confrontation happens, so you can’t convince the holy knight to murder a baby: he can do it but he’s probably not on the fence about baby-killings.

If the person can’t do what you want but they’re not on the fence, in your favor, they’ll help where they can but, ultimately, it’s just not in their power.

So, a peasant you saved would be totally willing to help you take down the evil overlord but he’s unable to do so, so no Confrontation.

If a person can do what you want but isn’t on the fence but this time against you, no Confrontation. You’re not going to be able to convince your blood enemy to help you out by healing your wounds and giving you a hot meal.

Characteristics

All that being said, that’s not the end all be all; you can initiate a Confrontation if you can present it in such a way that it appeals to one of their Goals, Traits, Devotions, Racial Temperament, or Flaws.

Goals

If a person can be convinced that doing what you want them to do would forward one of their Goals, that can put them on the fence, allowing a Confrontation.

So, if you know that your archenemy is looking to expand his power, you can approach them; if you team up, you can both take down the bigger threat.

They might still say ‘no’ but showing them that your Goals align might help pave over a rocky relationship.

Traits

If a person has a Trait (or Racial Temperament) that applies, it might put them on the fence or convince them to attempt the action anyway.

For instance, if the knight had the Trait “Those above me know what is best” and is then tasked with killing a baby by his superior officer, that might initiate a Confrontation as he wrestles with his decision.

Devotions

Invoking Devotions area also a way to get people to consider things they normally wouldn’t.

So, if that peasant had a Devotion to the party, even though he can’t win against the evil overlord, he might still attempt it simply due to his love for the party.

Flaws

Finally, Flaws can cause people do things they normally wouldn’t.

For instance, a normally good knight who has an anger issue, driven mad by what the orc tribe has done to his hometown could enter a Confrontation to resist going on a murderous rampage and kill all the orcs, every single one of them. And not just the men, but the women and the children, too. They’re like animals, and he’ll slaughter them like animals.

Racial Temperament

Races in Bonfire has a natural temperament to which they lean which causes them to behavior different than other species. This is called the "Racial Temperament".

When your Racial Temperament applies in a Confrontation, you add a free roll of your Honor Dice to the Check.

Playing it Out

Okay, so what does a Confrontation look like?

Essentially, both sides will go back and forth presenting arguments to the other, which either counters it with their own arguments or concedes the point.

This very much resembles a normal conversation punctuated with Checks.

The end goal is to get more points than the opposition.

Time Scale

Because they’re a battle of wills, Confrontations have an extremely elastic time scale.

Sometimes, an entire Confrontation can happen within a second (in the terms of making a snap-decision), while other times, it could take weeks (like a trial or diplomatic summit).

And sometimes, it’ll vary.

Say you’re trying to convince an expert to join your crew.

You meet with him in his office where you do your initial pitch, but you quickly realize that there’s a lot you don’t know.

So, you don’t push the point, tell him to have a good day and then do two weeks of research, after which, you approach him again.

Getting Points

So, during a Confrontation, there are 3 ways to get points: the first is through presentation of Evidence, the second is to make an emotional Appeal, and the third is to have the opposition Concede the Point.

Evidence

Evidence is facts: things that you or the opposition believe to be true, even if it isn’t true in actuality.

When using them to convince the opposition, they should be things the opposition believes. If you’re using it to counter an argument, it should be something you believe.

If you present Evidence that gets countered, neither side gets points.

Appeals

Appeals use a person’s emotions to try and convince them. It is using the power of your belief to sway the other person.

In this case, you make a Check using your character’s Traits and adding the Check Mod that reflect their approach (default is Pre but you could add Str’s if, for instance, you were physically threating someone).

The opposition can counter with either an Appeal of their own or Evidence.

If defending against Evidence with an Appeal, nobody gets points, but you gain d4! Stress. This is better than Conceding but it's still not ideal.

If both sides Appeal, the winner of the Check gets 2 points, the loser gets nothing, and both get d4! Stress, unless the winner beat the losers total by more than 5, in which case only the loser gets the Stress. The winner also gets an additional point for every 5 above that.

Conceding the Point

If a person can’t counter an argument with their own, either through an emotional Appeal or through Evidence, they have to concede the point. This is most often signaled by them changing the subject.

In this case, the other side gets 3 points.

Other Things to Note

So, an elf is trying to convince an orc to do something. Orcs dislike elves as a whole: they're effete and cowardly and can be extremely passive-aggressive to an orc, so immediately they start off on the wrong foot, giving the elf -3 points to convince the orc.

The elf, realizing that this is going to be harder than it needs to be because of the orc’s bullheadedness and general lack of forethought, goes to his changeling friend and tries to get him to sign on. Because changelings like elves, the changeling would get an automatic -1 point to be convinced by the elf instead.

Devotions

Devotions are a more powerful driving force than other Characteristics, meaning that, if any Devotions apply, you automatically subtract or add 1 point for every point you have in them, depending on the circumstances. This is normally at the start of of a Confrontation but it can sometimes apply mid-Confrontation. This also doesn't stop you from using it in an Appeal later on. So, if you approach a blood enemy, trying to convince him to help you, even if you’re being entirely reasonable, it’ll be much harder to do so. On the flip side, convincing a good friend will be much easier.

Multiple People in a Confrontation

If there are multiple people in a Confrontation, all making arguments, the opposition can choose which argument to respond to.

This doesn’t “waste” the unaddressed arguments – they can be tried again at a later time – but it doesn’t give points for using them at that moment

Getting Points

Okay, so a Confrontation will look very much like a normal conversation and there will be a lot more going on than just people presenting Evidence, making Appeals, or Conceding the Point.

Sometimes a side will just ask a series of questions to learn more about the other side or the whole Confrontation will just look like a tea party.

If there’s any confusion: ask what the intent is. If the intent is to try and convince the person, it might be time to add points.

If the point is anything else (information gathering, for example), then just keep things rolling.

Ending a Confrontation

In the end, one side will need to decide either way, which will end the Confrontation.

This can either be one side dropping it and walking away or it could be them pushing the point to get the other to make a decision.

Pushing the Point

When one side feels they may have the upper hand and that the other has run out of arguments, they can try to push the point, or end the Confrontation. He simply tries to push the other side to make a decision.

A couple of things can happen here.

If the other person has favorable disposition to the person pushing and they still have more to say, the Confrontation continues, with that person gaining 1 point.

If the other person doesn't disposition to the person pushing and they still have more to say, the otherside is considered to have Conceding every remaining argument that the other person has. The Confrontation ends and points are totalled.

If, however, it turns out that the pushed person does, in fact, have no more to say, regardless of their dispoition towards the other person, than the Confrontation ends and it’s time to compare point totals.

Victory or Defeat

At the end of the Confrontation, both sides reveal their points. The one with the most points gets their way.
