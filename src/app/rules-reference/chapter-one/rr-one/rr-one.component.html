This chapter outlines the baseline mechanics that Bonfire uses for its systems.

When to Make Checks

If you’re an old hand at RPGs, this might seem like a silly section, but it is something to think about and it something that new GMs should learn.

When do you actually roll dice? And what do those dice do? Let’s take this one question at a time.

When to Roll Dice

Think about an RPG session like a conversation: there’s a back and forth between GM and players and a back and forth between players and other players.

But, unlike a normal conversation, there’s a point where dice are rolled, and the results read.

Very simply, it looks like the flowchart below.

99% of all RPG sessions will follow this flow, and those that don’t, only vary in minor ways, which we’ll talk about.

This is an extremely in-depth subject, but this section is primarily for new GMs so I’m only going to cover the basics.

Meaningful Failure

I wanted to take a small aside to talk about meaningful failure because, if there isn’t a chance of it it is the GM’s prerogative to simply wave the check, saying that the players succeed.

This is just a matter of streamlining the session: if failure doesn’t affect anything, there’s no point spending time on it.

This is usually meant in a mechanic sense (getting wounded) but it can also be in a story sense (it has no effect on the events going forward).

Session Critical

This is a common mistake for new GMs.

If you need a check to result in a certain outcome you should rewrite the session you had planned.

An example I see all the time is GMs putting the adventure hook behind a check. A man has a job for the players but will only give it to them if they can demonstrate that they have the skills.

But they fail all the rolls. The GM stops a second and then gives them the hook anyway.

If that’s the way the session is written; go back to the drawing board because it makes everything seem faked and contrived.

For me, personally, it also inflicts doubt in my actual agency as a player. Did I really beat the villain because of what I did or did the GM just fudge the outcome because that’s what they wanted?

What Do Those Dice Mean?

Something a lot of GMs don’t consider is the idea of structures within a game system.

There’s the resolution mechanic (how do I determine success or failure) but what that resolution mechanic means is based in the context of what is going on.

For instance, rolling an attack roll in a combat means you’re trying to physically hit someone or something with your weapon with the intent to kill or subdue while rolling to attack in an interrogation scene is more about striking to scare rather than wound.

That context is often nested in a structure. For instance, the combat is nested in the “Combat” structure which determines how often that attacks can be made while the interrogation scene doesn’t work when you run it like a combat.

But a lot of games don’t give you structures to run different types of scenes: they don’t give you the structure that helps you (and your players) play that interrogation scene out.

Bonfire isn’t going to make that mistake and so the next 3 chapters are going to be 3 different structures that you can use for 3 very different types of scenes.

As you get familiar with them, you should play around with them; tweak them, add to them, remove parts from them, and combine them, but only do so once you’ve mastered them.

Doing so will open up and depth and breathe to roleplaying that will astound you and will give your players the tools to play some truly unique characters.

Structure Overview

As mentioned, Bonfire has 3 that you can use. Here is a quick overview:

Confrontations

Confrontations represent battles of wills: either with another person in an argument or with yourself at a moment of crisis.

Confrontations are largely about emotional and subjective belief in the world.

Combat

Combat represent a physical struggle between two or more fighters. Most likely this struggle is to the death, with the main tools being weapons, shields, and armor.

Challenges

Challenges are for everything else that doesn't fit in the first two structures and are often called "Skill Challenges" due to their reliance on Skills.

Challenges are considered the default of the structures since they're highly flexible and can cover a lot of ground. In fact, if you're not comfortable with using one of the other systems (or you're running a campaign that doesn't focus on combat, parse), you could easily use Challenges as a workable substitute.

Making Checks

When you need to determine the outcome of a question or action, you’re going to make a Check.

You’ll do this by rolling a d20! and then adding the relevant Check Modifier and Rank.

The opposition will also roll a d20! and add any relevant modifiers on their side.

The side with the high total wins the Check. What that means is dependent on the framework.

Untrained Skills Suites

Untrained Skill Suites (marked with a U) represent Skill Suites that your character has very little or no knowledge about.

When making a Check using either that Skill Suite or an Advanced Skill in that Skill Suite that you have no Ranks in, you instead roll it on a dice size 1 size smaller (normally, a d12!).

Untrained Combat Skill Suites

If you’re Untrained in Melee Weapons, you shrink your Attack and Defense Die by 1 size.

If you’re Untrained in Ranged Weapons, you shrink your Attack by 1 size and your Recovery is twice as long.

If you’re Untrained in Shields, while using one, you shrink your Defense Die by 1 size.

If you’re Untrained in Armor, while wearing a set, you shrink your Defense Die by 1 size.

So, if you’re Untrained in Melee Weapons, Armor, and Shields, and you go into battle wearing Leather Armor and wielding a buckler and longsword, you’d be rolling a d12! to attack (d20! - 1 dice size) and rolling a d8 to defend (d20! - 3 dice sizes).

Check Modifiers

Check Modifiers come from the stat that most influences that Check.

So, for instance, if your character is trying to lift a heavy boulder, they’ll add their Strength Check Mod while trying to convince someone might use Presence (or Intellect, if they’re trying to be logical about it).

A lot of times, the Check Mod is already determined by what you’re rolling. So, for instance, Attack rolls already have a set Check Mod; same with History Checks, although GMs might alter that based on the circumstances.

Rank

Ranks represent knowledge. Ranks are used in several different places; Skills, and Characteristics. We’ll only go into generalities below about when to apply them to a Check but details will be covered in their specific chapters.

Skills

Skills represent broad areas of knowledge (represented as Skill Suites) or specific areas of knowledge (represented by Advanced Skills). Advanced Skills are ‘nested’ in Skill Suites.

When making a Skill Check, if you don’t have the specific Advanced Skill, you use the Rank of the mother Skill Suite instead.

For instance, if Randy has a Rank 3 in Streetwise and a Rank 5 in Current Affairs, while George has a Rank 2 in Streetwise but hasn’t purchased Current Affairs, when the GM calls for a Current Affairs Check, Randy adds a +5 to the roll (the Rank of his Current Affairs), while George adds a +2 to his roll (the Rank of his Streetwise since he doesn’t have Current Affairs).

More information can be found in Chapter 2.5: Skills.

Characteristics

Characteristics is the broad, category term for anything related to your character’s personality. This is broken down into Convictions, Devotions, Descriptions, and Reputation.

These will be added to Checks that specifically test your character’s willpower and drive as well as inner conflicts they might have.

More information can be found in Chapter 2.1: Step-by-Step Overview.

Exploding Dice

You’ll notice an exclamation mark (!) behind a lot of dice notations. This marks the dice as exploding, which means that should the maximum of that dice is rolled, you get to roll it again and add what you rolled to the total.

This continues for as long as you roll the maximum dice.

So, if you roll a 3d8! for damage, you get a 5, 8, 8. Since you rolled the maximum value on two of your dice, you roll those dice again, and roll an 8, and 5, giving you a subtotal of 34 ( 5 + 8 + 8 + 8 + 5 ) but since you rolled another 8, you get to roll that dice again until you don't, adding those results to the total.

There are two things you should be aware of: 1d100! explode to a 1d20!, while 1d20! explode to a d6!.

The second thing is that explosion dice don’t add additional dice to the number. So, in the example above, all that damage is only counted as having been done with 3 dice (instead of the 5 that were actually rolled).

Round Against the Player

Unless otherwise stated, you round against the players.

Stress

Working under pressure can get to you. When your crew mates are relying on you to pull out a win, it can mess with your head. Your character can become stressed under these circumstances and stress can later manifest in odd behaviors and penalties.

Characters can gain Stress in a lot of ways and those are discussed in their various sections - taking damage, making Skill Checks, etc. - the only one we’ll go over here is from acting against your character’s Characteristics, especially when not playing them would be a huge benefit.

Simply put, if the GM can inflict 1d4! Stress if your character goes against one of their Characteristics or a Flaw's Severity if it's a Flaw.

Most of the time, this will be the GM tempting the player: "I know your character is Greedy: I think he'd want to see if he couldn't take that golden statue". You can still choose to have your character turn away from the obvious trap but they take Stress because it goes against who they are.

Mental States

Your Stress Threshold is divided into 4 categories which are, in order, UNSURE (U), NERVOUS (N), SHAKEN (S), BREAKING (Br). Each of these make up a fourth of your Stress Threshold.

These are a way to easily communicate how your character is doing (both player-to-player and GM-to-player) but crossing certain categories have additional penalties.

Panic Threshold

As stated, the more Stress you take, the harder it is to function. When this happens is noted by your Panic Threshold

Panic Threshold is matched to a Stress Category: hitting that Stress Category means that you start rolling your Defense Roll and all Skill and Confrontation Checks on 1 lower die. This stacks with Fatigue.

Your Panic Threshold is set by your Integrity Category at the beginning of the session.

Beyond Breaking & Mental Breakdowns

A character whose stress has gone beyond their Stress Threshold is suffering from a mental breakdown.

They seek to remove themselves from the stressful situation as quickly as possible and either gain a Flaw or increase the die size of an existing one. Determined by both the GM and player if the character survives.

This is specific to the situation, but the character has lost all rationality and so will take the most direct route in their mind, even if it exposes them.

For example, a character that’s having a mental breakdown in the middle of the fight drops their weapon, stands up, turns, and runs for the nearest exit at full speed: exposing themselves to the enemy.

Or they might simply drop their weapon and plead for mercy – refusing to defend themselves.

Healing Stress

Stress heals slowly over time: the more stress you have, the longer it takes to heal.

This is charted using Relaxation.

When a character’s Relaxation reaches their current stress, it resets to 0 and they lower their stress by 1, making high stress exponentially more difficult to heal.

But there are a lot of ways to gain Relaxation.

Taking a Breather

Taking a Breather heals both Stress and Damage and so it talked about in the Taking a Breather section below but it's a method you shouldn't forget to use.

Taking Time Off

A person is considered to spend 8 hours sleeping, with the rest of the day (16 hours), doing things.

However, for each hour your character takes to rest (read a good book, indulge in a hobby, take a bath), they get 1 Relaxation.

Additionally, by spending silver, you can increase that amount on a 10-silver-to-1-Relaxation basis.

So, going out to dinner for 2 hours would normally give you 2 Relaxation, but going to a fancy dinner and spending 50 sc on it, gives you 7 instead.

Hobbies

A character can also benefit from specific hobbies, which can be deduced from their Characteristics.

You can only gain the benefit of a Characteristic once per week for a number of hours equal to its Rank with each hour spent on the hobby giving an additional +1 Relaxation (since it combines with Taking Time Off), although you can stack multiple Characteristics so next time you go out drinking, bring a friend you have Devotion to along to hang out.

Flaws

Flaws can also help you heal Stress.

Should you succeed on a Check where a Flaw's penalty applies, you heal Stress equal to its Severity. This is Stress healing happens along side any Stress you would normally gain from the Check.

If a Flaw's penalty doesn't specifically apply to the situation but it would be reasonable it would, even if it's not a Check, players can apply a penalty equal to the Flaw's Severity to gain this benefit as well.

So, Whiskers is trying to pop a potion in the middle of combat. The Shaky Hands Flaw doesn't say anything about that specific case but it would be reasonable that this Flaw would make it harder for him to pop the cork. So he takes an additional 3 seconds to do so and heals 3 Stress.

Creature Comforts

Finally, you can indulge in creature comforts.

This is similar to spending silver while taking time off, but these represent permanent purchases, like getting a really nice mattress for your bunk.

In this case, whenever a Description would apply, you get an extra point of Relaxation, however you only get a max of one item's bonus per hour.

So, if you’re hiking but you have extra-padded, tailored boots, you would get 3 Relaxation.

Vitality

Vitality represents how close you are to passing out and dying. It is both your health and stamina. As you take damage, you will get closer to death and gain more penalties based on your Fatigue Threshold.

Physical States

Vitality is divided into 4 categories which are, in order, HURT (H), BLOODIED (B), WOUNDED (W), CRITICAL (C). Each of these make up a fourth of your Vitality.

These are a way to easily communicate a fighter’s health (both player-to-player and GM-to-player) but crossing certain categories have additional penalties.

Fatigue Threshold

As stated, the more damage you take, the harder the fight becomes. When this happens is noted by your Fatigue Threshold

Fatigue Threshold is matched to a wound category: hitting that wound category means that you start rolling your Defense Roll and all Skill and Confrontation Checks on 1 lower die. This stacks with Panic.

When completely unencumbered, your Fatigue Threshold is at the maximum level, Critical, meaning you only become fatigued once you are near death.

Wearing armor, shields, and carrying lots of stuff may lower your Fatigue Threshold to a less injured category (e.g. Bloodied or Hurt), meaning you become fatigued more quickly. In a pinch, it may be wise to doff your shield or drop some gear to increase your fatigue threshold a notch.

Note that if this would take you above Hurt, you become always Fatigued. If you go above 'always', every Category you would go passed that, you shrink your defense and Check die by an additional size. So, if you go 3 Categories above Hurt, you shrink from a d20 to a d8!.

Changing Equipment While Wounded

Since Fatigue Threshold is partly determined by the shield you're using, a quick way to up your Fatigue Threshold is to simply drop your shield.

Now, the flip side of this is that if you become MORE encumbered while wounded, you could get Fatigued earlier.

Beyond Critical

A character whose total damage is beyond Critical is dead: roll up a new character.

The only exception is if your character is moving towards their goal.

In this case, they can continue to keep fighting until the end of the combat or until they reach double what they would need to reach Critical again.

However, this is a short lease: they’re still dead and no healing or medicine can bring them back.

Healing Damage

Whenever a character takes damage, they record a wound.

After a number of days of rest equal to a wound’s current damage, you remove 1 damage from that wound.

This means that a 3-damage wound would take 3 days of rest to become a 2-damage wound, which would take 2 days to become a 1-damage wound, and so on.

The upside is that all wounds heal simultaneously: if you have 10 3-damage wounds, it will only take you 6 days to completely heal.

There are other ways to speed up healing as well:

Taking a Breather

Taking a Breather heals both Stress and Damage and so it talked about in the Taking a Breather section below but it's a method you shouldn't forget to use.

First Aid

By administering first aid within the first 10 minutes of a wound being inflicted, you lower the damage by 1.

You can only do this once per wound, however.

Medicine

By administering the proper medication, it can greatly speed up healing, removing 1 day from each wound for each point of damage, although there’s a minimum of 1 day.

So, for that 3-damage wound, it would only take 2 days to get to 2 damage, and 1 day to get to 1 damage.

Resting & Nursing

You can also take time off and not put a lot of stress and pressure on yourself: allowing your body to focus on healing.

In this case, if you have no Stress, every point of Relaxation gained that day is taken off the days to heal instead, however, you can only heal 1 damage a day and it only applies to 1 wound.

Fallen Comrades

Watching your friends die around you or abandon you is extremely Stressful, even if you’re winning.

Each time a side loses a quarter of their original number (either through dying, going unconscious, or running away), those remaining gain d10! Stress.

Taking a Breather

Pausing and resting for 30 minutes heals you half the damage and Stress (rounded down) that you've take since the last time you've Taken a Breather.

If the rest is broken for any reason, you lose all benefit from it.

Integrity

Integrity, or Grit for short, is a measure how not only how a person measures their own integrity but also how other people see them. Integrity isn’t about doing the commonsense thing; it’s about doing the heroic thing. People who run from a fight, even when outnumbered, lose Integrity. People who let others talk bad about them and don’t get even lose Integrity.

The question of Integrity isn’t a question of good and evil; many evil men have lots of Integrity due to being true to themselves. It’s more about being true to your inner self.

Categories & Bonuses

Integrity has its benefits. Confidence allows you to push yourself a little bit more than you otherwise would: tapping into mental reserves to snatch victory from the jaws of defeat. During the game, you can spend your Integrity points to influence anything, rolls or static metrics, whenever you want, on a 1-to-1 basis.

Doing so lowers your Integrity but might save your character’s life. So, if you roll a 13 and your opponent rolls a 14, you could spend 3 Integrity to beat their total. Likewise, if an enemy deals 8 damage to you and you have 6 Integrity, you could spend all of it and only take 2 damage.

Additionally, each category has a Grit Dice, which will be talked about in the next session.

Integrity cannot go above 25 or below 0. The size of the Grit Die is also determined at the beginning of session and doesn’t change during play even if the category does.

No Integrity

0 - 5 Integrity, No Grit Die

The lowest category, this person is unsure that they have what it takes to be a hero and so every decision is laced with hesitation and uncertainty.

Little Integrity

6 - 10 Integrity, d4!

This character hasn’t really proved anything, one way or another. Others and herself are waiting for her to make her mark on the world.

Average Integrity

11 - 15 Integrity, d6!

Having reached this category, the character has shown that they have potential and some follow through.

Lots of Integrity

16 - 20 Integrity, d8!

She has now separated herself from the common rabble and can stand a head above the crowd.

Full of Integrity

21 - 25 Integrity, d10!

This rare sliver represents those who can perfectly balance being ahead of others but not so much so that they invoke the ire of those below them as well as being confident while not being cocky.

Grit Dice

Fundementally, Grit Dice can be used in the same way as Integrity, where it can be spent like Integrity, only, instead of spending it on a 1-to-1 modifier, when you spend a Grit Die, you roll the corresponding die and add or subtract it.

Unless otherwise stated, uses of your Grit Dice are cross session so there's a place on your character sheet to record how many you have banked. As a new character, you'll start with 1 but as you go up Integrity Categories, you'll gain more.

GMs are also free to reward Grit Die for particularly heroic behavior.

Dice Size Hierarchy

Bonfire plays around a lot with dice sizes, so it’s important to know what dice to grab in those cases.

Dice sizes go as follows:

d2 → d3 → d4 → d6 → d8 → d10 → d12 → d20

If a d4 is exploding and drops to a d3 or lower, it stops exploding.

If the dice size increases past a d20, you’ll add an extra dice starting at a d4. Further dice sizes will increase this smaller dice. This can continue ad nauseum, adding more and more dice, but no matter how many dice there are, they’re always considered to be rolled on 1 dice.

So, increasing a d20 7 times to a 2d20! + d3! is still considered to be 1 dice.

Perfect Rolls on Shrunken Die

There are several places where rollings a natural 20 on a d20 offers additional bonuses, but your dice might be a smaller size for some reason (such as when you're defending while being flanked).

In those cases, you can still gain the benefits of natural 20 but only if the dice explode enough that the total value of those dice is equal to or over 20.

So, if you're rolling a d12! where you would normally roll a d20!, you'd need to get 1 explosion with the second roll getting higher than an 8.

Likewise, if you're rolling a d4! instead of a d20!, you'd need 5 explosions to get you there.
