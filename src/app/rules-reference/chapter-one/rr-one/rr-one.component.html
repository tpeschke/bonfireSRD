This chapter outlines the baseline mechanics that Bonfire uses for its systems.

When to Make Checks

If you’re an old hand at RPGs, this might seem like a silly section, but it is something to think about and it something that new GMs should learn.

When do you actually roll dice? And what do those dice do? Let’s take this one question at a time.

When to Roll Dice

Think about an RPG session like a conversation: there’s a back and forth between GM and players and a back and forth between players.

But, unlike a normal conversation, there’s a point where dice are rolled, and the results read.

Very simply, it looks like the flowchart below.

99% of all RPG sessions will follow this flow, and those that don’t, only vary in minor ways, which we’ll talk about.

This is an extremely in-depth subject, but this section is primarily for new GMs so I’m only going to cover the basics.

Meaningful Failure

I wanted to take a small aside to talk about meaningful failure because, if there isn’t a chance of it it is the GM’s prerogative to simply wave the check, saying that the players succeed.

This is just a matter of streamlining the session: if failure doesn’t affect anything, there’s no point spending time on it.

This is usually meant in a mechanic sense (getting wounded) but it can also be in a story sense (it has no effect on the events going forward).

Session Critical

This is a common mistake for new GMs.

If you need a check to result in a certain outcome you should rewrite the session you have planned.

An example I see all the time is GMs putting the adventure hook behind a check. A man has a job for the players but will only give it to them if they can demonstrate that they have the skills.

But they fail all the rolls. The GM stops a second and then gives them the hook anyway.

If that’s the way the session is written; go back to the drawing board because it makes everything seem fake and contrived.

For me, personally, it also inflicts doubt in my actual agency as a player. Did I really beat the villain because of what I did or did the GM just fudge the outcome because that’s what they wanted?

What Do Those Dice Mean?

Something a lot of GMs don’t consider is the idea of structures within a game system.

There’s the resolution mechanic (how do you determine success or failure) but what that resolution mechanic means is based in the context of what is going on.

For instance, rolling an attack roll in a combat means you’re trying to physically hit someone or something with your weapon with the intent to kill or subdue while rolling to attack in an interrogation scene is more about striking to scare rather than wound.

That context is often nested in a structure. For instance, the combat is nested in the “Combat” structure which determines how often that attacks can be made while the interrogation scene doesn’t work when you run it like a combat - you can roll attacks at will, for example, or maybe a single Check could represent multiple attacks.

But a lot of games don’t give you structures to run different types of scenes: they don’t give you the structure that helps you (and your players) play that interrogation scene out.

Bonfire isn’t going to make that mistake and so the next 3 chapters are going to be 3 different structures that you can use for 3 very different types of encounters.

As you get familiar with them, you should play around with them; tweak them, add to them, remove parts from them, and combine them, but only do so once you’ve mastered them.

Doing so will open up depth and breathe to roleplaying that will astound you and will give your players the tools to play some truly unique characters.

Structure Overview

As mentioned, Bonfire has 3 that you can use. Here is a quick overview:

Confrontations

Confrontations represent battles of wills: either with another person in an argument or with yourself at a moment of crisis.

Confrontations are largely about emotional and subjective belief in the world.

Combat

Combat represent a physical struggle between two or more fighters. Most likely this struggle is to the death, with the main tools being weapons, shields, and armor.

Challenges

Challenges are for everything else that doesn't fit in the first two structures and are often called "Skill Challenges" due to their reliance on Skills.

Challenges are considered the default structure since they're highly flexible and can cover a lot of ground. In fact, if you're not comfortable with using one of the other systems (or you're running a campaign that doesn't focus on combat, par se), you could easily use Challenges as a workable substitute.

Making Checks

When you need to determine the outcome of a question or action, you’re going to make a Check.

You’ll do this by rolling a d20! and then adding the relevant Check Modifier and Rank.

The opposition will also roll a d20! and add any relevant modifiers on their side.

The side with the high total wins the Check. What that means is dependent on the framework.

Untrained Skills Suites

Untrained Skill Suites (marked with a U) represent Skill Suites that your character has very little or no knowledge about.

When making a Check using either that Skill Suite, or an Advanced Skill in that Skill Suite, that you have no Ranks in, you instead roll it on a dice size 1 size smaller (normally, a d12!).

Untrained Combat Skill Suites

If you’re Untrained in Melee Weapons, you shrink your Attack and Defense Die by 1 size.

If you’re Untrained in Ranged Weapons, you shrink your Attack by 1 size and your Recovery is twice as long.

If you’re Untrained in Shields, while using one, you shrink your Defense Die by 1 size.

If you’re Untrained in Armor, while wearing a set, you shrink your Defense Die by 1 size.

So, if you’re Untrained in Melee Weapons, Armor, and Shields, and you go into battle wearing Leather Armor and wielding a buckler and longsword, you’d be rolling a d12! to attack (d20! - 1 dice size) and rolling a d8 to defend (d20! - 3 dice sizes).

Check Modifiers

Check Modifiers come from the stat that most influences that Check.

So, for instance, if your character is trying to lift a heavy boulder, they’ll add their Strength Check Mod while trying to convince someone might use Presence (or Intellect, if they’re trying to be logical about it).

A lot of times, the Check Mod is already determined by what you’re rolling. So, for instance, Attack rolls already have a set Check Mod - same with History Checks - although GMs might alter that based on the circumstances.

Rank

Ranks represent knowledge. Ranks are used in several different places; Skills, and Characteristics. We’ll only go into generalities below about when to apply them to a Check but details will be covered in their specific chapters.

Skills

Skills represent broad areas of knowledge (represented as Skill Suites) or specific areas of knowledge (represented by Advanced Skills). Advanced Skills are ‘nested’ in Skill Suites.

When making a Skill Check, if you don’t have the specific Advanced Skill, you use the Rank of the mother Skill Suite instead.

For instance, if Randy has a Rank 3 in Streetwise and a Rank 5 in Current Affairs, while George has a Rank 2 in Streetwise but hasn’t purchased Current Affairs, when the GM calls for a Current Affairs Check, Randy adds a +5 to the roll (the Rank of his Current Affairs), while George adds a +2 to his roll (the Rank of his Streetwise).

More information can be found in Chapter 2.6: Skills.

Characteristics

Characteristics is the broad, category term for anything related to your character’s personality. This is broken down into Convictions, Relationships, Descriptions, and Reputation.

These will be added to Checks that specifically test your character’s willpower and drive as well as inner conflicts they might have.

More information can be found in Chapter 2.2: Step-by-Step Overview.

Exploding Dice

You’ll notice an exclamation mark (!) behind a lot of dice notations. This marks the dice as exploding, which means that should the maximum of that dice is rolled, you get to roll it again and add what you rolled to the total.

This continues for as long as you roll the maximum dice.

So, if you roll a 3d8! for damage, you get a 5, 8, 8. Since you rolled the maximum value on two of your dice, you roll those dice again, and roll an 8, and 5, giving you a subtotal of 34 ( 5 + 8 + 8 + 8 + 5 ) but since you rolled another 8, you get to roll that dice again until you don't, adding those results to the total.

Something to not is that explosion dice don’t add additional dice to the number. So, in the example above, all that damage is only counted as having been done with 3 dice (instead of the 5 that were actually rolled).

Multitasking

Let’s be realistic here: you’re probably going to end up trying to do two things at once, like climbing a wall and doing so quietly.

If that’s the case, you’re going to have to “split” your d20! into smaller dice and assign those to each of the Checks you’re trying to make, because your attention is divided. The total max value of the dice can never exceed 20 but it can be lower than it. The other side will still be rolling full d20!s against you.

So, if you’re climbing a wall and trying to do so quietly, you’d have to make a Climbing and a Move Silently Check. You could split it like so: d12! for Climbing and d8! for Move Silently, or d10! for Climbing and d10! for Move Silently, or d12!+d4! for Move Silently and d4! for Climbing.

Or you could just say “screw being quiet” and roll a d20! for Climbing and take a 0 for Move Silently. In this case, the GM would just roll against your Skill Rank. So, if you choose to ignore Move Silently but have a Rank 4 in it, the GM would roll their dice, and you can hope they don’t roll higher than 4.

Either way, besides this, it works like a normal Check, except that, if there is a Stress penalty, you're going to roll 1d4! for each time you split your dice but only use the highest if applicable.

Attacking & Defending

All that being said, Attack and Defence Checks are treated as one action, even if you're doing both on the same second. That means that they're going to share the same base dice (barring other circumstances that will be discussed more in Chapter 1.3: Combat). If you're Multitasking with another action, for example, when you split the die, one die goes to the other action and the other goes to both Attacking and Defending.

So, let's say that you're climbing a cliff when you're suddenly attacked by harpies. You don't want to fall but you also want to fight back so you assign a d12! to your Climb Check and a d8! to Attack and Defense Checks.

Not Paying Attention

Multitasking is also used when someone isn't paying attention, such as a guard who's on watch but he's been there for several hours at this point so he's not doing it so closely. In this case, start at a d10!, lowering it further as needs be.

Stress

Working under pressure can get to you. When your crew mates are relying on you to pull out a win, it can mess with your head. Your character can become stressed under these circumstances and stress can later manifest in odd behaviors and penalties.

Characters can gain Stress in a lot of ways and those are discussed in their various sections - taking damage, making Skill Checks, etc. - the only one we’ll go over here is from acting against your character’s Characteristics, especially when ignoring them would be a huge benefit.

Mental States

Your Nerve is divided into 4 categories which are, in order, UNSURE (U), TENSE (T), SHAKEN (S), BREAKING (Br). Each of these make up a fourth of your Nerve. There's technically also "Never" but that's reserved for special occasions and is exceptional: it represents beyond Breaking.

These are a way to easily communicate how your character is doing (both player-to-player and GM-to-player) but crossing certain categories have additional penalties.

Panic Threshold

As stated, the more Stress you take, the harder it is to function.

Your baseline Panic Threshold is set to Breaking: hitting that Stress Category means that you roll your Defense, all Skill, and Confrontation Checks on 1 lower die. This stacks with Fatigue.

Beyond Breaking & Mental Breakdowns

A character whose stress has gone beyond their Nerve is suffering from a mental breakdown. They seek to remove themselves from the stressful situation as quickly as possible. This is specific to the situation, but the character has lost all rationality and so will take the most direct route in their mind, even if it exposes them.

If they survive, their confidence is shattered and they’ll find themselves a more neurotic person: they’re permanently locked out of the highest Stress Category available to them and their Panic Threshold drops by one category as well. Going beyond the last category results in another Mental Break. This does stack so the first time, they Panic at Shaken and have a Mental Break at Breaking; the second time they Panic at Tense and have a Mental Break at Shaken and so on. If they go below Unsure, they’ve completely lost control of their neurosis and retire, a broken man with only sad stories to tell as the rest of his tired life slowly melts away.

To deal with the trauma of what they went through, they also gain a Flaw of their choosing that represents how the character is trying to cope.

For example, a character that’s having a mental breakdown in the middle of the fight drops their weapon, stands up, turns, and runs for the nearest exit at full speed: exposing themselves to the enemy.

Or they might simply drop their weapon and plead for mercy – refusing to defend themselves.

Healing Stress

Stress heals slowly over time: the more stress you have, the longer it takes to heal. This is charted using Relaxation.

When a character’s Relaxation reaches their current stress, subtract their current Stress from their Relaxation and then lower their stress by 1, making high stress exponentially more difficult to heal. If your Stress and Damage goes to 0, your Relaxation also drops to 0 and you can't gain any more.

But there are a lot of ways to gain Relaxation.

Taking a Breather

Taking a Breather heals both Stress and Damage and so it talked about in the Taking a Breather section below but it's a method you shouldn't forget to use.

Rallying

You can also Rally someone (or yourself) to heal Stress, which is handled via Confrontation, however, it can only be done when a person Panics and again when someone Breaks and it takes a number of seconds equal to 1 * the number of Arguments the person who is rallying is using.

A Rally Confrontation heals 1 Stress per 5 points over the opposition’s total.

Taking Time Off

A person is considered to spend 8 hours sleeping, with the rest of the day (16 hours), doing things.

However, for each hour your character takes to rest (read a good book, indulge in a hobby, take a bath, etc.), they get 1 Relaxation.

By spending silver on the finer things, you can increase that amount. To heal 1 Stress, you spend an amount of sc equal to your current Stress.

So, going out to dinner for 2 hours would normally give you 2 Relaxation, but going to a fancy dinner and spending 43 sc on it, gives you 45 instead - going from 9 Stress to 0.

Hobbies

A character can also benefit from specific hobbies, which can be deduced from their Characteristics.

You can only gain the benefit of a Characteristic once per week for a number of hours equal to its Rank with each hour spent on the hobby giving an additional +1 Relaxation (since it combines with Taking Time Off), although you can stack multiple Characteristics so next time you go out drinking, bring a friend you have Relationship to along to hang out.

Characteristics

Characteristics can also help you heal Stress.

If a Characteristic could hinder you, you can subtract its Rank from a Check. If you do, you don’t take the normal d4! of Stress and you also heal Stress equal to its Rank.

The exception to this are Flaws: in that case, you can decide what you want the penalty to the Check to be and you heal Stress equal to twice that penalty instead.

So, Whiskers is trying to pop a potion in the middle of combat. The Shaky Hands Injury doesn't say anything about that specific case but it would be reasonable that this Injury would make it harder for him to pop the cork. Since its Rank 3, he takes an additional 3 seconds to do so and heals 3 Stress.

If you take an action that aligns with your Characteristics that is a bad idea but wouldn't be a Check (like deciding to go down the blood-soaked tunnel that you can hear screaming echoing from), you can heal 1d4! Stress. You can heal Stress multiple times from the same action if it results in multiple penalties (like getting attacking in the blood-soaked tunnel and realizing your out of rations because you got trapped by a portcullis that slammed down behind you).

Creature Comforts

Finally, you can indulge in creature comforts.

This is similar to spending silver while taking time off, but these represent permanent purchases, like getting a really nice mattress for your bunk.

In this case, once a day, whenever a Description would apply, for every hour its useful to you, you get an extra Relaxation for each Description that applies, up to a number of hours equal to the Description Rank.

So, if you’re hiking for one hour and you have extra-padded (+3), tailored (+1) boots, you would get 2 Relaxation, however if you went hiking for 4 hours, you would get a total of 4 extra Relaxation (since tailored caps out at 1 hours and extra-padded would cap after 3).

Vitality

Vitality represents how close you are to passing out and dying. It is both your health and stamina. As you take damage, you will get closer to death and gain more penalties based on your Fatigue Threshold.

Physical States

Vitality is divided into 4 categories which are, in order, HURT (H), BLOODIED (B), WOUNDED (W), CRITICAL (C). Each of these make up a fourth of your Vitality. There's technically also "Never" but that's reserved for special occasions and is exceptional: it represents beyond Critical.

These are a way to easily communicate a fighter’s health (both player-to-player and GM-to-player) but crossing certain categories has additional penalties.

Fatigue Threshold

As stated, the more damage you take, the harder the fight becomes.

Your baseline Fatigue Threshold is set to Critical: hitting that Wound Category means that you roll your Defense, all Skill, and Confrontation Checks on 1 lower die. This stacks with Panic.

Wearing armor, shields, and carrying lots of stuff may lower your Fatigue Threshold to a less injured category (e.g. Bloodied or Hurt), meaning you become fatigued more quickly. In a pinch, it may be wise to drop your shield and/or other gear to increase your Fatigue Threshold.

Note that if this would take you above Hurt, you're always Fatigued. If you go above 'always', every Category you would go pasted that, you shrink all your non-Attack Check Die by an additional size. So, if you go 3 Categories above Hurt, you shrink from a d20 to a d8!.

Changing Equipment While Wounded

Since Fatigue Threshold is partly determined by the shield you're using, a quick way to up your Fatigue Threshold is to simply drop your shield.

Now, the flip side of this is that if you become MORE encumbered while wounded, you could get Fatigued earlier.

Beyond Critical

A character whose total damage is beyond Critical is bleeding out and dying. They fall unconscious and take an additional 1 damage / second: when they reach their Con in the negative, they’re dead. This can be temporarily stopped by another person stemming the bleeding. This requires no Check but the person is considered Multitasking for as long as they’re doing so, unless they’re also trying to bind the wounds.

If the bleeding is able to be stopped via a Check, the character has survived but with a permanent scar. This scar takes the form of an Injury: either a random, new one (at a quarter of its maximum Rank) or doubling the Rank of an old one. Rules and tables for Injuries are found in Chapter 2.5: Burdens & Injuries. You don’t gain the Integrity Injuries gained this way.

The only time a bleeding-out character can remain conscious is if they’re moving towards their goal. In this case, they can continue to keep fighting, however, they continue to bleed out. When they hit their Con in the negative, they can keep fighting but they’re still dead and nothing can bring them back. They keep fighting until the end of the combat or until they reach double their Vitality in damage.

If they stop to get their bleeding stemmed or wounds patched up, they fall unconscious.

Healing Damage

Whenever a character takes damage, they record a wound.

After a number of days of rest equal to a wound’s current damage, you remove 1 damage from that wound.

This means that a 3-damage wound would take 3 days of rest to become a 2-damage wound, which would take 2 days to become a 1-damage wound, and so on.

The upside is that all wounds heal simultaneously: if you have 10 3-damage wounds, it will only take you 6 days to completely heal.

There are other ways to speed up healing as well:

Taking a Breather

Taking a Breather heals both Stress and Damage and so it talked about in the Taking a Breather section below but it's a method you shouldn't forget to use.

First Aid

By administering first aid within the first 10 minutes of a wound being inflicted, you lower the damage by 1.

You can only do this once per wound, however.

Medicine

By administering the proper medication, it can greatly speed up healing, removing 1 day from each wound for each point of damage, although there’s a minimum of 1 day.

So, for that 3-damage wound, it would only take 2 days to get to 2 damage, and 1 day to get to 1 damage.

Resting & Nursing

You can also take time off and not put a lot of stress and pressure on yourself: allowing your body to focus on healing.

In this case, if you have no Stress, every point of Relaxation gained that day is taken off the days to heal instead, however, you can only heal 1 damage a day and it only applies to 1 wound.

Fallen Comrades

Watching your friends die around you or abandon you is extremely Stressful, even if you’re winning.

Each time a side loses a quarter of their original number (either through dying, going unconscious, or running away), those remaining take d10! Stress.

Taking a Breather

Pausing and resting for 30 minutes heals half your damage and Stress (rounded down), however, you can't go the below your lowest Nerve/Vitality Category + 1 for the number of Breathers you've previously taken.

So, the first time you Take a Breather, if you have 40 Vitality, you couldn't go below 0, since that would take you out of Hurt (Unhurt + 0 previous Breathers). The third time you Took a Breather, you couldn't go below 30, since that would take you out of Wounded (Unhurt + 2 previous Breathers).

If you manage to go below a Nerve/Vitality Category due to other types of healing/relaxation, that Category is 'unlocked'. So, if your minimum was Shaken, by healing enough Stress to drop you down to Unsure, that becomes your new minimum (effectively reducing the number of previous Breathers by 2)

If the rest is broken for any reason, you lose all benefit from it and it also does count against you.

Integrity

Integrity, or Grit for short, is a measure of how not only how a person measures their own integrity but also how other people see them. Integrity isn’t about doing the commonsense thing; it’s about doing the heroic thing - being true to yourself. People who run from a fight, even when outnumbered, lose Integrity. People who let others talk bad about them, and don’t get even, lose Integrity.

The question of Integrity isn’t a question of good and evil; many evil men have lots of Integrity due to being true to themselves. It’s more about being true to your inner self.

Categories & Bonuses

Integrity has its benefits. Confidence allows you to push yourself a little bit more than you otherwise would: tapping into mental reserves to snatch victory from the jaws of defeat. During the game, you can spend your Integrity points to influence anything, rolls or static metrics, whenever you want, on a 1-to-1 basis.

Doing so lowers your Integrity but might save your character’s life. So, if you roll a 13 and your opponent rolls a 14, you could spend 3 Integrity to beat their total. Likewise, if an enemy deals 8 damage to you and you have 6 Integrity, you could spend all of it and only take 2 damage.

Additionally, each category has a Grit Dice, which will be talked about in the next section.

Integrity cannot go above 25 or below 0. The size of the Grit Die is also determined at the beginning of session and doesn’t change during play, even if the category does.

No Integrity

0 - 5 Integrity, No Grit Die

The lowest category, this person is unsure that they have what it takes to be a hero and so every decision is laced with hesitation and uncertainty.

Little Integrity

6 - 10 Integrity, d4!

This character hasn’t really proved anything, one way or another. Others and herself are waiting for her to make her mark on the world.

Average Integrity

11 - 15 Integrity, d6!

Having reached this category, the character has shown that they have potential and some follow through.

Lots of Integrity

16 - 20 Integrity, d8!

She has now separated herself from the common rabble and can stand a head above the crowd.

Full of Integrity

21 - 25 Integrity, d10!

This rare sliver represents those who can perfectly balance being ahead of others but not so much so that they invoke the ire of those below them as well as being confident while not being cocky.

Grit Dice

Fundementally, Grit Dice can be used in the same way as Integrity, where it can be spent like Integrity, only, instead of spending it on a 1-to-1 modifier, when you spend a Grit Die, you roll the corresponding die and add or subtract it.

Unless otherwise stated, uses of your Grit Dice are cross session so there's a place on your character sheet to record how many you have banked. As a new character, you'll start with 1 but as you go up Integrity Categories, you'll gain more.

GMs are also free to reward Grit Die for particularly heroic behavior.

Dice Size Hierarchy

Bonfire plays around a lot with dice sizes, so it’s important to know what dice to grab in those cases.

Dice sizes go as follows:

d2 → d3 → d4 → d6 → d8 → d10 → d12 → d20

If a d4 is exploding and drops to a d3 or lower, it stops exploding.

If the dice size increases past a d20, you’ll add an extra dice starting at a d4. Further dice sizes will increase this smaller dice. This can continue ad nauseum, adding more and more dice, but no matter how many dice there are, they’re always considered to be rolled on 1 dice.

So, increasing a d20 7 times to a 2d20! + d4! is still considered to be 1 dice.

Perfect Rolls on Shrunken Die

There are several places where rollings a natural 20 on a d20 offers additional bonuses, but your dice might be a smaller size for some reason (such as when you're defending while being flanked).

In those cases, you can still gain the benefits of natural 20 but only if the dice explode enough that the total value of those dice is equal to or over 20.

So, if you're rolling a d12! where you would normally roll a d20!, you'd need to get 1 explosion with the second roll getting higher than an 8.

Likewise, if you're rolling a d4! instead of a d20!, you'd need 5 explosions to get you there.

Round Against the Player

Unless otherwise stated, you round against the players.
