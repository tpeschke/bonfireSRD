The GM’s Best Friend

These rules are supplemental material to help GMs with common things that might come up during play.

However, like all rules, they’re not required and, if you find yourself in a game where one of these applies but you want to keep play going, use the GM’s Friend instead, which is:

If your players get a bonus or penalty and you don’t remember what it is or isn’t covered, give them a +/-2 as appropriate.

Falling

When your character falls, they take damage based on how far that surpasses their DR from armor, however, they can do somethings to help their decent.

After you find the effective distance you're falling, roll 1d4!. If below 40 ft and you roll equal to or under the 10s digit on the height you fell, you sustain an injury and take that many Wound Categories worth of damage, spread over 1d12! Wounds. The injury has a 50% chance of lowering your Movement Category by 1 step per Wound Category of damage you took or lowering all arm-related Checks by 1 dice size per Wound Category. This penalty is healed as the damage is healed.

If above 40 ft, any result causes an injury as noted above and you take Wound Categories according to what you roll but, also, if you roll equal to or under the 10s digit - 3 on the height you fell, your character dies. In cases where the Wound Category damage is enough to bring you beyond Critical, but you don't roll for auto-death, you're just knocked you out and start to bleed out as normal.

Illumination

For sake of simplicity, there’s five categorizations of how much light there is in an area:

Blinding

This is like staring into the sun. It's very painful and not very pleasant.

Assuming that you cover your eyes, you'll be acting as if you're blind. Even then, a moment's exposure even temporarily results in a d10! second delay as you try to recover. If you continue to expose yourself to this level of illumination, you'll be unable to act at all and will do blind in a number of seconds equal to your Con.

Bright

This is the level of light of starting into a fire or just having your eyes near it. It sucks but it probably won't do permanent damage.

This doesn't really affect your aim or anything like that, but it does make it difficult to focus on things, delaying any action by 1d4! seconds as you try and squint and see what you're doing.

Skills Checks that require sight are also rolled on a dice size 1 size smaller and take longer.

Normal

This is normal daylight and occurs no operating penalties.

Dim

This the equivalent of a dark room or nighttime. There’s some visibility; the character can make out shapes and shadows but no fine details.

In melee combat, those in dim-lighting defend and attack with a dice size 1 size smaller and using ranged weapons is even more of challenge. However, if you insist, you’ll be attacking with a dice size 4 sizes smaller (normally, a d6!).

Skills Checks that require sight are also rolled on a dice size 1 size smaller.

Additionally, Sprinting is impossible without acquiring a Stress per second moved.

Dark

This is pitch black with the character not even being able to see their hands in front of their faces. This is a dungeon after the torch goes out.

Combat becomes difficult as anyone in darkness defends and attacks with a dice size 4 sizes smaller (normally, a d6!) And ranged combat is right out, but, if you insist, you’ll be attacking with a dice size 5 sizes smaller (normally, a d4!).

Skills Checks that require sight are also rolled on a dice size 4 sizes smaller (normally, a d6!).

And moving above Crawling speed is extremely nerve racking, netting a Stress per Speed Category per second. So, moving at a Run for 3 seconds gives you 9 Stress (3 for the Run for 3 seconds).

Long-Term Exhaustion

Long-Term Exhaustion
Need Rationing Going Without
Food 1d6! halved damage, 1d6! halved Stress, rounded up 1d6! Stress, 1d6! Damage
Water 1d6! Damage 2d6! Damage
Sleep 1d6! Stress 2d6! Stress

Your character isn’t immortal, or at least, they didn’t start out that way, and so still needs to fulfil their basic needs of food, drink, and sleep. Without those basic requirements, he may find himself a bit more lethargic or, worse case scenario, dead.

There are three things your character needs to take care of, as mentioned above: Food, Water, and Sleep, and three levels associated with each, Normal, Rationing, and Going Without, which related to how much you have of each.

If a person sleeps the normal amount (assumed to be 8 hours), eats a healthy amount of food, and drinks a good amount of water, they take no damage or Stress.

However, if they cut back on any of three (called rationing) or don't get any sleep, food, or water (called going without), they take the associate damage and/or Stress as noted by the table.

This is on 1 Wound per need but does stack: having no food and water gives you 3d6! damage and d6! Stress.

Additionally, a Wound doesn't heal until the associated need is returned to normal.

Small- & Large-Sized Creatures

Obviously, a smaller creature requires less food while a larger one requires more, so Small sized creatures half each threshold while Large creatures double it.

Changing Size

Since it can happen, what with all this Weird running around, the benefits for increasing size are as follows: +1 to Measure, -3 to Defense, +5 to Vitality & Knock-Back Threshold. The modifiers are reversed for shrinking sizes, although you can't lower your Vitality by more than half its base.

Stat Score Damage

Stat Score damage occurs in several places and it’s fairly straightforward. Then why have this section, you ask? As a quick aside to keep the game moving.

The sudden change in ability is incredibly unnerving, meaning that until the person can stop, take a breath and re-adjust to their new reality (meaning the player can adjust the stats on his character sheet without breaking off Combat, a Confrontation, a Challenge or any narrative tension the GM has built up), he gets a penalty of 1 for each point lost to anything that Stat Score influences.

So, a -3 to Dexterity would net a temporary -3 to Attack, and Defense, all while reducing the Confrontation Die by 3 sizes, if applicable.

If the amount drained is more than the score, the person becomes appropriately incapacitated, but not dead, until the Stat Score damage is healed. To remove 1 -1, you need to rest a number of days equal to the current penalty but all stats heal concurrently.
