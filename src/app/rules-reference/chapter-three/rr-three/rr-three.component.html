Introduction

What is combat?

Combat is what happens when one side wants to kill the other and are trying to do it right now, on a second-by-second basis.

I don’t think I really need to define combat because, as a naturally violence species, combat often comes second nature to us but I thought it would be an interesting exercise.

Starting a Combat

The Clock

The Clock begins at 1, and then progresses to 2, 3, 4 etc, ad infinitum. When the Clock matches a character’s Initiative, they go. Each of these numbers represent 1 second of time passing. What second a character's next action is referred to as their initiative (or "where they are on the Clock")

This can be a difficult when you first start so we recommend that you start with only running two or three enemies until you get the hang of it.

Initiative

Initiative Die
Dice Readiness
d12 Not Ready at All

d4 Within-the-Hour Combat

Each side rolls an initiative die. The lower the die, the more prepared they are for the attack, with the instigator going on second 1. The initiative’s die is based on the general combat preparedness of the company, which is generally based on how likely they think danger is.

The table provided gives you the extreme ends of the spectrum as well as the default but the initiative die can be anywhere in between.

Surprise

Those whose initiative hasn't come up are surprised and, on second 1 of the combat can choose to do one of three things: contine what they’re doing, stand still (if they’re not going faster than a Jog), or flee away from the danger (if there is a clear escape route, otherwise, this isn't an option).

Whatever they choose, they have to keep doing that action until their Initiative comes up: They can take no other actions, not even to defend themselves, thus they roll their defense die on a dice size 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

You can lower your Initiative (thus lowering the amount of time you're surpised) by either making a lot of noise or by being hit.

If someone is making a lot of noise, for whatever reason, you lower everyone's (enemy and ally alike) initiative by 2. Multiple sources of noise doesn't stack.

If the surprised person is hit, the pain quickly brings them into the reality of the situation, and they may act 2 seconds later (or before that if their initiative was the next second).

This can be done by either an enemy hitting you with their weapon or by a friend, running up and slapping you across the face.

Ambushes

Surprise happens regardless of whether or not the danger was visible and known to the characters but in cases of ambush, where the danger is unknown until suddenly its not, the GM can call for an applicable Check, which represents characters cluing into subtle clues that the manure is about to hit the windmill.

Each Boon gives a -2 to the Initiative roll while each Complication gives a +2 to the roll.

For example; Kelso, Bran Redhand, and Dalsini the Odd are traveling through the wilderness. Kelso, being clever, asked about the route they were traveling before and got rumors that there’s been an uptick in bandit attacks in the area so the company is wary while traveling.

Their second day out, they cross a bridge; unknowingly, the ambush point of the single bandit that’s been causing so much trouble.

They’re not taking care to be particularly quiet or stealthy as they’re traveling by road so the bandit, in his hiding place, sees them coming from awhile away. As they start to cross the bridge (Redhand in front, Kelso in back, and Dalsini in the middle), the bandit sneaks out and attacks Kelso from behind in an ambush.

The bandit goes on count 1 with their attack since he's completely ready for them. The company would normally roll d8s since they're traveling in a generally dangerous place, however, because they're on the lookout for bandit attacks, they instead roll on d6s since, you know, they're getting attacked by a bandit.

Redhand has a +2 Initiative Modifier but got 2 Boons on his Warfare Check (since he's using his knowledge of battle to help spot potential ambush sites) so, when he rolls a 1, his total is -1, so he goes on second 1. Dalsini has a +0 Initiative Modifier and got 1 Boon on his Check and his roll was 6, so he goes on second 4.

Finally, Kelso got 2 Complications on his Check and with an -2 Initiative Modifier and roll of 6, he goes on second 8, although, if the bandit hits, he’ll be able to act on second 3.

On second 1, Kelso stops to figure out what’s going on until his initiative, while Dalsini, being a weak weird-adept, decides he’s going to run for it, so starts running towards the other end of the bridge - away from the present danger.

Redhand, however, leaps into action on second 1, drawing his sword as he rushes to his friend’s aid.

Combat Basics

Taking Actions

Every action takes a certain amount of time. On a character’s Initiative, they declare their action and are told how long that action will take. After that time, the action is complete.

So, if a character wants to walk across a room, which will take 8 seconds, and his Initiative is 5, once the Clock reaches 5, he starts walking and once it reaches 13, he’s gotten the other side of the room.

At any point, you may choose to forfeit your action and change your course. If, when halfway across the room (at second 9), the character sees that his buddy is in trouble, he can immediately decide that, no, he’d rather be there, his character stops walking across the room and instead heads over to help his friend.

The time it takes most actions can be rationalized from common experience but here are a few very common scenarios with their times added to help with arbitration mid-game. Note that these times represent someone in a hurry and under some sort of duress

Movement

While, for the most part, you’ll be using your common sense to determine how long things will take, to unify at least one thing, movement rates are given below.

A person may move up 2 movement categories, starting at a Walk, or down 2 in a second.

Movement
Speed
Stroll/Crawl Walk Jog Run Sprint
2.5 ft / sec 5 ft / sec 10 ft / sec 15 ft / sec 20 ft / sec

For every 10 seconds of Running, you take damage. This starts at a d4! but increases by 1 dice size every 10 seconds, capping at a d20!.

For Sprinting, this happens every 5 seconds.

This damage is reduced by your Endurance (or Athletics) Rank, down to a minimum of 0. This is modified by your armor's Skill Adjustment penalty because sprinting in heavy armor is hard.

You can push your character as hard as you'd like, continuing to take damage until they die from fatigue, or are forced to stop due to failing a Trauma Check, but if you take damage from Running or Sprinting and you stop, you can't Run or Sprint again until you've waited 10 seconds for every time your damage dice was increased.

Also, moving from a Run to Sprint, or vice versa, doesn’t reset that count, so don’t think you can skip taking damage by moving from a Sprint to a Run, you cheeky devil.

Zones of Engagement

There are 3 positions that you can have relative to your enemy. These positions aren’t reciprocal by nature: you can be threatened by someone and not have them be threatened by you.

Unengaged

This is basically the default. You are too far away for them to reasonable do anything.

Threatened

This means that you’re within 5 feet + your opponent’s weapon’s measure. From here, if you move faster than a Jog and/or if they’re within half their weapon Recovery, they can attack you.

If you go from Engaged to Threatened, your weapon initiative doesn't reset, so if you become Engaged again for any reason before your weapon's Recovery, you have to wait the rest of your Recovery to attack again.

If you go from Threatened to Unengaged, you need to wait half your weapon's Recovery + your Initiative modifier to reorient yourself before you attack again (minimum 1 second). If someone Threatens you in that time, you go back to having to wait your full Recovery before attacking (minus the time you already waited).

Engaged

You’ve closed the distance and can actually make an attack. If both combatants aren't surprised, the person with the highest weapon measure attacks first. If both weapons are exactly the same measure, the attacks happen simultaneously.

Note that this is only for the first attack: if subsequent attacks happen on the same second, they happen simultaneously, meaning that two people with different measure, attacking on the same second, can kill each other at the same time.

If you're Surprised when you become Engaged, you're unable to attack back and, once you leave surprise, you must wait your weapon's Recovery before attacking.

If you move from being Engaged to being Threatened while moving faster than a Walk, you're using the Flee Tactic.

Melee Combat

Melee Attack & Defense

Basic melee attacks always roll a base d20! + any attack modifiers, while defenders roll a d20! + any defense modifiers

Perfect Attacks & Defenses

A Perfect Attack or Defense is one that puts you in a better position to win. A Perfect Attack occurs on an unmodified roll of 20 on your Attack roll, while a Perfect Defense is an unmodified 20 on your Defense roll, but only if your final totals are still greater than your opponent’s. If not, the attack still hits for Perfect Attacks and you still dodge the blow for Perfect Defenses but you don’t gain the auxiliary benefits.

If both those qualifiers are met, you gain the following benefits on top of the normal ones:

Perfect Attack

You gain a free automatically successful, well-executed, Unarmed Combat attack. If you have Ranks in the Unarmed Skill Suite or any of its Advanced Skills, you may roll for the additional benefit, although, even on fumble, this doesn't affect the normal bonus for delievering a perfect attack.

Perfect Defense

You gain a chance to riposte, gaining a free full attack the next second if you’re wielding a melee weapon. This attack doesn’t affect your current count.

Near Perfect Attacks & Defenses

An unmodified roll of 19 on either Attack or Defense results in a Near Perfect Attack or Defense, where you may Shift for no penalty (or Shift twice that second if you were doing so already) and, for attacks, gain an extra d4! to damage (which doesn't count as additional dice for /d DR), or, for defenses gained a free riposte with your off-hand weapon if it’s smaller than your Size, a sidearm, or shield, or an unarmed attack if you have at least 1 hand free.

Fumbles

On the flip side, rolling an unmodified 1 on your Attack or Defense roll, whose total is also lower than your opponent's, results in a 'fumble', with your opponent getting a free attack on the next second that doesn't reset their next attack.

Fumbling when your opponent gets a Perfect Attack or Defense results in the normal bonuses and the free attack while both sides rolling a fumble results in the person with the highest total getting their way (so, if the attacker has the highest total, they hit) but no free attack for either.

Dealing Damage

Once both sides have rolled and added all modifiers, compare the totals against each other.

If the defender’s is equal to or greater than the attacker, the attack misses.

Parrying

If a melee attack roll is within the Parry Threshold of the defender’s total and they're not surprised, the defender may choose to deflect some of the incoming attack.

The attacker rolls their damage and subtracts the shield or weapon’s DR (2/d DR for weapons), which subjects them to a chance of breakage, and then their armor’s DR. Any leftover is still done to the defender. For piercing weapons, the /d DR of the shield or weapon is tripled.

So, a defender with a Parry Threshold of 4, and a total of 10 for their defense roll vs an attack roll of 14, could choose to parry the blow, taking less damage.

However, if it’s not, the attack lands and the attacker rolls his weapon’s damage die and adds any appropriate damage modifiers. The defender than subtracts their Damage Reduction and the remaining total is subtracted from their Vitality and added to their list of wounds.

Damage Reduction

Armor allows you to reduce the amount of damage taken. This is often times shorted to DR. There are two types: Static, noted by just a number, and Per Die, noted by a number /D.

Static simply subtracts that number from the damage and is done.

/D subtracts that number from each dice but can't bring any individual dice below 0, however, any extra DR is removed from the Str Damage Mod. So, if someone is attacking you with a slashing weapon (2d6!) with a +2 Str Damage Mod, and you have armor that gives you 4/D, and you roll a 5 and a 3. you do 2 damage.

When you roll, your GM will tell you if the DR is /D, they'll tell you what it is so you can quickly do the math.

Either way, if the attacker chooses to remain in engaged, he then adds his weapon’s Recovery to the current second on the Clock. Once the Clock reaches that new number, he may attack again.

When you parry with a shield or weapon, or when your armor absorbs damage, if the damage after DR is greater than a certain threshold (which can be modified Shield Skills, see Chapter 2.5: Skills), the item gains 1 Wear, which leads to a greater chance of it breaking.

If it doubles or triples this threshold, you gain double and triple the Wear, and so on.

More on Wear can be found in Chapter 2.6: Equipment.

Knock-Backs

If the pre-DR damage is higher than your enemy’s Vitality Size Modifier + 1 for every M worth of items you're carrying, the attack also moves them back 5 feet. If it’s twice the damage needed, they get knocked back 10 feet, and so on. Anything above a single Knock Back also knocks the enemy prone.

If a person is Engaged with someone who is Knock-Back, they can immediately close the distance, keeping the same Zone of Engagement.

Ranged Combat

Ranged Weapon Firing Break Down

If you’re wondering what you’re doing at any given second when firing a ranged weapon: here’s a breakdown for you.

So, if you already loading and cranked (drawing) your crossbow, it would only take 5 seconds to fire it.

Missile Retrieval

This is drawing the missile from its quiver or pouch. For firearms, that means drawing out the powder and bullets.

Fuse

This is the process of lighting the fuse. This can be dropped to 1 second but increases the chance of backfiring from 1 to 5.

Loading

This is knocking the arrow or loading the bullet and powder in the barrel.

Draw

This is drawing back the bow, cranking the crossbow, or cocking your arm for the throw. This can be prepped ahead of time for the belly- and crossbows but not the composite, long, or war bows

Aiming

This is actually sighting your target and lining up the shot.

Firing

This is pulling the trigger or loosing the arrow.

Advanced Training that lowers Recovery take from Missile Retrieval, Fuse, Loading, Drawing, and Aiming evenly but they can never reduce a single category to below 1 second. So, the minimum firing Recovery for a Composite Bow is 5 seconds.

Ranged Attack & Defense

Ranged attacks and defenses function identical to melee attacks and defenses (including Flanking), only get a few additional bonuses and penalties.

The first is that range (of course) and the second is movement.

When defending against a ranged attack, you get a +2 for each Movement Category above standing still. So, if you're getting shot at, get the lead out.

Perfect Attacks

A Perfect Attack is one that puts you in a better position to win. A Perfect Attack occurs on an unmodified roll of 20 on your Attack roll.

If you’re firing a ranged weapon, you managed to hit a sensitive area, lowering their max movement speed by one category, minimum Crawl speed. This continues until that wound is completely healed.

Near-Perfect Attacks

An unmodified roll of 19 on your Attack results in a Near Perfect Attack, giving you an extra d4! to damage.

Cover

Cover is anything that gets between you and the person trying to shoot at you.

For every 5% of the target covered, you get a +1. So, 25% would be a +5, 50% would be a +10 and so on. At 100% covered (+20), you can rule that no roll is needed: the attack simply misses.

For people or monsters, start at 50% cover (+10). Each difference in size shrinks or increases that cover buy 15% (+/- 3).

If you’re crouching behind cover, you double the bonus but are limited to Crawling or Strolling. If the bonus goes above +20, you’re considered completely covered and all attacks automatically miss.

Firing Into Melee

Starting at the person closest to the archer or gunner, roll their Cover dice but subtract that person's Defense modifiers and subtract any Cover that is unique to them (such as a shield's Cover). This has a minimum of zero.

This total is added to the original target's Defense, just like normal Cover. If the person's Cover is enough to turn a hit into a miss, the attack hints them instead of the target in which case you roll damage against them; they don't get a defense roll.

If they were not hit, then move to the next person giving Cover and resolve in the same manner.

If the next person in line would also gain Cover from the previous possibly targets, add their Cover rolls to the total as well. If not, just add the new person's Cover roll to the target's Defense.

If there are any objects between the archer and their original target, resolve them according to their relative position to the intervening combatants. So, if a tree is between two fighters, resolve the closest fighter, than the tree, than the other fighter.

If none of the interposing combatants were hit, resolve any Cover the original target has (such as a shield). If that didn't help, the missile landed true: roll damage against the original target.

If the ranged attack was a miss, see the Ranged Misses section below.

Dealing Damage

Dealing damage with a ranged weapon works almost exactly like dealing damage with a melee weapon, however, you’re unable to Parry a ranged attack (that’s what Cover is for)

Ranged Misses

On a miss with a ranged weapon, the missile doesn’t magically disappear into oblivion; there’s a good chance it might hit someone else.

In the case of a miss, roll a d4. An odd result means it goes left, an even result means it goes right.

The difference between the attack and defense roll determines how many feet away the missile strayed.

Draw an imaginary line between the archer and that point. Everyone within 5ft of that line needs to roll a defense vs the original attack roll, starting with the person closest to the archer and ending with the furthest possible target, ignoring the original target.

Inanimate objects may provide enough cover to be considered targets in both Firing into Melee and Ranged Misses, such as a large boulder or tree. Resolve using the normal rules.

Misc. Combat Rules

Facing & Fighting Multiple People

The likelihood is that at some point you’ll want to be flanking or someone with want to flank you, to try and gain an advantage.

Defending right in front of you is pretty easy and so brooks no penalties, however, every 45 degrees off that the defender lowers their Defense dice by 1 size, with those being attacked from directly behind defend on a dice size 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!) since they take advantage of a huge blind spot.

This penalty can be mitigated through the use of shields. Small shields allow you to defend against an additional assailant, while Medium and Large ones allow you to defend against an extra 2 and 3, respectively. You still defend on a reduced Defense Die but you do get your shield's Parry to the roll. This only applies attacks coming from the side the shield is on and can never affect an attack directly from behind but otherwise, the player can decide who they're blocking.

A maximum of 8 people at a time can effectively attack person at once, assuming that they’re out in the open. After that, every assailant’s attack die is lowered by 1 size per extra person.

Trauma

If someone does more damage after DR than your Trauma Threshold, this triggers a Trauma Check: that person rolls a d20 and tries to get under half their Con stat.

Failure means that your character has been overcome with pain to the point that he’s unable to act, leaving him completely defenseless for a number of seconds equal to how much he failed by times 3.

Double Trauma

A character that gets dealt their full Vitality in damage in a single blow has suffered a grievous wound - the scar of which, if he survives, will be with him until the day he dies.

When this happens, the trauma is too much, and they simply die where they stand if they fail their check. Even if they succeed, they’re still suffer Trauma for the next 30 seconds but gain a wicked looking scar.

Battle scars are marks of pride and honor. Characters who become scared gain a Rank 1 Description “Battle Scars”. If they would get this Description again, increase that Rank by 1.

Coup De Grace

A helpless enemy is one completely at your mercy.

If a character is completely helpless, either from failing a Trauma check, being bound, or deep asleep, a character can kill the target instantly.

This move takes 10 seconds to complete as it takes a bit of positioning to pull off. The character’s whose throat is being slit gives out a cry of pain as they expire.

Equipment

Your kit can really pull you through in a pinch and these are catchall rules for the use of equipment that doesn’t already come with auxiliary benefits, such as weapons (damage) and armor (DR).

On an applicable Check or roll, you add an extra d6! + the Rank of any applicable Descriptions the equipment might have. If there are multiple pieces of equipment, you increase the dice size by 1 per piece and add their Descriptions as well. More on equipment Descriptions is found in Chapter 2.6: Equipment.

If the equipment doesn't really suite the purpose you're using it for, like trying to pry open a door with a weapon, you apply a d4! instead, although the same rules apply for stacking pieces of equipment.

Finally, if there is a specific use for the piece of Equipment (like weapons and armor), you can redeem an applicable Description to gain a temporary bonus to a roll or static modifier.

So, if you have Fine-Steel (+2) armor, if you get hit, you can increase the armor's DR by 2 for that damage roll specifically.

Redeeming this bonus causes the tool degrades, giving it (and any other equipment you use) 1 Wear, whether you succeed or not. More on Wear can be found in Chapter 2.6: Equipment.

Tactics

These rules add another layer of strategy to combat and should be implemented as soon as you have the basics down.

Each Tactic, at the top, when you can employ them and which other Tactics they can be combined with which. For “On Defense” and “On Attack”, the Tactic has to be declared before you roll.

Once committed to, Tactics can only be switched out on your next Attack or when you are no longer Engaged. So, if you’re Active Dodging, you have to commit to that Tactic on your defense but cannot stop Active Dodging until your next attack.

Active Dodging

On Defense

Compatible Attacks Hold Back

Compatible Defenses All except Brace Yourself, and Step Back

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

This move relies on actively focusing on dodging against incoming attacks.

A defender commits to how much he wants to focus on dodging incoming attacks, both melee and ranged, choosing from a bonus to his defense of either +3 or +5, but netting an equal penalty to his attacks (-3 or -5).

Additionally, this increases the range for Near Perfect Defenses by 1, so you score them on a base natural 18 - 19.

But focusing on defense does slow you down, and you are limited to 5 ft / second of movement.

Choking Up On Your Weapon

On Defense

Compatible Attacks All except Careful Aiming, Charge, and In Their Guard

Compatible Defenses All except Fend Off, and Long Guard

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

When you’ve choked up on your weapon, your weapon’s Measure is halved, and you add that number, rounded down, to its Recovery. Your halved Measure is considered your new Measure for determining who attacks first and what Tactics can be employed on you as well.

However, if an opponent is In Your Guard and you choke up on your weapon, you defend with a standard d20! and deal full weapon damage.

Feint

On Attack

Compatible Attacks Called Shot, Charge, In Their Guard

Compatible Defenses Choking Up, Hold Back, Long Guard

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

When performing this move, you (hopefully) get your enemy to commit his guard to a different location than you plan on hitting. You receive a +5 to that Attack but receive a -3 to Defense.

Fend Off

On Attack

Compatible Attacks Hold Back, Jab, Phalanx Fighting

Compatible Defenses Long Guard, Step Back

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

If your weapon's measure is at least 1 foot greater than your opponent's, you can try and fend them off. This means that you may attack them freely, while they can't do the same to you.

If you attack first when first closing with someone, you may forfeit that attack to automatically be Fending Off. After that, while Fending Off someone, you can attack as normal.

To become fully engaged with you, they'll have to knock your weapon aside with a regular attack that deals no damage. Should they hit, they become fully engaged and can attack as normal, although they need to wait their full weapon Recovery.

Until they do so, they’re only considered to be Threatening you so they’re unable to attack you directly.

You can fend off multiple people at once provided that your weapon is at least 1 foot longer than each of theirs, they are all in front of you, and you are currently or are about to Fend Off at least one of them, even if the others approach after the initial engagement. If all these requirements are met, any such attackers are fended off automatically when they approach. If one is able to knock your weapon aside then you are no longer considering fending off any of the other attackers as well.

Flee

Any Time While Engaged

Compatible Attacks All except Charge

Compatible Defenses All

While engaged, you can turn around and move at a jogging speed or higher to attempt to disengage. This also applies to running by an opponent who Threatens you.

However, you open yourself up to an attack. If you have any foes who are within half of their weapon recovery they may make a attack against you but it does reset their weapon count.

Note: because of the inherent risks involved in Fleeing, people will typically not choose to do so unless forced to by a mental break or some other means.

In Their Guard

On Attack

Compatible Attacks All except Careful Aiming, Charge, and Phalanx Fighting

Compatible Defenses Step Back

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

If you're engaged with a person and have a weapon that is at least 1 foot shorter than your opponent, you may attempt to get in their guard.

On a successful attack, the shorter weapon combatant has successfully knocked his foe's weapon aside and is now fighting at extreme close range, causing them huge penalties.

The attacker deals no damage but the longer weapon defends on a dice 1 size smaller against all attacks made against them and all damage dice is also reduced by one size (3d4! → 3d3!) on all attacks they make.

Power Attack

On Attack

Compatible Attacks Charge, In Their Guard, Phalanx Fighting

Compatible Defenses Brace Yourself

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

This is the fighting equivalent of baseball batting your enemy, swinging with all your might.

With this attack, you receive a -3 to your Attack and next Defense but your damage increases.

Piercing weapons increase 1 die by 1 size. Slashing weapons increase all dice by 1 size and crushing weapons increase its highest die by 2 sizes.

This only affects the base damage dice the weapon has: not damage added via Skills.

Shift

Any Time While Engaged

Compatible Attacks All except Careful Aiming, and Charge

Compatible Defenses All except Brace Yourself, and Step Back

While Engaged, you can move at your walking speed to either side of your opponent or change 1 facing per second but suffer a -3 to your next Attack and Defense. This penalty doesn’t stack.

However, if you Shift the same second you use another, compatible Tactic, you don’t receive this penalty and gain a +3 that can be either used to increase a bonus or decrease a penalty from the other Tactic.

Snap Shot

On Ranged Attack

You don't really need to aim, if you don't want but your shots are going to be less accurate. when you use this Tactic, you lower your Recovery by 4 seconds but you roll attack on a d12! instead of the standard d20!. This can't bring a weapon below its minimum weapon Recovery but if it would bring your next attack below the current second, you fire instantly.

Step Back

On Defense

Compatible Attacks In Their Guard, Phalanx Fighting

Compatible Defenses All except Brace Yourself and Shift

This is a defensive tactic that involves moving directly back from your opponent’s blows at your walking speed.

Before your defense roll, if you declare a Step Back, you receive a +5 to your next Defense roll and can decide to move directly back or back and to the side.

If you move directly back you move your Jogging speed backwards for 1 second.

If you move back and to the side, you move at a Walking speed for 1 second.

Either way, you gain a -3 to Attack. If you perform this Tactic multiple times between attacks, you gain an additional -3 to Attack for each time you do so.

If your opponent is using the Flurry of Blows, Feint, or the Power Attack tactic, if they do hit, you only receive half damage, before DR.

Ending Combat

Combat ends when one side is incapacitated, reached a truce, or have fled the field. At that point the Clock stops, and play moves back to narrative time.

If you engaged in a Morale Confrontation, everyone on the side that won the combat gains the difference in points to their Integrity, regardless of whether or not their side has less. So, if the players have 3 points and the goblins had 7, every player still gains 4 Integrity.

The losing side subtracts that with the same caveats.
