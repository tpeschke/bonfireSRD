Introduction

What is combat?

Combat is what happens when one side wants to kill the other and are trying to do it right now, on a second-by-second basis.

I don’t think I really need to define combat because, as a naturally violence species, combat often comes second nature to us but I thought it would be an interesting exercise.

Starting a Combat

The Clock

The Clock begins at 1, and then progresses to 2, 3, 4 etc, ad infinitum. When the Clock matches a character’s Initiative, they go. Each of these numbers represent 1 second of time passing. What second a character's next action is referred to as their initiative (or "where they are on the Clock")

This can be a difficult when you first start so we recommend that you start with only running two or three enemies until you get the hang of it.

Each side rolls an initiative die. The lower the die, the more prepared they are for the attack, with the instigator going on second 1. The initiative’s die is based on the general combat preparedness of the company, which is generally based on how likely they think danger is.

The table provided gives you the extreme ends of the spectrum as well as the default but the initiative die can be anywhere in between.

Surprise

Those whose initiative hasn't come up are surprised and, on second 1 of the combat can choose to do one of three things: contine what they’re doing, stand still (if they’re not going faster than a Jog), or flee away from the danger (if there is a clear escape route, otherwise, this isn't an option).

Whatever they choose, they have to keep doing that action until their Initiative comes up: They can take no other actions, not even to defend themselves, thus they roll their defense die on a dice size 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

You can lower your Initiative (thus lowering the amount of time you're surpised) by either making a lot of noise or by being hit.

If someone is making a lot of noise, for whatever reason, you lower everyone's (enemy and ally alike) initiative by 2. Multiple sources of noise doesn't stack.

If the surprised person is hit, the pain quickly brings them into the reality of the situation, and they may act 2 seconds later (or before that if their initiative was the next second).

This can be done by either an enemy hitting you with their weapon or by a friend, running up and slapping you across the face.

Ambushes

Surprise happens regardless of whether or not the danger was visible and known to the characters but in cases of ambush, where the danger is unknown until suddenly its not, the GM can call for a Listening Check, which represents characters cluing into subtle clues that the manure is about to hit the windmill.

Each Boon gives a -2 to the Initiative roll while each Complication gives a +2 to the roll.

For example; Kelso, Bran Redhand, and Dalsini the Odd are traveling through the wilderness. Kelso, being clever, asked about the route they were traveling before and got rumors that there’s been an uptick in bandit attacks in the area so the company is wary while traveling.

Their second day out, they cross a bridge; unknowingly, the ambush point of the single bandit that’s been causing so much trouble.

They’re not taking care to be particularly quiet or stealthy as they’re traveling by road so the bandit, in his hiding place, sees them coming from awhile away. As they start to cross the bridge (Redhand in front, Kelso in back, and Dalsini in the middle), the bandit sneaks out and attacks Kelso from behind in an ambush.

The bandit goes on count 1 with their attack since he's completely ready for them. The company would normally roll d8s since they're traveling in a generally dangerous place, however, because they're on the lookout for bandit attacks, they instead roll on d6s since, you know, they're getting attacked by a bandit.

Redhand has a +2 Initiative Modifier but got 2 Boons on his Warfare Check (since he's using his knowledge of battle to help spot potential ambush sites) so, when he rolls a 1, his total is -1, so he goes on second 1. Dalsini has a +0 Initiative Modifier and got 1 Boon on his Check and his roll was 6, so he goes on second 4.

Finally, Kelso got 2 Complications on his Check and with an -2 Initiative Modifier and roll of 6, he goes on second 8, although, if the bandit hits, he’ll be able to act on second 3.

On second 1, Kelso stops to figure out what’s going on until his initiative, while Dalsini, being a weak weird-adept, decides he’s going to run for it, so starts running towards the other end of the bridge - away from the present danger.

Redhand, however, leaps into action on second 1, drawing his sword as he rushes to his friend’s aid.

Tracking Initiative

The easiest way to track what second you go on is via a number line and a token. For the number line, 1 - 20 works and, for the token, a penny works. As long as you can place the token on the number and have it be clear what number it's referring to.

When you first roll Initiative, simply place the token on that number: when it comes up on the clock, declare your action. If you attack, resolve that, and then add your Recovery to the current number: move your token to that number. If you're doing something else, your GM will let you know how long it will take: move your token to that number. If you go over 20, simply subtract 20 from the number and place the token there - adding a +20 in your mind.

So, you roll a 4 for your Initiative so you place a penny on position 4. When you're turn comes up, you decide to run over to some goblins who're 35 feet away. You get up to a Jog (moving 10 feet) this second, and then up to a Run (moving 15 feet) the next second (second 6) and then reach them on second 7. You could have either done this calculation while other things were resolving and placed your token on position 7 or just done it during each section.

In any case, on second 7, you attack with your zweihander, cleaving one goblin but remaining engaged with two others. You decide, for now, to stay engaged so now you have to wait your Recovery (17 seconds) to attack again. You add 17 to 7, which gives you 24: so you place your token on position 4 and remember to now add 20 to all the positions.

A number line (as well as some other useful info) is provided on the Basic Combat Cheat Sheet.

Combat Basics

Taking Actions

Every action takes a certain amount of time. On a character’s Initiative, they declare their action and are told how long that action will take. After that time, the action is complete.

So, if a character wants to walk across a room, which will take 8 seconds, and his Initiative is 5, once the Clock reaches 5, he starts walking and once it reaches 13, he’s gotten the other side of the room.

At any point, you may choose to forfeit your action and change your course. If, when halfway across the room (at second 9), the character sees that his buddy is in trouble, he can immediately decide that, no, he’d rather be there, his character stops walking across the room and instead heads over to help his friend.

The time it takes most actions can be rationalized from common experience but here are a few very common scenarios with their times added to help with arbitration mid-game. Note that these times represent someone in a hurry and under some sort of duress

A person may move up 2 movement categories, starting at a Walk, or down 2 in a second.

For every 10 seconds of Running, you take damage. This starts at a d4! but increases by 1 dice size every 10 seconds, capping at a d20!.

For Sprinting, this happens every 5 seconds.

This damage is reduced by your Endurance (or Athletics) Rank, down to a minimum of 0. This is modified by your armor's Skill Adjustment penalty because sprinting in heavy armor is hard.

You can push your character as hard as you'd like, continuing to take damage until they die from fatigue, or are forced to stop due to failing a Trauma Check, but if you take damage from Running or Sprinting and you stop, you can't Run or Sprint again until you've waited 10 seconds for every time your damage dice was increased.

Only Taking a Breather resets this count but if you move from Running to Sprinting (or vice versa), 1 second of Sprinting is worth 2 seconds of Running and vice versa.

Improvised Weapons

It is entirely possible that you’ll find yourself unarmed and must defend yourself with a chair leg or broken bottle. In these cases, treat is as a club but penalize it for 3 seconds and drop its Parry to 0 for not being balanced properly.

Now, it’s also possible that you have superhuman strength and so you can rip a door off its hinges and start bashing people with want and abandon. In this case, it’s entirely possible to do more than a zweihander in sheer concussive force, however, you’ll find that because a door (or similar) isn’t properly weighted or balanced, you’ll find yourself taking a long time to swing and unable to defend yourself properly. In extreme cases like these, the GM can give you a Defense Modifier of somewhere around -8 and the Recovery should never be quicker than 20 seconds.

On top of this, these wide swings make an otherwise threatening object easy to dodge and so less of a danger, meaning you may still be counted as unarmed while trying to use it.

Weapon Styles

The way your character wields a weapon can tell a lot about their priorities in a combat and a weapon can be versatile in the extreme if some thought is put into how it's held.

1-Handed Weapon with Shield

This is style comes with no additional benefits beyond the added defensive options of a shield.

2-Handing a 2-Handed Weapon

This is style comes with no additional benefits beyond the raw power of a 2-handed weapon.

1-Handed Weapon with Free Hand

With one hand free, the character can focus a bit more on their defense. They gain +4 Parry and gain an additional bonus equal to half their Parry Adv Skill Ranks with that weapon.

Note that this is only if you're not also attempting to make unarmed attacks with the free hand, in which case, use 2 1-Handed Weapons (attacking).

2 1-Handed Weapons

When duel-wielding, you're going to Multitask between attacking and defending. Information on that is found in Chapter 1.1: Basic Mechanics but there are some further things to note.

When you make an attack with off-hand weapon, it's going to be 3 sizes smaller than the last attack die of your primary hand. If this would bring the attack die below a d0, you don't get an attack with it, however you do get a +4 to your Parry and gain an additional bonus equal to half their Parry Adv Skill Ranks with the off-hand weapon, if the off-hand weapon is Small or a sidearm.

So, you've got an arming sword in your main hand an court sword in your off hand. You decide to attack with both, favoring attack, so you put a d12! as your Attack Die and a d8! to your Defense Dice.

You then get into an engagement with someone. When you defend, you're going to get that d8! as a base. When you attack with your arming sword (main hand), you roll a d12!. When you attack with your court sword (off hand), you roll a d6! (d12! - 3 dice sizes).

Your off-hand attacks are always a number of seconds equal to its unmodified Recovery after the primary makes its attacks.

If your off-hand weapon is smaller than your main-hand weapon or if it's a sidearm, use its Parry (modified as normal) instead.

On top of this, if the off-hand weapon is Small, on a Near Perfect with your main hand, add its damage instead of a d4!. On a Perfect Attack, you also get a free attack on a d20!.

If your off-hand weapon is larger than your main-hand weapon or they're the same size, your Parry value drops to 0 and reduce your defense dice by an additional 2 sizes.

You can only switch Tactics and dice around on your primary attack.

You can attack with both on your first attack but only if 1) the enemy unable to defend themselves for some reason, like being surprised or tied up and 2) you're not trying to make a surgical strike (backstabing, called shot, etc.).

2 Handing 1-Handed Weapon

Using two hands where one is sufficient allows you to leverage more of your strength and deal a harsher blow but slows you down.

When using this style, you increase your damage by +4, however, you get a +3 to Recovery.

This style can’t be effectively used in combat on weapons smaller than your size.

Double Shields

While it gives no offensive benefit, the double shields style allows you to cover more facings to gain a greater defense. If two shields have overlapping faces, use the higher of the two's Parry.

Weapon in your Off-Hand

This may come about because your main hand is disabled, or you just want to show off. But this implies the main hand is free. If not, see the 2 1 Handed Weapons style.

Non-Ambidextrous people attack with a dice size 1 size smaller And gain a +5 Recovery penalty to off-handed attacks.

1 Handing a 2 Handed Weapon

Even if you have the strength to carry a larger weapon, trying to use one one-handly can cause trouble due to the weapon not being properly balanced or built to be handled that way.

Still, realistically, nothing is stopping you but, if you choose to do so, you're going to receive the following penalties:

First, double base Recovery of the weapon and lose all Parry from all sources. Secondly, for every size the weapon is above you, shrink your attack dice by 2 sizes and your defense by 1 size and gain a +1 to Fatigue.

So, a human (M) weilding a Great Hammer (H) one-handed has a base Recovery of 40 second and 0 Parry (not that it really mattered in this case). They also attack on a d6! and defend on a d10! and are Fatigued at Hurt (assuming they started at Critical).

2 Hands Free (No Weapon)

If you have no weapons in combat, you’re not really seen as a threat and so an attacker doesn’t really have to do the dance to try to avoid you blows while trying to strike you.

In this style, your opponent attacks at half their weapon Recovery and they get a free attack whenever you try and close in an attack them. That includes moving ranges.

Dropping Your Weapon to Grapple

Unarmed moves are more effective with two hands rather than one so you may decide to drop your weapon to try and grapple your opponent easier. If the unarmed attack is on the same second that you drop your weapon, your opponent doesn’t receive the free attack on you.

Zones of Engagement

There are 3 positions that you can have relative to your enemy. These positions aren’t reciprocal by nature: you can be threatened by someone and not have them be threatened by you.

Unengaged

This is basically the default. You are too far away for them to reasonable do anything.

Threatened

This means that you’re within 5 feet + your opponent’s weapon’s measure. From here, if you move faster than a Jog and/or if they’re within half their weapon Recovery, they can attack you.

If you go from Engaged to Threatened, your weapon initiative doesn't reset, so if you become Engaged again for any reason before your weapon's Recovery, you have to wait the rest of your Recovery to attack again.

If you go from Threatened to Unengaged, you need to wait half your weapon's Recovery + your Initiative modifier to reorient yourself before you attack again (minimum 1 second). If someone Threatens you in that time, you go back to having to wait your full Recovery before attacking (minus the time you already waited).

Engaged

You’ve closed the distance and can actually make an attack. The person with the highest weapon measure attacks first. If both weapons are exactly the same measure, the attacks happen simultaneously.

Note that this is only for the first attack: if subsequent attacks happen on the same second, they happen simultaneously, meaning that two people with different measure, attacking on the same second, can kill each other at the same time.

If you're Surprised when you become Engaged, you're unable to attack back and, once you leave surprise, you must wait your weapon's Recovery before attacking.

If you move from being Engaged to being Threatened while moving faster than a Walk, you're using the Flee Tactic.

Melee Combat

Melee Attack & Defense

Basic melee attacks always roll a base d20! + any attack modifiers, while defenders roll a d20! + any defense modifiers

Perfect Attacks & Defenses

A Perfect Attack or Defense is one that puts you in a better position to win. A Perfect Attack occurs on an unmodified roll of 20 on your Attack roll, while a Perfect Defense is an unmodified 20 on your Defense roll, but only if your final totals are still greater than your opponent’s. If not, the attack still hits for Perfect Attacks and you still dodge the blow for Perfect Defenses but you don’t gain the auxiliary benefits.

If both those qualifiers are met, you gain the following benefits on top of the normal ones:

Perfect Attack

You gain a free automatically successful, well-executed, Unarmed Combat attack. If you have Ranks in the Unarmed Skill Suite or any of its Advanced Skills, you may roll for the additional benefit, although, even on fumble, this doesn't affect the normal bonus for delievering a perfect attack.

Perfect Defense

You gain a chance to riposte, gaining a free full attack the next second if you’re wielding a melee weapon. This attack doesn’t affect your current count.

Near Perfect Attacks & Defenses

An unmodified roll of 19 on either Attack or Defense results in a Near Perfect Attack or Defense, where you may Shift for no penalty (or Shift twice that second if you were doing so already) and, for attacks, gain an extra d4! to damage (which doesn't count as additional dice for /d DR), or, for defenses gained a free riposte with your off-hand weapon if it’s smaller than your Size, an unarmed attack if you have at least 1 hand free, a sidearm, or a shield.

Fumbles

On the flip side, rolling an unmodified 1 on your Attack or Defense roll, whose total is also lower than your opponent's, results in a 'fumble', with your opponent getting a free attack on the next second that doesn't reset their next attack.

Fumbling when your opponent gets a Perfect Attack or Defense results in the normal bonuses and the free attack while both sides rolling a fumble results in the person with the highest total getting their way (so, if the attacker has the highest total, they hit) but no free attack for either.

Dealing Damage

Once both sides have rolled and added all modifiers, compare the totals against each other.

If the defender’s is equal to or greater than the attacker, the attack misses.

Parrying

If a melee attack roll is within the Parry Threshold of the defender’s total and they're not surprised, the defender may choose to deflect some of the incoming attack.

The attacker rolls their damage and subtracts the shield or weapon’s DR (2/d DR for weapons), which subjects them to a chance of breakage, and then their armor’s DR. Any leftover is still done to the defender. For piercing weapons, the damage is reduced to 0.

So, a defender with a Parry Threshold of 4, and a total of 10 for their defense roll vs an attack roll of 14, could choose to parry the blow, taking less damage.

However, if it’s not, the attack lands and the attacker rolls his weapon’s damage die and adds any appropriate damage modifiers. The defender than subtracts their Damage Reduction and the remaining total is subtracted from their Vitality and added to their list of wounds.

Damage Reduction

Armor allows you to reduce the amount of damage taken. This is often times shorted to DR. There are two types: Static, noted by just a number, and Per Die, noted by a number /D.

Static simply subtracts that number from the damage and is done.

/D subtracts that number from each dice but can't bring any individual dice below 0, however, any extra DR is removed from the Str Damage Mod. So, if someone is attacking you with a slashing weapon (2d6!) with a +2 Str Damage Mod, and you have armor that gives you 4/D, and you roll a 5 and a 3. you do 2 damage.

When you roll, your GM will tell you if the DR is /D, they'll tell you what it is so you can quickly do the math.

Either way, if the attacker chooses to remain in engaged, he then adds his weapon’s Recovery to the current second on the Clock. Once the Clock reaches that new number, he may attack again.

Knock-Backs

If the pre-DR damage is higher than your enemy’s Vitality Size Modifier + 1 for every M worth of items you're carrying, the attack also moves them back 5 feet. If it’s twice the damage needed, they get knocked back 10 feet, and so on. Anything above a single Knock Back also knocks the enemy prone.

If a person is Engaged with someone who is Knock-Back, they can immediately close the distance, keeping the same Zone of Engagement.

Attacking & Defending on the Move

It's not really possible to effectively melee attack on the move, at least, if you're going above a Walk. Jogging reduces your Attack die by 2 sizes while Running or Sprinting makes it impossible to attack.

You can, however, defend normally against melee attacks while moving at any speed.

Ranged Combat

Ranged Weapon Firing Break Down

If you’re wondering what you’re doing at any given second when firing a ranged weapon: here’s a breakdown for you.

So, if you already loading and cranked (drawing) your crossbow, it would only take 5 seconds to fire it.

Missile Retrieval

This is drawing the missile from its quiver or pouch. For firearms, that means drawing out the powder and bullets.

Fuse

This is the process of lighting the fuse. This can be dropped to 1 second but increases the chance of backfiring from 1 to 5.

Loading

This is knocking the arrow or loading the bullet and powder in the barrel.

Draw

This is drawing back the bow, cranking the crossbow, or cocking your arm for the throw. This can be prepped ahead of time for the belly- and crossbows but not the composite, long, or war bows

Aiming

This is actually sighting your target and lining up the shot. This can be skipped, making it so you fire faster, but you roll attack on a d12! instead of the standard d20!. This can't bring a weapon below its minimum weapon Recovery.

Firing

This is pulling the trigger or loosing the arrow.

Advanced Training that lowers Recovery take from Missile Retrieval, Fuse, Loading, Drawing, and Aiming evenly but they can never reduce a single category to below 1 second. So, the minimum firing Recovery for a Composite Bow is 5 seconds.

Ranged Attack & Defense

Ranged attacks and defenses function identical to melee attacks and defenses (including Flanking), only get a few additional bonuses and penalties.

The first is that range (of course) and the second is movement.

When defending against a ranged attack, you get a +2 for each Movement Category above standing still. So, if you're getting shot at, get the lead out.

Perfect Attacks

A Perfect Attack is one that puts you in a better position to win. A Perfect Attack occurs on an unmodified roll of 20 on your Attack roll.

If you’re firing a ranged weapon, you managed to hit a sensitive area, lowering their max movement speed by one category, minimum Crawl speed. This continues until that wound is completely healed.

Near-Perfect Attacks

An unmodified roll of 19 on your Attack results in a Near Perfect Attack, giving you an extra d4! to damage.

Cover

Cover is anything that gets between you and the person trying to shoot at you.

For every 5% of the target covered, you get a +1. So, 25% would be a +5, 50% would be a +10 and so on. At 100% covered, you can rule that no roll is needed: the attack simply misses.

For people or monsters, start at 50% cover (+10). Each difference in size shrinks or increases that cover buy 15% (+/- 3).

If you’re crouching behind cover, you double the bonus but are limited to Crawling. If the bonus goes above +20, you’re considered completely covered and all attacks automatically miss.

Firing Into Melee

Whenever you would hit with a ranged attack, if there are any creatures within 5 ft of the line of fire, treat each interposing creature as providing Cover and check each in order to see if they were hit instead.

Starting at the person closest to the archer or gunner, roll their Cover dice but subtract that person's Defense modifiers and subtract any Cover that is unique to them (such as a shield's Cover). This has a minimum of zero.

This total is added to the original target's Defense, just like normal Cover. If the person's Cover is enough to turn a hit into a miss, the attack hints them instead of the target in which case you roll damage against them; they don't get a defense roll.

If they were not hit, then move to the next person giving Cover and resolve in the same manner.

If the next person in line would also gain Cover from the previous possibly targets, add their Cover rolls to the total as well. If not, just add the new person's Cover roll to the target's Defense.

If there are any objects between the archer and their original target, resolve them according to their relative position to the intervening combatants. So, if a tree is between two fighters, resolve the closest fighter, than the tree, than the other fighter.

If none of the interposing combatants were hit, resolve any Cover the original target has (such as a shield). If that didn't help, the missile landed true: roll damage against the original target.

If the ranged attack was a miss, see the Ranged Misses section below.

Shooting on the Move

It is possible for ranged weapons to be fired while the person is moving but the Attack roll is reduced by 2 dice sizes for every Movement Category so even going at a Crawl results in rolling the attack on a d10!, assuming no other modifiers.

Dealing Damage

Dealing damage with a ranged weapon works almost exactly like dealing damage with a melee weapon, however, you’re unable to Parry a ranged attack (that’s what Cover is for)

Ranged Misses

On a miss with a ranged weapon, the missile doesn’t magically disappear into oblivion; there’s a good chance it might hit someone else.

In the case of a miss, roll a d4. An odd result means it goes left, an even result means it goes right.

The difference between the attack and defense roll determines how many feet away the missile strayed.

Draw an imaginary line between the archer and that point. Everyone within 5ft of that line needs to roll a defense vs the original attack roll, starting with the person closest to the archer and ending with the furthest possible target, ignoring the original target.

Inanimate objects may provide enough cover to be considered targets in both Firing into Melee and Ranged Misses, such as a large boulder or tree. Resolve using the normal rules.

Misc. Combat Rules

Facing & Fighting Multiple People

The likelihood is that at some point you’ll want to be flanking or someone with want to flank you, to try and gain an advantage.

Defending right in front of you is pretty easy and so brooks no penalties, however, every 45 degrees off that the defender lowers their Defense dice by 1 size, with those being attacked from directly behind defend on a dice size 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!) since they take advantage of a huge blind spot.

This penalty can be mitigated through the use of shields. Small shields allow you to defend against an additional assailant, while Medium and Large ones allow you to defend against an extra 2 and 3, respectively. You still defend on a reduced Defense Die but you do get your shield's Parry to the roll. This only applies attacks coming from the side the shield is on and can never affect an attack directly from behind but otherwise, the player can decide who they're blocking.

A maximum of 8 people at a time can effectively attack person at once, assuming that they’re out in the open. After that, every assailant’s attack die is lowered by 1 size per extra person.

Trauma

If someone does more damage after DR than your Trauma Threshold, this triggers a Trauma Check: that person rolls a d20 and tries to get under half their Con stat.

Failure means that your character has been overcome with pain to the point that he’s unable to act, leaving him completely defenseless for a number of seconds equal to how much he failed by times 3.

Double Trauma

A character that gets dealt their full Vitality in damage in a single blow has suffered a grievous wound - the scar of which, if he survives, will be with him until the day he dies.

When this happens, the trauma is too much, and they simply die where they stand if they fail their check. Even if they succeed, they’re still suffer Trauma for the next 30 seconds but gain a wicked looking scar.

Battle scars are marks of pride and honor. Characters who become scared gain a Rank 1 Description “Battle Scars”. If they would get this Description again, increase that Rank by 1.

Coup De Grace

A helpless enemy is one completely at your mercy.

If a character is completely helpless, either from failing a Trauma check, being bound, or deep asleep, a character can kill the target instantly.

This move takes 10 seconds to complete as it takes a bit of positioning to pull off. The character’s whose throat is being slit gives out a cry of pain as they expire.

Equipment

Your kit can really pull you through in a pinch.

If there is already a specific use for the piece of Equipment (like weapons, armor, and shields), you can redeem an applicable Description to gain a temporary bonus to a roll or static modifier.

So, if you have Fine-Steel (+2) armor, if you get hit, you can increase the armor's DR by 2 for that damage roll specifically.

If equipment has no other bonuses , or is being used in a way that it wouldn't normally, on an applicable Check or roll, you add an extra d6! + the Rank of any applicable Descriptions the equipment might have. If there are multiple pieces of equipment, you increase the dice size by 1 per piece and add their Descriptions as well. More on equipment Descriptions is found in Chapter 2.6: Equipment.

If the equipment doesn't really suite the purpose you're using it for, like trying to pry open a door with a weapon, you apply a d4! instead, although the same rules apply for stacking pieces of equipment.

So, using a weapon as a crowbar would give you a d4! to the roll while you couldn't normally redeem the bonus dice when using it to to attack since that's its actual purpose.

Either way, redeeming this bonus causes the tool degrades, giving it (and any other equipment you use) 1 Wear, whether you succeed or not. More on Wear can be found in Chapter 2.6: Equipment.

Tactics

These rules add another layer of strategy to combat and should be implemented as soon as you have the basics down.

Each Tactic, at the top, when you can employ them and which other Tactics they can be combined with which. For “On Defense” and “On Attack”, the Tactic has to be declared before you roll.

Once committed to, Tactics can only be switched out on your next Attack or when you are no longer Engaged. So, if you’re Active Dodging, you have to commit to that Tactic on your defense but cannot stop Active Dodging until your next attack.

Active Dodging

On Defense

Compatible Attacks Hold Back

Compatible Defenses All except Brace Yourself, and Step Back

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

This move relies on actively focusing on dodging against incoming attacks.

A defender commits to how much he wants to focus on dodging incoming attacks, both melee and ranged, choosing from a bonus to his defense of either +3 or +5, but netting an equal penalty to his attacks (-3 or -5).

Additionally, this increases the range for Near Perfect Defenses by 1, so you score them on a base natural 18 - 19.

But focusing on defense does slow you down, and you are limited to 5 ft / second of movement.

Brace Yourself

On Defense While Standing Still

Compatible Attacks All except Charge, Feint, and In Their Guard

Compatible Defenses Choking Up, Fend Off, Long Guard

Maybe you look at your opponent and say "No, you move". You root your stance and ready yourself, sacrificing some maneuverability to hold the line.

While using this Tactic, a character adds +15 to their Knock-Back Threshold and gains a +5 to their Trauma Checks, however, they defend on a dice size 1 size smaller.

Called Shot

On Melee Attack

Compatible Attacks Feint, In Their Guard

Compatible Defenses Brace Yourself, Choking Up, Fend Off, Hit the Deck

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

By carefully aiming your melee attack, a character can reduce the effects of heavy armor by taking more time to target gaps.

When employing this tactic, on a successful attack, a character halves their effective number of damage dice (rounded up) for considering /D DR, however, it takes a bit longer to set up the strike and so they increase their Recovery by 5 seconds.

Crushing weapons are unable to employ this tactic.

Careful Aiming

On Ranged Attack

Compatible Defenses Brace Yourself

By taking a moment or two, you can sight in with any ranged weapon, giving you a better chance to hit your target, however, there are diminishing returns after a bit.

Using this Tactic, you delay your next ranged attack by 3 seconds but gain a +5 to hit.

This Tactic can only be performed when the shooter is standing still. On top of this, the combatant takes 3 Stress when they finally make the shot.

Charge

Any Time Moving at >Jog

Compatible Attacks Feint, Flurry of Blows, Power Attack

A character using this Tactic must be moving at least a Run to throw their full force behind the below.

A charge is a terrifying tactic: if the blow lands (even if it does no damage), the defender takes 2d4! Stress. The damage they take is also considered doubled for the purposes of determining Knock-Backs. A person can only gain Stress from a Charge once a combat.

The attacker then defends with a dice size 1 size smaller for 5 seconds or until they’re no longer Engaged.

Choking Up On Your Weapon

On Melee Defense

Compatible Attacks All except Careful Aiming, Charge, and In Their Guard

Compatible Defenses All except Fend Off, and Long Guard

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

When you’ve choked up on your weapon, your weapon’s Measure is halved, and you add that number, rounded down, to its Recovery. Your halved Measure is considered your new Measure for determining who attacks first and what Tactics can be employed on you as well.

However, if an opponent is In Your Guard and you choke up on your weapon, you defend with a standard d20! and deal full weapon damage.

Feint

On Melee Attack

Compatible Attacks Called Shot, Charge, In Their Guard

Compatible Defenses Choking Up, Hold Back, Long Guard

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

When performing this move, you (hopefully) get your enemy to commit his guard to a different location than you plan on hitting. You receive a +5 to that Attack but receive a -3 to Defense.

Fend Off

On Melee Attack

Compatible Attacks Hold Back, Jab, Phalanx Fighting

Compatible Defenses Hit the Deck, Long Guard, Step Back

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

If your weapon's measure is at least 1 foot greater than your opponent's, you can try and fend them off. This means that you may attack them freely, while they can't do the same to you.

If you attack first when first closing with someone, you may forfeit that attack to automatically be Fending Off. After that, while Fending Off someone, you can attack as normal.

To become fully engaged with you, they'll have to knock your weapon aside with a regular attack that deals no damage. Should they hit, they become fully engaged and can attack as normal, although they need to wait their full weapon Recovery.

Until they do so, they’re only considered to be Threatening you so they’re unable to attack you directly.

You can fend off multiple people at once provided that your weapon is at least 1 foot longer than each of theirs, they are all in front of you, and you are currently or are about to Fend Off at least one of them, even if the others approach after the initial engagement. If all these requirements are met, any such attackers are fended off automatically when they approach. If one is able to knock your weapon aside then you are no longer considering fending off any of the other attackers as well.

Flee

Any Time While Engaged

Compatible Attacks All except Charge

Compatible Defenses All

While engaged, you can turn around and move at a jogging speed or higher to attempt to disengage. This also applies to running by an opponent who Threatens you.

However, you open yourself up to an attack. If you have any foes who are within half of their weapon recovery they may make a attack against you but it does reset their weapon count.

Note: because of the inherent risks involved in Fleeing, people will typically not choose to do so unless forced to by a mental break or some other means.

Flurry Of Blows

On Melee Attack

Compatible Attacks Charge, In Their Guard

Compatible Defenses Brace Yourself, Choking Up

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

This tactic involves releasing a rain of quick attacks that rely more on speed than accuracy or power. This tactic can be declared any time after your attack if you're not using another Tactic that can't stack with this one.

While employing this tactic you attack at half speed, however, you suffer a -3 to Attack and Defense and your damage doesn’t explode.

Hit The Deck

Any Time

Compatible Attacks Called Shot, Hold Back, Jab, Phalanx Fighting, Snap Shot

Compatible Defenses Active Dodging, Choking Up, Fend Off, Step Back

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

When a sudden arrow flies, there might be no other choice but to dive for it at all costs.

When you use this Tactic, you fall prone, gaining a +5 to your Defense vs ranged attacks. If you were standing still, you can move an additional 5 ft that second - if you were moving you can move up to 10 additional feet.

However, if you dive behind non-shield cover, you also get the bonus for crouching behind it (although you're still prone).

This Tactic can be done when surprised.

Hold Back

On Melee Attack

Compatible Attacks Feint, In Their Guard

Compatible Defenses All except Long Guard, and Step Back

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

Sometimes, you don't want to kill your enemy: you want to take them alive so you hold back.

When using this technique, dropping a person to 0 Vitality, doesn't kill them: instead it knocks them unconscious for 24 - their Con Stat hours.

However, when using this technique, halve the number of damage dice you roll (removing the smallest dice first), rounded up, and shrink the remaining by 1 size but this base damage is doubled for triggering Trauma.

So, using this strategy with a dusack results in 2d3! damage while using it with a Mace results in 1d10! Damage.

In Their Guard

On Melee Attack

Compatible Attacks All except Careful Aiming, Charge, and Phalanx Fighting

Compatible Defenses Step Back

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

If you're engaged with a person and have a weapon that is at least 1 foot shorter than your opponent, you may attempt to get in their guard.

On a successful attack, the shorter weapon combatant has successfully knocked his foe's weapon aside and is now fighting at extreme close range, causing them huge penalties.

The attacker deals no damage but the longer weapon defends on a dice 1 size smaller against all attacks made against them half their damage (before DR).

Jab

On Melee Attack

Compatible Attacks In Their Guard, Phalanx Fighting

Compatible Defenses All

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

A strike that is meant to purposely to open your opponent’s guard and get them off their balance by making them shift their defense. This tactic can be declared any time after your attack if you're not using another Tactic that can't stack with this one.

The Jab attack happens at half weapon Recovery (rounded up) for Piercing and Slashing weapons. This can take them below their normal minimum Recovery, down to one. Jabs are also uneffectived by Parry.

Instead of a d20!, the defender uses a dice size 1 size smaller but on a successful hit, no damage is done, and the defender nets a -1 to their next defense against a non-jab attack.

This penalty doubles with each jab, however, it only lasts until the defender’s next full defense (so -2 for the second, -4 for the third, -8, etc.).

Crushing weapons may use this tactic but it’s not any faster than their regular Recovery.

Additionally, special weapon bonuses don’t apply, unless they specifically reference the Jab Tactic.

Long Guard

On Melee Attack

Compatible Attacks Feint, Jab, Phalanx Fighting

Compatible Defenses Active Dodging, Brace Yourself, Fend Off

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

By placing your weapon further out in front of you and trying to line the body of the weapon with the defender’s eye line, this may allow a person with a short weapon the first strike.

While using this tactic, you increase your weapon’s measure by 1/2 ft but you reduce your damage’s largest die by 1 size.

Crushing weapons are unable to employ this tactic.

Phalanx Fighting

Any Time

Compatible Attacks Power Attack, Jab, Long Guard

Compatible Defenses All except Active Dodging

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

This tactic requires you to have a piercing or slashing weapon that is equal to or greater than 5 + your partner's measure, but, if you have that, this allows you to attack over that partner in combat to reach enemies beyond you.

There is a trade-off. First you attack on 1 dice smaller. Second, you reduce all damage dice by 1 size (removing any d3!s entirely). Crushing weapons are unable to use this Tactic at all.

You can fight over multiple allies if your measure is larger than (5 * number of allies fighting over) + the front rank’s measure, however you shrink the Attack dice by 1 for every person you’re fighting over.

Power Attack

On a Melee Attack

Compatible Attacks Charge, In Their Guard, Phalanx Fighting

Compatible Defenses Brace Yourself

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

This is the fighting equivalent of baseball batting your enemy, swinging with all your might.

With this attack, you receive a -3 to your Attack and next Defense but your damage increases.

Piercing weapons increase 1 die by 1 size. Slashing weapons increase all dice by 1 size and crushing weapons increase its highest die by 2 sizes.

This only affects the base damage dice the weapon has: not damage added via Skills.

Shift

Any Time While Engaged

Compatible Attacks All except Careful Aiming, and Charge

Compatible Defenses All except Brace Yourself, and Step Back

While Engaged, you can move at your walking speed to either side of your opponent or change 1 facing per second but suffer a -3 to your next Attack and Defense. This penalty doesn’t stack.

However, if you Shift the same second you use another, compatible Tactic, you don’t receive this penalty and gain a +3 that can be either used to increase a bonus or decrease a penalty from the other Tactic.

Snap Shot

On Ranged Attack

Compatible Defenses Hit the Deck

You don't really need to aim, if you don't want but your shots are going to be less accurate. when you use this Tactic, you lower your Recovery by 4 seconds but you roll attack on a d12! instead of the standard d20! and your damage doesn't explode. This can't bring a weapon below its minimum weapon Recovery but if it would bring your next attack below the current second, you fire instantly.

Step Back

On Melee Defense

Compatible Attacks In Their Guard, Phalanx Fighting

Compatible Defenses All except Brace Yourself and Shift

This is a defensive tactic that involves moving directly back from your opponent’s blows at your walking speed.

Before your defense roll, if you declare a Step Back, you receive a +5 to your next Defense roll and can decide to move directly back or back and to the side.

If you move directly back you move your Jogging speed backwards for 1 second.

If you move back and to the side, you move at a Walking speed for 1 second.

Either way, you gain a -3 to Attack. If you perform this Tactic multiple times between attacks, you gain an additional -3 to Attack for each time you do so.

If your opponent is using the Flurry of Blows, Feint, or the Power Attack tactic, if they do hit, you only receive half damage, before DR.

Unarmed Combat

Unarmed Combat’s primary use is to either disable or control your foe, thus winning the battle by maneuvering, rather than raw damage output.

To use an unarmed attack, roll an attack on a dice size 2 sizes smaller (normally, a d10!), adding your normal attack modifiers, but also adding your Damage Modifier. Your opponent rolls a regular defense (including parry unless otherwise stated) but also adds their Damage Modifier as well, regardless of whether they're armed or not.

If you hit and weren't parried, you successfully pulled off the move, dealing your unarmed damage and gaining the benefit of the technique. If you were parried, the attack counts as a miss.

If the difference between the attack and defense totals were more than the target’s Vitality Size Modifier, you may gain additional benefits listed in the move (called a "well-executed" move). If the difference is double the Target’s Vitality Size Modifier, either they can the well-executed version of the move or they may immediately make a second unarmed combat attack for free the same second - this attack can be well-executed but can't result in another attack. Triple means that you get both.

If you’re doing the move or defending with two hands, roll your attack on a d20!. Also note that performing unarmed moves with your off-hand gives no further penalties as it is more about leverage and technique.

Grab

This move is meant to disable or gain control of a limb or item, whether that’s their belt, their leg, or their sword. The target must be declared before the roll. The defender and attacker get to add their Vitality Size Modifier sto their Checks.

Attempting to grab a shield means the target doesn't get to add their Shield Defense Ranks since a larger shield is easier to grab. Additionally, attempting to grab most parts of the body involve grabbing armor, if they're wearing any; Add your target's armor's Skill Adjustment as well as applicable.

The exact bonus will depend on the situation but here are some guidelines:

Movement While Grabbing

While grappling, the attacker is considered to be carrying the defender and vice versa; this increases their encumbrance based on the Size of the other grappler but character sizes don’t equate to item sizes so here is a handy conversion chart of the 3 most common cases.

Small Character = Large Item

Medium Character = Huge Item (3L)

Large Character = Giant Item (9L)

A full conversion chart is found in 1.7: Misc Rules.

The grappler with the highest speed category determines movement.

Arm

Requirements This area of the body can only be targeted if the arm has no weapon or shield: otherwise, the attack needs to grab those first.

Defender Penalties The defender defends on a dice size 1 size smaller (normally a d12!) vs Pushes and can’t change their facing willingly. Against normal attacks, they defend on a die 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

They’re movement is also limited (see Movement While Grappling sidebar above).

Attacker Bonuses All attacker bonuses from grabbing the defender’s weapons and/or shield if applicable.

Attacker Penalties Against normal attacks they defend on a die 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

They’re movement is also limited (see Movement While Grappling sidebar above).

Well-Executed or Grabbed Again The attacker can grab either the head, the torso, the other arm, or they can deal their unarmed damage, ignoring non-natural DR.

Head

Requirements This area of the body can only be targeted if the defender is surprised or if the attacker already has a hold of the defender’s arms or torso.

Defender Penalties The defender takes a d4! + attacker’s damage modifier (no DR) automatically whenever they try to break free.

Against normal attacks, they defend on a die 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

They’re movement is also limited (see Movement While Grappling sidebar above).

Attacker Bonuses The attacker can choose to do an automatic d4! + their damage modifier damage (no DR) instead of another unarmed maneuver using their normal unarmed Recovery.

Attacker Penalties Against normal attacks they defend on a die 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

They’re movement is also limited (see Movement While Grappling sidebar above).

Well-Executed or Grabbed Again The attacker can deal their unarmed damage, ignoring non-natural DR.

Held Item

Defender Penalties The attacker has hold of the held item, preventing it from being used effectively, although what that means is determined on a case-by-case basis: potions aren’t able to be drunk, alms scripts aren’t able to be burnt, etc.

Against normal attacks, they defend on a die 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

They’re movement is also limited (see Movement While Grappling sidebar above).

Attacker Penalties Against normal attacks they defend on a die 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

They’re movement is also limited (see Movement While Grappling sidebar above).

Well-Executed or Grabbed Again The attacker can wretch the item out of the defender’s hand, grab the defender’s arm, or they can deal their unarmed damage, ignoring non-natural DR, to either the item or the character.

Leg

Defender Penalties The defender can no longer move or change facings.

Against normal attacks, they defend on a die 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!) and defend against pushes, throws, and tackles on dice 2 sizes smaller (normally, a d4! with the -3 dice size the defender already gets).

They’re movement is also limited (see Movement While Grappling sidebar above).

Attacker Penalties Against normal attacks they defend on a die 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

They’re movement is also limited (see Movement While Grappling sidebar above).

Well-Executed or Grabbed Again They attack can knock the defender prone (and keep them from getting up), or they can deal their unarmed damage, ignoring non-natural DR

Shield

Note The defender doesn't get to add their shield's Parry (but can add their weapon's) to their roll.

Defender Penalties The defender no longer gains any bonus from their shield but still has the defense and Fatigue penalties.

Against normal attacks, they defend on a die 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

They’re movement is also limited (see Movement While Grappling sidebar above).

Attacker Penalties Against normal attacks they defend on a die 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

They’re movement is also limited (see Movement While Grappling sidebar above).

Well-Executed or Grabbed Again The attacker can wretch the shield out of the defender’s hand, grab the defender’s arm, or they can deal their unarmed damage, ignoring non-natural DR.

Torso

Requirements This area of the body can only be targeted if the defender is surprised or if the attacker already has a hold of the defender’s arms.

Defender Penalties If the defender had one or more of their arms grabbed, they continue to have those penalties so see those related penalties.

If their weapon arm is still free, their attack is impaired: they can only effectively attack with Small sized weapons, otherwise they attack on a die 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

Against normal attacks, they defend on a die 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

They’re movement is also limited (see Movement While Grappling sidebar above).

Attacker Penalties Against normal attacks they defend on a die 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

They’re movement is also limited (see Movement While Grappling sidebar above).

Well-Executed or Grabbed Again The attacker can grab the head instead or deal their unarmed damage, ignoring non-natural DR.

Weapon

Note The defender gets a free attack if the move isn’t successful and doesn't get to add their weapon's Parry to their roll, if applicable.

Defender Penalties The defender can no longer attack and is considered unarmed.

Against normal attacks, they defend on a die 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

They’re movement is also limited (see Movement While Grappling sidebar above).

Attacker Penalties Against normal attacks they defend on a die 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

They’re movement is also limited (see Movement While Grappling sidebar above).

Well-Executed or Grabbed Again The attacker can disarm the defender, grab the defender’s arm, or they can deal their unarmed damage, ignoring non-natural DR.

Push

By pushing you opponent in just the right way, you can move them or shift them in the direction you wish. The defender and attacker get to add their Vitality Size Modifier sto their Checks.

Using a shield while performing this move gives you a bonus to your Attack roll: +10 for Large shields, +5 for Medium shields. Its Recovery matches your normal unarmed Recovery.

On a successful Push, your target changes one facing or moves 5 ft to the left, right, or directly back. You may immediately Shift to follow them without penalty.

Well-Executed Bonus

You gain an additional facing change, gain 5 ft of movement, or deal normal unarmed damage (no non-natural DR) on a well-executed Push.

Throw/Tackle

This move attempts to throw your opponent to the ground and is easier to pull off if you go prone with them. The defender and attacker get to add their Vitality Size Modifier sto their Checks.

For this move, you can increase the attack dice size by 1 if you’re at running speed when you Tackle them. You may increase it by a further 1 if you choose to go to the ground with them, however, no matter what, if you lose the struggle, you still end up on the ground.

Getting forced to the ground resets your opponent's Count and they're considered prone.

Well-Executed Bonus

The extra bonus for this move is being able to remain standing if you so choose, throw them 5 ft or possibly disarming them if they fail a Str check vs d20! + your Str, or deal normal unarmed damage (no non-natural DR).

Strike

A strike is a punch with any part of your body or shield. It’s not really about doing damage but more about throwing your opponent off.

Using a shield while performing this move gives you a bonus to your Attack roll: +10 for Small and Medium shields, +5 for Large shields. Its Recovery matches your normal unarmed Recovery.

On a successful Strike, you delay your opponent's next attack by 1d4! seconds - their natural DR but they can otherwise function normally. Striking an already stunned opponent doesn't delay them further.

Well-Executed Bonus

The extra bonus for this move is that you increase the delay time by a dice size (normally going from a d4! to a d6!) or deal an additional dice of unarmed damage (d4! for while unarmed, d8! while using brass knuckles).

Break Free

There will probably come a time when you want to break free from a grab, pin, or choke hold.

The grappled combatant can add their Rank in Escape Artist if they have the Advanced Skill: otherwise, it is resolved in the normal way with success resulting in the person breaking free

Well-Executed Bonus

The extra bonus is that the grappled person is able to turn it around on their enemy: they get a free, unarmed maneuver or the grappled person can deal normal unarmed damage (no non-natural DR).

Mounted Combat

Whether you're playing a knight or a bounty hunter, you might find it's to your advantage to find yourself something to ride on and you'll probably want to know how you can use that mount in combat, so here are some rules to help you out.

So, you’re not making constant Skill Checks all the time, Riding, in this case, is handled a little bit differently. Your mount will have a Turning, Acceleration, and Deceleration stat: this tells you how long, at base, to perform the corresponding action. You can lower all of these base times by up to your Riding Skill, however, you can never go below 3 seconds.

You can also never perform more than one action at a time, and you can’t change actions until the last one you declared is finished since your mount has its own inertia.

Turning

This is the base time it takes to turn one Facing (45 degrees). This is, however, affected by your speed, adding 1 second to the base time for every Speed Category above Walking your mount is going.

Acceleration/Deceleration

This moves your mount up or down 2 Speed Categories, respectively.

Doing Something Dangerous

Your mount might also be smarter than you and decide that it really doesn't want to put itself in danger. This could be charging into a wall of pikes or just a wall. In this case, you’ll have to convince your mount into taking the action.

This acts exactly like a Confrontation with the rider adding his Riding Skill Rank and the mount adding its Bravery. Note that if the Bravery is negative, it gets added to the opposition's roll.

And finally, the opposition will also add dice equal to how much damage if could do to the mount (not including DR), though if it’s non-piercing, you’re going to half the damage dice.

Since this is an Appeal, Stress is still given out to both rider and mount.

So, charging into a pike (3d6! Damage) would mean rolling vs a d20!+3d6!, while charging a man brandishing a schiavona would be a d20!+d6! (or 2d3!, either is valid and up to you). Trying to get it to leap a chasm with a 40 ft drop would also cause you to make a roll vs d20!+3d12!.

So, if you have to think further ahead in combat so that you don't run into anything, what's the point of using a mount? 1) because it's awesome and 2) you get a lot of extra bonuses for doing so.

For starters, if you get +d4! to damage if your opponent isn't mounted or equalizing your height somehow, as well as additional damage dice if you're using a weapon specifically meant to be used from horseback (though you get this die even if you're on a camel, lizard, or what-have you), regardless of height difference.

On top of this, based on the Speed you're moving, you get a bonus to your Defense and bonus to your effective damage when considering Knock-Back. Should you gain a successful double Knock-Back, your opponent is trampled under hoof, dealing d8! damage and allowing you to continue past them. Morale

Combat is not simply about who can do the most damage the quickest. Battles, at their heart, are manifestations of the fever of conflict going on between the two sides. This is why sometimes a small, untrained force of zealots can overcome a larger professional army.

This can be represented by a Confrontation happening during the combat, or a Morale Confrontation.

Morale Confrontations

At the beginning of combat, if at least one side has a captain, a Confrontation is also started with the two opposing sides are the two opposing forces (the players vs the goblins, for example).

This runs like a normal Confrontation except the company captains are in charge and get to dictate pretty much everything.

So as the battle progresses, the captains are going to run the Confrontation in parallel with it, trying to swing the battlefield to their advantage.

Unlike a regular Confrontation, however, it is not about gaining points but about who wins or loses that round.

If you win the Confrontation round, the target behaves the way you want. This represents either them being too panicked or not having enough information to make a good decision, or effective leadership and a clear grasp of what’s going on.

With a Captain

During combat, a captain can, at any time, state that they want a combatant (or group of combatants) to perform an action or behave in a certain way.

For instance, the captain might want a combatant to switch his Tactic to be more aggressive, or swap who he’s aiming for.

He can also do this for enemy combatants: the only exception is they can’t make them run away (which can happen, but we'll talk about that a bit later).

So, for example, you’re the company captain and you’re trying to convince the enemy goblin that he should try and get at your company’s ranger but currently he’s making a beeline for your combatant. In that case, you can use the combatant’s Characteristic “You Don’t Want to Mess with Me” to make an Appeal to get the goblin to second guess himself and go after your ranger.

A captain can affect any number of combatants; however, they need to be easily “grouped”: there needs to be a good reason why every combatant “targeted” would be affected by the same Appeal.

Stress is divided between anyone that helped with the Check as the captain sees fit.

Field Promotions & No Presence

There might be the case a captain dies, and a lieutenant is suddenly promoted, however the chain of command should have been determined beforehand.

In this case, subtract 1 from the original Captain’s Pre Check Modifier and use that for the lieutenants.

It’s better than nothing because it keeps people from just running for it and they gain less Stress but it’s still not great.

Vs a Side with No Captain

If one side has a captain but they're removed from combat for any reason (including going unconscious) with no replacement, everyone on their side immediately has their Panic Threshold lowered by 1 Category, so keep the company captain safe!

A side with no captain can’t actively go “on the attack”, meaning that they can no longer initiate a Confrontation round but only defend against them.

If they don't have any applicable personal Characteristics or Skills, they at least get a Check but gain double the Stress they normally take (2d4!), which can only be reduced by 1 dice if the difference is greater than 5.

A person (or side) can auto-fail a Check and gain no Stress, however.

So, for goblins, having no warfare, no Presence, or no Intellect, they would auto-fail but if you’re facing an ettin and try to intimidate him based on strength, he would roll based on its strength (and maybe some other things because he's a big boy who can really hurt you).

Additionally, sides with no captains are now vulnerable to having their combatants break and run away.

Running Away

Normally, running away is only caused by a combatant reaching their Stress Threshold, but, with no captain, any Panicked or Fatigued combatant can be convinced to turn tail and run, inflicting any Stress they would gain on the remaining combatants. All combatants that are both Fatigued and Panicked will automatically run.

Big Events

As noted, you can use a Confrontation just to inflict your opponent any with Stress. The same is also true for Morale Confrontations. Sometimes, it's not about forcing your opponent into making a tactical error: sometimes it's just about breaking their spirit.

In this case, you can use any major event in the combat (Trauma, captain dying, Fatiguing someone, etc.) to just inflict Stress on your enemies as per the normal rules.

Other Ways to Trigger a Check

You can still have Confrontation Check, even when there's no big event attached to it, if you purposely take time to intimidate your opponent.

This can pair this with an action (such as brutalizing a fallen foe or gloating over a kill) or it can be an independent action (Like screaming in your enemy's face) or it can be issuing orders (to have a companion change their combat tactic before their attack is up) but either way, it takes a base of 1d4! seconds.

If you stop to do it, you can increase that dice size by however much you want but you also get the result of the roll as a bonus to your Check.

Additional Arguments

On top of that you’ll also have additional places you can pull from for your arguments that only really apply to Morale Confrontations, such as having a seasoned commander, outnumbering the opposing side, having a common Devotion to why they’re fighting and so on.

Here are common things you can draw from:

Outnumbering the Enemy

Each side can add their total numbers to an Appeal, thus favoring the side with the most people.

Catching an Enemy by Surprise

A side is treated as having no captain until their captain’s Initiative has come up.

Having Your Battle Standard Up & Visible

If your battle standard is up and visible, the party captain (or acting captain) can add their Pre Check Mod to any Appeals made.

Also, any abilities that the Party Captain has that effect Confrontations can also be used during this Confrontation.

Otherwise, a person will need to be able to see their captain directly to gain this benefit.

Devotions

Normally, Devotions would add to your points automatically but since points don’t matter, it would make them useless in Morale Confrontations but, as we all know, why you fight does matter.

If a Devotion applies to all the people being targeted that round, they add the lowest applicable Devotion to their Check.

Who Controls the Morale

It may be the case that the Party Leader, who should be responsible for the Morale Confrontation is also your main line combatant and is already busy juggling an enemy or two.

You can have a player who runs a less combat intense character, such as the magic user or diplomat, oversee the Confrontation in that case, however, only the actual Party Leader’s abilities affect the number or size of those dice applied.

Confrontations within Morale Confrontations

Whether or not you have a captain, individual combatants can also initiate their own, personal Confrontations with those they’re fighting.

These are typically 1 round affairs (or treated as such), and only involve the combatants’ Characteristics and any situational arguments that apply.

Ending Combat

Combat ends when one side is incapacitated, reached a truce, or have fled the field. At that point the Clock stops, and play moves back to narrative time.

If you engaged in a Morale Confrontation, everyone on the side that won the combat gains the difference in points to their Integrity, regardless of whether or not their side has less. So, if the players have 3 points and the goblins had 7, every player still gains 4 Integrity.

The losing side subtracts that with the same caveats.
