Favor

Favor is a measure of your standing with a given god. It’s not an absolute measure, however, but more a measure of the chance that a Divine will answer your prayer. How far your Favor goes ranges widely based on how devoted you are to a god and how much Integrity you have.

Invoking Miracles

Now that you’ve got a nice stash of Favor, you probably want to spend it. That’s pretty simple: pray out loud for the Miracle in question. Silent prayers don’t work because no Divine is going to answer a prayer, they’re not going to get credit for.

For every Favor you spend, you get another use of your Grit Dice but comes with a few differences: the first, and most major, is that you can spend it on ANYTHING - you want to run faster or jump higher? You want to heal your wounds? You want to attack slightly faster? You can with Favor, however, the effects are only temporary, of course.

The second is that you can spend to change the world around you. Every Favor you spend can change a 5 ft square area with the following restriction: it can’t directly contradict things that have already been said.

For instance, you cannot wish to have an avalanche suddenly on the steppe or an inn to appear before the party that wasn’t there before, but you could wish for a flash flood or for an inn to be over the next rise, which was just out of view when you prayed.

Restrictions

Miracles do come with several restrictions.

Covenant

Miracles are bound by Covenant: you can’t pray for something your Divine doesn’t have power over, so a Divine of the city can’t really help you in the mountains, for example.

But this is where being in a pantheon does help out: you can pray to another Divine in your pantheon for their blessing instead, but it requires twice as much Favor, and you don’t gain the benefit of your Cosmic Link.

Room for Doubt

Another restriction is that the Miracles need to be subtle: no angels coming down from the skies to keep you from falling to your death but perhaps there’s water or a passing hay cart that catches you just in time to soften your landing. There needs to be room for doubt.

Banking Grit Dice

The final restriction is that the Grit Dice must be spent on what was prayed for and they must all be spent before the end of the session (the Divines being fickle with those who keep them waiting). Otherwise they’re lost.

This can mean that they’re temporarily banked but it doesn’t mean that you can spend them freely.

Miraculous Healing

Servants can invoke miracles to heal their companions if their Covenant is specifically healing related.

However, the number that it rolled is subtracted from the days a wound has left to heal, not from the damage of the wound.

Miraculous Payday

Servants can also invoke Miracles to come across needed equipment and money with their roll representing how many sc they can get from it, however, characters who try to abuse this might end up receiving less, if not nothing - miracles are meant for those in need, not those soaked in greed.

Antagonistic Faiths

Different faiths don't always get a long. This can either be from a doctrinal dispute, bad blood from bad history, or just a personal dislike. But, whatever the case, beneficial Miracles of a faith never affect members of a rival faith, even if it would sense that they do.

For instance, a priest being chased be a cleric of an enemy faith finds himself at a window with a long jump. He says a quick pray and looks down again only to find a soft pile of hay that he didn't notice before (but was put there by his pray of faith). He jumps and lands in it, taking reduced damage.

The enemy cleric, hot on his heels, looks down and sees the hay so jumps to, but he takes full damage, regardless of landing in the hay. Serves him right for being a heretic.

Sacred & Profane Spaces

Holy places are those areas that are closer to the God’s Realm, where the Middle Realm is weaker. There the faithful can almost feel the Divines walking around.

In a sacred space dedicated to a certain Divine, the Grit Dice you get becomes 1 size larger. It also fills the devoted with a sense of purpose, temporarily increasing their Devotion by 1 for Confrontations and, if defending the space, their Devotion applies to Morale, of course.

There are also those areas that are abhorrent to the gods. Desecrated altars, dwarven holds, and other spaces the Divine presence is absent.

These Profane spaces net Grit Dice 1 size smaller, down to a d2 for each Favor spent to the Divine space is Profane to. The unanointed are completely unable to invoke Miracles.

Acquiring Favor

Servants turn their eyes from the earth below to higher worlds, both literally and metaphorically. In this way, they’re the keeper of the times and seasons, as well as the calendar to note the occurrence of Holy Days.

As such, players who play Servants will need to keep track of the passage of time. Doing so gives them special bonuses according to the schedule laid out below.

You’ll note that it follows the Gregorian Calendar layout with holy days happening according to the Judeo-Christian traditions. The assumption that the characters will be following an in-game calendar with random Holy Days specific to their cult but we’re “translating” into the Gregorian calendar, in the same way that we assume the characters are speaking in their native fantasy tongues and not in English.

Doing this makes it easier for players to keep track because the calendar is a familiar shape. They won’t have to track out a whole year and remember that Beltane is on the 5th of Seed’s Row.

Rules for using your own calendar and keeping all Servants relatively balanced against each other is going to be the Deluxe Rules.

Favor is unique to the Divine you’re petitioning. Gaining Favor with another Divine, means that you lose an equal amount of Favor with all others because gods are bad at sharing.

Daily Sacrifices

Every Day

These are small, daily sacrifices. These tend to cost about 1 or 2 sc, 5 to 6 points of Stress, or 1 or 2 Vitality, depending on your Divine's preferences. They can be the sacrifice of a small, sacred animal, the burning of a portion of your meal, or the lighting of incense during morning prayers.

Servants

These sacrifices net 1 Favor for Servants of that Divine.

Anointed

The anointed gain 1 Integrity for observing these sacrifices.

Unanointed

Nothing initially. An unanointed character that strictly observes their daily sacrifice can gain a free Devotion to the faith; the first step to becoming anointed.

Sabbatic Holy Days

Every 7 Days

This is a day of rest and contemplation, where the faithful are instructed to put aside the things of the world and focus on the Divine with activities related to their Covenant.

There is an emphasis on ritual over practicality to emphasize this divide, so while a Divine of Battle encourages his followers to fight every day, on his sabbath, he only wants them to focus on ritualistic combat and controlled bloodshed, as opposed to actual combat.

Again, this is to remind his followers of the holiness of the action and by forcing his followers to stop and think about their actions, he forces them to contemplate the deeper realities of combat.

The sabbath takes up 1 to 3 hours and the rest of the day can be spent at the faithful wants.

Servants

Servants who observe the sabbath of their Divine gain all Favor back.

Anointed

Anointed worshippers make a d20! + Pres Check Mod + applicable Religion Rank vs d20! + 6.

Success gives them all their Favor back.

Unanointed

Unanointed worshippers gain nothing.

Lunar Holy Days

4 Sabbatic holy days

Where normal Sabbaths are days of rest, Lunar Holy Days are about sacrifices and consecration, which are thematically appropriate to the cult’s Covenant. Those observing the holy day need to stop and spend the whole day or they gain no benefit.

Servants

Servants gain all Favor back plus extra Favor equal to their Pres Check Mod (although this can never penalize them).

This extra Favor can take them over their normal max and lasts until the next Lunar Holy Day.

Anointed

The anointed gain all Favor back.

Unanointed

Unanointed who celebrate the Lunar Holy Day gain 1 Favor.

Solar Holy Days

Every 3 Lunar Holy Days

These holy days celebrate the turning of the seasons and where the other festivals can be severe, with fasting and reading and nerd stuff like that, Solar Holy Days are about feasting and letting loose a bit. The Divines demand their faithful to party all day.

The anointed faithful gain their full Favor back and the unanointed gain their Min Favor from Presence if they roll a 4 or better on their Grit Die adding one Devotion related to that Divine

Servants

Servants gain all Favor back plus extra Favor equal to their applicable Devotion.

This extra Favor can take them over their normal max and lasts until the next Solar Holy Day.

Anointed

The anointed gain all Favor back plus extra Favor equal to their Pres Check Mod (although this can never penalize them).

This extra Favor can take them over their normal max and lasts until the next Lunar Holy Day.

Unanointed

Unanointed worshippers make a d20! + Pres Check Mod + applicable Religion Rank vs d20! + 6.

Success gives them all their Favor back.

Annual Holy Days

Every 4 Solar Holy Days

The Annual Holy Day either celebrates the new year or an important event in the cult, such as the birth of their messiah or the founding of their faith. It is a 2 day affair and is often occupied by feasting, gift giving, public rituals, and other such nonsense.

Servants and Anointed followers gain all Favor back with Servants also gaining extra Favor equal to their Devotion and the Anointed followers gaining extra Favor equal to a roll of their Grit Die. For Servants, this extra Favor is reset on the next Annual Holy Day (which means you can’t stockpile it). The unanointed gain 1 Favor.

Servants

Servants gain all Favor back plus extra Favor equal to their applicable Devotion, their Pres Check Mod (minimum 0), and a roll of their Grit Die.

This extra Favor can take them over their normal max and lasts until the next Annual Holy Day.

Anointed

The anointed gain all Favor back plus extra Favor equal to their applicable Devotion.

This extra Favor can take them over their normal max and lasts until the next Annual Holy Day.

Unanointed

Unanointed worshippers gain all their Favor back.

Alm Scripts

Special

An alm script is a small prayer, inscribed on a piece of vellum. When burnt, the burner receives an amount of temporary Favor equal to what is invested in the alm script

This Favor can bring a person over their Max Favor but it has to be used immediately in with a prayer that is uttered when the script is burnt.

Creating Alm Scripts

Any Servant can create an alm script, however, few outside of chantries and monasteries do.

It requires 20 sc of special materials (consecrated inks of various colors, silver-inlaid quills, and vellum made from an animal sacred to the Divine) and a day’s worth of work where the Servant only works on transcribing the script.

For every 10 Favor the Servant spends while making the script, 1 Favor is invested in the script at the end of the process.

Chantries and monasteries will produce alm scripts as a way of supplementing their income (see the Alm Script entry in the Goods, Adventuring table of Chapter 2.6: Equipment).

Taking an Oath

Special

Not a sacrifice or holy day parse, Taking an Oath is essentially getting Favor on loan from the Divines for the promise that, if you get out the bad situation you're in, you'll make a huge sacrifice to them soon after.

By Taking an Oath, you gain a die worth of Favor for every 20% of treasure you gain from the adventure you promise to the Divine, should you survive.

This die is equal to your Grit Dice if you’re anointed to the Divine you’re petitioning.

The Unanointed must also promise to become Anointed in addition to the cash and goods, in which case they gain a d6! worth of Favor. This Favor can put you over your max but must be spent on a Miracle immediately.

The Divines are extremely litigious. If you don't fulfill your oath in 1 month + 1 for every Integrity Category higher than Average Integrity you're in, they will seek vengeance.

You may no longer gain any Favor with any Divine that shares a pantheon with the one you're in and what you do have will decay, causing you to lose 1 Favor per day.

Priests of the Divine will also condemn you as an oath breaker, a very serious accusation, causing you to drop 1 Integrity Category per month and preventing you from increasing your Integrity.

Should you fall into No Integrity, you gain the Excommunicated Flaw.

Crusades & Pilgrimages

Special

There are holy places to visit and holy things to be done.

Crusades and Pilgrimages are special rewards the GM can give out for furthering the Divine’s will. This can either be through an actual holy war or by waging a spiritual war of conversion. These don’t even need to be things done on purpose and can be part of the campaign.

The GM should award a roll of the crusader or pilgrim's Grit Dice to their Favor, allowing it to go over the Max Favor, and awarding them Integrity for furthering their deity’s influence in the Middle Realm.

Additionally, if it was an amazing feat of faith, the GM may also increase the character’s Devotion size.

The unanointed gain no benefit from crusades and pilgrimages.

Condemnation & Absolution

Special

Servants and Anointed followers are expected to hold to the Divine’s tenets more closely and so can come under condemnation for disobeying their deity.

GMs may subtract Favor from Servants and Anointed followers for sins against their religion or prevent them from gaining Favor for prolonged transgressions.

Servants and Anointed followers with the Religion Advanced Skill in their Covenant receive a warning before they act (having studied their religion to some extent).

Those that have transgressed must seek absolution before they gain the benefit of holy days and sacrifices again. What this means varies based on the magnitude of the sin.

Greater Miracles

Greater Miracles are the most primitive, and powerful, expressions of Divine power and Weird-Shaping. The first Servants and Weird-Adepts were the wise that would listen to the winds of fate and read the runes.

It is old as the Bones of the Earth and just as potent.

Great Miracles can do and teach much but come at a high cost, meaning that they’re typically only done for things of great importance

Requirements

If you’re trained in Lore or Weirdcraft, you know about and can perform Greater Miracles, regardless of Archetype.

Outcomes

A Greater Miracle has 1 of 2 outcomes that must be selected before Reading the Fates:

1) Physical Phenomena

2) Secrets Revealed

Physical Phenomena

Physical phenomenon includes any natural event of any kind, scale, or reach. A localized thunderstorm or flooding; a nation-wide drought or the sinking of a continent all count as physical phenomena.

It also includes unnatural changes, such as turning every man in a village to stone, putting a princess to sleep, or sealing someone's mouth shut.

Scope and scale are not an issue.

Secrets Revealed

This lets the unknown be known and what was lost be found again.

All knowledge is able to be revealed through Greater Miracles.

What Greater Miracles Can’t Do

Greater Miracles have immense power but are limited in some ways.

There are two major things that a Greater Miracle can’t do is:

1) Raise the dead back to full life

2) Bend someone to your will

That said, lesser versions can be attempted, such as bringing someone to half-life or bringing them back to a short period of time.

Or stupefying someone or altering their personality.

The Greater Miracle

A Greater Miracle requires a number of conditions to be met. Some of these are metaphorical, some are physical, some are performative, and some are more esoteric. Not all are linked to what is being asked.

Causing a Physical Phenomena requires 1d6! conditions per level of feat while Secrets Revealed requires 1d4! conditions per level of feat.

For Physical Phenomena, 1 dice would be a person or town, 2 dice would be between town and kingdom, 3 dice would be a continent, 4 dice would be the whole world.

For Secrets, 1 dice would be learning something that was recently forgotten and might still be found normally. It might also be learning the truth about something remembered but not understood (like a local legend). 2 dice would be something that was recently forgotten and can long longer be remembered. 3 dice would be something forgotten some time ago. 4 dice would be to learn something never known or something that is normally unable to be known.

So, reversing the enchantment on a town or power might require 1d6! conditions while sinking a continent might require 3d6! conditions.

On the other hand, revealing someone hidden about a person might be 1d4! conditions while learning the answer to the Ultimate Question of Life, the Universe, and Everything, known only to the Divines might by 4d6! conditions.

The GM has leeway in both the number of required conditions and the specifics so a smaller ask will have a small condition, even if, categorically, it’s the same condition.

For instance, if the players are asking to reveal the goal of their enemy, and the GM chooses “Sacrifice a Superior or Wondrous item”, they might choose any +1 item, while ones that are trying to detonate the fantasy equivalent of a nuke on the capital city might be required to sacrifice an ancient artifact, which they then have to track down, retrieve, and then destroy by tossing into a volcano.

Performing The Greater Miracle

The actual performing the Greater Miracle relies on its conditions but often requires returning to the Place of Power and performing the actions, swearing an oath, or some other thing that performatively shows that the requirements have been completed and that the Greater Miracle is finished.

The effects of the Greater Miracle then takes place in a suitable thematic or dramatic way, including visions, great winds and thunderstorms, the sun behind blotted out, divine fire or multi-colored fairy lights shooting into the sky and descending on those the Greater Miracle effects.

The specifics are left up to the GM.

Limits Of The Performance

There are two things to note: the Miracle is always one-time use and has to be done by those that were present at the Reading of the Fates. Even if the augur wrote down all the instructions and passed it off to a band of mercenaries, who were not present at the reading, to fulfill, and they did everything that was required, the Miracle wouldn’t occur. The Miracle’s power is bound to the weird of those that are seeking it.

Once a Greater Miracle is performed, meeting the requirements again doesn’t do anything; the augur would have to read the Fates again to learn the new requirements.
