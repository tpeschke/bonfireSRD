Introduction

Challenges cover a lot of ground: in fact, it’s easier to say that pChallenges are anything that’s not covered by Confrontations or Combats.

I know, that doesn’t help, so here’s a more practical definition:

A Challenge is when the characters have a thing they want to accomplish(an Objective) but one or more things that would result in meaningful failure stand in their way (Obstacles).

Challenges are a framework that you can use as a catch-all for all the other types of situations that might come up in play.

Check Die

When making a Check using this framework, you’re going to be rolling on a d20!.

Objectives

What do the players want? What thing, material or immaterial do they want? Where do the players want to be?

This is the Objective and is often determined by the Goals of the characters involved.

Setting up the endpoint allows you to get the scale of a Challenge down, which can help you get a scale and number of the Obstacles the characters are going to face.

Obstacles

Obstacles are places along the path to the Objective where meaningful failure could occur.

And we’re defining “meaningful” here as “things that will either effect the likelihood and shape of the Objective or the next Obstacle”.

So, for instance, if you’re breaking into house, climbing the wall could result in meaningful failure as a fall could damage the character, making it more difficult for them to perform the next task.

Or, if you’re researching a spell, an Obstacle could be taking notes: poor notes could mean that either you take longer to get the spell or you miss an important part of the shaping, and your spell now has a side effect.

The Obstacles should make sense: don’t feel like you need to force them or hit an arbitrary number, although you might find that how characters deal with Obstacles could result in new Obstacles or another Challenge.

For instance, fixing the serious side effects from shaping that new spell could result in another Challenge where the characters now have to figure out a way to undo a negative enchantment.

Or it could just result in another Obstacle. It all depends on the focus of the game.

Difficulty

Difficulty is a dice that is added to the Skill Check based on things that might be going on at the time to either help or hinder your success. This difficulty is noted just as a single dice (+d6!, for example) with you’ll add to the d20!.

Complications & Failures

Failing a Check doesn’t automatically mean that you’re instantly screwed: Failing a single Move Silently Check doesn’t result in the guards catching you. In Bonfire, there are Complications and hard failures.

Every Obstacle will have a number of Complications it can gain before it hard fails. This is called its Failure Threshold. You gain Complications by failing Skill Checks: every 5 points below what you needed to get gives the Obstacle 1 Complication, rounded up (so a failure by 1 - 5 is still considered a Complication).

Complications represent complications, such as the guard hearing something, or you leaving a clue behind that might give someone a heads up that you're there. An Obstacle gets +2 for every Complication is has on it.

When an Obstacle gets more Complications than its Failure Threshold, it hard fails. Hard failures are when something bad actually happens. In this case, it does result in you getting caught by the guards, or it results in your falling off the wall and taking damage. Even after a hard failure, you can sometimes try again, like in the case of falling off the wall.

Failure doesn’t diminish during the Skill Challenge, but it does reset for a new one.

Complications in the Narrative

Complications don’t mean that the Skill Challenge grinds to a halt while the players keep reattempting that Skill: instead, it should represent the players coming closer to danger.

For a quick example, let’s say that there’s an adventurer, we’ll call him New York Smith, York for short, who is attempting to jump a chasm, which has a Failure Threshold of 1.

He rolls his Check but fails by 7, giving the Obstacle 1 Complication. In this case, he obviously jumped but also didn’t make it. What does that mean? Well, York didn’t quite make it but managed to grab the ledge as he fell, so he didn’t fall to his death, but he isn’t safe either.

York is grabbing hold of the ledge so he can't make another Jumping Check, but he does reason that he can use Climbing. Makes sense to the GM so they roll again. If York fails this time, he falls, but if he succeeds, he manages to scramble up the cliff, safe on the other side, but he is working at a disadvantage: the GM gets +2 to her roll this time around so he better hope he put Ranks in Climbing.

Boons

These are the opposite of Complications: they give you a small benefit or boost for accomplishing a test extra well. You get 1 Boon for every 5 full points above what you needed.

Fallout

It is assumed that, during a Challenge, a character is working their hardest to win, therefore, every Check, whether successful or not, causes d4! Stress or 2d4! Stress if using a Weirdcraft Advanced Skill.

However, a Boon can be spent to not gain this Stress.

Time Scale

Like Confrontations, Challenges don’t have a set time scale they work off of, however, unlike Confrontations, once their time scale is set, there’s very little variation.

So, for instance, breaking into a house could have a time scale of a couple of hours, while tracking a bandit could have a scale of days, weeks, or months.

Sometimes an Obstacle will be on a longer or shorter time scale but that should be handled on a case-by-case basis.

For instance, while tracking Corwin, the characters find themselves lost in the old mines. This is an Obstacle that will take a few days to overcome.

Later, they come across a chasm that used to have a utility bridge, but it was destroyed after Corwin crossed it, so they need to find another way to get across.

Another Obstacle within the same Challenge but this time it only takes hours to resolve.

Strategies

Assets & Contacts

These represent static pools of knowledge or information that you can pull from if you have time. So, for instance, a book on the subject, or a library are examples of an Asset but also an old friend who used to be a ranger and who could give you a few pointers could also be considered one.

Anything you can think of that might apply might be an Asset you can draw on.

Assets have two stats: their Knowledge and their Organization. Basically, how much they know and how easy that knowledge is to get.

Each of these is given a qualifier of either Poor, Average, or Good.

For Knowledge, Poor represents a +3, Average +6, and Good +9 while for Organization Poor is Days, Average is Hours, and Good is Minutes.

Spending 1 unit of an Asset’s Organization gives you +1 bonus up to their Knowledge. So, if you’re searching through a poorly taken care of but extensive library (Knowledge: Good, Organization: Poor), spending 5 days there gives you a +5, while spending two weeks there only gives you a +9.

That bonus can then be redeemed on a Skill Check later on. Although it can only be redeemed once, it does apply to any rerolls made against the same Obstacle.

You don’t automatically know the Rank or the Time Investment of an Asset. This can also be applied to Weapon, Armor, and Shield Trainings through manuals and sparring.

Another benefit of Assets is that it is often multifaceted and can be used to get different bonuses. That old ranger friend can help you with navigation but also probably knows a thing or two about botany.

Assets can also be increased and expanded upon as well, increasing its qualifiers. A good relationship with a contact might give you information quicker, donating to the local library may increase its selection, or hiring excavators to dig up a ruin may unearth more artifacts that help you decipher that ancient script.

There may be some special cases where an Asset will take care of itself, but you may have to check back on it, such as Snaring in the forest. You set your trap and then a day or two later, you can go back and check it to see if you caught anything - in the meantime, you can get other work done.

Redeeming Contacts

Characters get a number of starting contacts based on their Presence, with the expectation they'll gain more as they adventure, but these starting contacts can prove useful as they can be redeemed at any time and defined on the spot.

So, if the players need, for example, access to a monastery’s library, one of the players can remember that one of his buddies is actually a monk there and might be able to help, redeeming one of his contacts.

After that, of course, contacts can be used time and time again: they're part of the world.

How a contact helps is largely determined by the circumstances of why they were redeemed but the GM should, for the most part, consider them Assets starting with Poor Knowledge and Organization. A player can increase that when they redeem a contact by sacrificing additional contacts, raising one of their stats on a one-for-one basis.

So, a character with 3 unredeemed contacts can bring one in as a smuggler buddy who knows a lot about the local underworld, which would normally only cost him 1 of his contacts, but, instead, he sacrifices his 2 remaining ones to increase his smuggler buddy's Knowledge to Good. He can't redeem any more contacts but at least his smuggler buddy knows what he's talking about.

Roleplaying Out a Scene vs Using Assets

Assets are not meant to replace specific roleplaying areas where you need specific pieces of information: they’re meant to reward players for remembering to do otherwise boring and mundane things that might still help their character. You can also combine the two.

For instance, if the players are hunting a griffon, they might consider talking to a local monster hunter, who, though a scene, tells them specifics of how the griffon hunts and fights. During this time, the players learn that griffons tend to nest in mountains, so, after the talk is finished, one of the players hits up an old friend who knows a lot about climbing and spends a few hours training and getting points with him.

The first scene is roleplayed in detail while the second is handwaved because there’s nothing specific that the player will learn that will help them. Additionally, the first scene could also give the players a bonus to their Monstercraft roll for remembering additional things about the beast.

Using Other Players as Assets

I think it's completely reasonable that players would want to use each other's characters as Assets and why shouldn't they? So, in this case the player’s Knowledge is instead based around the Rank they have in the applicable Skill with their Organization being based on their Characteristics.

If they have 3 or fewer Characteristics related to teaching or communication, they’re considered Poor; 4 to 6 they’re considered Average; greater than 6, Good.

GMs should also feel free to reward players who sit there and talk things out and plan by awarding the Asset bonus as well, even if they don't spend a full hour+ planning.

Equipment

Your kit can really pull you through in a pinch and these are catchall rules for the use of equipment that doesn’t already come with auxiliary benefits, such as weapons (damage) and armor (DR).

Players are assumed to be using all equipment that would help them with a Check if they have time so they shouldn’t feel the need to go through their list of climbing gear every time they scale a wall, although, if time is short and they need to rush, that may be appropriate.

On a Skill Check, the first piece of equipment you use gives you a free reroll, taking the higher of the two results. After that, each additional piece gives you a +1, although if this additional gear has Traits that apply, you can use that instead of the +1. More on equipment Traits is found in the Deluxe version of Chapter X: Equipment.

Redeeming this bonus causes the tool degrades, giving it (and any other equipment you use) 1 Wear, whether you succeed or not. More on Wear can be found in Chapter X: Equipment.

Asset- & Equipment-Based Skilled

Some skills are only going to be useful if you have the proper equipment or the right Asset. For instance, if you’re not out in the wilderness, you can’t really use Snaring or if you don’t have the proper tools or a woodshop, Maintenance won’t do you any good. In cases, being able to make the Check in the first place is considered an auxiliary benefit and so this bonus doesn't apply. This will need to be handled on a case-to-case basis.

Helper Skills

Let’s say that you’re tracking someone in the wilderness. You have Hunting at Rank 4 and Endurance at Rank 3. Seems like being able to move quickly over a longer period of time, should help you catch the guys, and it does.

If you have another Advanced Skill that you think might help you, you can add it as a bonus, however doing so costs you d4! Stress for every Skill you apply. This can't be reduced by Not Sweating It.

This may seem like it is nearly identical to Preparation, and they are very close. The difference is that Preparation is a general thing that applies to a Skill Check you’re going to make in the future, while using a helper Skill is in the moment and is specific to the scenario. You can use both at the same time, since the help Skill gives a bonus and Preparation gives a reroll.

Skill Mobbing

For GMs, note that Helper Skills are how you handle Skill Mobbing, where everyone just overpowers a roll by all rolling against it. Instead, only one person rolls and only those with an applicable Advanced Skill and willing to take that Stress add their Rank to help out.

Multi-Tasking

Let’s be realistic here: you’re probably going to end up trying to do two things at once, like climbing a wall and doing so quietly.

If that’s the case, you’re going to have to “split” your d20! into smaller dice and assign those to each of the Checks you’re trying to make, because your attention is divided. The total max value of the dice can never exceed 20 but it can be lower than it. The other side will still be rolling full d20!s against you.

So, if you’re climbing a wall and trying to do so quietly, you’d have to make a Climbing and a Move Silently Check. You could split it like so: d12! for Climbing and d8! for Move Silently, or d10! for Climbing and d10! for Move Silently, or d12!+d4! for Move Silently and d4! for Climbing.

Or you could just say “screw being quiet” and roll a d20! for Climbing and take a 0 for Move Silently. In this case, the GM would just roll against your Skill Rank. So, if you choose to ignore Move Silently but have a Rank 4 in it, the GM would roll their dice, and you can hope they don’t roll higher than 4.

Either way, besides this, it works like a normal Check, except that, for the Stress gain, you're going to roll 1d4! for each Skill they're attempting but only use the highest if applicable. They need to succeed all Checks by 5 to completely remove the Stress gain.

Not Paying Attention

Multi-Tasking is also used when someone isn't paying attention, such as a guard who's on watch but he's been there for several hours at this point so he's not doing it so closely. In this case, start at a d10!, lowering it further as needs be.

Helping Others

It feels good to help people and Bonfire is all about group cooperation. The fighter helps the skill monkey survive combat and the skill monkey helps the fighter not look like an idiot while doing detective work.

And how you help other characters will really depend on the situation. Perhaps your Preparation can be used to help another person hide or perhaps you have a bit of knowledge that they don’t.

Really, this will be done to what feels right in the moment, but this note is here to draw attention to someone helping you as an Asset and someone adding their Rank to yours with a Helper Skill, and how they’re different.

And the difference is time. If you have plenty of time to coach someone while they’re climbing a cliff face, you’d be an Asset and you wouldn't take Stress.

But if you’re being chased by the guards and it’s a matter of seconds or minutes, you’d add a Helper Skill, and you would need to take the Stress.

Note that if the skill monkey is also trying to climb the cliff face and coach as they go, that would be considered a d10! worth of effort for Multi-Tasking, though you don’t have to actually roll to see if you help.

Not Sweating It

While the default is to assume that characters actually care about the outcome of the Check, that's not always the case since not everything is equally important. In the cases of lesser importance, the character can simply not sweat it.

In this case, they take no Stress from the Check but take a -4 penalty.

Preparation

Preparation is anything your character does to help a specific Skill Check that will be made in the future (even if that future is 2 seconds from now). Note that this doesn't have to be a Check you make: it can also apply to an ally's Check (if it makes sense).

Usually, these only take a couple of seconds but can vary widely depending on the circumstances. Throwing a rock is a matter of moments while constructing a gilly suit will take a lot longer.

As long as there is time, there is no chance of failure or, if the players have plenty of time, Preparation just happens. Mark what you did for later in the Skill Challenge.

However, if there is a time crunch or a chance of failure, Preparation works like a normal Skill Check with success means the preparation is actually useful.

Either way, you only get one Prep Check per Advanced Skill that applies for each upcoming Check, unless you have Assets but remember that they do take time.

That means that if you know you're going to have to make a jump, you can use Acrobatics to stretch, Endurance to do a few practice sprints to the edge, and Sports (long jumping) to remember techniques all to prep for the same Jump check.

When you redeem Preparation that you’ve readied, you can reroll Obstacle's d20, taking the worse of the two results.

Planning & Action Phases

While it isn’t entirely black and white, it’s helpful to think of Challenges as composing two phases: planning and action; the laying out the plan and then the heist.

Planning is gathering intel, getting your team and equipment together, and planning your route of attack. This is where you’ll use Preparation and Assets mostly.

Action phase is when you’ll be doing a lot of the Checks and Stress, as well as equipment and companions - using what you learned in the Prep Phase to help you get through the sticky parts.

But don’t get too hung up on separating these ideas though. Even during the heist, you might find it worth your time to sit on your heels and take your time while doing some Preparation as there will almost certainly be problems that crop up that you need to adjust to.

Rushing It

If you’re in a hurry to get the job down, you can just rush it. This lowers the time a Check actually makes but gives you a -5.

The bonus for this is that Complications you would get from the Check don't get added to Universal or Linked Obstacles in the Challenge, though Hard Failures do add 2 Complications to them.
