Skills are used as a way to determine the success or failure of a given action, based on what your character knows about the given subject or about how to perform the given task.

You don’t have to use these rules all the time, i.e., you don’t have to roll to see if your character successfully walks across a room, but at times they’re a good way to semi-randomly determine the outcome of a tricky task, i.e., walking across a room full of traps.

TYPES of SKILLS

There are two types of skills that you’re going to be pulling from: Skill Suites and Advanced Skills.

Skill Suites represent broad swathes of knowledge that cover a variety of subjects. Suites are hard to increase because there’s so much ground to cover but can be used if you don’t have the specific Advanced Skill. An example of this is the Survival and Lore Skill Suites.

Advanced Skills are more tightly focused, covering one intellectual discipline. They’re easier to master but may not be applicable in every case. An example of this would be the First Aid, Fire Building, and Foraging skills in the Survival Skill Suite and the Arcana, History, and Medicine skills in the Lore Skill Suite.

ACQUIRING SKILLS

Throughout your character’s career, she’ll acquire and improve her skills. Both types of skills are purchased with CrP, that is spent either at Character Creation or on leveling up. All skills have a unique cost that represents how easy or difficult they are to master. You pay the price, you improve the skill.

SKILL SUITES

Skill Suites have a starting base amount of the relevant ability or the average of the relevant abilities listed.

ADVANCED SKILLS

Advanced Skills start at 0%: if you haven’t bought them, you can’t use them; you’ll have to use their parent Skill Suite instead.

If you’ve paid the price and got the skill, that Advanced Skill’s starting percentage is equal to its mother Skill Suite. So, the Religion skill would use Lore’s percentage as it’s base.

From there, you’ll roll a d12! plus the modifier from that specific skill’s relevant ability and add that to the base percentage, and wahla, your new percentage.

You character’s Rank in that skill is equal to the 100 and 10s digit. So, having a 36% in a skill gives you a Rank of 3.

The most likely scenario is that you’ll, at some point, want to improve one of your skills so that you’ll be better at it.

In both cases (Suites and Advanced Skills), you pay the price of that skill and you get to improve it.

Roll the dice that’s indicated based on that skill’s Rank. Add the modifier based on that skill’s relevant ability (or the average of them). Add that total to the skill’s percentage. That’s the new percentage.

Repeat this process for each time you purchased the skill.

SKILL CHALLENGES

A skill check is rather easy: roll the difficulty dice set by the GM. If you get under your Skill Rank, you succeed. If not, you fail.

Skill Rank 0

If you have sub 10 in a stat and are untrained in a Skill, you still succeed on Skill check if you roll a natural 1, however, you take 1 Stress, even if you weren’t Stressing about the roll before hand.

Because not all tasks are the same in terms of how challenging they are (building a house is harder than building a simple table), skills challenges are given variable difficulties that are represented by different sized dice. The bigger the dice, the harder the task.

This difficulty dice is an abstraction, of course, and represents a number of things, and so, therefore, can be changed based on planning and equipment used, or the amount of time granted to complete it. So, for example, a character wishing to pick a lock underwater might be rolling a d12!, but one doing the same task while blindfolded might be rolling a d20!.

It may be possible, because this is a fantasy game, to go beyond a Heroic level task, in that case add an additional smaller die to a d20!.

Following the previous example, if someone was trying to pick a lock underwater while blindfolded and having to do so under a minute, he might roll a d20! + d4!, hoping that that total is under his Skill Rank.

Note on notation: whenever something says it increases the difficulty by 1, that means it increases it by 1 step. So, a Challenging check increased by 2 becomes a Heroic check.

COORDINATED EFFORT

🜂 Advanced Rule

Others may offer to help you out with a Skill Challenge if they’re trained in the specific Advanced Skill you’re rolling for, but not for a general Skill Suite check. Doing so increases your effective Rank by 1 temporarily, however, the more people the less benefit, and the number of people it takes to get the next +1 to your Rank doubles each time, so it takes an additional 2 people (3 total) to raise your Rank to +2.

OPPOSED CHECKS

Now, some checks won’t be your character battling against the elements or an object, she’ll be facing off against another character, who has their own set of skills and can think and act for themselves.

This case, you roll an opposed check. Both sides roll a d20! + their Skill Rank and the side with the higher total wins, whatever that may mean for the situation.

One character may also have an advantage over the other. Increase or lower the dice size of what each person rolls as appropriate.

RETRYING CHALLENGES

There may be times where a character has enough time to try a skill challenge again, however, he must change the circumstances in a unique way before he proceeds.

For example, a character climbing a wall fails his first attempt. He’s got rope and crampons on his shoes that made the attempt a little easier but he just didn’t make it. He can’t simply roll his check again.

He studies the wall for a bit and his player decides that his character might be better off finding footholds if he removes his shoes, so he does so. He is now qualified to try again. His GM decides that the difficulty remains the same.

The climber fails again. He can’t simply put his shoes back on: he’s already tried that, so he knots his rope to make it easier to grip. Now he’s eligible for another try, but since he’s knotted the rope, the GM decides that it’s a bit easier so this challenge is attempted as if it was one difficulty lower. This time, he succeeds.

A character gains 1 Stress per retry, regardless of the outcome, meaning that are intrepid scaler comes out of the situation with 2 additional Stress.

Parts of a Skill

It’s important to think about Skills in Bonfire having three parts: Mechanics, Knowledge, and Creativity.

Mechanics are the ‘solid’ part of the Skill and give that Skill a purpose that can always be utilized. This gives every Skill a definite thing it can do and reason why you’d spend your precious CrPs on it.

Knowledge is a step beyond the mechanic of the Skill. This is most evident in the Advanced Lore Skills but it applies to every Skill. A climber can use her Climbing Skill to deduce how hard an ascent is going to be or a person with Use Rope can get a sense for how strong a length of rope is.

Creativity is the final and perhaps the most important part of a Bonfire Skill. This is applying and repurposing the Skill in creative ways that aren’t layouted specifically by the rules. For example, using your Quarter Mastering Skill to stow illegal items in your pack in such a way as to not be detected by the searching guards.

This suite covers all things that requires physical prowess and, to a lesser extent, pure manual dexterity.

CLIMBING This skill covers climbing and repelling up and down walls of various heights and textures.

Difficulty should be based on height, with about every 40 ft increasing the difficulty by 1. Textures should also play a major factor in not just how tough the check is but also how many checks need to be made for a given wall.

So, if there’s a wall, where the base is stone at a 45% slant for 20 ft, then it moves to close knit stones for another 40 ft, and then finally to a wood overhang for just 5 ft, and then to a wooden wall for 10 ft, there would be 4 Climbing checks in this order: Trivial, Challenging (Average for base but because it’s 40 ft, it gets bumped up 1), Challenging, and Easy.

DISARM TRAP It takes nerves of steel to be able to cut the right wire or trip the trap in such a way that the least bad thing happens

Disarming a trap takes a base of 30 minutes if you don’t want to just set it off. If you’re in a hurry, you can half that time but you increase the difficult by 1 each time you do so. So, reducing the time to 7 minutes, would bump a Trivial trap up to Average in difficulty.

A failure indicates that you set the trap off accidentally.

ESCAPE ARTIST This allows the character to escape bonds and restrictions and is always rolled opposed, either against the Rope Use skill of the person who tied them or against the quality of the manacles or binds the person was put in.

The character can make a straight Average check, not to break the bounds or get free, but to instead move his body to an advantageous position.

JUMPING You need to cover a distance without touching the ground: jump.

For every point you scored under what you needed, you travel either 2 ft horizontally or 1 ft vertically.

Jumping from a stand still is an Average check, while doing so with a running start is an Easy check. Add 1 to your roll for every Level of Fatigue your character has.

SPORT (specific) This covers the actually playing of a specific sport as well as an in-depth knowledge of the game’s rules and famous players and techniques. If it becomes a plot point, the GM should feel free to make this skill apply to only certain positions, like pitching or shortstop.

STEALTH When you want to move silently, you’ll want to use this skill. This involves being able to feel your way along and compensate for creaks.

This is always an opposed check vs any potentially listener’s Listening, with a failure indicating that the listener has heard something suspicious, though the person attempting Stealth may not be aware that they were detected.

SWIMMING This skill allows a character to not drown in water. Without at least basic training in this skill, a character can’t attempt to swim with simply an Athletics check.

The difficulty of the check is determined by the water (or other liquid, I don’t judge) you’re attempting to swim in, though simply trying to float on your back and letting the current take you reduces the difficulty by 2, though it is risky because you could get pulling into an aquifer and drowned that way or get pulled over a waterfall.

LORE SKILL SUITE

This suite covers general knowledge, which is mostly academic in nature. Most of it is pretty heavy stuff but the basics are second nature or stuff that’s been picked up here and there.

ADMINISTRATION While they’ll never inspire the awe or respect that other leaders do, people who oversee the day to day functions of an organization keep the lights on so that others can make the speeches.

Administrators can make an Average skill check to gain a clue about an item the party might need on their adventure, or a Challenging one to retroactively remember that they did buy an item back at the market for just such an occasion.

Each of these checks they make increases the difficulty of the next one by 1 step.

ARCANA The science of magic is an oft studied but little understood branch of Lore that requires a deep knowledge of the nature of the Painted World.

With this skill, someone can identify a spell, point out needed regents and processes for ritual castings, detect if something is magical, or even detect if a spell has been cast in an area, and have a chance of finding out what it was.

All of these, except for the last two, are Average checks, with detecting whether or not a spell was cast in an area is Trivial + 1 for every day since the casting, or to detect what specific spell it was, Challenging + 1 for every day since the casting.

HISTORY Everyone knows certain large events, but few can tie those events into an overarching narrative and understand their effects on later events.

The History skill allows a character to not just recall events but also to link them to the present day, to certain traditions and rituals of a culture, or the behavior of certain members of a culture, giving insight on how to proceed.

LANGUAGE (specific) A character receives this skill for free in their native tongue and it allows them to speak and be understood by others with skill in that language.

Checks are only made when timing is critical, such as communication in a battle, or when understanding is key, such as when debating foreign policy.

If one of the characters is in a Social Conflict with another in a tongue that’s not their native one, that person can only bring in as many Characteristic Die per escalation as he has Ranks in that Language.

Additionally, when two native speakers are in a Conflict, the one with the higher Language Rank gains a Situational Die (see Applicable Skills under the Situational Modifiers Advance Rule in Chapter 9: Social ). In case of tie, nothing happens.

The Lore Skill Suite can’t be used in place of a Language Challenge for tongues you don’t know unless you have the Language Skill in a related Language. Using a real world example, someone with the Language (Swedish) Advanced Skill couldn’t use the Lore Skill Suite to understand a person with Language (French) but could use it when talking to someone with Language (Norwegian) or Language (Danish).

LAW (culture) This skill covers the intricacies of the legal system, including landmark rules and common punishments for common crimes. A character can make their case or find loopholes to exploit the legal system.

A character’s Lore skill can only be used to substitute in for their native culture but not others. If the character does wish to branch out, his mastery of Law for that culture starts at 0, as he must learn everything from the ground up. Diving more into his own culture’s laws (buying this Advanced Skill for his culture) uses the Lore skill as base, as per usual.

LITERACY (language) Unlike other skills, this skill can’t be attempted unless you’ve specifically bought this Advanced Skill and it’s mastery starts at 0. That’s right; every character starts illiterate.

This skill allows a character to read and write in a language’s alphabet, giving them the power to pen books and poems, or passive aggressive notes.

MATHEMATICS While this may seem like just a skill that allows you to be able to juggle figures and add totals and calculate the area of a circle like a huge nerd, Mathematics has a more practical use: correctly guessing distances.

While you can try to eyeball how far away something is, an Average Mathematics check allows you to do rough calculations, giving you the ability to know exactly what penalty you’re going to get for using a ranged weapon or who’s going to be affected by the Area of Effect of a spell you’re going to cast.

MEDICINE With proper treatment and nursing, a wound that would lay you out for weeks, may only keep you down for a couple of days. This skill involves the knowledge of long term care, when bandages should be changed, which poultices will have the greatest effect, and what minor surgeries need to be performed.

A character makes a Trivial check adding 2 for each Severity of the Wound he’s administering to. On a success, this Wound heals 1 day quicker at each Severity.

So, a 5 Severity Wound would be an Average check. A success means that is was treated properly and takes 10 days to completely heal ( 4 + 3 + 2 + 1 + 0 ) instead of the normal 15.

Additionally, an Average check allows character to reroll their save vs Toxins, Poisons, or Diseases.

MONSTER CRAFT This Advanced Skill is under both the Lore and Survival Skill Suite but still counts as 1 skill, so you don’t have to buy it twice - they share their Rank. It simply represents the different ways that each Skill Suite approaches the craft.

Monster Craft gives you insight into the creatures that roam the Middle Realm. Starting with something simple, such as their name, and the progressively learning more and more about them, such as they’re are fundamentally two different types of Incorporeal Undead or that you can convince Demons to do things if you win a Conflict with them.

The knowledge should be fairly general - weaknesses or where they draw their power from, so it will never reveal anything meta such as what the monster’s attack or Health is but it can let you know if the creature is known for having a lot of Health or is good at hitting.

Each Monster Craft check starts as an Average Skill Challenge and answers one question the player has on a success but each successive check on the same monster increases the difficulty by 2. On top of this, for rare creatures or hard questions, the GM may up the Difficulty as she feels is appropriate.

NATURE This area of study covers the topography of the Middle Realm. It’s forests and rock formations and biomes. It helps predict whether or not it’s going to rain or whether the next summer is going to be a hot one.

Predicting what’s coming in the next hour is a Trivial check, with the difficulty increasing by 1 for each time you double the prediction window. So, detecting the weather in the next 2 hours is Easy, while doing so in the next 16 hours is a Hard check.

RELIGION (cult) This skill acquaints the character with a knowledge of cults, their common Covenants and Miracles, as well as their hierarchies, means of advancement and important members.

It also allows a character to gain a knowledge of their rituals and sacrifices, as well as the taboos of their clergy and how to appease their heavenly messengers and avenging angels.

The Religion Skill is taken for a single Covenant, but can purchased independently for different Covenants.

A character adds the tens digit of this skill to their chance of gaining Favor when making sacrifices to the god of that cult.

STREETWISE SKILL SUITE

This suite covers knowledge that a character might pick on the street or in the school of hard knocks. While Lore is book smart, Streetwise is street smart.

CURRENT AFFAIRS A character with this skill is well informed about the day to day affairs of regional powers and men and woman of importance, their dealings and alliances, as well as their hatreds. He can pick out celebrities and know how and why someone got rich and famous. They can also use this skill to guess the Honor category of someone by watching how people react to them.

DECEPTION This covers all sort of deception, where you want the person to believe opposite the truth, or, to put it simply, when you want to lie to them.

The level of difficulty to pull off a lie is based on how much of what you’re saying isn’t the truth, strictly speaking. This is always opposed by either the other character’s Deception or Perception but only if the character has reason to suspect that you’re lying to them.

Even if you succeed, this isn’t a permanent way of changing their mind. New evidence presented to that person may cause them to rethink their opinion on what you tried to sell them so be careful about what falsehoods you spin.

FORGERY Mostly commonly used for creating fake currency, forgery is a skill that allows a character to reproduce something effectively and fool others. This skill acquaints the character with common forging techniques and knowledge with what possible agents will being looked for.

The forger must also have the appropriate corresponding Craft or Trade skill, so he can’t forge coins if he doesn’t have the Metalworking skill, though that’s within reason: a character forging an official passport with one at hand doesn’t need to have Literacy as he can just copy the letters, though he may also need to have Artistry (wood cutting) to create the heraldry on it, but someone trying to forge an official declaration from skratch does need to have the Literacy skill in that language plus Artistry (wood cutting).

Forgery is opposed by Perception, the applicable skill, or Forgery but only if the character looking has reason to suspect there’s something else. A lazy guard probably won’t check your passport too closely right before lunch, but a particularly astute one who’s on the lookout for a promotion will, thus granting him a check.

GAMBLING/GAMING This skill doesn’t give the character a gift for winning games, just that he knows the rules and knows the odds, which could in turn lead to him winning the game.

INTUITION The ability to read a person’s face and gain insight into their desires and dreams allows a character to better understand what tact she should use to get her way. An Average Intuition check allows the character to read what is the strongest Trait, Passion, or Devotion that a character has. They can increase the difficulty of this check by 1 to either learn the actual dice size, or learn the next more powerful driving force that character has. To gain this insight, they must either watch them for a total of 3 hours + 1 hour for every additional thing they wish to learn, or talk to them for 10 minutes + 5 minutes for every additional. You can speed up the learning process, but 1 hour or 5 minutes, respectively, but doing so also increases the difficulty by 1.

LISTENING While Perception handles the other senses, Listening has to deal with your character’s auditory senses. This goes beyond simply hearing something: listening allows the character to know when an otherwise innocuous noise is actually something to pay attention to though it might not always be apparent why at first.

LOCK PICKING It takes a lot more know how then you might think to pick a lock. The amount of time it takes to pick any lock is based on your skill level and the quality of the lock.

Subtract the Time Off x how much you succeeded your check by from the Base Time.

PERCEPTION This covers four of the five senses that a character has: sight, touch, taste, and smell. Technically, your character is always receiving inputs from these senses but Perception helps filter out the most important ones. Can he detect if there are orcs in the nearby bush? Can she taste the hidden poison in her drink before she swallows? These are all Perception checks

Like Listening, sometimes Perception will help the character notice something that may be important, even if it’s not obvious why it’s grabbing your attention.

SLEIGHT OF HAND This skill covers all things that require you to do quick, delicate movements with your fingers. This may be slipping a card up your sleeve, picking a pocket, or doing cat’s cradle while bored.

TRAP FINDING This actually covers a lot more than simple detecting trip wires or arrow holes in a dungeon. This can detect pits and snares, as well as buttons disguised as bricks or switches hidden as sconces.

A character can sweep an entire room with a Hard check but a character may be awarded a modifier secretly by the GM if they point out 2 or 3 spots they want to pay extra close attention to if one or more of those places actually houses the trap. Obviously, you don’t earn that modifier if you just name everything in the room and the more specific you are with what you’re searching, the higher that modifier should be.

SURVIVAL SKILL SUITE

This suite covers everything that a character might need to survive in the wild; Knowledge of what plants to eat and the skills to hunt and breed animals.

BOTANY This gives a knowledge of what the herbs used in cooking, alchemy, and medicine look like in their natural environment and it allows you to identify their common uses.

Mostly, this is used for detecting poisonous plants, which is a Trivial check, but someone with the Medicine skill can also find any local plants that might help him in his cures with an Average check, lowering his Medicine check by 1 if succeeds.

CAMOUFLAGE This involves more than just ducking behind a crate or sliding into the shadow of a doorway; the skill of camouflage represents a character’s ability to blend into their surroundings, given enough time and patience. It also always them to help others hide more effectively as well.

Assuming that the character has time and equipment to do so, a character gains his skill percentage in Camouflage to his Hiding temporary. Additionally, under the same conditions, he can also give this bonus to other’s Hiding checks.

When used to hide something in plain sight, like a cart, building, or weapon, he may roll a check based on its size.

FIRE BUILDING Against the cold, lonely nights out in the wild, there is only the fertility symbols you carve for yourself and the roar of the fire. This skill allows you to build one of those things: the fire.

Under normal conditions, it takes about 30 minutes and a Trivial check to setup and light a fire, assuming you don’t have to go far out of your way to find the wood.

FIRST AID This gives the character a crude understanding of battlefield medicine: making splints and staunching wounds.

A character with this skill can make a check for each Wound sustained within the last 30 minutes with the difficulty dice equal to the Severity of the Wound rounded up to the closest dice size. So, a Severity 5 Wound has a d6! difficulty. Success means that the person can take 1 level of Severity off that Wound. So a 5 Severity Wound drops immediately to a 4 Severity Wound.

Each Wound takes about 3 minutes to deal with but he can lower that by 30 seconds for each level of difficulty he’s willing to increase his check by, down to 10 seconds. Additionally, you can lower the Wound by an another Severity if you increase the Difficulty of the check by 2.

HANDLE ANIMAL (specific) This skill deals with the handling and care of a specific animal, from birth to the slaughter house. It also deals with training and common illnesses to make sure the stock is healthy and viril.

The base price of this skill is calculated towards domesticated animals. If the player wishes to learn how to deal with wild animals, this skill costs thrice as much to purchase.

HIDING This allows a character to make the most of a situation or environment to cloak themselves against prying eyes. This is always opposed by Perception, with the difficulty determined by the area. Character can also use this to instantly get an idea of how well they can hide in the given space.

MONSTER CRAFT This skill is the same as the Monster Craft under the Lore Skill Suite. Look there for details on it.

NAVIGATION Either using the stars or the position of the moons and sun, this character can track their way even through the most untamed wilderness without getting lost. It also allows them to tell the time.

Without proper equipment, a character can only give rough estimations of where they are, anchored on larger geographic feature (like mountains or rivers but not towns or roads) to the nearest 10 miles.

SCAVENGING There’s a gift in high speed appraisal. This is often used by those picking over battlefields for anything they could sell, but it’s also found a niche in adventurers rifling through pockets for change and those looking for food or good meat off a rotting carcass.

A Trivial check tells a character which of two things considered is worth more. This can’t be used for coinage, but can be any other type of good. It also gives the character an idea of whether broken equipment can be reasonably salvaged.

This doesn’t give even a ballpark of what anything is worth but it only takes a few seconds of consideration.

SNARING This teaches the character how to properly create and place snares to trap smaller wildlife. It also gives some cursory knowledge on the best places to put them.

A successful Trivial check nets 1 lbs of usable game meat at the end of the day, with the amount you can catch increasing by 1 for each additional difficulty.

TRACKING Most of the time, you’re not going to down your query in one go, and so the hunt is on as your watch for subtle signs of their passing. While this skill was originally used to hunt wildlife, it has been repurposed by wardens and bounty hunters trying to capture the most dangerous game.

The base for a Tracking check is Average difficulty, however, a lot of different factors play into a hunt. Each condition either raises or lowers the difficulty by the noted amount. These are not all the conditions that come into play but it should give you a good base line.

On a failure, the Tracker loses the trail for a number of days equal to the amount they failed by. They must double back and can try a second check after the time elapsed to see if they’ve found the trail again. Should they fail the second time, they’ve completely lost the trail.

If your query is actively trying to hide their tracks, it becomes opposed by the query’s Tracking skill.

USE ROPE Covering all kinds of knots and bindings, the Use Rope skill is pretty self explanatory, though it should be noted that you can also use this skill to get a sense of the tensile strength of a rope or how much you’re going to need for a certain job.

To keep your players on their toes, I suggest you make the check, not when they tie the knot, but when it’s tested.

TACTIC SKILL SUITE

Combat isn’t just about raw physical power, but more about how it’s directed and used. This Skill Suite deals with logistics and making the most out of what you have.

COMBAT STYLE (style) By studying the best tactics and how to fight in certain circumstances, a warrior can take advantage of his surroundings and combat styles emerge. Combat Styles can be anything but involve to parts: a situation and a metric. This situation is when the Combat Style is most effective. For instance, it can be “in the forest”, “fighting on rooftops”, “being outnumbered”, “being in a duel”, “fighting while prone”, etc.. Really anything (as long as your GM approves). The second part of the Combat Style is the metric is applied to. This can be anything you could normally specialize in.

If the situation is applicable, the character receives a +1 to that metric for every Rank of that skill he’s obtained.

So a character with the Combat Skill (unarmed fighting, attack) at 56 who gets into a tavern brawl gets a +5 to attack as long as he doesn’t draw his weapon and simply uses fisticuffs.

DISTRACTION More than simply yelling “hey, look” at someone, the art of distraction is the art of holding people’s attention.

The amount of time that someone is delayed is based on the difficulty of the check and how bent on their current activity they are.

The time given below is the time between checks, however, each successive check gets 1 difficulty harder, with the same result. So, distracting someone for 1d3 minutes the first time is Average, but the next 1d3 minutes you’re trying to hold their attention is Challenging.

This skill is less effective when the person knows they’re in immediate danger. In this case, as long as they’re not actively engaged in melee with someone, the time is reduced to equivalent amount of seconds. So, a successful Challenging check only delays someone for 1d4 seconds instead. Each successive check gets 2 difficulties harder in these cases.

LEADERSHIP The difference between a leader and an administrator is that the latter gets stuff done and the former gets others to get stuff done but hooking into what they believe and how they view themselves. Assuming you have a willing audience, you may make an Average Leadership skill check vs a social characteristic you think the person or group might have. If you succeed the check and they have the characteristic you’re looking for (or close enough - you don’t have to be exact), you increase their Morale by a number of points equal to the maximum value of one of the dice of that characteristic. So, a 3d4 Trait will only net 4 points of Morale.

You can increase the number of characteristics you’re aiming for by increasing the difficulty of the check by a like number, but the audience only gains the bonus from the largest of the characteristics.

QUARTER MASTERING There’s an art to packing and making sure that everyone is well fed and prepared. A Quarter Master reduces the total carry weight of person who’s gear he’s helped pack by 1% for every Rank he’s achieved in this skill.

RALLY When manure hits the windmill, no speeches or grand narratives will keep a person from bolting. Only the cold will of a competent commander can keep a battle from becoming a route. This skill is rolled to rally fleeing allies and to force them to fight on or to get the enemy to break. This is always rolled opposed against the enemy’s Rally skill. More information is found in the Morale Advanced Rules of Combat (which are not in the basic edition).

RECRUITING Knowing where to find the right person is a crucial skill for any long term expedition. A character with this skill can find a random person willing to hire on with a Trivial check in a number of days equal to their roll.

Now, finding specialists or someone with a certain skill is another matter. Every additional criteria adds 1 level of difficulty to the search.

Where you’re looking also makes a difference, as well.

This doesn’t guarantee that the person found will take the job or doesn’t have some serious issues, like a crippling fear of dogs: it only guarantees that they have the skills you’re looking for and that they’re somewhat interested in being hired on. Pay and comfort make a lot of difference to a prospective hire.

RIDING This skill teaches a character how to maintain their stability while on horseback and making them capable of feats of equine prowess.

A character without this skill Attacks and Defends with a d8! on horseback but is only able to do so while the horse is relatively still. Each speed category above a stand still, lowers that dice by 1 size, down to simply not being able to Attack or Defend at all.

For each difficulty they can pass without rolling, they increase that dice size by 1, back up to their full Attack and Defense. Flanking and other modifiers may lower this again, in the normal manner.

SIGNALLING This is the knowledge of how to communicate over long distances, either using semaphore, smoke signals, signal lamps, or horn blowing.

When using this means of communication, each side rolls a check based on how complicated the message is and how quickly they need to send it. Signalling is an ad hoc language with no grammatical rules or complicated tenses and so even at the best of times, only simple messages can be sent.

WARFARE The Warfare skill hands directing troops on the battlefield, weighing options and devising plans on the fly. Those with this skill can roll it to temporarily pause combat to ask one question and receive one answer from a specific member of the party, or to simply tell them something. On top of this, you can also use this, out of combat, to confer with players who’re characters are not present in the scene.

Checks start at Trivial but increase by 1 difficulty each time they’ve been rolled that combat.

TRADES SKILL SUITE

This suite covers the crafting and creating skills. While there are a wide array and breathe of professions, this list is pared down to those areas of knowledge where the common man and the adventurer overlap in their needs.

APPRAISAL (specific) It’s wise to know what something is worth before selling and buying it. This skill handles that and is taken for a specific type of good, such as books & scrolls, livestock, jewelry, armors & armor, and so on.

If a character has the appropriate skill covering the item he’s looking to sell/purchase, he can get within her roll, expressed at a percentage, of what the item is currently worth, with the challenge based on how common the item is and how closely it relates to his skill’s focus.

Additionally, the character should get a bonus if he has any skill that’s applicable to the thing he’s purchasing.

ARTISTRY (specific) While some many not find the Fine Arts a worthy way to spend time, others may disagree. Artistry covers all artistic endeavors, including painting, sculpture, embroidery and anything else that is done for art’s sake.

COOKING/BAKING On the road, a hot meal can count for a lot. A character who has a hot meal subtracts 1 day off the amount of time it takes to heal their Stress per Stress, so, they’re able to heal 15 Stress in 120 days, instead of 105.

On top of this, a character can prep otherwise unpalatable or poisonous food in such a way as to make the most of it, though it is a Challenging check to do so, meaning they can stretch rations for longer.

Unless the character is trying to prep a feast worthy of His Imperial Majesty, basic daily cooking only requires a Trivial check.

CRAFT (specific) This skill covers any skill that makes something that isn’t noted here, such as carpentry, tailoring, basket weaving, pottery, etc.. The GM may adjust the price for a particularly difficult to master study.

LEATHERWORKING From the moment it leaves the beast to the moment the final touches are finished, leatherworking handles the process of making and crafting works from the hides of animals, including skinning and tanning.

MAINTENANCE The skill of tinkers and adventurers, this gives the person a general knowledge of easy and ad hoc fixes that can be done on the fly. The level of difficulty in repairing something is equal to the total penalty it gives, rounded up to the nearest dice size.

So repairing a spear with a -1 to Attack and Damage is Trivial (d4!), while repairing one with a -3 to Attack and Damage is Easy (d6!).

METALWORKING This covers the process of changing crude ore into a beautiful weapon, armor, or decorative piece. It also covers the construction and repair of more mundane things, like plows, but if you’re picking this skill up, I doubt you’re doing it to corner the farmer’s market.

MUSICIAN (specific) This covers the mastery of specific instrument, including singing. At the end of the day, the dulcet tones of a neatly played mandolin can ease the ache of a long day. A character who hears someone play an instrument subtracts 1 day off the amount of time it takes to heal their Stress per Stress, so, they’re able to heal 15 Stress in 10 days, instead of 15.

This doesn’t stack with others playing, however, and does no good if you’re in a stressful situation, like being hunted.

Unless the character is trying to serenade a queen, basic daily ditties only require a Trivial check.

PERFORMANCE This skill covers everything from making your case before a court or a stirring speech before charging into battle. On a success, anyone listening gains a temporary d4 Devotion to what the performer wants: whether that’s themselves or an organization or a case. If the Devotion already exists, the size is temporarily increased. Additionally, the Devotion dice size can be increased 1 for every extra level of difficulty the orator passes their check by.

PERSUASION You may think that the truth is powerful enough to carry your argument but you can be beat out by a master of deception. Persuasion is the skill of rhetoric and formulating the truth in such a way that make it the most convincing.

The level of difficulty to convince someone is how much evidence you can supply to back up your arguments. This is opposed by Deception or Persuasion but only if someone is trying to actively trying to deceive or convince the same person as you.

Even if you succeed, this isn’t a permanent way of changing a person’s mind. Others may lie more effectively than you can tell the truth and that may cause them to rethink their opinion on what you tried to sell them.
