A person who sets off on an adventure doesn’t return the same person as they were when they left. Trials and hardships not only hone their skill, but also who they are and what they stand for.

In Bonfire, this change is measured by Experience (Ex), which is counted at the end of every session.

The only way to gain Experience is to move towards your Goals or help others move towards theirs.

At the end of each session, any party member who completed their session benchmark or who’s helped another party member with their benchmark receives 25 Ex, even if the characters were unaware they were doing it.

There is a bonus of another 50 Ex for each member if everyone in the party has completed a benchmark that session.

On top of this, if a party member actually completes one of their Goals, they gain an additional 50 Ex and a bonus of 10% of your current Fame for a Short Term one, an additional 150 Ex and 25% of your current Fame for a Medium Term one, and if they complete a Long Term one, they automatically jump to the number of Ex they need to level and 50% of your current Fame with everyone else in the party gaining half the Ex.

The GM may award additional Ex for completing plot important points in the story.

You don’t receive Ex for random things, such as winning a Social Conflict, Combats, or Skill Challenges. Those are more about gaining Honor.

You gain Ex for challenging who your character is. If they gain Ex from a Combat it is not from just defeating a random foe or just being in Combat. It could be from your character facing their fears or rival, killing a person for the first time, or learning to be a leader in a stressful situation.

Those things are a means to an end, not an end in and of themselves.

LEVELING UP

Once a character has received enough Ex to level, they must take a week to think and meditate on what they’ve learned.

During this week, they reflect on their journey and how they’ve changed. Each day, they complete one of the steps of leveling up.

This self-reflection takes a large amount of the day. If the character is interrupted by any strenuous activity, they’ve lost the day and must pick up their reflection when they can from the same point.

For example, If Ironfoe is on day 3 of his reflection when his battle brothers decide to do a raid on a local village 2 days away, to be able to join in, he must break off his leveling (having gained his CrP and increased his Ability Scores but not having assessed how he sees himself).

When the raid is over, and he’s returned to his clan stronghold, 4 days later, he can pick up where he left off and rethink what he’s doing with his life.

DAY 1: RECEIVE CrP

At the start of his reflection, a character receives a number of CrP equal to his LvL CrP from his Intellect. This behaves exactly like CrP from Character Creation and can be used to buy things at any stage or reroll everything but Health.

You can spend these are different points over the level up process but you can also save them up to purchase more expensive things if you don’t currently have enough CrP for what you want.

DAY 2: INCREASE ABILITY SCORES

Your character will now receive 7 dice: d20!, d12!, d10!, d8!, d6!, and a d4!.

Roll each one in turn and add that bonus to one of your Ability Score’s percentile. You may only roll once for each Ability Score, with the exception of the d4! Which can be rolled at the end for any of the Ability Scores.

After your rolls you may spend CrP to improve any of your Scores as per Step 6 of Chapter 1: Character Creation.

DAY 3: ASSESS PERSONALITY

FLAWS A character can grow, overcoming things that used to hold her down. A character can buy off a mental or behavioral Flaw for its CrP bonus (regardless of how much you got for it). If you do so, you gain a 2d6 Trait “Overcame X” with X being the Flaw. Background Flaws that have resolved through play can also give you this Trait according to the GM's discretion.

TRAITS As a character grows, things that used to be important to them no longer are and things they never used to identify with become a part of them.

A character can switch around their Traits, switching a Strong one for a Medium one, or a Medium one for a Weak one. This swaps the number of dice - not the size of those dice. So, swapping a 1d8 trait for 3d10 trait makes one 3d8 and the other 1d10.

They may also swap out a Weak one for an entirely new one.

They may only do this swap once per level, however.

PASSIONS Passions die out and are relit. A character may increase the dice size of a Passion by decreasing the size of another.

This can drop the Passion to below a d4, allowing the character to develop a new one, however, Passions that’re a result of Skills can’t be dropped below the Situational Dice they'd give you. So a Skill with a Rank of 8 can't be lowered lower than a d8.

You may only do this trade once per level.

GOALS During this time, the character carefully turns over their aims in life and can ask themselves if this is really the path they want to travel down.

Goals may be switched out, dropped or added freely at this stage but most total 3 Short, 2 Medium, and 1 Long.

DAY 4: INCREASE CLASS ABILITIES

Some Class Abilities grew stronger as you level. Check your selected Archetypes to see if they have any of their Abilities that either grow stronger (i.e. the Thug’s Precision Strike), gain additional free Skills (i.e. any of the Expert Archetype’s Skill Focuses), or gain something else for free (i.e. the Magical Archetypes gaining new spells).

DAY 5: TRAIN AND RETRAIN SKILLS & MASTERIES

Now, it’s time to hone in on what you’ve learned and any new skills you’ve acquired or hidden talents you’ve discovered during your travels.

At this stage you may spend your CrP to purchase and improve your Skills and Masteries.

A character may also replace old knowledge with new. He may refund one of his Masteries or Skill purchase, gaining the CrP he spend on them back to spend on something else.

He may only do this once per level up and he may not refund free Masteries or Skills earned from his Archetypes.

For Skill, remove the average number of points of the dice you would roll if you were increasing the Skill + 1. So, for example, you have 28 points in Mathematics and wanted to gain back 7 CrP, you’d subtract 6 points, leaving you with 22 in the Skill.

DAY 6: INCREASE HEALTH

Finally, it’s time to reflect on the fact that your travels have made you just a little bit tougher, a little bit better at facing what life throws at you.

At every even level, the character gets a free reroll of their Health Die from their last level, taking the higher of the two rolls, however, if both rolls are under their Minimum Health from their Constitution, they receive that value at least.

At every odd level, they gain a free roll that’s added to their total Health. This is not affected by their Con’s Min Health and is just a straight roll. You’ll need to record it on your character sheet.

If your Class has a “choose the higher of 2” for their Health die, your Health Dice drops to one of those dice. So, “the higher of 2d6” becomes a single d6 for subsequent levels.

Remember to recalculate your Wound Categories

DAY 7: RECEIVE HONOR

Now the other players will silently judge the character, rating him 1 to 10 in 3 categories: Flaws, Teamwork, Respect.

FLAWS How well has this player played their Flaws? Do they only seem to be brought up when it’s convenient but then forgotten they need to get away with something?

Does she use her Flaws to further the story and character development or does she use them to annoy others?

A character that leans more to the former, rather than the latter, deserves a higher score.

TEAMWORK Does this player work well with others? Do they pull their load and put themselves on the line or do they pick fights that derail the session? Are they on top on things and help set up or are they late and spend all their time on the phone?

Again, a character that leans more to the former, rather than the latter, deserves a higher score.

RESPECT How do you think this character is viewed by those he interacts with, by both the party’s character and NPCs? Has he kept his word (or at least, not gotten caught) or does he have a habit of backstabbing other?

The GM should then take the scores and average them across each category, rounding down. Those three averages are then totaled and compared to the following table to determine whether they gain or lose an Honor category at the completion of their level.

For example, Claire receives scores of 6, 4, and 5 for her Flaws, 3, 7, 7, for her Teamwork, and 9, 7, 9 for Respect, giving her an average of 5 for Flaws, 5 for Teamwork, and 8 for Respect.

Totaling those averages, she nets a total scores of 18. At the end of leveling up, she moves up 1 Honor category

The individual scores and who gave them should be kept a secret, though the player can know how much they scored in each category to help them improve.
