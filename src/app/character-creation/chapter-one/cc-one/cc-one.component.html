Welcome to Bonfire Character Creation! You'll notice that there's a lot of steps and, if you're familiar with other TTRPGs, that it's a bit longer and a bit more complicated than other games. Why is that?

The first is that it gives you, the player, more choice. Do you want to build a cunning fighter than actually using their brains instead of their brawns to defeat others? Do you want to play a sly diplomat that crushes her opponents' morale without even lifting a finger? Or perhaps you want to play a power wizard that concocts his spells on the fly, building the perfect one for each scenario?

More importantly, do you want those different characters to actually feel different when you play them? So that convincing some actually feels different than just beating them to death?

This is the first thing that Bonfire offers you.

The second thing is that it allows you to be surprised during creation: through your choices and rolls, you'll get a more fully formed character than in other systems. You'll get what you want with extra twists that help add interest and depth to your character that you wouldn't get in other systems.

And not only that, but you'll also have those choices, twists, and depth mean something in play. Is your character brash? You'll be rewarded for playing that way in the game.

So, what are you waiting for? Let's get started!

Step 1: Read How to Make a Good Character

Making a good character isn't just about mechanical know-how; it's also about the soft skills that often go under appreciated. As such, before you begin, go read what's found in Chapter 2.1: How to Make a Good Character. I thought it was so important, I put it in the front so that you would have to passby every time you open the book, so, even if you've read it before, I encourage you to read it again.

Step 2: Roll Stats

Your character has 6 attributes that define your character physically and mentally. These are Strength (Str), Dexterity (Dex), Constitution (Con), Intellect (Int), Willpower (Will), and Pre (Presence).

Roll 3d6 for each of these Abilities to generate that score.

This should result in something similar to this:

Str

13

Dex

7

Con

10

Int

14

11

6

Strength

Strength (Str) represents your character’s pure brawn. How much can she lift and carry? How strong are her punches?

It governs the base damage you do, how much you can carry, and strength-based skills.

Dexterity

Dexterity (Dex) is a measure of how agile your character is. Can she dodge arrows, or can she execute a graceful pirouette? Dexterity is crucial for avoiding blows in combat or traps in a hallway.

It covers how accurate you are, how well you can dodge, how quickly you get up in moving in situations, and dexterity-based skills.

Constitution

Constitution (Con) represents your character’s overall health and toughness. Can he shrug off the Dancing Death or does he succumb to a head cold? Can she take a solid blow in combat and not even blink? These are the things Constitution oversees.

It controls your base Vitality, the amount of minimum Vitality at every other level up, and how much damage you can suck down without going comatose, and constitution-based skills.

Intellect

Intellect (Int) measures your character’s raw knowledge: how much data can he remember? Can she do mathematical calculations or remember who the Elector of Stanitz is? Intellect doesn’t help in applying that knowledge, but it does give you knowledge to draw on.

It governs how accurate you are and intellect-based skills (which are a lot of them).

Willpower

Willpower (Will) is applied knowledge. Does she process information quicker and learn from other’s mistakes? Does he understand life is more than just a set of numbers of probabilities? Can she see other’s viewpoints? How well can she overcome adversity or keep her ground?

It controls your starting Nerve, how likely you are to get hit, how quickly you get up and moving in situations, and willpower-based skills.

Presence

Presence (Pre) represents force of personality. Does he bend others to his will? Is she the focus of any room she enters? Do people look up to them or what to be him? Presence is not looks or even likeability: it represents this animal magnetism that people follow.

Presence covers how much bonus Favor you get when worshipping the Divines, your starting Integrity, how many contacts you start the game with, how many followers you can have, and presence-based skills.

A Note About Low Stats

Bonfire is specifically balanced for you to roll 3d6 in order, with 10 being the average result but don't worry about feeling like you have low scores or they're not in the order you want: you'll have a chance to rearrange and modify them later.

But even if you have objectively lower stats than the rest of the company, you'll still be able to contribute in a meaningful way in your own area of expertise because Bonfire is built as a team game, so nobody is going to be good at everything.

In Bonfire, you'll almost always be weaker than your opponent: what makes a hero is not supernatural abilities, but the simple fact that he fights longer and harder for what he believes in than other men.

Helpful Tips for New Players

Bonfire is a little different than most RPGs so it's important to keep things in mind:

1) You will never be good at everything: you'll have to rely on teammates.

2) Utilizing different skill sets to help each other is the best strategy to overcome any obstacle. If something seems hard or tedious, it’s because you need to adapt and use a new strategy to overcome it.

Each additional skill set you can incorporate into any challenge increases your chance of surviving exponentially.

3) Don't try to read and understand every rule. Yes, read through the rules generally, but start with a specific character concept and focus on how your character interacts with the world.

Think outside the box: Bonfire is built to reward that kind of thinking.

4) You are going to die. Don't get married to your first few characters.

The pace of the game is intended to be slower and more thoughtful: the more you slowdown and think, the more likely you are to succeed (whatever that means to you) but remember to keep moving forward.

5) Monster body parts are worth a lot.

Step 4: Swap Stats

Alright, you have your scores and your Integrity, now is time to switch your stats around (if you want). This may be because you have almost the right stats, but you have a specific character concept in mind. For instance, using the example stats in Step 1, your party may need a diplomatic character more than a strong character. In this case, it would really help to swap Strength and Presence.

You can freely switch around your stats as need but, in that case, you lose all your Integrity.

If you switch only 4 of your stats, you lose half your Integrity.

If you switch 2, you lose a quarter of your Integrity.

And if you keep your stats the way they are, with no changes, you keep all your Integrity

Example

If I wanted to, I could swap Pre for Wis and to more of a Servant or swap Will for Dex and lean into the backline fighter - all of which would mean I would lose 5 Integrity (18 * .25, rounded against the player) but I’m actually happy with the idea of going Expert so I’m going to keep them where they’re at and my full 18 Integrity.

Step 6: Select Class & Subclass

Your character has an area of expertise represented by two Classes; a Main and a sub Class.

Descriptions of the different Classes are found in Chapter 2.4: Classes along with more details on how to combine them.

Once you pick your Classes, you pay the price for each in Integrity based on your Ancestry’s Class Cost section.

If you’ve chosen to swap around all your stats and have no Integrity to work with, you need to select Classes that cost you nothing.

What Do You Take From Each

You combine both the free Advanced Skills (listed under the Main Skill Suite) and the Class Abilities sections, though you completely remove 1 ability marked with * from your Sub Class, unless you Double your Class.

If two characters have the same ability, unless it says otherwise, those abilities stack.

Doubling Your Class All Classes, except for the Servant, can be selected as both your Main and Sub Class - called “Doubling".

If you Double, you don't receive your abilities twice; instead you receive each ability once but most will have their power increased in some way. This is noted in the "Double X" sidebar at the end of the Class.

Example

I already talked about going Expert/Expert as a general game plan.

For my Main Expert, in terms of Skill Suites, I’m going to go with Athletics, Weirdcraft, and Streetwise. While I have poor physical stats, choosing Athletics here seems like a good choice because it gives me a lot of movement Skills, like Endurance and Move Silently, which are nice for keeping me out of combat.

It also means that, as I level, it’ll also balance out where I’m bad at.

I picked Weirdcraft because I like the idea of an elven cat burglar who uses Weird to fortify their abilities and picking it allows my character to use cantrips.

And I picked up Streetwise because it'll give me a lot of the traditional thief skill set.

That puts me at 12 Integrity (-3 for Main Expert and Sub Expert ).

Step 7: Roll Burdens & Injuries

All heroes have things from their past that haunt them. Sometimes it’s emotional and sometimes it’s physical. That’s what makes them relatable and human (even if they’re an orc). Character Burdens and Injuries can also give you the opportunity to heal Stress during the game.

For more info, see Chapter 2.5: Burdens & Injuries.

Example

Alright, onto my favorite step: rolling Burdens & Injuries.

For the first one I roll a 1 (Burden) and then a 7 which gives me Allergy, Misc for a base 5. That’s pretty good so I’m going to keep it and I’m actually going to keep it at Rank 1.

For my next one, I get 3 (Injury) and then a 80, which is Myopia. It has a base 6 but since it’s my second Flaw, it would give me a base 3.

But I kind of think that my character is going to be dealing with a lot of stuff at close range so I’m actually going to pump it to Rank 2 which gives me 5 extra CrP for a total of 8 CrP for this Flaw.

So, for all Burdens & Injuries, I get a total of 13 Integrity, which brings my total Integrity to exactly 25 - which is lucky because anymore and it would have been wasted.

Step 8: Calculate Character Vitals

Now it’s time to flesh out character’s vitals; their Vitality, Integrity, Fatigue Threshold, and level of induction into whatever faith they choose.

Starting Nerve

Your starting Threshold is your Ancestry's Nerve Modifier + your Will score + a roll of your main and sub Class Category's Nerve Dice.

Divide that by 4: that’s the amount of the Stress in each Category.

For fractions on Stress Categories, round up on Unsure but down on the rest.

So, if you’re a Human Double Socalite (2d4) and you roll a 2 with a Will of 8, you have a 25 Nerve. Your Unsure is 1 - 6, Tense between 7 - 12, Shaken between 13 - 18, and Breaking between 19 - 25.

Record your Nerve on your character sheet.

Example

Being an elf gives me a 20 Nerve Modifier and being a Expert/Expert gives me a +2d6, with which I rolled a 3 and a 4, giving me a total of 40 for my Nerve ( 20 + 3 + 4 + 13 (Will) ).

Not too shabby.

The way to quickly calculate Stress Categories is to divide in half and then divide in half again. Unsure is 1 to halved half, Tense is halved half + 1 to half, Shaken is half + 1 to half + half halved, Breaking is half + half halved + 1 to your full Nerve.

Confusing enough?

So, for a 40 Nerve, half is 20 and half of 20 is 10.

Unsure is 1 to 10.

Tense is 11 (10 + 1) to 20.

Shaken is 21 (20 + 1) to 30 (20 + 10).

Breaking is 31 (30 + 1) to 40.

Starting Vitality

To calculate your base Vitality, add a roll of your Main and Sub Class’s Vitality Die to your Constitution + their Vitality Size Modifier.

Divide that by 4: that’s the amount of the Vitality in each Category.

Your Trauma Threshold is a quarter of that (rounded up). Trauma is talked about more in Chapter 1.3: Combat.

For fractions on Wound Categories, round up on Hurt but down on the rest.

So, if you’re a Human with and your Vitality Dice is a d6 and a d4, if you roll a 3 and 2, with a Con of 12, you have 32 Vitality. Your Hurt is 1 - 8, Bloodied between 9 - 15, Wounded between 16 - 24, and Critical between 25 - 32.

Record your Vitality Die on your character sheet.

Example

This is going to be rough.

Being an elf gives me a 10 Vitality Modifier.

Since I'm a Expert/Expert, my Vitality Dice are a d6 and a d6, and I roll a 1 on both.

That gives me a total of 15 for my Vitality ( 10 + 1 + 1 + 3 (Con) ). This means that, if the person is using a large weapon, I could easily be killed in one shot so I need to stay out of combat, especially melee combat.

The way to quickly calculate Wound Categories is the same as calulating Stress Categories but swapping the names.

So, for 15 Vitality, half is 7.5 and half of 7.5 is 3.75.

Hurt is 1 to 4 (because we round up).

Bloodied is 5 (4 + 1) to 7.

Wounded is 8 (7 + 1) to 10 (7 + 3 (rounded down this time)).

Critical is 11 (10 + 1) to 15.

Starting Fatigue Threshold

Your Fatigue Threshold starts at Wounded, however, it can be lowered by wearing armor and wielding shields you're not fully trained in.

Example

This is easy: we start at Wounded.

Levels of Induction & Max Favor

There are 3 levels of commitment to a cult, that determines how often you get Favor and how much Favor you can have:

Unanointed

These are the lowest level: those outside the cult and its mysteries. They gain no benefits of any kind.

Anointed

These are those that are the lay members of the faith. They gain a bonus to their max Favor for the deity they’re devoted to. You can only be anointed to one Covenant at a time.

If you have a positive Relationship to a deity, one of their cults, or one of their Covenants, you can choose to begin the game as an anointed to that deity. Otherwise, you can become Anointed during gameplay once you have that Relationship.

You can only be anointed to one deity at a time.

Servants

These are the highest rung of the commitment ladder and serve as the officers of the cult of the god. They’re priests, abbots, hierophants, pontiffs, nazarites, hermits, etc. These are men and women that have taken vows to devote their life to the cult.

In game terms, this means that these are those who have a Servant Class as either their Main or sub Class.

Max Favor

A person can only have so much Favor, with the Divines giving a little extra leeway for those devoted to them.

Characters have a Max Favor based on their Burdens and Injuries (if they've taken any and if they chose that bonus), while Anointed members gain a on top of that +2 Max Favor. Servants also get that +2 but also have abilities that will further increase their Threshold.

Starting Favor

Regardless of what level of induction a person chooses, they start with their Favor at max.

Example

Because I’m pretty squishy, having a small bank of Favor wouldn’t be a terrible thing to have in my back pocket but it does cost me a Relationship that fleshes out my relationship to my faith.

I think it’s worth it so I start as Anointed, which gives me a Max Favor of 3 and 3 Favor to start with.

I’ll also need to consider how my faith reflects my character but that can wait.

Out of Order Character Creation

If you’re a little bit more familiar with Bonfire Character creation, the 3 next steps (step 9, 10, and 11) can be taken in any order you’d like, even skipping back and forth between them.

Step 9: Skills

Skills are additional areas of expertise that your character has obtained above his or her Ancestry and Class.

Skills are explained in Chapter 2.6: Skills but, before you rush over there, you need to know that you cannot buy Skill Ranks at this point and all you need to do right now is to determine the starting Ranks for your Skill Suites.

Keep in mind, you don't add any of your free purchases from your Class at this step.

Calculating Starting Ranks

There are 7 non-Combat Skill Suites:

  • Athletics
  • Lore
  • Streetwise
  • Survival
  • Strategy
  • Trades
  • Weirdcraft

There are 5 Combat Skill Suites:

  • Armor
  • Melee Weapons
  • Ranged Weapons
  • Shields
  • Unarmed

To determine your starting Rank in each, follow the guide below. Note that, if you get Ranks in a Skill Suite here, you automatically gain Training in that Suite.

  • Your Main Class's Main Skill Suite starts at Rank 4.
    • If you get two (noted with an "and", not "or"), one starts at Rank 4, the other starts at Rank 3, otherwise pick one to start at 4.
  • Your sub's Main Skill Suite starts at Rank 3.
    • If you would normally get two, one starts at Rank 3, the other starts at Rank 2
    • If you're a Double Expert, your 3rd and 4th Skill Suites start at 1.
  • If, for any reason, a Skill Suite doubles up, you only get a +1 to its Rank.
    • This is true each time this happens (so tripling up gives you an additional +1; quadrupling up gives another +1, etc.)
  • Select 2 Skill Suites: the first starts at Rank 2, the other at Rank 1.
  • Finally, select 3 Skill Suites: these start at Rank 0.
    • If you're a Fighter, your chosen Skill Suites should match to Combat Skill Suites you’ve already picked or you need to select them now.
      • Remember that 2 of these have to be different Skill Suites.
    • You can, instead, choose Skill Suites you already have Ranks in, but it only increases its Rank by 1 and cannot bring a Skill Suite above Rank 4.
    • All other Skill Suites start as Untrained (marked with a U).

Note that you get the Advanced Language Skill for free in your native language with its Rank starting at your Int.

Alright, onto purchasing Skills but first, let’s get some Skill Suite Ranks.

Remember that I’m an Expert/Expert who chose Athletics, Weirdcraft, and Streetwise for their Main Skill Suites.

I also get a fourth Skill Suite to start at Rank 1, which I haven't picked yet. I think I'm going to pick Lore because knowing is half the battle.

I’m actually kind of thinking that my character was kind of a jock growing up and was very physical until they got sick and their stats plummeted; so I have the know-how, I just lost the natural talent for it.

Finally, I need to pick 3 Skill Suites to start at Rank 0 with the rest being Untrained, which means I’ll get a penalty when using them.

I’m going to pick Melee and Ranged Weapons so I might be able to participate as a flanker and then boost my Weirdcraft by 1 Rank.

Step 10: Flesh Out Personality

Your character also has a personality (shocker) that sets them apart.

What do they want? What do they believe? Who do they like? What do people think of them?

These questions are answered using Goals, Convictions, Relationships, Descriptions, Flaws, and Reputation, which we’ll fill out now.

The “Characteristics”, as they’re collectively called, are also given a Rank that reflects their strength (Goals and Flaws being the exceptions).

Goals

Goals serve two purposes.

First, they help your GM figure out what you want to do with the character: where you want to take them and what they’re trying to accomplish. At this stage, if you haven't already, you should bring your GM to talk about the shape of the campaign and what Goals will work the best.

Second, they help you get CrPs that you can then turn around and use to make your character stronger.

A character can have 3 Goals at any one time, although it’s fine if you only start with 1.

You should discuss with your GM what Goals are going to work for the campaign to get on the same page. The GM has the final say on what Goals are appropriate to take.

Have a Goal that you can continue to work for but also have a Goal(s) that are actionable as you get bonus CrP for completing them but you only get this bonus once per level so you should aim to have a goal to be completed before the end of first level, a goal to be completed by the end of second, and a goal to be completed by the end of third.

Example

This would be the time to talk to the GM about what time of game they’re running so the character’s Goal can align with that idea and the other players’.

For this game, it’s going to be set in the fantasy equivalent of the Italian city-states with the law just being another one of the plethora of gangs roaming the streets and the players are going to be starting their own crew with the hope of rising up through the ranks.

So, for that, I think that my character wants to found a merchant house: basically go legitimate. Or as legit as you can go in this setting.

Technically, I get 3 but I only need 1 so I’m going to wait for the game to get rolling before filling out the other 2.

Ancestral Characteristics

Once you’ve figured out the number and Rank of your Characteristics, you’re going to start filling them out, starting with your culture & Ancestry. These are found in the "Characteristic" area in the Ancestry's entry in Chapter 2.3: Ancestries but ask your GM if they have a list of Characteristics that better reflects the Ancestries (and cultures) of their world. If you're using the official campaign setting, you'll find that information under Nations in the World Codex, found here.

Now, I said “based on” because you don’t have to use this exact wording - you can shift the wording around to give it shades of new meaning that represent your character’s particular views on things. You can always change a Characteristic from one type to another. But, more importantly, you can choose to reject a common cultural Characteristics but, if you do, you must have another Characteristic that explains why.

So, if you’re an elf, you can change “Driven to create” Relationship to “Wants to create a Masterwork Painting” - thematically the same but with the second being more active.

Or you can change the “Beautiful” Description to the “Beautify is the highest good” Conviction since that leads to your elf focusing on beauty in their behavior.

Or, finally, you can take the “Ugly” Description and pair that with a Conviction along the lines of “Beauty is only skin deep” or “Looks aren’t everything” - a specific rejection of elven cultural norms.

Convictions

Convictions are beliefs, worldviews, and areas of confidence that your character has. They emotionally ground your character.

They can be simple “People are good” or complex “Nobody wants to be bad at their job”. They can also be inward facing “I’m brave”.

Convictions don’t reflect objective reality; only reality as that character sees it.

So a character with 3 Strength and the “I’m Strong” Conviction isn’t strong; he’s just very confident he is.

You can also have conflicting Convictions because people are complicated but individual Convictions can’t have conjunctions of any kind in them.

The starting number of Convictions your character has is based on their Int. Your highest Conviction starts at a Rank determined by your Wisdom, with each successive Conviction being 1 lower, down to a minimum of 1.

Conflicting Characteristics

Because human beings are wonderful, chaotic being, they're capable of believing two entirely opposing things at the same time and this is represented in Bonfire by having conflicting Characteristics.

I would actually recommend you start with a conflicting Conviction (or Relationship). It adds a bit of depth and interest to your character, points a struggle that you would be interested in roleplaying out to your GM so that they can weave that into the adventure or campaign, and gives you a place to grow. Let yourself be surprised by who your character becomes.

Example

This character gets 4 Convictions due to their 13 Int and the first Rank starts at 3.

So, my Convictions look like this:

Conviction 1: Rank 3

Conviction 2: Rank 2 (3 - 1)

Conviction 3: Rank 1 (3 - 2, min 0)

Conviction 4: Rank 0 (3 - 3, min 0)

I want to figure out what Conviction 1 and 2 are before the game begins because it gives a base from which to build off my character but leaving 3 and 4 open gives me the space to fill those in once I know what kind of character will work with the style and party.

I think the first one is going to be “Riches are the ultimate security” because I want to explain why he has his Goal and why he would join a gang.

The second one I’m going to randomly generate using the Conviction Generator. I got “The Fates are cruel - Don’t give them extra chances to torture you”. Interesting; I like it.

Relationships

What relationships does he have? What organizations does your character associate with? Is he religious and, if so, is his loyalty more to his cult or to his god? How does he feel about that thing?

Relationships between individuals don’t need to be reciprocal or even contemporary. A young fighter may be inspired by a girl who doesn't love him back or by a long dead king he greatly admires. These are still viable candidates for his Relationship.

Everyone receives two free Relationships: one to their character’s clan/family and another of their choice. Each Relationship comes with a "relationship verb" and the thing itself. So, his clan Relationship could be "Hate Clan" or "Have mixed feelings about Clan" or "Loyal to Clan"

One Relationship starts at Rank 6 and the other starts at 2.

Example

So, I get two Relationships one of which has to do with my character's faith since they’re part of a cult so let’s take care of that first.

I can talk to my GM about what faiths are available in the campaign world and, if one doesn’t fit, we can discuss other options.

Most games have a Divine of Thieves so that seems like a good choice but now I need a relationship verb.

I actually think that it would be interesting to do ‘admires’ and have the Divine being a crippled god that uses their wits to outsmart others and get what they want; I think that would really appeal to my character and give a good reason for him to want to join the cult.

So the full Relationship is “Admires the Divine of Thievery”.

For the second one, it relates to his clan. Talking to the GM, this means the character’s crew so this outlines how I feel about the gang, although I could single out a single player or NPC that’s in that crew.

I’m going to go with “is using” as the verb; “Is Using the Crew”.

He sees the crew as a means to his Goal, not as a goal themselves. That should keep him loyal and create some opportunities for the GM to test that with perhaps the character slowly growing to care for his gang or growing apart and betraying them.

Descriptions

Descriptions are physical things about your character, whether it’s something specific (“I have blonde hair”, “Tall”) or something that more relates to your demeanor (“Grumpy”, “Charming”): these are things that relate to what people notice about you when they first meet you.

Descriptions don’t always reflect objective reality; only reality as other characters see it. The rule of thumb is that, if it's covered by something else (a stat or skill, for example), it is how others perceive you, otherwise treat it as objective.

So, a character with 3 Strength and the “Strong” Description isn’t strong; he’s just perceived as though he is. A character with the "Grump" and "Tall" Description, however, is actually grumpy and tall (for whatever Ancestry they are) and people can spot that almost right away.

You start with 3 Descriptions starting at Ranks 4, 2, and 2, respectively.

The highest Rank Description will be what people notice about you first: does your character seem agile? Are they aggressive in their movements? Are they exceptionally clean?

Example

What are the first three things a person would notice about my character? I imagine him as sickly, tall, and determined. Those are my Descriptions. Done.

Flaws

Flaws are personality quirks that your character has: vices that they just can’t seem to break. These are mental or personality weaknesses that are hard to overcome and that they struggle with. A lot of times, these are coping mechanisms a character uses during times of stress. In fact, these will serve as ways to relieve Stress but, be warned, Flaws are not entirely under a character’s control and may lead to problems.

Each character starts with 2 Flaws. Unlike other Characteristics, Flaws don’t have Ranks: they just are. You’re free to define your own Flaws but consider what underlying things they might be saying about your character’s personality.

Roleplaying Out Flaws

Flaws never give you permission to cross lines or make people uncomfortable, whatever side of the screen you're on.

On the flip side, don't take Flaws that you would be, on some level, uncomfortable roleplaying out. If you have any doubt, discuss with your GM and other players before the game starts and, if you're still unsure, don't pick that Flaw.

Example

What coping mechanisms does my character have? The first one that comes to mind is that he's a bully because he used to be a jock and that seems right, although there is now an added dimension that his bullying is a response to him feeling powerless.

The second one: I'm not sure so I'm just going to roll until one feels right. I ended up with "Impatient". They feels like it will work for now, which is fine because, until 3rd level, I can rework my character however I want so a more fitting Flaw might come up during play.

Reputation

Finally, Reputation, which acts like a Description but is controlled by your GM.

Reputation, in a nutshell, is what your character is known for and how other people see them. So, if you want to convince someone that you’ll keep your word, having a Reputation for doing so will help you out.

Reputation takes the form of 3 incomplete sentences starting with “I’m known for...”. Each time you finish a period of training, your GM will finish or change out one or more of those sentences and assign points to them that represent their strength. She’ll choose how to finish them based on how your character has been acting or the results of their actions.

That’s right: just like in real life, you don’t really have control over your Reputation, though there is a way to influence it as you go.

All your Reputations start at 0.

Step 11: Buy Equipment

A character starts with one of the following kits. After doing so, he rolls the kit's Extra Cash dice to determine any extra starting money to buy additional gear. When buying new items, use the Source price.

The individual sizes are notes next to the item. Note that anything with * at the end denotes that the item is worn and so the size has been adjusted accordingly.

You're also given the sellback price (in sc) in case you don't want the item. For weapons, you'll notice that you're giving a generic size of weapon you can select from - the sellback price is unaffected by which weapon you would have picked up.

Free Heirloom

After you pick your kit, you're going to select one of the pieces of equipment to be an heirloom - a special piece of equipment that's a little nicer than the rest of your gear for whatever reason. This item starts with a +2 Description of your choice. See the Superior Equipment section in Chapter 1.6: Equipment for more details.

Integrity for Cash

You may also cash Integrity for sc at 1 Integrity for 25 sc, but you don’t have to. You can only do this during character creation, however.

After all the buying and selling don’t forget to adjust your Carry.
