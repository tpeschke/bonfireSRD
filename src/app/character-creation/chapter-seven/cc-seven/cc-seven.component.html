End Of Session Rewards

A person who sets off on an adventure doesn’t return the same person as they were when they left. Trials and hardships not only hone their skill, but also who they are and what they stand for.

In Bonfire, this change is measured by CrP and Integrity, which are counted and rewarded at the end of every session.

CrP

The only way to gain CrP after character creation is to move towards your Goals or help others move towards theirs.

You don’t receive CrP for random things, such as winning a Confrontation, Combats, or Skill Challenges. Those are more about gaining Integrity.

You gain CrP for challenging who your character is. If they gain CrP from a Combat, it is not from just defeating a random foe or just being in Combat. It could be from your character facing their fears or rival, killing a person for the first time, or learning to be a leader in a stressful situation.

Those things are a means to an end, not an end in and of themselves.

At the end of each session, if you've moved towards a Goal, whether your own or another player's, whether your character knew about it or not, and whether you did it "off-screen" as player or in-character or not, you gain 5 CrP. If you moved towards additional Goals, you get an extra 2 CrP for each one you helped with.

However, they only get the bonus CrP for helping another person if their Goals aren't identical to yours.

So, let's say that you moved towards one of your Goals and helped another person with theirs. You would get 7 CrP (5 + 2).

Or let's say that there wasn't enough time to do anything on your Goal, but you managed to help two other players; you'd still get 7 CrP at the end of the session (5 for the first, 2 for the second).

Or, or, if you were a real wunderkind, and moved towards your Goal AND helped the two other players, you'd get 9 CrP (5 + 2 + 2).

There is a bonus of another 5 CrP for each member if everyone in the party has moved towards their Goals that session.

Completing Goals

On top of this, if a party member actually completes one of their Goals, they (and anyone else who helps them) gains an additional 10% of their current total (spent and unspent CrP) as well as 5 Integrity (though this only applies to the person who completed it). If multiple Goals were completed, you get an additional 5% bonus but no additional Integrity.

Once a Goal is completed, it can't be taken again, with GMs having the final say on what a Goal can be.

The GM may award additional CrP for completing plot important points in the story.

Follower Experience

Followers don't gain CrP like normal characters: instead, getting half the CrP of their boss. They get no other CrP unless they're Proteges.

In this case, they’re treated like a normal PC in terms of gaining CrP with the expectation that the player only needs to move towards his or one of his protege’s Goals to count towards whether or not everyone has moved towards their Goals.

Additionally, a character can give up to 50% of their earned CrP from a session to their protege in forms of advice, training, and general guidance.

They do come at a cost and with a requirement. The requirement is that the candidate for protege must have a Devotion to the character. The cost is that they count as 2 followers towards your limit.

Once a follower has been declared a protege, they cannot go back to being a regular follower: they must either be killed, dismissed, or promoted to a full character.

Integrity

At the end of each session, the players will elect each other’s characters for things they think that character deserved Integrity for. It is generally poor form to elect yourself but is also reasonable if you pulled off something amazing and the rest of the group didn't seem to notice.

These elections need to fit into one of these three categories:

Strength

This character demonstrated strength based on how their character’s culture defines strength, which could be physical prowess but could also be cunning or grifting.

Mastery

This is given when a character solves a tricky problem or came up with a creative solution. Note, this shouldn't be given for good rolls unless the players want to lose Integrity for bad rolls; this is about out of the box thinking.

Courage

When the chips were down, did your character give up the fight or did they really show what it means to be a hero and stare down death?

Roleplaying

Did the player really role play his character? Where his decisions about what he thought gave him the best chance to win or was it about what his character thought would be a good idea? Note, that since players are electing each other, jackasses who use “that’s just what my character would do” to dick over the party won’t get rewarded for this much. This should be specifically linked to a character's Confrontation Characteristics.

For each of these categories, the GM can also dock Integrity from a character. Did they conveniently forget any Characteristics that would have screwed them over?

Gaining Grit Dice

Whenever you go up a Integrity Category, you gain a free use of your Grit Dice. Also, when you reach 25 Integrity, you get one as well, even though you didn't technically go up a category. Once used, they go away but they stay with your character as long as they live.

Certain Archetype abilities may also give you them temporarily, in which case, the Archetype will note for how long you have them.

Post Mortem Rewards

GMs can award the CrP and Integrity that would have gone to a dead character that session (including bonuses for fullfilling their Goals based on the dead character's Ex) to the player's new character.

Spending CrP

During the game, your character can spend CrP to buy anything with Ranks (Skills, Convictions, Devotions, etc.), Stat increases (specifics noted below), buy off Flaws, or specializing in an Archetype.

If the amount of spent CrP goes over 50 * your current level, you automatically, and immediately, level.

Use the steps in the Leveling Up section below.

Increasing Stats Through CrP

As stated, you can also spend CrP you increase your stats, similar to how you did during character creation, although, this time, you can spend Integrity to modify the roll. The price to increase a Stat is still 5 CrP. Each purchase gives you a roll of a d20!: if the result is higher than the stat you want to increase, you increase that stat by 1. You must select the stat you want to increase before making the roll and this, also, can't take a stat above 23.

Spent CrP Needed To Level

Any leftover character-creation CrPs won’t count towards this threshold.

So, you’ll need to track your spent and unspent CrPs and when your next level is at.

Archetype & Total Level If you purchase your Specialist Archetype after first level or independently of one another, your total level is going to be different than your individual Archetype level.

Unless otherwise stated as your "total level", "level" always refers to that Archetype's level.

Your total level is the levels you gained in your Generalist Archetype plus the levels you gained in your first Specialist Archetype - 1.

For example, if, as a 3rd level double-downed Socialite, you decide to make your Primary Archetype the Advocate Specialist Archetype, you would then be an Advocate 1/Socialite with a total level of 3. If, 5 levels later, you purchased Diplomat for your Secondary slot, you would then be an Advocate 6/Diplomat 1 with a total level of 8.

Specializing Mid-level

If you choose to specialize your Archetype(s), your Archetype(s) doesn't get replaced until you level up.

Buying Off Flaws

You can buy off certain Flaws by spending the amount of CrPs they gave you but you also gain another bonus: you gain a Description “Overcame X” with X being the name of the Flaw and its Rank equal to the Severity you’ve currently bought off.

If your Flaw came with a bonus, you don’t lose that bonus. Additionally, if the Flaw came with a one-time penalty, that penalty isn’t taken away.

So, if you have the Bastard Flaw at Severity 2. That gave you a total of 40 CrP (25 + 15) at character creation.

But, over the course of an esteemed career, your character has gained enough of a reputation to overcome their birth circumstances and so can buy down that Flaw.

To buy down the first Severity, it costs 15 CrP and to buy down second (and final), it costs 25.

But doing so gives the character the “Overcame their Bastard Birth” at Rank 2 and allows them to max their Integrity out again. It doesn’t however give them back the items they had to remove initially.

You can only buy off Flaws that are mental or social in nature: you cannot buy off physical Flaws so no regrowing your hand.

Increasing Severity

At this point, with the permission of your GM or at their dictate, your character can gain Flaws or increase the Severity of Flaws you already have.

Doing so gives you the normal CrP as if you rolled or increased them during character creation.

Training Time

Spending CrP requires 1 day per 10 CrP being spent (rounded up).

This self-reflection and training takes a large amount of the day. If the character is interrupted by any strenuous activity (gaining Stress and/or damage), they’ve lost the day and must start over.

They can also heal and gain Relaxation while doing this training as normal.

Skipping Training Time

You might be in a position where you’re unable to spend the required time to train.

If that’s the case, you can skip the time requirement by forfeiting 5 unspent CrP for each day of training you want to skip.

CrP spent this way is completely lost and doesn’t go towards your next level.

Healing & Relaxing

You heal and relax as normal during your training time.

Leveling Up

Step 1: Increase Stats

At 3rd total level and every 5 total levels after that (8, 13, 18, etc.), you may increase 1 of your Stats by 1

And at 5th total level and every 5 total levels after that (10, 15, 20, etc.), you increase a random Stat by 1. This is rolled on a d6, with 1 being Strength and 6 being Presence. You can't influence this with your Integrity.

These can’t bring a stat above 23. If it does, you don't receive the bonus that level.

Step 2: Assess Personality

Now it’s time to consider how your character has grown: who they’ve become, who they're becoming, and how their travels have changed them.

This involves rearranging Ranks and purchasing increases.

Goals

During this time, the character carefully turns over their aims in life and can ask themselves if this is really the path they want to travel down.

Goals may be switched out, dropped or added freely at this stage but can't total more than 3.

Convictions

As a character grows, things that used to be important to them no longer are and things they never used to identify with become a part of them.

A character can redistribute a number of temporary “Conviction Points” equal to their Int.

These Conviction Points go away at the end of this day of training: any leftovers are lost.

They may also swap out a Conviction at Rank 0 for an entirely new one.

After your 3rd total level, they may only do this swap once per level, however, before then, you may swap your Conviction around freely: after all, you may still be getting on your feet in terms of the type of character that would best fit in the group and campaign.

You can also buy new Convictions or increase the ones you already have via CrP.

The cost is 7 + (the dice cost* 2).

A new Conviction is only 7 CrP and starts at Rank 0.

Devotions

Now is a time to reflect on the relationships your characters have cultivated through the course of their adventuring and which ones have died and soured.

A character gains a free point at each level up that they can use to either increase an existing Devotion by 1 or to create a new one a Rank 1, following the normal rules.

Additionally, they may redistribute all their Devotion Ranks freely, even past 3rd level.

You can also increase your Devotions or purchase new ones as well through CrP.

All new Devotions start at Rank 1 and cost 7 + (Number of Devotions * 2) CrP to purchase.

From there, you can increase their size which costs 7 + (the Devotion’s Rank * 6).

Descriptions

A character can physically change as they travel: the events becoming etched on their faces and posture.

A character gains a free point every 3rd level after the first that they can use to either increase an existing Description or create a new one at Rank 1.

After that, they can redistribute 1 point + for every 3rd level after the first that they’ve obtained but can also buy new Descriptions or increase existing ones with CrP if they wish.

The base cost for Descriptions are the number of your character’s Descriptions * 6.

New Descriptions start at Rank 1.

Increasing the Rank of a Description by 1 is the Base Cost + (Rank * 12) CrP.

Racial Temperament

A character can also increase their Racial Temperament as well but they can never lower it or reduce it.

Increasing the Rank of your Temperament by 1 costs 6 + (Rank * 8) CrP.

Reputation

The GM will also adjust your Reputation and complete your Reputation sentences as she deems appropriate. She gets to add 1 point at this point, but she can also move as many existing points around as she wants. She may also change the ending to any Reputation sentences she wants.

She may also ‘free’ them up to distribute later: so, if your character moves far away where people haven’t heard of them, you could lose your Reputation, which might be good or bad.

Note to GMs: while you can complete the sentences at any time, giving the player a heads up as to where their Reputation is headed, it isn’t advised that you start putting points into the other two Reputations until the character reaches their 5th total level.

Step 4: Increase/replace Archetype Abilities

Check your selected Archetypes to see if they have any of their Abilities that either grow stronger (i.e., the Thug’s Precision Strike), gain additional free Skills (i.e., any of the Expert Archetype’s Skill Focuses), or gain something else for free (e.g., the Weird-Adept Archetypes gaining new Rudiments - rules to which can be found in Acquiring Rudiments sidebar under the Weird-Adept Generalist Archetype of Chapter 2.3 Archetypes).

After that, if you’ve spent CrP to specialize into a new Archetype, you do so now, following the normal rules set forth in Chapter 2.3: Archetypes.

Teaching Your Spell to Others

Another option that Weird-Adepts have is to learn spells from each other but spells are complex things with more layers than an onion, so it’s not a simple task to teach another a Rudiment you know, even if they’re using the same magic system.

The first step is to be taught the basic of the Rudiment. This is an Weirdcraft Skill Check with the penalty being set by which type of magic is doing the teaching and learning.

For instance, a Sourcerer’s understanding of magic is innate, making it hard to articulate how they’re able to do something to someone who understands that same thing through study. In the same way, Wizards and Mages shape magic completely differently than Speakers who shape spells using the Divine Language.

This takes a 1d4! days of study and teaching, as well as ‘dry’ runs, after which the Check is made.

If it’s successful, the person has the basics and may gain that Rudiment as part of their normal level up (instead of rolling a random spell) or they may learn is instantly, but this takes additional work, costing them CrP to the tune of 25 - the learner’s Int score, as they have to devote additional time to finally cementing it in memory, time that might be taking up with other things.

A failure indicates that more time and study must be taken, increasing the days of study by 1 dice size (d6! days of study for the second try, d10! for the fourth attempt and so on).

Finally, it’s time to reflect on the fact that your travels have made you just a little bit tougher, a little bit better at facing what life throws at you.

Vitality

At every even total level, the character gets a free reroll of their Vitality Die from their last total level, taking the higher of the two rolls.

However, if both rolls are under their Minimum Vitality from their Constitution, they receive that value instead.

At every odd total level, they gain a free roll of their Vitality Die that’s added to their total Vitality.

This is not affected by their Con’s Min Vitality and is just a straight roll. You’ll need to record it on your character sheet.

Remember to recalculate your Wound Categories and your Trauma Threshold.

Small note: if you're Specializing at an odd total level where you get a reroll, you're going to use the new Vitality Dice to do the roll but you're still rolling against your last roll.

Step 6: Adjust Integrity & Followers

Integrity

Now the other players will have a chance to adjust your Integrity by silently judging the character, rating him 1 to 10 in 3 categories: Flaws, Teamwork, Respect.

Flaws How well has this player played their Flaws? Do they only seem to be brought up when it’s convenient but then forgotten they need to get away with something?

Does she use her Flaws to further the story and character development, or does she use them to annoy others?

A character that leans more to the former, rather than the latter, deserves a higher score.

Teamwork Does this player work well with others? Do they pull their load and put themselves on the line or do they pick fights that derail the session? Are they on top of things and help set up or are they late and spend all their time on the phone?

Again, a character that leans more to the former, rather than the latter, deserves a higher score.

Respect How do you think this character is viewed by those he interacts with, by both the party’s character and NPCs? Has he kept his word (or at least, not gotten caught) or does he have a habit of backstabbing others?

Calculating the Bonus/Penalty The GM should then take the scores and average them across each category and then subtract 5 from each of them. Those three modified averages are then totaled: this is how much Integrity they gain or lose.

For example, Claire receives scores of 5, 4, and 6 for her Flaws, 3, 5, 1 for her Teamwork, and 10, 8, 10 for Respect, giving her an average of 5 (0 after the -5) for Flaws, 3 (-2) for Teamwork, and 10 (5) for Respect.

Totaling the modified average, she gains 3 Integrity.

The individual scores and who gave them should be kept a secret, though the player can know how much they scored in each category to help them improve.

Followers

If you’ve reached your 10th total level, your GM should also roll to see if you’ve attracted a follower.

Learning Non-standard Abilities

Players who go out of their way to roleplay their character learning a new ability can get that ability irrespective of leveling, Archetype combination, and/or CrP spending, but only if they’ve taken some penalty for doing so (suffered for it) and only at the discretion of the GM.

Example: the fighter is realizing that he’s finding his faith as the adventure progresses. He’s actively doing things to gain Favor but beyond that, while the rest of the characters are relaxing and healing in town, he’s off at the local temple, learning about his patron Divine.

Because of this, while the other players get Relaxation, he’s forfeiting that so the next couple of adventures see him with a higher average Stress (and thus closer to having a Mental Break) than the rest of the party.

Additionally, he sought out priests of his cult and paid them to teach him, thus sacrificing his own coin on their lessons rather than spending it on repairs, fine foods, or new gear.

After a few sessions and adventures, he’s more than earned the Favored of the Divines ability that’s usually reserved for the Priest Archetype.

The rule of thumb is that a player must have gotten in at least 2d4!+2 situations where penalties they accrued in pursuit of their new ability have come into play. So, the fighter needs to go out on 5 adventures with his higher Stress for him to gain that ability.

You can adjust this as a GM: suppose the fighter got into a combat where his weapon broke due to him not repairing it and he nearly died because of it. In that case, you can count that as more than one.

The primary purpose of this is to dissuade players who are just trying to game the system and dipping here and there for specific abilities. This should be a reward for players who roleplay their character well and not for min-maxers who abuse the system so feel free to adjust these guidelines as you go for each individual player.

Who Tracks This?

The GM has a lot on her plate and a lot to track so it is on the players to track and advocate for getting this bonuses if they're actively seeking them out.

If you're looking for a particular ability, give your GM the heads-up before starting and discuss what you might do to get it. This will make life much easier for both of you.

Rewards Beyond Wondrous Items

Non-standard abilities can also be used as a reward in lieu of Wondrous Items. So, if you would give out a Relic or Occult Item, give out an ability instead, either through a divine boon or because of some talent a character has discovered they have.

Leveling Abilities

You can also give out leveling abilities but, once gained, the character will need to continue to sacrifice and train to increase them: they don’t level up automatically as they don’t come naturally to them, although this can be for a lesser cost.

If you don’t want to keep track of that (and I don’t blame you), you can have these abilities increase at half the rate as they normally would but still increase automatically at level up.

Made-up Abilities

Maybe you can’t find an Archetype ability that matches what your player is going for. A common example that I’m sure will come up for you is ambidexterity.

In this case, use the same rules but give them that ability.

So if a character is constantly using his off-hand in combat, giving him a heavy penalty, after 2d4!+2 combats, he’s gotten the hang of it and gains ambidexterity.

CrP Discounts For Skills

Beyond abilities, you can also use this to reward players by either discounting Skills or just giving them to them. So, a character who uses a weapon in a Combat Skill Suite they're not Trained in, can get that Skill Suite at a discount or for free at the next level up (or even during play).

Followers

While a character may use the Recruiting skill to find hirelings at any time, after a character reaches total level 10, they start having people seek them out. At that point, and at every level (or 2 for those with a Weird-Adept Archetype) level above that, the GM rolls on the Follower Attracted table found below but to give context to it, let's talk about the different types you can attract.

Follower Types

These come in three varieties:

Groupies

These are those that wish to swim in the wake of the big fish, primarily profiting off the other followers of the party and are often washerwoman, prostitutes, or errand boys. They don’t ask anything of the party but are happy to be hired for specialty jobs as long as they pay well.

They’re also the least loyal and they’ll never put their lives on the line for the party.

Hirelings

Hirelings make up the paid help. They often seek out the party directly and have a basic skill or two they can offer. The idea is that the party will help them further their craft and connections for a bit of pay. They’ll make themselves as helpful as possible - such as repairing armor and weapons for the cost of room and board.

Hirelings won’t break contracts unless the captain of the party falls into Little Integrity and will be more loyal than groupies, however, they’ll only risk their lives if it’s in their contract to do so.

Retainers

These are those that could be full adventurers on their own but has hitched their wagon to your train in hopes of getting more. They’re there out of loyalty but they do demand a greater share of the loot due to the fact that they bring more to the table.

These are, essentially, lower-level characters that you can run.

Sidekicks

Sidekicks are something special. A Sidekick is a person who is completely loyal to a character. They would lay down their lives for a character. A character has a limited number of Sidekicks that he can call upon over the course of his whole lifetime.

Sidekicks are childhood friends, people who deify the character, or other such unquestionably loyal followers so you only get 1 in their entire life.

A Sidekicks is essentially a second Player Character that the player gets to run.

Any new follower can be declared by the player to be a Sidekick, having sought out the party, he is recognized as an old friend or a new friend who swears undying loyalty to the character. Additionally, a follower can be elevated to the status of a Sidekick if he or the character saves the other’s life.

Even before total level 10, a character can search out his Sidekick, however, they must be old friends from his past in this case.

Only severe abuse can break the bond between the hero and his Sidekick, but should this happen, the Sidekick becomes an enemy of equivalent zeal.
