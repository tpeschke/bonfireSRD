Carry

Instead of each item having a weight in pounds or ounces, each item is assigned a size that represents, not only how much it weighs, but also its general bulky and awkwardness. These sizes correspond to the naming conventions set up in Chapter X: Misc Rules, however, just because something is Medium sized here, doesn’t mean it’s the size of a human - again, item sizes are based on their weights, bulkiness, and awkwardness to carry.

Your Carry matches up with that, so if you have 2M as your Carry, you can carry 2 Medium items - or 2M items and is based on your strength

You can also swap out 1 for 3 of the size lower. So instead of carrying 2M items, you could carry 1M item and 3S items.

Each individual instance of an item counts towards this: carrying two daggers, both S items, would count as 2S for Carry.

Wearing Items

Normally what is written under the Carry column for each item is its Size. The only exception is clothes, armor, and personal containers. If worn on your person, not carried or stuffed in a backpack, these become less encumbering since their weight is distributed across basically the entire body.

In this case, shrink them 1 size for Carry purposes. Small items become negligible. Wearing boots, a Small item, then makes them effectively weightless, while any Heavy Armor makes them only M.

Personal Containers

Personal Containers, such as backpacks or sacks, go one step further. Since it is assumed that your Carry weight is how much you can hold effectively, it does take into account that some things are too awkward to effectively carry despite being fairly light.

These containers increase your Carry by the noted amount.

The exceptions, sort of, to this are coin purses, which make coins negligible (otherwise they’re 10 = 1S), quivers which do the same thing but with arrows, and scabbards and sheaths allow you to wear weapons - shrinking them 1 size.

Going Over Carry

You can go over your Carry, of course, but it will slow you down. Going over your Carry prevents you from Sprinting.

Beyond that, each M of weight over your limit removes the next quickest Movement Category (so, removing Running, Jogging, Walking, and then Crawling) until your character is unable to move.

Unless proven otherwise, the GM should assume the characters are carrying their max Carry if they pick up something new while out adventuring.

Currency

The denomination of coinage is fairly simple and is done in divisions of 10.

10 cc = 1 silver coin (sc)

10 sc = 1 gold coin (gc)

10 gc = 1 platinum coin (pc)

So, 100 cc is equal to 1 gc.

The silver coin is the base of the economy and is what most day-to-day interactions use. If a denomination isn’t noted, it’s assumed to be sc.

Note that 100 coins is 1S

Superior Equipment

Not all equipment is built the same and, if you have some extra coin, it might benefit you to invest in some good quality gear.

Superior gear is always unique and even two +1 longswords can be different. This is done through Traits and they're like the Traits that your character has: they're used to describe the gear.

During a Confrontation, Combat, or Challenge, if that Trait applies, you can gain an extra d4! to what is going on. This is in addition to the normal d6! for gear you get in a Confrontation and the reroll that you would normally get in a Skill Challenge.

So, for instance, let's say that you get some better armor. You ask to make it extra padded and that becomes its Trait: Extra Padded. A little while later, you find yourself in combat and you take a particularly nasty blow and decide that you don't want to die, and the Trait applies here so you add a temporary d4! to your armor's DR.

This, however, comes at a cost: when used this way in Combat or a Skill Challenge, your gear becomes a little more worn, gaining 1 Wear.

You can have additional Traits on a single weapon and/or increase the dice size of Traits you already have. Increasing the power simply increases the size of the bonus dice you get.

Every time you do so adds an extra 50% the cost of the item.

Wear

Wear represents how close your weapon is to breaking.

Simply put: if, on a Skill Check, Attack roll (for weapons), or Defense roll (for shields and armor), you roll equal to or below the Wear of that item on the d20, that item breaks and becomes functionality unusable until it’s repaired.

There are some specific cases that should be noted:

  1. You still maintain any penalties (Fatigue, Defense Modifiers from armor and shields, for example) that item gives you unless you discard it.
  2. In the case of weapons, you can still use the broken weapon - you just half all the damage it does.
  3. And finally, instead of having the item break, if it’s a Superior quality item, you can instead reduce one of its Traits by 1 dice size as long as you have at least Trait at a d4!.

Repairing

Since the Maintenance Skill will only get you so far, you might need to remove Wear from your gear through blacksmithing. This is done through personally having the applicable Skill (Leather- or Metalworking, for example) or through contract work.

Hiring It Out

If you just hand off your gear to a blacksmith, the price for repairs is 10% the base Source price for the item (not factoring in the price increase for it being Superior if that's the case) per point of Wear and takes 1 day for every 2 sc the repairs cost.

Doing It Yourself

If personally done through the appropriate Skill, the cost for raw materials is 5% the base Source price for the item per point of Wear with the base time being 1 day for every 4 sc, however, two things to note:

The first is that you need to roll a Check vs d20! + the amount of Wear rounded to the next largest dice size (so, 4 Wear becomes a d6!) as the end of that time. Failure results in an amount of leftover Wear that will require twice the time and raw materials to fix at which point you'll need to make another Check.

So, if you're repairing a glaive (base cost 49 sc) with 8 Wear on it. The cost per Wear is 2sc 4cc so the total for all 8 would be 19sc 2cc, which will take just about 5 days.

At the end of those 5 days you'd roll a Check vs d20!+d10!.

If you fail by 10, that means that you were only able to repair 6 Wear, leaving 2 left over. To fix that it would be an additional 9sc 6cc (since it's 4sc 8cc this time per Wear) and take another 4 days or so.

Note that your total comes to 28sc 8cc where going to a blacksmith would have cost you 39sc 2cc.

The last thing to consider is that if you don't have a forge for metalworking, you'll have to rent one, which can range from 1cc to 1sc per day depending on how busy the forge (good luck trying to rent one at a military camp).

Mount Stats

Each mount is different though they do have ranges based on the breed and this is represented by their unique set of stats that are rolled for each mount you look at while shopping. These can usually be discerned by a Handle Animal Check that's, of course, appropriate to the type. So, nice try, but Handle Animal (horse) won't help you figure out which riding lizard is best.

These stats are then used to determine the specifics of each mount: how fast can they get up and go? How long can they run for? Each of the main stats has a positive effect on some of these derived stats and a negative effect on others so figuring out the right balance for what you want out of a mount can be tricky. Averages for each breed are found under that breed's profile in the Bonfire Bestiary.

This number is subtracted from the damage dealt by a weapon.

All armor weighs you down and makes it harder to dodge incoming attacks. This number is added to your Defense roll.

Armor is restricting, even at the best of times. This is added to your Initiative roll at the start of combat.

Rec Mod

Again, armor is restricting. This is added to your weapon’s Recovery.

This is how heavy your armor is for Carry when your armor is carried on your person.

This donates at what Wound Category you become Fatigued. A for Always, H for Hurt, B for Bloodied, and W for Wounded.

Armor, regardless of how fitted, is restricting and heavy, making it harder to perform certain skills in certain gear. This is the number applied to physical Skill Checks.

Hirelings

Artisans and Mercenaries starts with 3 points. These points can be spent on skills, training, or equipment. The price to increase something = bonus + (current bonus * 2), minimum 1, except equipment which is a number of points = Source price/100, rounded up.

You get to distribute a number of points equal to your Recruiting Rank - the GM gets to spend the rest.

Basic Food & Lodgings

This is not normally included. +1 sc/day.

Hazard Pay

Artisans and Unskilled Laborers will demand a share of the loot or double pay for anything dangerous. Mercenaries normally don't unless the job is more dangerous than you let on when you hired them.

More Skilled Artisan And Mercenaries

1 extra point per +1 sc/day they demand.

Improved Morale

Doubling the pay per day gives a d4! to morale checks. Each doubling after increases it by 1 dice size.

Charisma

Subtract Charisma in cc from price/day.
