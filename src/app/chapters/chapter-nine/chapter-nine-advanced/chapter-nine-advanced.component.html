Your character is a multi-faceted representation of a fictional persona. She has her hopes and her dreams, her strengths and her weakness. All of these add up to hopefully make someone that has a compelling story of victory over her flaws, or defeat from the same.

In game play terms, these are represented by 6 characteristics:

GOALS represent what your character is trying to achieve.

TRAITS represent what sets your character apart from other characters.

DEVOTIONS represent their relationships, either with people, organizations, or otherworldly powers.

REPUTATION represent how other people see them - what are they rumored to have done or what kind of people do others think they are.

FLAWS are also considered Characteristics so they're back!

These characteristics will be provided dice that will be used in Confrontations addressed below and represents how strong it is, with the bigger the dice, the better it is.

Goals

Characters can start with up to 3 Goals, but needs to have at least one at all times. They receive Experience and Honor, as well as increasing the other 3 Characteristics, as you progress along the path to completing them.

What does you character want? Why can‚Äôt they get it? These are the two fundamental questions that should be answered for each Goal.

Overlapping Characteristics

You may think you‚Äôre pretty smart by having a Trait and Devotion both be the same thing so that you always get to roll them at the same time. However, consider that you‚Äôre putting your eggs in one basket and it would be worth it to vary it a little to cover more bases, allowing you to use them more often and perhaps leveraging characteristics to bring in others.

Ideally, they should include other party members and the overarching plot of the campaign.

These goals can be anything from the incorporeal (‚ÄúI want to find a home‚Äù or ‚ÄúI want to deal with this anger inside me‚Äù) to the very concrete (‚ÄúI want to find my father‚Äôs sword‚Äù or ‚ÄúI want to stop this invasion‚Äù).

The best goals are those that are measurable and obtainable.

Being motivated by a Goal allows you to push yourself beyond what you thought was possible. When a Goal applies to a Confrontation, when you lose an Appeal, you take half the Stress.

Traits are things that stick out about your character. Things that people tend to notice about them or that the character defines his identity by. These can be something specific (‚ÄúI have blonde hair‚Äù) or something general (‚ÄúI have an intimidating demeanor‚Äù) or somewhere in between (‚ÄúI‚Äôm an orc‚Äù).

Traits don‚Äôt have to be physical in nature. It could be how your character views himself (‚ÄúI‚Äôm confident‚Äù or ‚ÄúI‚Äôm good at math‚Äù). They can also be something about your character that he just can‚Äôt explain (‚ÄúI‚Äôve always been good with dogs‚Äù or ‚ÄúI can read people easily‚Äù).

The sky's the limit when it comes to Traits, so go wild, though they can't have exceptions. So, "I'm an angry person" works but "I'm an angry person but I don't let it affect my work" doesn't.

You start off with 5 Traits of varying levels: 2 weak, 2 average, and 1 strong. These are represented by dice of varying size. How strong these Traits are depends on your Charisma. So, if you have a 13 Charisma, you gain 5 Traits - two at d4, one 2d6, one 2d8, and one at 3d8

While naturally charismatic characters come out of the gate with strong personalities, as you adventure, your character will also grow and change, gaining and strengthening their Traits. This can be done by either increasing your Charisma, which also raises your Traits, or by buying Traits up directly.

Thinking of Traits

Thinking of Traits on the fly is hard. For new players, there's two ways of handling this: either picture a character from fiction you like or find interesting, and try to describe them. These are their Traits, and you can crib off them to start with.

Or if you have no idea where to start or are just a risk taker, you can use the Trait Generator, which will give you a random Trait. These Traits may not always make sense, but they can often serve as a jumping off point for your imagination.

Racial Temperament

Your Racial Temperament also tends to come into play fairly regularly during Confrontations. Like a Trait, you can add its dice in an Appeal. But what is the dice size? Well, it's your Honor Dice.

So, basically, if your Racial Temperament applies, you get your Honor Dice that round. Pretty easy, pretty straightforward.

Devotions

The final aspect are the Devotions that your character has. What relationships does he have? What organizations does your character associate with? Is he religious and, if so, is his loyalty more to his cult or to his god? How does he feel about that thing?

Relationships between individuals don‚Äôt need to be reciprocal or even contemporary. A young fighter may be inspired by a girl who doesn't love him back or by a long dead king he greatly admires. These are still viable candidates for his Devotion.

Everyone receives two free Devotions: one to their character‚Äôs clan/family and another of their choice. Each Devotion comes with a "relationship verb" and the thing itself. So, his clan Devotion could be "Hate Clan" or "Have mixed feelings about Clan" or "Loyal to Clan"

These Devotions start a 2.

Devotions are a more powerful driving force than other Characteristics, meaning that, if a Devotion applies, you automatically subtract 1 point for every point you have in it. So, if you approach a blood enemy, trying to convince him to help you, even if you‚Äôre being entirely reasonable, it‚Äôll be much harder to do so.

All new Devotions start at 1 and cost 4 CrP to purchase. From there, you can increase their size which costs 6 + (2 * your current Devotion). So, to go from a 2 to a 3 Devotion, it'll cost you 10 CrP ( 6 + ( 2 * 2 ) ).

Reputation

Reputation, in a nutshell, is what your character is known for and how other people see them. So, if you want to convince someone that you‚Äôll keep your word, having a Reputation for doing so will help you out.

Reputation takes the form of 3 incomplete sentences starting with ‚ÄúI‚Äôm known for...‚Äù. Each time you level up, your GM will finish or change out one or more of those sentences and assign points to them that represent their strength. She‚Äôll choose how to finish them based on how your character has been acting or the results of their actions.

That‚Äôs right: just like in real life, you don‚Äôt really have control over your Reputation, though there is a way to influence it as you go.

So, for instance, Banwulf the fighter is a good hearted warrior who wants to protect the people of the town. After a raid, however, he finds himself in possession of a number of raider women and children.

The town‚Äôs leader, a highly influential person, tells him that he needs to put the captives to the sword since they‚Äôll only grow up to continue the cycle of violence against the town but Banwulf demurs and, in the middle of the night, he releases them so they can escape.

Banwulf, of course, receives Honor for his decision, but at level up, the GM completes one of his Reputation sentences. He becomes known for betraying his friends. This is because the town, who he previously protected, feels betrayed at his actions.

In a Confrontation, Reputation counts as a form of Evidence, but instead of getting the standard 3 points, you instead get the Reputation‚Äôs strength in points. It can be combined with other Evidence or adds a number of Honor Dice equal to its strength if you're using it as an Appeal.

Flaws

Let's circle back to Flaws because they are considered part of your Confrontation Characteristics, after all, they make up who your character is as well. You start with your Flaws giving you 1d4! + your current total Stress you have in Confrontations should you bring them in.

Using your Flaws in such a way is extremely relaxing and so removes any Stress gained during that Confrontation.

When To Use These Rules

These rules may not be appropriate to break out every time two people talk to each other, or even disagree on something. They‚Äôre more of a framework that help you track how compelling a side is and, you‚Äôll find, that you can quietly use these rules in the background as your players roleplay.

While there are other reasons to start a Confrontation, they‚Äôll mainly be started by you for a specific purpose: things situations are covered by the Game Master‚Äôs Guide so what we‚Äôll talk about here is when to know when a player-initiated Confrontation should happen and that‚Äôs fairly easy.

Think of your NPCs in terms of their GOALS and METHODS. Goals are pretty obvious and map one-to-one with the Goal Characteristics, but Methods are how they get things done based on their Characteristics. Methods are kind of the NPC‚Äôs morality.

If the NPC feels like what the player is asking them to do aligns with their Goals and their Methods, no Confrontation happens: they don‚Äôt need to be convinced.

If, on the other hand, the Goal aligns but the Methods do or if the Methods align but the Goal doesn‚Äôt, a Confrontation is a great tool for determining whether the NPC agrees.

And if neither Goal nor Method aligns, no Confrontation happens: The NPC can‚Äôt be convinced.

Of course, if you think it‚Äôs reasonable, the players can enter into a Confrontation to convince the NPC that their Goals and Methods align, but that‚Äôs up to you and how much the players are trying to abuse it.

Playing Out the Confrontation

The Confrontation is done in a series of back-and-forths called ‚Äúrounds‚Äù, in which each person is trying to get the other person to back down. This involves an argument-counterargument structure.

A round starts when someone puts forth an argument and ends when the other person feels they‚Äôve countered it or they‚Äôve had to concede, so a round can be multiple exchanges with each side talking back and forth, asking and answering questions.

Both sides begin with points equal to any applicable Devotions they have, with the side who didn‚Äôt starting with an extra 1 point.

Arguments come in two flavors: Evidence and Appeals.

EVIDENCE

Evidence is facts: things that are believed to be true, even if they‚Äôre not true in actuality.

These are flexes in power: I‚Äôm the king and so do you a favor if you do this for me.

These are also things you know about the other person, or subjects involved: if you know that a person is caring or has a temper, you can use that against them as Evidence.

If your opponent concedes to your Evidence, you get 3 points, otherwise no one gets anything. Note that the only thing your Evidence needs to be considered counted by your opponent is the fact that your opponent believes it, honestly.

So, while a normal person has to concede to the sky being blue, a color blind one could counter with the fact that the sky isn‚Äôt blue to them and that‚Äôs valid.

Evidence is the surest way of convincing your opponent, but it also tends to be more work: you have to know about your opponent and know their situation.

Generally, this is signaled by placing a finger up in the air or pressing it against the table.

APPEALS

Appeals are appeals to a person‚Äôs emotions, their sense of self, or their Honor.

Traits are often (but not always) used this way. It can sometimes be a jab at someone‚Äôs character or an insinuation of skill, through bragging about how good of a climber you are or maybe displaying your weapon in a threatening manner. It can also be how famous your character is.

Appeals vary in the number of points they get you.

Versus Evidence, Appealing is considered to be talking past someone to go straight for the emotions. The Appealer gets them as many points and Stress as dice you put up, but it also gives the other side 3 points.

Versus another Appeal, each person rolls the dice assigned to the Characteristic they‚Äôre using.

The person who rolls the highest total gets that points equal to the number of dice they rolled and half that much Stress, rounded up. So, if they roll 3 dice, and win, they get 3 points and 2 Stress.

The other person gets nothing but gets Stress equal to the number of dice they rolled.

This makes this a sound move for very charismatic people, but it is still risky.

This is signaled by placing the dice in front of you, or holding them up, though you‚Äôre not obliged to reveal how many or their sizes.

Tracking Points

Each side is responsible for their own points but to make it easier, you can use the E/A method.

Write an E and A on top of each other, and then draw a line between them. When you gain points through Evidence, put a single tally next to the E.

When you win an Appeal, note how many dice are added by the A.

When you're totaling your points at the end just multiple the tallies next to E by 3 and add that to the sum of the numbers by A. This should allow you to quickly jot things down and keep them straight during the Confrontation.

Here‚Äôs some areas you can use to Appeal to your enemy and what their corresponding dice might be:

HONOR Your standing can carry you through the day, even if your arguments are weak. The character with the highest Honor gets to apply a free use of their Honor die to their pool.

STRESS When your opponent is overworked and they're at maximum whelm, it's much easier to get them to give in. You can redeem a d4! worth of Leverage for every Stress penalty your opponent has, however, you don't have to redeem all the d4!s all in one round and can spread them out over the course of the Confrontation.

EQUIPMENT It may be the case that equipment can sway an argument. Often this is through imitation or torture, but gold can also persuade a hesitant sellsword or the right equipment might convince a thief to take on a risky job. A badge of authority may also win you some influence.

Equipment used for this purpose gains the character a base d8. Equipment that‚Äôs substandard or that‚Äôs being re-purposed from its original use (a knotted rope used to whip a victim, or a broken glass bottle used to threaten someone) only nets a d4.

STAT CONFRONTATION DIE Having an exceptionally high Strength or Dexterity might be relevant to the situation. If that‚Äôs the case, add that Abilities Confrontation Die to your pool.

APPLICABLE SKILLS You may roll an additional die for any applicable Advanced skill based on that Skill‚Äôs Rank. Take the Rank of that Skill and round up to the nearest dice size. So, a Rank 5 Skill would net a d6 dice while a Rank 14 Skill would net a d20.

But that is by no means a comprehensive list and plays should be rewarded for out-of-the-box thinking. Appeals start at a d4!, but this can vary. For instance, a very skilled swordsman with a lot of Advanced Training in his weapon of choice makes a more powerful Appeal than a rookie.

You can use the same Appeal multiple times during a single Confrontation.

Characteristic as Evidence vs Appeals

There‚Äôs no hard rule that can be given of when a Trait qualifies as one over the other, but there are some guidelines.

Generally, if the person whose Characteristic is being used is not directly involved and they‚Äôre known by both parties, it is Evidence.

If, however, one of the people is involved in the argument, especially if it is defense, or you‚Äôre trying to bring in the Characteristic of someone that the other person doesn‚Äôt know, then it is considered an Appeal.

Evidence as an Appeal

Truthfully, there may be a time where you have Evidence but it's in your best interest to reinforce the idea with emotion that makes it closer to an Appeal than Evidence.

In this case, you treat the Evidence as an Appeal with a base dice pool of your Intellect or Charisma's Confrontation Die, a die from your Articulation Skill (if you don't have that skill, you don't get this dice), and your Honor Die for the argument itself. From there, you can add other Characteristics to the pool.

The GM can say no to a proposal if the counter argument doesn‚Äôt directly relate to the argument, so careful phrasing could limit what your opponent can do. This is usually the indication that the round isn‚Äôt finished yet and the person who was rebuffed can try a different tact or argument.

Bluffing, or in other words, Lying

As per normal, a character only needs to roll a Deception Check if the other person is suspicious of them, but even then, if one side accuses the other of something, trying to get them to reveal a secret, they can use Deception to try and bluff, hiding their actual reaction.

If the person is, in fact, telling the truth but someone is still suspicious of them, they‚Äôd roll an Articulation Check to convince their accuser of their sincerity.

A strong willed or well disciplined person, when faced with insurmountable odds will often fight and/or argue long after the cowards have run, either gaining victory or assuring his death. Targeting these weaker willed people might also make or break a side. This is represented by a character‚Äôs Panic Threshold.

Check the character‚Äôs Honor to determine their starting Panic Threshold. Record this for your character by circling the Wound Category in your Vitality Section. When you reach that Wound Category, you become Panicked.

Panicked people have several disadvantagous: the first being that they half their Stress Threshold until they're out of danger or the argument they're in (often just conceding to get away).

The second is that they gain double the amount of Stress from Appeals, win or lose.

In combat, a Panicked person can't take aggressive actions: they'll still attack, but if the other side flees, they won't chase them and they'll try to fall back to more defensible positions, if they can.

If a person goes above their Stress Threshold, they immediately lose the Confrontation. If they're in combat, they simply turn and flee, unable to face their own morality. Captains can try and rally them with a Check immediately.

If cornered and given no space to run, these routed fighters enter a frenzy - only using aggressive Tactics and becoming immune to Stress penalties.

FAST TALKING

üúÇ Deluxe Rule

There may be times when you don‚Äôt want to/can‚Äôt get into a protracted argument and just want to overwhelm your opponent into agreeing with you. For example, you need to fast talk your way past a guard or convince a henchmen to go first down a trap filled corridor before he remembers he doesn‚Äôt have insurance.

In those cases, you want to fast talk the person.

This is, essentially, a Confrontation in a single round: either by showing a blatant, time-sensitive, piece of Evidence (‚ÄúLet me in! The orcs are RIGHT THERE‚Äù) or by making an Appeal that stuns the person with your Charisma.

If the person can‚Äôt match you or beat you, you won them over: for the next 30 minutes or so and then they‚Äôll come calling.

Fast Talking is not a way to permanently get a person on your side - it is a band-aid tactic to buy you a little extra time.

ENDING THE ROUND

Like I said, a round ends when an argument is countered or conceded.

Quickly jot down the points gained that round and move on.

These points are kept secret until the end of the Confrontation.

Multiple People in a Confrontation

You can, of course, have people help out in a variety of support roles during a Confrontation but they can often trip over each other, giving the opposition a chance to rebuttal.

The big thing to note is that, if you‚Äôre not careful, you can step on each other‚Äôs toes as the opposition can choose which argument to focus on if many are provided, allowing them to peak the weakest one.

So, if multiple emotional Appeals are presented, he can pick the lowest result.

This doesn‚Äôt mean that those arguments are wasted: they can be reused later in the Confrontation and, in fact, seeing which arguments the opposition chooses to ignore might give you some clues about where their argument is weakest.

Players may take sidebars, of course, (See the Taking a Break Sidebar below) but they may also use the Warfare Advanced Skill to temporarily pause and discuss with each other.

If you‚Äôre facing multiple people, note that any Evidence has to apply to/be believed by everyone on the opposition to be effective, thus making Evidence less and less helpful in a situation where you have to convince a group or mob.

TRUMP CARDS

There are some arguments that are so good that they can counter multiple points. These are considered trump cards and should be played carefully because they can only counter arguments that your opponent has already put forward.

Trump cards give a number of points equal to their base times the number of arguments they counter.

So, if you put forward Evidence that counters 3 arguments, it would give you 9 points, while having a 4 dice Appeal that counts against the same number gives you 12 points.

It doesn‚Äôt matter if the arguments have already been counter, in fact, that‚Äôs when you get the most benefit out of trump cards.

ESCALATING A CONFLICT

üúÇ Deluxe Rule

A debate between friends is on a different level than two enemies mudslinging or threatening to cut each other‚Äôs throat if the other doesn‚Äôt listen. This is represented by levels of escalation.

At baseline, the Confrontation is simply a battle of ideas, with each side having no person stake in the matter. It is the default and only inflicts Stress.

However, one or either side can make it PERSONAL, which means pride and personal image is starting to get in the way. This happens when one side uses more than 3 Appeals in a Confrontation or when one side purposely makes an insulting comment.

When a Confrontation becomes Personal, Appeals become more powerful, being worth 1 extra point on a success, however, people often dig their heels into the ground when you start questioning their character, making others things more dangerous.

The first is that you need to not just beat your opponent, but beat his total points by 5 plus a roll of his Honor Dice (which represents his mood).

But, it can be to your benefit: the winner gains Honor equal to how much he beat his opponent by, while the loser loses that much.

Above Personal Confrontations are INTIMIDATION Confrontations. This means that you‚Äôre now threatening personal harm to either the person, or someone the person cares about. You can now bring in that shiny blade or talk about what a shame it would be if that person‚Äôs house burned down.

This is similar to a Personal Confrontation, except that, if you lose and you don‚Äôt then escalate to violence and follow through on your threats, you lose face, losing 5 Honor.

But, if you escalate to violence and also get your buttkicked, you lose 10 Honor so if you‚Äôre going to start threatening people, make sure you either win or that you‚Äôre as tough as you say you are.

You can jump up levels, going from a standard Confrontation to intimidating them in a single round, however, you can‚Äôt de-escalate.

PUSHING THE POINT

When one side feels they may have the upper hand and that the other has run out of arguments, they can try to push the point, or end the Confrontation. He simply tries to push the other side to make a decision.

If it turns out that the other person does still have more to say, the Confrontation continues, gaining 1 point.

If, however, it turns out that the pushed person does, in fact, have no more to say, than the Confrontation ends

Taking a Break

It may take days or weeks to sway someone, making sure you know them, their likes and dislikes, and that you‚Äôve got all your bases covered.

In this way, it is perfectly acceptable, should it make sense, that you suspend the Confrontation, possibly indefinitely.

FOLDING

üúÇ Deluxe Rule

Folding is what happens when you want to try and give them a lie or are not planning on doing what the winner wants. In this case, you give in early.

If your opponent pushes, you can choose to Fold by saying that you have no more arguments, but if the other person suspects that you‚Äôre Folding they can try and call you on it, in which case it's an opposed Deception Check if you are and a Articulation Check if you‚Äôre not.

The Articulation Check is to make it harder to meta-game whether or not you're bluffing.

If you are Folding and you fail the Deception check, the Confrontation continues.

Note that obviously your accuser now knows you were lying but that doesn‚Äôt give them a way to prove to other people that was the case. It‚Äôs communicated to them with a definite drop in the voice or the way you looked away.

If you‚Äôre Folding and succeed at your Deception Check, or if you weren‚Äôt and you fail on your Articulation, the accuser pushes the issue but there‚Äôs no proof and so he loses face.

The accuser gives you all the Honor he would have gotten from the win and only gains it back if he can prove publicly that you were lying.

Ending The Confrontation

At the end of the Confrontation, both sides reveal their points. The one with the most points gets their way.
