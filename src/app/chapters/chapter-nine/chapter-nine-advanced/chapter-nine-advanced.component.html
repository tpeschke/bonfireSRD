Your character is a multi-faceted representation of a fictional persona. She has her hopes and her dreams, her strengths and her weakness. All of these add up to hopefully make someone that has a compelling story of victory over her flaws, or defeat from the same.

In game play terms, these are represented by 4 characteristics:

GOALS represent what your character is trying to achieve.

TRAITS represent what sets your character apart from other characters.

PASSIONS represent interests and things that get your character’s blood pumping.

DEVOTIONS represent their relationships, either with people, organizations, or otherworldly powers.

FLAWS are also considered social characteristics so they're back!

These characteristics will be provided dice that will be used in social conflicts addressed below and represents how strong it is, with the bigger the dice, the better it is.

GOALS

Characters can start with up to 3 Goals, but needs to have at least one at all times. They receive Experience and Honor, as well as increasing the other 3 social characteristics, as you progress along the path to completing them.

What does you character want? Why can’t they get it? These are the two fundamental questions that should be answered for each Goal.

Overlapping Characteristics

You may think you’re pretty smart by having a Trait and Passion both be the same thing so that you always get to roll them at the same time. However, consider that you’re putting your eggs in one basket and it would be worth it to vary it a little to cover more bases, allowing you to use them more often and perhaps leveraging characteristics to bring in others.

Ideally, they should include other party members and the overarching plot of the campaign.

These goals can be anything from the incorporeal (“I want to find a home” or “I want to deal with this anger inside me”) to the very concrete (“I want to find my father’s sword” or “I want to stop this invasion”).

The best goals are those that are measurable and obtainable.

Being motivated by a Goal allows you to push yourself beyond what you thought was possible. When a Goal applies to a Conflict, you gain a +8 from Stressing as opposed to +6. You can only have 1 Goal apply per Conflict.

Starting Characteristics

If you’re bringing in a new character to a new campaign, it’s highly probable that you won’t know what the game is going to be about, especially if the GM wants to keep it a secret.

It’s perfectly acceptable to leave your Goals, Traits, Passions, and/or Devotions blank until you’ve got your feet under you but you should have at least a few Medium term goals and your strongest Trait filled out relating to your character’s personal arch.

Traits are things that stick out about your character. Things that people tend to notice about them or that the character defines his identity by. These can be something specific (“I have blonde hair”) or something general (“I have an intimidating demeanor”) or somewhere in between (“I’m an orc”).

Traits don’t have to be physical in nature. It could be how your character views himself (“I’m confident” or “I’m good at math”). They can also be something about your character that he just can’t explain (“I’ve always been good with dogs” or “I can read people easily”).

The sky's the limit when it comes to Traits, so go wild, though they can't have exceptions. So, "I'm an angry person" works but "I'm an angry person but I don't let it effect my work" doesn't.

You start off with 6 Traits of varying levels: 3 weak, 2 average, and 1 strong. These are represented by dice of varying size. How strong these Traits are depends on your Charisma. So, if you have a 13 Charisma, you gain 6 Traits - three at d4, one 3d6, one 3d8, and one at 4d8

While naturally Charismatic characters come out of the gate with strong personalities, as you adventure, your character will also grow and change, gaining and strengthening their Traits. This can be done by either increasing your Charisma, which also raises your Traits, or by buying Traits up directly.

Thinking of Traits

Thinking of Traits on the fly is hard. For new players, there's two ways of handling this: either picture a character from fiction you like or find interesting, and try to describe them. These are their Traits and you can crib off them to start with.

Or if you have no idea where to start or are just a risk taker, you can use the Trait Generator, which will give you a random Trait. These Traits may not always make sense but they can often serve as a jumping off point for your imagination.

If you want a little more control over your Traits, their strength and how many, instead of just accepting what's on the above chart, you can use your Trait points from Charisma to modify your Traits how you will. From there, you buy up your Traits using those points in the same way as if you were buying them up with CrP.

You can also then go back and further increase them with CrP but only after you're done using all your Trait Points.

If you want to increase your Traits further, you can spend CrP to give them a boost. This represents your character spending time developing their personality rather than spending time learning other things. If you built your character in Basic and are now switching over to Advanced, you can refund your Trait buy ups to redo them, but only once.

Weak, Medium, and Strong Traits all cost different amounts to raise. Why? Well, in the Basic game, there was a very simple strategy to winning and we wanted to keep things simple so you could learn the basics. Now we're giving you the power to try different ways of convincing people so we needed to make sure the numbers made sense and that you weren't "wasting" your CrP were it wouldn't benefit you, so you'll notice that Weak Traits actually get cheaper the more you buy.

You can also buy new Traits for the base Xd4 price.

So, for example, if you want to increase a Medium Trait from 2d6 to 2d8, it would cost 12 CrP.

Please note that you can't buy a Trait past a d12

Starting Characteristics

If you’re bringing in a new character to a new campaign, it’s highly probable that you won’t know what the game is going to be about, especially if the GM wants to keep it a secret.

It’s perfectly acceptable to leave your Goals, Traits, Passions, and/or Devotions blank until you’ve got your feet under you but you should have at least a few Medium term goals and your strongest Trait filled out relating to your character’s personal arch.

PASSIONS

What does your character do in their downtime? What’re their hobbies and what do they spend their time thinking about? These are their Passions.

You have a number of Passion Points from their Intellect that can be used to buy and increase your Passion for a subject. New Passions cost 12 points and start at a strength of 1. You can then spend 8 points to increase that by 1, but you can do this multiple times. You can also spend CrP as well in a like manner.

Once per Conflict, if your Passion applies, you can increase a single dice size a number of times equal to that Passions strength. This can be done at any time during a Conflict and you don't have to spend all the increases form a Passion at one time. You can do this once for every applicable Passion but you can't use multiple Passions on a single dice.

If you do spend a point to increase a die you've already rolled, you get to roll again, taking the second result, even if it's worse.

DEVOTIONS

The final aspect are the Devotions that your character has. What relationships does he have? What organizations does your character associate with? Is he religious and, if so, is his loyalty more to his cult or to his god? How does he feel about that thing?

Relationships between individuals don’t need to be reciprocal or even contemporary. A young fighter may be inspired by a girl who doesn't love him back or by a long dead king he greatly admires. These are still viable candidates for his Devotion.

Everyone receives two free Devotions: one to their character’s clan/family and another of their choice. Each Devotion comes with a "relationship verb" and the thing itself. So, his clan Devotion could be "Hate Clan" or "Have mixed feelings about Clan" or "Loyal to Clan"

Servant Archetypes gain an additional Devotion which is to their god or cult which is assumed to have a positive aspect (love, loyal, faithful to, etc.).

Devotions are a more powerful driving force than other Characteristics, meaning that, if a Devotion applies, you can bring it in again and again, however, each time you do so, you shrink its size by 1, down to a d4.

BUYING NEW AND INCREASING DEVOTIONS

🜂 Advanced Rule

All new other Devotions start at 1d4! and cost 4 CrP to purchase. From there, you can increase their size which costs 4 + (4 * the number of times you've purchase this Devotion), up to a d12!. So, to go from a d6! to a d8! Devotion, it'll cost you 12, because you've bought it up twice so far ( 4 + ( 4 * 2 ).

Facing the Powers that Be

There may come a time when you’re facing someone that’s related to your Devotions. This may be the leader of your family, a higher up in your cult, or the person that your Devotion is centered around trying to get you to do something.

In these cases, your Devotion makes you more likely to follow them without question and so they can add your Devotion die to their social dice pool when in conflict with them.

In fact, all Characteristic dice can be used against a person if you or they know your strengths and weaknesses but to a greater or less extent. If using a person's Trait against them, you can bring them into your pool but reduced by 1 dice size.

FLAWS

Let's circle back to Flaws because they are considered part of your Social Characteristics, after all, they make up who your character is as well. You start with your Flaws giving you 1d4! + your current total Stress you have in Social Conflicts should you bring them in.

WHEN TO USE THESE RULES

These rules may not be appropriate to break out everytime two people talk to each other, or even disagree on something. If you think your character can make a compelling argument that would convince an NPC to change his ways or give him a discount, don’t bother getting your dice out.

Additionally, if the GM thinks that what the player is asking isn’t probable, they can turn them away at the door. Sure, it’s possible that the blacksmith with give you that sword for free, but is it probable? Absolutely not.

However, it is probable that he’s at least willing to give you a discount. The other person must also see a possible benefit to engaging with you.

So, if you have a Conflict over a discount, that gives him a window to upsell you if you lose the Conflict. That might be worth it to him, while just having him give you the sword for away for free results in no Conflict unless the fighter convinces the blacksmith that the town is going to be shortly overrun by a goblin horde and that he needs a better weapon to fight them off, perhaps the blacksmith would be willing to part with the weapon for free, or maybe, if he's particularly stingy, he'd be willing to lend it to him temporarily for free.

And so setting the stage for a Conflict is often helping the NPC see what they could possibly get from you should they win.

Another way to start a Conflict is to get a person to act on their Characteristics, such as provoking a very aggressive person to attack you, or getting someone who is a softtouch to give you money. In that case, you simply state that, based on what you know about the person, they should probably act this way. If you're actually correct, you can start a Conflict with them. If you're wrong, you lose 1 Honor.

So, for instance, you know that an NPC is a known liar so you want him to lie to make a bold faced lie in front of the king to out him as the enemy. You state something along the lines of "You know, I think this guy is just a compulsive liar that he'll try and get away with lying to the king". If that's correct, you start the Conflict.

But let's say that the NPC doesn't have a Trait, Flaw, or Passion that related to lying. Turns out, the man has a Passion for manipulation instead. He wasn't lying because he was a complusive liar, he was lying for a very specific purpose. In that case, you'd lose 1 Honor.

Other Reasons to Start a Conflict

There may be other reasons why you feel a Conflict is appropriate. This may be the case of a demon who forces his will on another’s demon or, in a more mundane case, in the case of someone who wants to tell the player a secret but also may not trust them entirely or doubt the wisdom of doing so.

There may also be certain circumstances where one person is forced into a Conflict, most often through magical means, but this is most often done as part of a plot point. More information is found in the Conflict as a Plot Point in Bonfire Advanced.

STARTING the CONFLICT

Most commonly, The characters have to decide what’s at stake; this is what they’re butting heads about and why they’re willing to put themselves on the line. More importantly, each side has to define what they want to happen to the stakes. If the stakes are high, this might change how much each side to willing to fight to get what they want or what tactic they’ll use to get it.

You also have to take into account what the other person is just straight up not willing to do. You can't convince a priest to desecrate the altar of his Divine, for instance, or someone to give away a family heirloom just because you want it. Additionally, both sides have to see some sort of benefit, should they win.

So, for instance, a blacksmith and a fighter want to get in a conflict over the price of a sword. That’s what’s at stake. The fighter wants it at a discount, the blacksmith wants to make a bigger profit.

If you're starting the Conflict because you're trying to get someone to act on one of their Characteristics, you don't have stakes in the same way. Instead, if you win the Conflict, they act on their Characteristic, if they win, they manage to restrain themselves and you might lose 1 Honor (plus any additional you may have ponyed up).

Your GM has the right to simply say that an argument wouldn't work against someone and simply reject letting you put certain dice into your pool. So, for instance, you wouldn't get a Leverage die for having a hostage while having a Conflict with an orc who really doesn't care about the hostage's life, or you won't be able to bring in your Trait "I'm a gentle soul" to convince the hardened tyrannt's heart by making a heartfelt plea for him to stop skinning all the children.

Non-Binary Outcomes

The standard Conflict is a winner-take-all affair but that’s not always going to be the case. Sometimes there is room for compromise, such as when you’re haggling or trying to get a raise.

In this case, the stakes are set at the max each side is willing to give. After that, when a player loses Advantage, they can move their max to be closer to the other side. If you do, you also gain a d8! Leverage dice.

If what you want and what they want becomes the same, the Conflict is over with the Honor split equally between the two parties.

So, for example, a fighter and thief are dividing up the reward. Both willing to get into a Conflict over the shares because it means they both can gain something, the stakes are set at a 30/70 split.

After a few rounds, the fighter loses Advantage. He says that he’s willing to concede to a 40/60 split, with the thief getting the 40, if he wins.

Note that if the fighter wins, the thief will get 40% of the reward with the fighter pocketing the rest while if the thief wins, he’ll get 70% while the fighter will only get 30%, so even if he loses, by compromising the thief gets more than the starting 30%.

If both get to the point where they’re both aiming for a 50/50 split, the Conflict is over and they divide the spoils evenly between them, both Honor and treasure.

PLAYING OUT the CONFLICT

The Conflict is done in a series of back and forths called “rounds”, which each person is trying to get the other person to back down.

The person with Advantage always go first. They’ll be referred to as the “Attacker”. If this is the first round of the Conflict, the person who initiated it has d4 Advantage.

The round goes like this:

1. Attacker picks an argument, roleplays his argument, and then rolls the appropriate dice

2. Defender picks their counter argument, roleplays it, and then rolls the appropriate dice

a. Before you roll, you can also Stress, giving you a +6, or +8 if it relates to a Goal you have

i. You gain 1 Stress Dice for each time you do this

b. Either side at this point may remove a dice from their winning pool to turn any dice to its max side

3. The person with the highest total puts all their dice into their winning pool, minimum 1

a. Ties go to the person with the highest Honor, should they ante up an Honor

4. Increase the Advantage Dice by 1 size and give it to the winner of that round

5. Go back to Step 1.

PICKING AN ARGUMENT & DICE

Both attacker and defender are going to pick an argument and get dice based on what they choose.

Typically, this will be centered on one of their Characteristics. So, for instance, a person with “Friendly 4d8!” would try to frame his argument in a friendly way. Chatting up the person, getting to know him before trying to get what he wants: “Can’t you help out a friend?”, he might say, before rolling that 4d8!.

Or it can be based on the situation: in this case, he brings in Leverage dice. Let’s say he’s got his mark alone in an alley and gets a knife to his throat: he might roll the Social Dice for his Strength, a d6 for the knife and d6 for catching the guy by surprise.

Leverage can be basically anything but they typically start at a d6. Here are some ideas of Leverage you can bring in:

HONOR Your standing can carry you through the day, even if your arguments are weak. The character with the highest Honor gets to apply a free use of their Honor die to their pool.

FAME The sad truth is that the more famous you are, the more people assume you know what you’re talking about. If your Fame is greater than your opponents by 25, add a d4. Increase this die one size for every additional 25 points you have above them.

STRESS When your opponent is overworked and they're at maxium whelm, it's much easier to get them to give in. You can redeem a d4! worth of Leverage for every Stress penalty your opponent has, however, you don't have to redeem all the d4!s all in one round and can spread them out over the course of the Conflict.

EQUIPMENT It may be the case that equipment can sway an argument. Often this is through imitation or torture, but gold can also persuade a hesitant sellsword or the right equipment might convince a thief to take on a risky job. A badge of authority may also win you some influence.

Equipment used for this purpose gains the character a base d6. Equipment that’s substandard or that’s being re-purposed from its original use (a knotted rope used to whip a victim or a broken glass bottle used to threaten someone) only nets a d4.

ABILITY SOCIAL DIE Having an exceptionally high Strength or Dexterity might be relevant to the situation. If that’s the case, add that Abilities Social Die to your pool.

APPLICABLE SKILLS You may roll an additional die for any applicable Advanced skill based on that Skill’s Rank. This chart is also used to determine the base size of the Passion dice for that Skill. Take the Rank of that Skill and round up to the nearest dice size. So a Rank 5 Skill would net a d6 dice while a Rank 14 Skill would net a d20.

You can only bring in one Trait at a time, but you can bring in as much Leverage and as many Devotions as you think can apply.

The GM can say no to a proposal if the counter argument doesn’t directly relate to the argument, so careful phrasing could limit what your opponent can do.

SEIZING ADVANTAGE

Normally, the Advantage Dice goes to the person that won the round but while they’re selecting their argument and dice, the defender side can seize advantage by paying Honor.

The amount they pay starts a 1, with it doubling for each time its been stolen during the Conflict. It can only be stolen once per round.

So, if Person 1 has stolen it twice and then Person 2 steals it again, Person 2 has to pay 4 Honor.

Helping Out in a Conflict

It is possible to help out a buddy during a Conflict, or have multiple buddies help out you.

If this is the case, each person takes a Position, with the person leading the Conflict being 1st Position by default.

This Position does matter as you shrink all your dice 1 size for each Position ahead of you. So, 2nd Position shrinks all their dice by 1 size but 4th Position shrinks it all of theirs by 3.

Besides this, things work out pretty much the same, with you selecting your argument and selecting dice during Steps 1 and 2, but you can choose to sit out a round but be warned, each Position that sits out during a round add 2 dice to the other team’s winning pool.

When totals are compared, each Position compares their total with 1st Position’s total on the other team. If your total beats theirs, you add your Position number in dice to your team’s winning pool. This is regardless of how many dice you put up.

Ganging up may seem like a good idea but it can leave the other side feeling trapped and bullied, forcing them to fight harder to win. The side with the few positions gains an additional +2 times the difference in number to their bonus when they Stress. So, if Side A has 5 people and Side B has 2, when anyone on Side B Stresses, they gain a +12 ( 6 + (5 - 2 ) * 2 ) to their total (or +16 ( 8 + (5 - 2 ) * 2 if it relates to any of their Goals).

CHANGING POSITION

If you also beat the Position above you’s total and they’re okay with it, you can swap Positions. You can move up as many Positions as you beat and who is okay with it. You have to meet both conditions, however; you can’t just jump and rearrange Positions willy-nilly. Regardless of whether or not you become 1st Position, if you beat theirs, you gain the Advantage, however this is shrunk based on your Position.

Swapping into 1st Position makes it your Conflict.

REVISITING the POINT

Sometimes you don’t think a point landed as well as it should or the person didn’t really listen to you, so you double back on a previous argument.

Revisiting the Point allows you to reuse a Characteristic that has already been used, however, each reuse causes you to gain Stress for each dice you bring in this way multiplied by how many times you’ve Revisited the Point this Conflict.

So, if you’ve already Revisited the Point earlier in the Conflict but you want to do so again, this time bringing in your 4d6! Trait. The cost for doing so would be to gain 8 Stress.

ESCALATING

You can always raise the stakes but doing so many cost you.

Either side may decide to Escalate during the Conflict, in which case all people in the Conflict instantly roll all Stress Dice they’ve gained and add the totals to their Stress, with Repercussions still taken care of after the Conflict is over.

The person who made the threat and put everyone more on edge gains an additional Stress Dice for each time they’ve previously Escalated, though this is only gained after the rolls and totals are made.

ENDING the CONFLICT

The Conflict ends if one side runs out of arguments or folds.

If the other side has Advantage, they get Last Words: essentially parting remarks that remained unmet. In game terms, they add a Trait’s worth of dice to their winning pool, along with any applicable Leverage.

Since it’s hard to tell when someone is wrapping up their arguments, you may make an Intuition Skill Check vs their Intuition - success means you can get in Last Words, even if you don’t have Advantage.

The winner of the Conflict, and the person who gets what they want, is the one with the most dice in their winning pool.

The difference between the winning pools gets added to the winner’s honor, subtracted from the loser’s. Don’t forget to subtract any additional Honor that you anted up during the Conflict. This can result in a negative sum.

If you have helpers, 1st Position can distribute gained and lost Honor as they will between all their helpers.

Finally, Conflicts are stressful for both sides and so everyone in the Conflict gains Stress equal to the the difference in the winning pool's total, though each Position reduces that Stress gain by 1 for each Position from Position 1 they are.

FOLDING

🜂 Advanced Rule

Folding is what happens when you want to try and give them a lie or are not planning on doing what the winner wants. In this case, you give in early.

If you wish to Fold, you simply state that you want to end the Conflict: you have no more dice left. Essentially, you’re going to try to end the Conflict as normal, with the other side gaining all the Honor in the pot as normal.

But if the other person suspects that you’re Folding they can try and call you on it, in which case it's an opposed Deception Check if you are and a fake Persuasion Check if you’re not.

The fake Persuasion Check is to make it harder to meta-game whether or not your bluffing.

If you are Folding and you fail the Deception check, the Conflict continues with a forced round on your part.

Note that obviously your accuser now knows you were lying but that doesn’t give them a way to prove to other people that was the case. It’s communicated to them with a definite drop in the voice or the way you looked away.

If you’re Folding and succeed at your Deception Check, or if you weren’t and you fail on your Persuasion, the accuser pushes the issue but there’s no proof and so he loses face.

The accuser gives you all the Honor he would have gotten from the win and only gains it back if he can prove publicly that you were lying.

PRESSING the ISSUE

🜂 Advanced Rule

Sometimes your opponent is done, but you’re not.

If your opponent tries to end the Conflict, either from lack of arguments or trying to Fold, you may sacrifice a dice from your winning pool. Doing so causes you to roll a Persuasion vs their Insight.

Winning forces the person back into the Conflict, with you gaining Advantage. Losing, however, causes you to gain a number of Stress dice equal to the dice you sacrificed.

Pressing Issue can be done multiple times in a Conflict but each time increases the amount of dice you have to sacrifice by 1.

ESCALATING TO COMBAT

🜂 Advanced Rule

You may think that escalating to a combat is the best thing to do if you’re receiving a thorough tongue lashing but that may not always be the case.

Remember that you gain an amount of Stress for Repercussions which does lower the Morale of your enemy, making them break easier.

If one side escalates to a combat, the other side not only receives Last Words but also gains Leverage equal to the difference in winning pools in d8!s for the length of the combat that they can use at any time to sway Morale. See the Conflict in Combat Advanced Rule in Chapter 10: Combat.

BEING A SMART ASS

🜂 Advanced Rule

You have to be careful who you provoke. Sure, you may have a sharper wit than the bully, but he may have a sharper sword. You may find that people are not impressed by the jackass who can’t put his money where his mouth is.

If a Conflict ends in combat, and the last man standing is a Fighter Archetype and his opponents were not, the victor gains an additional d8! Honor that’s subtracted from the amount of Honor he lost from losing the Conflict in the first place. This may not put him at a net positive for the whole encounter but it may alleviate some of his hurt pride.

REPERCUSSIONS of a CONFLICT

Those that have assisted in the Conflict get back any Honor they put into the pool and that’s it. The winner of the Conflict, however, may give them some of the credit, bestowing on them a portion of the Honor he won.

Anyone with Stress Dice now rolls a d6! for each one. Total the two highest dice.

If the total is less than or equal to 8, the character suffers only a short term effect and takes that total in Stress from the encounter.

If the total is greater than that, the character gains that total in Stress and suffers a long term effect. Choose one of the following:

🜂

Reduce the dice size of one of their Traits, Passions, Devotions by 1

🜂

Lose a dice from one of their Traits

🜂

Lose a Trait, Passion, or Devotion

🜂

Change a Goal

If you rolled any 1s, the character is also changed for the better. It doesn’t matter how many 1s you roll, you only choose one of the following:

🜂

Gain a Passion, Devotion, or Trait at d4

🜂

Change a Goal

🜂

Add a dice to one of their Traits

🜂

Receive a temporary d8 to your next conflict

🜂

Increase the dice size of 1 of their Characteristics by 1 for each time you rolled a 1 on your Stress Dice, up to the number of dice associated with that Characteristic and up to a d12!.
