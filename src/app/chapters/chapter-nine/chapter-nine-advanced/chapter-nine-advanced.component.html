Your character is a multi-faceted representation of a fictional persona. She has her hopes and her dreams, her strengths and her weakness. All of these add up to hopefully make someone that has a compelling story of victory over her flaws, or defeat from the same.

In game play terms, these are represented by 4 characteristics:

GOALS represent what your character is trying to achieve.

TRAITS represent what sets your character apart from other characters.

PASSIONS represent interests and things that get your character‚Äôs blood pumping.

DEVOTIONS represent their relationships, either with people, organizations, or otherworldly powers.

FLAWS are also considered social characteristics so they're back!

These characteristics will be provided dice that will be used in social conflicts addressed below and represents how strong it is, with the bigger the dice, the better it is.

GOALS

Characters start out with 6 Goals; 3 Short term, 2 Medium, and 1 Long. They receive Experience and Honor as well as increasing the other 3 social characteristics as you progress along the path to completing them.

What does you character want? Why can‚Äôt they get it? These are the two fundamental questions that should be answered for each Goal.

Overlapping Characteristics

You may think you‚Äôre pretty smart by having a Trait and Passion both be the same thing so that you always get to roll them at the same time. However, consider that you‚Äôre putting your eggs in one basket and it would be worth it to vary it a little to cover more bases, allowing you to use them more often and perhaps leveraging characteristics to bring in others.

Ideally, they should include other party members and the overarching plot of the campaign.

These goals can be anything from the incorporeal (‚ÄúI want to find a home‚Äù or ‚ÄúI want to deal with this anger inside me‚Äù) to the very concrete (‚ÄúI want to find my father‚Äôs sword‚Äù or ‚ÄúI want to stop this invasion‚Äù). Generally, abstract goals should be the Medium to Long term ones.

The best goals are those with clear benchmarks for your character. At the beginning of every session, you‚Äôll select a benchmark to work for.

For example, a character with the Goal of ‚ÄúGet Married‚Äù decides that his benchmark is to talk to a member of the opposite sex.

You may not select the exact same benchmark twice, however, you may choose a modified version of it to be more specific or more difficult.

Following the previous example, that character may not select ‚Äútalk to a member of the opposite sex‚Äù again, however, they can set a benchmark to ‚Äútalk to a member of the opposite sex my character is attracted to‚Äù or ‚Äúflirt with a member of the opposite sex‚Äù.

Characters receive a d8 for each Goal that is related to any Social Conflicts they find themselves in.

Traits are things that stick out about your character. Things that people tend to notice about them or that the character defines his identity by. These can be something specific (‚ÄúI have blonde hair‚Äù) or something general (‚ÄúI have an intimidating demeanor‚Äù) or somewhere in between (‚ÄúI‚Äôm an orc‚Äù).

Traits don‚Äôt have to be physical in nature. It could be how your character views himself (‚ÄúI‚Äôm confident‚Äù or ‚ÄúI‚Äôm good at math‚Äù). They can also be something about your character that he just can‚Äôt explain (‚ÄúI‚Äôve always been good with dogs‚Äù or ‚ÄúI can read people easily‚Äù).

The sky's the limit when it comes to Traits, so go wild, though they can't have exceptions. So, "I'm an angry person" works but "I'm an angry person but I don't let it effect my work" doesn't.

You start off with 6 Traits of varying levels: 3 weak, 2 average, and 1 strong. These are represented by dice of varying size. How strong these Traits are depends on your Charisma. So, if you have a 13 Charisma, you gain 6 Traits - three at d4, one 3d6, one 3d8, and one at 4d8

While naturally Charismatic characters come out of the gate with strong personalities, as you adventure, your character will also grow and change, gaining and strengthening their Traits.

Thinking of Traits

Thinking of Traits on the fly is hard. For new players, there's two ways of handling this: either picture a character from fiction you like or find interesting, and try to describe them. These are their Traits and you can crib off them to start with.

Or if you have no idea where to start or are just a risk taker, you can use the Trait Generator, which will give you a random Trait. These Traits may not always make sense but they can often serve as a jumping off point for your imagination.

ASSIGNING YOUR TRAIT POINTS

üúÇ Advanced Rule

If you want a little more control over your Traits, their strength and how many, instead of just accepting what's on the above chart, you can use your Trait points from Charisma to modify your Traits how you will. You start off with 6 Traits of varying levels: 3 weak (1d4 each), 2 average (3d4 each), and 1 strong (4d4).

You can then spend 1 of your Trait Points from your Charisma or CrP. This can bring the dice size of a Trait up to a max of a d8. The CrP cost is equal the number of sides of all smaller dice plus the number of sides of the dice you‚Äôre increasing to, times two, but this increases all dice (so it costs the same to increase a weak Trait as it does to increase an average or strong one). So, to raise a 3d6 Trait to a 3d8 it would cost 36 CrP ( ( 4 + 6 + 8 ) x 2 = 36 ). Spending CrP can increase a Trait past a d8, however, this is only down after all the Trait Points are distributed.

You can also spend 2 of those Trait points or 4 CrP on gaining another weak Trait, which can then be increased as normal.

PASSIONS

What does your character do in their downtime? What‚Äôre their hobbies and what do they spend their time thinking about? These are their Passions.

Your number of Passions and their strength is based on your character's Intellect. See the above table.

If your character needs to select Passions related to their Skills and/or Masteries. They don't need to be one for one, though that's the easiest way, but they should be at least tangentially related. Increase the Passion's dice size for every Rank you have in that Skill or +1 you have in that Mastery.

ASSIGNING YOUR PASSION POINTS

üúÇ Advanced Rule

You have a number of Passion Points from their Intellect that can be used to buy and increase your Passion for a subject. New Passions cost 5 points and start at a d4. You can then spend points to increase that dice size. Each point increases the dice size by 1, up to a d12.

Characters already get their Skills as Leverage in a Social Conflict with the size being determined by Rank, but they can further increase that if they make that Skill a Passion. You increase the size of that dice by investing Passion Points in it in the normal manner, though it can be increased up to a d20 instead. This can only be done with Advanced Skills and Masteriesy but not Skill Suites.

If your character selects a Passion related to one of their Adv Skills or Masteriesy, you're considered to have a free Passion in that Skill or Mastery that starts at a d4 and increases by 1 size for every Rank or +1 you have in it. Passion points increase that dice size by 1 per and they can go above a d12 in this way. Increasing your Mastery or Rank later doesn't increase your Passion's dice size.

DEVOTIONS

The final aspect are the Devotions that your character has. What relationships does he have? What organizations does your character associate with? Is he religious and, if so, is his loyalty more to his cult or to his god?

Relationships between individuals don‚Äôt need to be reciprocal or even contemporary. A young fighter may be inspired by a girl who doesn't love him back or by a long dead king he greatly admires. These are still viable candidates for his Devotion.

Everyone receives two free Devotions: one to their character‚Äôs clan/family and another of their choice.

Servant Archetypes gain an additional Devotion which is to their god or cult.

STARTING AND INCREASING YOUR DEVOTIONS

üúÇ Advanced Rule

All Devotions start at 1d6 but you can increase the dice size by 1 for 5 CrP. These dice can be increased up to a d12. You may also purchase additional Devotions for 10 CrP.

Facing the Powers that Be

There may come a time when you‚Äôre facing someone that‚Äôs related to your Devotions. This may be the leader of your family, a higher up in your cult, or the person that your Devotion is centered around trying to get you to do something.

In these cases, your Devotion makes you more likely to follow them without question and so they can add your Devotion die to their social dice pool when in conflict with them.

In fact, all Characteristic dice can be used against a person if you or they know your strengths and weaknesses but to a greater or less extent. If using a person's Trait against them, you can bring them into your pool but reduced by 1 dice size.

FLAWS

Let's circle back to Flaws because they are considered part of your Social Characteristics, after all, they make up who your character is as well. You start with your Flaws giving you a 2d4 in Social Conflicts should you bring them in.

WHEN TO USE THESE RULES

These rules may not be appropriate to break out everytime two people talk to each other, or even disagree on something. If you think your character can make a compelling argument that would convince an NPC to change his ways or give him a discount, don‚Äôt bother getting your dice out.

Additionally, if the GM thinks that what the player is asking isn‚Äôt probable, they can turn them away at the door. Sure, it‚Äôs possible that the blacksmith with give you that sword for free, but is it probable? Absolutely not.

However, it is probable that he‚Äôs at least willing to give you a discount. The other person must also see a possible benefit to engaging with you.

So, if you have a Conflict over a discount, that gives him a window to upsell you if you lose the Conflict. That might be worth it to him, while just having him give you the sword for away for free results in no Conflict unless the fighter convinces the blacksmith that the town is going to be shortly overrun by a goblin horde and that he needs a better weapon to fight them off, perhaps the blacksmith would be willing to part with the weapon for free, or maybe, if he's particularly stingy, he'd be willing to lend it to him temporarily for free.

And so setting the stage for a Conflict is often helping the NPC see what they could possibly get from you should they win.

Another way to start a Conflict is to get a person to act on their Characteristics, such as provoking a very aggressive person to attack you, or getting someone who is a softtouch to give you money. In that case, you simply state that, based on what you know about the person, they should probably act this way. If you're actually correct, you can start a Conflict with them. If you're wrong, you lose 1 Honor.

So, for instance, you know that an NPC is a known liar so you want him to lie to make a bold faced lie in front of the king to out him as the enemy. You state something along the lines of "You know, I think this guy is just a compulsive liar that he'll try and get away with lying to the king". If that's correct, you start the Conflict.

But let's say that the NPC doesn't have a Trait, Flaw, or Passion that related to lying. Turns out, the man has a Passion for manipulation instead. He wasn't lying because he was a complusive liar, he was lying for a very specific purpose. In that case, you'd lose 1 Honor.

Other Reasons to Start a Conflict

There may be other reasons why you feel a Conflict is appropriate. This may be the case of a demon who forces his will on another‚Äôs demon or, in a more mundane case, in the case of someone who wants to tell the player a secret but also may not trust them entirely or doubt the wisdom of doing so.

There may also be certain circumstances where one person is forced into a Conflict, most often through magical means, but this is most often done as part of a plot point. More information is found in the Conflict as a Plot Point in Bonfire Advanced.

STARTING the CONFLICT

Most commonly, The characters have to decide what‚Äôs at stake; this is what they‚Äôre butting heads about and why they‚Äôre willing to put themselves on the line. More importantly, each side has to define what they want to happen to the stakes. If the stakes are high, this might change how much each side to willing to fight to get what they want or what tactic they‚Äôll use to get it.

You also have to take into account what the other person is just straight up not willing to do. You can't convince a priest to desecrate the altar of his Divine, for instance, or someone to give away a family heirloom just because you want it. Additionally, both sides have to see some sort of benefit, should they win.

So, for instance, a blacksmith and a fighter want to get in a conflict over the price of a sword. That‚Äôs what‚Äôs at stake. The fighter wants it at a discount, the blacksmith wants to make a bigger profit.

Non-Binary Outcomes

The standard Conflict is a winner-take-all affair but that‚Äôs not always going to be the case. Sometimes there is room for compromise, such as when you‚Äôre haggling or trying to get a raise.

In this case, the stakes are set at the max each side is willing to give. After that, when a player escalates, they can change what they want to be closer to what the other wants.

If it‚Äôs deemed as reasonable by your opponent, you only have to pony up 1 Honor for the escalation, regardless of how many times you‚Äôve escalated before.

If what you want and what they want becomes the same, the Conflict is over with the Honor split equally between the two parties.

So, for example, a fighter and thief are diving up the reward. Both willing to get into a Conflict over the shares because it means they both can gain something, the stakes are set at a 30/70 split.

After a few raises and matches, the fighter runs out of dice and wants to escalate. He says that he‚Äôs willing to concede to a 40/60 split, with the thief getting the 40, if he wins.

The thief says that‚Äôs unreasonable and so the fighter has to pony up the standard Honor.

A little while later, the fighter is again strapped for dice and so says he‚Äôs willing to go with a 45/55 split with the thief. That‚Äôs way more reasonable so the thief agrees. The fighter ponies up only 1 Honor, despite it being the second time he‚Äôs escalated in that Conflict.

Note that if the fighter wins, the thief will get 45% of the reward with the fighter pocketing the rest while if the thief wins, he‚Äôll get 70% while the fighter will only get 30%, so even if he loses, by compromising the thief gets more than the starting 30%.

If both get to the point where they‚Äôre both aiming for a 50/50 split, the Conflict is over and they divide the spoils evenly between them, both honor and treasure.

If you're starting the Conflict because you're trying to get someone to act on one of their Characteristics, you don't have stakes in the same way. Instead, if you win the Conflict, they act on their Characteristic, if they win, they manage to restrain themselves and you might lose 1 Honor (plus any additional you may have ponyed up).

To start off, you're going to select dice that represent your starting argument. You can bring in any of your Characteristics you feel are applicable to how you're going to make your argument, though you are limited to bringing in only one Trait per escalation. You can also bring in as much Leverage you think might help your case. Note that if you're not directly trying to convince your opponent through peer pressure, you'll only be able to use Leverage to do so. This will most likely be the case with Conflicts started to get someone to act on a certain Characteristic.

Your GM has the right to simply say that an argument wouldn't work against someone and simply reject letting you put certain dice into your pool. So, for instance, you wouldn't get a Leverage die for having a hostage while having a Conflict with an orc who really doesn't care about the hostage's life, or you won't be able to bring in your Trait "I'm a gentle soul" to convince the hardened tyrannt's heart by making a heartfelt plea for him to stop skinning all the children.

Once you've gotten all the dice you think we'll be applicable, you'll roll them and set them aside. This wil be your dice pool for the Conflict and where you'll be pulling dice from to represent the conviction behind your argument. You'll also be able to add to it as the Conflict goes on. As an aside, everyone can know how many dice the other side is rolling but not the results of the role.

The player who initiated the conflict goes first, putting forward two dice and giving their total.

LEVERAGE (SITUATIONAL MODIFIERS)

üúÇ Advanced Rule

Leverage is dice you may or may not roll depending on the circumstances. This is not a comprehensive list. If you feel like something might influence the Conflict, feel free to petition the GM to add an extra die, using the below as a guideline. Typically, you'll be adding a d6.

HONOR Your standing can carry you through the day, even if your arguments are weak. The character with the highest Honor gets to apply a free use of their Honor die to their pool.

FAME The sad truth is that the more famous you are, the more people assume you know what you‚Äôre talking about. If your Fame is greater than your opponents by 25, add a d4. Increase this die one size for every additional 25 points you have above them.

EQUIPMENT It may be the case that equipment can sway an argument. Often this is through imitation or torture, but gold can also persuade a hesitant sellsword or the right equipment might convince a thief to take on a risky job. A badge of authority may also win you some influence.

Equipment used for this purpose gains the character a base d6. Exceptional equipment (a really terrifying sword, or lots of gold) can net the player up to a d12. Equipment that‚Äôs substandard or that‚Äôs being re-purposed from its original use (a knotted rope used to whip a victim or a broken glass bottle used to threaten someone) only nets a d4.

ABILITY SOCIAL DIE Having an exceptionally high Strength or Dexterity might be relevant to the situation. If that‚Äôs the case, add that Abilities Social Die to your pool.

APPLICABLE SKILLS You may roll an additional die for any applicable Advanced skill based on that Skill‚Äôs Rank. This chart is also used to determine the base size of the Passion dice for that Skill. Take the Rank of that Skill and round up to the nearest dice size. So a Rank 5 Skill would net a d6 dice while a Rank 14 Skill would net a d20.

PLAYING OUT the CONFLICT

Each character goes back and forth, ponying up two die from their pre-rolled dice pool and roleplaying their arguments based on the die they use. The two dice‚Äôs rolled sum has to either match or exceed the rolled sum of the last dice put forward.

These dice represent their arguments and counterarguments and are coupled with roleplaying it out. What is said in-character should be reflective of the social characteristic that brought the dice in.

If the character can only match with two dice, he also puts 1 Honor Point on the table. His argument was weak and didn‚Äôt have the force behind it that it should have, and others noticed.

If a character can‚Äôt exceed the sum with just two dice, he can either pass (ending the conflict) or he has to put in additional dice forward to raise his total above his opponent‚Äôs. Once totalled, the number of extra dice that were needed are put aside for later.

ESCALATING A CONFLICT

If you have no more dice left in your pool, you may escalate by trying a different tack. This allows you to roll another Trait‚Äôs worth of dice and add any more Leverage that you think have become applicable. You don‚Äôt have to put up another Trait if you don‚Äôt have any left that‚Äôre applicable, so your escalation could be just Leverage, like in the example below.

So, if you‚Äôre in a battle of wits and you‚Äôre losing, you may choose to intimidate your foe by unsheathing your sword. In this case you‚Äôd get an extra d6 for the sword, a d6 for strength, and maybe an additional d6 for your mastery in it.

You also put 1 Honor Point on the table to escalate, however this amount doubles for each time that person has already escalated in this conflict.

From here you return to playing out the Conflict.

ASSISTING IN A CONFLICT

Other characters may also help (if applicable) by putting one of their own dice in. The amount is added to the total but these bonus dice aren‚Äôt put aside for later. They can bring in any Characteristics or Leverage they think are applicable. If they run out of dice, they may also escalate to bring in more, but they also put 1 Honor Point in the pot.

UNDERMINING AN ARGUMENT

üúÇ Advanced Rule

By predicting what your opponent is going to say, you can sometimes take the wind out of their sails prematurely, though you may end up looking the fool after arguing with a strawman.

When you put up your dice, you may also put up a number of d6s, preferably hidden from your opponent.

This dice is your bet about how your opponent is going to respond. If a dice is set on its 1 or 2 face, you bet they‚Äôre going to raise. 3 or 4 means your bet they‚Äôre going to match and 5 or 6 means you think they‚Äôll raise but they‚Äôll put up more dice to do it (this includes dice from their mates helping out).

Should you be correct, you increase your next total by that amount. So, if you put a 2 and they raise, you increase your effective total by 2. If you put up a 3 and they match, you increase your effective total by 3.

Should you guess wrong, you add half that total to what they ponied up.

So, you raise with a 2 and 4 and undermine their argument by putting a d6 with its 2 face up. The total to beat is 6 ( 2 + 4 ).

Your opponent responses by offering up a 3 and 4, meaning they raise for a total of 7 ( 3 + 4). You reveal your dice, meaning you guessed correctly.

You now put up a 3 and 3, which is still a raise because your total is 8 ( 3 + 3 + 2 from undermining their argument successfully).

You can put up as many dice as you wish, but they cancel each other out. So, putting up a d6 with a 2 and one with a 4, and having them just raise, nets you a -2.

ENDING A CONFLICT

The conflict ends when either one side has no more dice and doesn‚Äôt escalate, when one side passes, one side walks away, or one side hits the other (i.e., initiates combat). The other ‚Äòwins‚Äô the conflict.

In the case of a tie, the Conflict is unresolved, with no side gaining what they want. They can only re-engage in another Conflict with the same stakes if something changes.

FOLDING

üúÇ Advanced Rule

Folding is what happens when you want to try and give them a lie or are not planning on doing what the winner wants. In this case, you give in early.

If you wish to Fold, you simply state that you want to end the Conflict: you have no more dice left and won‚Äôt escalate. Essentially, you‚Äôre going to try to end the Conflict as normal, with the other side gaining all the Honor in the pot as normal.

But if the other person suspects that you‚Äôre Folding they can try and call you on it, in which case it's an opposed Deception Check if you are and a fake Persuasion Check if you‚Äôre not.

The fake Persuasion Check is to make it harder to meta-game whether or not your bluffing.

If you are Folding and you fail the Deception check, the Conflict continues with a forced escalation on your part.

Note that obviously your accuser now knows you were lying but that doesn‚Äôt give them a way to prove to other people that was the case. It‚Äôs communicated to them with a definite drop in the voice or the way you looked away.

If you‚Äôre Folding and succeed at your Deception Check, or if you weren‚Äôt and you fail on your Persuasion, the accuser pushes the issue but there‚Äôs no proof and so he loses face.

The accuser gives you all the Honor he would have gotten from the win and only gains it back if he can prove publicly that you were lying.

ESCALATING TO COMBAT

üúÇ Advanced Rule

You may think that escalating to a combat is the best thing to do if you‚Äôre receiving a thorough tongue lashing but that may not always be the case.

Remember that you gain an amount of Stress for Repercussions which does lower the Morale of your enemy, making them break easier.

Players, who aren‚Äôt affected by the Morale rules except under certain circumstances, may see this is a win, but not so.

If the Conflict escalates to combat, any excess dice left in the pool are immediately transferred for free to the Morale Dice pool of their enemy. See the Conflict in Combat Advanced Rule in Chapter 10: Combat.

REPERCUSSIONS of a CONFLICT

A few things happen at the end of a conflict. The first is that the winner gets to decide what happens with the stakes. Going back to the original example of the blacksmith and the fighter haggling over the price of the sword; If the fighter wins that Conflict, then he receives the discount he‚Äôs looking for and if the blacksmith wins the Conflict, he sells it at the increased price.

The winner receives an amount of Honor equal to the difference between what he put on the table and his opponent put on the table. This can end up being a negative sum. The loser deducts all the Honor they put on the table from his total. More on this is found in Chapter 6: Character Vitals under the Changing Honor Category section.

Those that have assisted in the Conflict get back any Honor they put into the pool and that‚Äôs it. The winner of the Conflict, however, may give them some of the credit, bestowing on them a portion of the Honor he won.
