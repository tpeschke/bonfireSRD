Your character is a multi-faceted representation of a fictional persona. She has her hopes and her dreams, her strengths and her weakness. All of these add up to hopefully make someone that has a compelling story of victory over her flaws, or defeat from the same.

In game play terms, these are represented by 6 characteristics:

GOALS represent what your character is trying to achieve.

TRAITS represent what sets your character apart from other characters.

PASSIONS represent interests and things that get your character‚Äôs blood pumping.

DEVOTIONS represent their relationships, either with people, organizations, or otherworldly powers.

REPUTATION represent how other people see them - what are they rumored to have done or what kind of people do others think they are.

FLAWS are also considered social characteristics so they're back!

These characteristics will be provided dice that will be used in social conflicts addressed below and represents how strong it is, with the bigger the dice, the better it is.

Goals

Characters can start with up to 3 Goals, but needs to have at least one at all times. They receive Experience and Honor, as well as increasing the other 3 social characteristics, as you progress along the path to completing them.

What does you character want? Why can‚Äôt they get it? These are the two fundamental questions that should be answered for each Goal.

Overlapping Characteristics

You may think you‚Äôre pretty smart by having a Trait and Passion both be the same thing so that you always get to roll them at the same time. However, consider that you‚Äôre putting your eggs in one basket and it would be worth it to vary it a little to cover more bases, allowing you to use them more often and perhaps leveraging characteristics to bring in others.

Ideally, they should include other party members and the overarching plot of the campaign.

These goals can be anything from the incorporeal (‚ÄúI want to find a home‚Äù or ‚ÄúI want to deal with this anger inside me‚Äù) to the very concrete (‚ÄúI want to find my father‚Äôs sword‚Äù or ‚ÄúI want to stop this invasion‚Äù).

The best goals are those that are measurable and obtainable.

Being motivated by a Goal allows you to push yourself beyond what you thought was possible. When a Goal applies to a Conflict, you lower the dice size of any Repercussion Dice by 1.

Traits are things that stick out about your character. Things that people tend to notice about them or that the character defines his identity by. These can be something specific (‚ÄúI have blonde hair‚Äù) or something general (‚ÄúI have an intimidating demeanor‚Äù) or somewhere in between (‚ÄúI‚Äôm an orc‚Äù).

Traits don‚Äôt have to be physical in nature. It could be how your character views himself (‚ÄúI‚Äôm confident‚Äù or ‚ÄúI‚Äôm good at math‚Äù). They can also be something about your character that he just can‚Äôt explain (‚ÄúI‚Äôve always been good with dogs‚Äù or ‚ÄúI can read people easily‚Äù).

The sky's the limit when it comes to Traits, so go wild, though they can't have exceptions. So, "I'm an angry person" works but "I'm an angry person but I don't let it affect my work" doesn't.

You start off with 5 Traits of varying levels: 2 weak, 2 average, and 1 strong. These are represented by dice of varying size. How strong these Traits are depends on your Charisma. So, if you have a 13 Charisma, you gain 5 Traits - two at d4, one 2d6, one 2d8, and one at 3d8

While naturally Charismatic characters come out of the gate with strong personalities, as you adventure, your character will also grow and change, gaining and strengthening their Traits. This can be done by either increasing your Charisma, which also raises your Traits, or by buying Traits up directly.

Thinking of Traits

Thinking of Traits on the fly is hard. For new players, there's two ways of handling this: either picture a character from fiction you like or find interesting, and try to describe them. These are their Traits and you can crib off them to start with.

Or if you have no idea where to start or are just a risk taker, you can use the Trait Generator, which will give you a random Trait. These Traits may not always make sense but they can often serve as a jumping off point for your imagination.

Racial Temperament

Your Racial Temperament also tends to come into play fairly regularly during social situations. Like a Trait, you can add its dice in an Appeal. But what is the dice size? Well, it's your Honor Dice.

So, basically, if your Racial Temperament applies, you get your Honor Dice that round. Pretty easy, pretty straightforward.

Passions

What does your character do in their downtime? What‚Äôre their hobbies and what do they spend their time thinking about? These are their Passions.

You have a number of Passion Points from their Intellect that can be used to buy and increase your Passion for a subject. New Passions cost 12 points and start at a strength of 1. You can then spend 8 points to increase that by 1, but you can do this multiple times. You can also spend CrP as well in a like manner.

Once per Conflict, if your Passion applies, you can increase a single dice size a number of times equal to that Passions strength. This can be done at any time during a Conflict and you don't have to spend all the increases form a Passion at one time. You can do this once for every applicable Passion but you can't use multiple Passions on a single dice.

If you do spend a point to increase a die you've already rolled, you get to roll again, taking the second result, even if it's worse.

Devotions

The final aspect are the Devotions that your character has. What relationships does he have? What organizations does your character associate with? Is he religious and, if so, is his loyalty more to his cult or to his god? How does he feel about that thing?

Relationships between individuals don‚Äôt need to be reciprocal or even contemporary. A young fighter may be inspired by a girl who doesn't love him back or by a long dead king he greatly admires. These are still viable candidates for his Devotion.

Everyone receives two free Devotions: one to their character‚Äôs clan/family and another of their choice. Each Devotion comes with a "relationship verb" and the thing itself. So, his clan Devotion could be "Hate Clan" or "Have mixed feelings about Clan" or "Loyal to Clan"

These Devotions start a 2.

Devotions are a more powerful driving force than other Characteristics, meaning that, if a Devotion applies, you automatically subtract 1 point for every point you have in it. So, if you approach a blood enemy, trying to convince him to help you, even if you‚Äôre being entirely reasonable, it‚Äôll be much harder to do so.

Reputation

Reputation, in a nutshell, is what your character is known for and how other people see them. So, if you want to convince someone that you‚Äôll keep your word, having a Reputation for doing so will help you out.

Reputation takes the form of 3 incomplete sentences starting with ‚ÄúI‚Äôm known for...‚Äù. Each time you level up, your GM will finish or change out one or more of those sentences and assign points to them that represent their strength. She‚Äôll choose how to finish them based on how your character has been acting or the results of their actions.

That‚Äôs right: just like in real life, you don‚Äôt really have control over your Reputation, though there is a way to influence it as you go.

So, for instance, Banwulf the fighter is a good hearted warrior who wants to protect the people of the town. After a raid, however, he finds himself in possession of a number of raider women and children.

The town‚Äôs leader, a highly influential person, tells him that he needs to put the captives to the sword since they‚Äôll only grow up to continue the cycle of violence against the town but Banwulf demurs and, in the middle of the night, he releases them so they can escape.

Banwulf, of course, receives Honor for his decision, but at level up, the GM completes one of his Reputation sentences. He becomes known for betraying his friends. This is because the town, who he previously protected, feels betrayed at his actions.

In a Conflict, Reputation counts as a form of Evidence, but instead of getting the standard 3 points, you instead get the Reputation‚Äôs strength in points. It can be combine with other Evidence or as a d8 if you‚Äôre using it to make an Appeal.

ATTRACTING NPCS

While a character may use the Recruiting skill to find hirelings at any time, after a character reaches level 10, they start having people seek them out. At that point, and at every 2 levels (or 3 for those with a Weird-Adept Archetype) level above that, the GM rolls on the Follower Attraction table.

These come in three varieties:

CAMP FOLLOWERS These are those that wish to swim in the wake of the big fish, often profiting off the other followers of the party and are often washerwoman, prostitutes, or errand boys. They don‚Äôt ask anything of the party but are happy to be hired on for specialty jobs.

They‚Äôre also the least loyal, abandoning the party should the party start to lose Retainers and they‚Äôll never put their lives on the line for the party.

RETAINERS Retainers make up the paid help. They often seek out the party directly and have a basic skill. The idea is that the party will help them further their craft and connections for a bit of pay. They‚Äôll make themselves as helpful as possible - such as repairing armor and weapons for the cost of room and board.

Retainers won‚Äôt break contracts unless the captain of the party falls into Low Honor and will be more loyal than camp followers, however, they‚Äôll only risk their lives if it‚Äôs in their contract to do so.

SIDEKICKS Sidekicks are something special. A Sidekick is a person who is completely loyal to a character. They would lay down their lives for a character. A character has a limited number of Sidekicks that he can call upon over the course of his whole life-time.

Sidekicks are childhood friends, people who deify the character, or other such unquestionably loyal followers.

A Sidekicks is essentially a second Player Character that the player gets to run.

Any new follower can be declared by the player to be a Sidekick, having sought out the party, he is recognized as an old friend or a new friend who swears undying loyalty to the character. Additionally, a follower can be elevated to the status of a Sidekick if he or the character saves the other‚Äôs life.

Even before level 10, a character can search out his Sidekicks, however, they must be old friends from his past in this case.

Sidekicks start with an automatic Devotion 4 to the character and the character gains an equivalent Devotion in return.

Only severe abuse can break the bond between the hero and his Sidekick but should this happen, the Sidekick becomes an enemy of equivalent zeal.

Flaws

Let's circle back to Flaws because they are considered part of your Social Characteristics, after all, they make up who your character is as well. You start with your Flaws giving you 1d4! + your current total Stress you have in Social Conflicts should you bring them in.

When To Use These Rules

These rules may not be appropriate to break out every time two people talk to each other, or even disagree on something. If you think your character can make a compelling argument that would convince an NPC to change his ways or give him a discount, don‚Äôt bother getting your dice out.

Additionally, if the GM thinks that what the player is asking isn‚Äôt probable, they can turn them away at the door. Sure, it‚Äôs possible that the blacksmith with give you that sword for free, but is it probable? Absolutely not.

However, it is probable that he‚Äôs at least willing to give you a discount. The other person must also see a possible benefit to engaging with you.

So, if you have a Conflict over a discount, that gives him a window to upsell you if you lose the Conflict. That might be worth it to him, while just having him give you the sword for away for free results in no Conflict unless the fighter convinces the blacksmith that the town is going to be shortly overrun by a goblin horde and that he needs a better weapon to fight them off, perhaps the blacksmith would be willing to part with the weapon for free, or maybe, if he's particularly stingy, he'd be willing to lend it to him temporarily for free.

And so setting the stage for a Conflict is often helping the NPC see what they could possibly get from you should they win.

Another way to start a Conflict is to get a person to act on their Characteristics, such as provoking a very aggressive person to attack you, or getting someone who is a soft touch to give you money. In that case, you simply state that, based on what you know about the person, they should probably act this way. If you're actually correct, you can start a Conflict with them. If you're wrong, you lose 1 Honor.

So, for instance, you know that an NPC is a known liar so you want him to lie to make a bold faced lie in front of the king to out him as the enemy. You state something along the lines of "You know, I think this guy is just a compulsive liar that he'll try and get away with lying to the king". If that's correct, you start the Conflict.

But let's say that the NPC doesn't have a Trait, Flaw, or Passion that related to lying. Turns out, the man has a Passion for manipulation instead. He wasn't lying because he was a compulsiveliar, he was lying for a very specific purpose. In that case, you'd lose 1 Honor.

Other Reasons to Start a Conflict

There may be other reasons why you feel a Conflict is appropriate. This may be the case of a demon who forces his will on another‚Äôs demon or, in a more mundane case, in the case of someone who wants to tell the player a secret but also may not trust them entirely or doubt the wisdom of doing so.

There may also be certain circumstances where one person is forced into a Conflict, most often through magical means, but this is most often done as part of a plot point. More information is found in the Conflict as a Plot Point in Bonfire Deluxe.

Starting The Conflict

Most commonly, The characters have to decide what‚Äôs at stake; this is what they‚Äôre butting heads about and why they‚Äôre willing to put themselves on the line. More importantly, each side has to define what they want to happen to the stakes. If the stakes are high, this might change how much each side to willing to fight to get what they want or what tactic they‚Äôll use to get it.

You also have to take into account what the other person is just straight up not willing to do. You can't convince a priest to desecrate the altar of his Divine, for instance, or someone to giveaway a family heirloom just because you want it. Additionally, both sides have to see some sort of benefit, should they win.

So, for instance, a blacksmith and a fighter want to get in a conflict over the price of a sword. That‚Äôs what‚Äôs at stake. The fighter wants it at a discount, the blacksmith wants to make a bigger profit.

If you're starting the Conflict because you're trying to get someone to act on one of their Characteristics, you don't have stakes in the same way. Instead, if you win the Conflict, they act on their Characteristic, if they win, they manage to restrain themselves and you might lose 1 Honor (plus any additional you may have ponied up).

Your GM has the right to simply say that an argument wouldn't work against someone and simply reject letting you put certain dice into your pool. So, for instance, you wouldn't get a Leverage die for having a hostage while having a Conflict with an orc who really doesn't care about the hostage's life, or you won't be able to bring in your Trait "I'm a gentle soul" to convince the hardened tyrant's heart by making a heartfelt plea for him to stop skinning all the children.

Non-Binary Outcomes

The standard Conflict is a winner-take-all affair but that‚Äôs not always going to be the case. Sometimes there is room for compromise, such as when you‚Äôre haggling or trying to get a raise.

In this case, the stakes are set at the max each side is willing to give. After that, when a player loses Advantage, they can move their max to be closer to the other side. If you do, you also gain a d8! Leverage dice.

If what you want and what they want becomes the same, the Conflict is over with the Honor split equally between the two parties.

So, for example, a fighter and thief are dividing up the reward. Both willing to get into a Conflict over the shares because it means they both can gain something, the stakes are set at a 30/70 split.

After a few rounds, the fighter loses Advantage. He says that he‚Äôs willing to concede to a 40/60 split, with the thief getting the 40, if he wins.

Note that if the fighter wins, the thief will get 40% of the reward with the fighter pocketing the rest while if the thief wins, he‚Äôll get 70% while the fighter will only get 30%, so even if he loses, by compromising the thief gets more than the starting 30%.

If both get to the point where they‚Äôre both aiming for a 50/50 split, the Conflict is over and they divide the spoils evenly between them, both Honor and treasure.

Playing Out The Conflict

The Conflict is done in a series of back and forths called ‚Äúrounds‚Äù, in which each person is trying to get the other person to back down. This involves an argument-counterargument structure. A round starts when someone puts forth an argument and ends when the other person feels they‚Äôve countered it or they‚Äôve had to concede, so a round can be multiple exchanges but is commonly only a single back and forth.

The side who started the Conflict starts with 1 point, which isn't great because you want the lowest number of points, like golf.

Points come in two flavors: Evidence and Appeals.

EVIDENCE

Evidence is facts: things that are true, that both people know are true. These are flexes in power: I‚Äôm the king and so do you a favor if you do this for me. It can also be things you know about the other person, or subjects involved: if you know that a person is caring or has a temper, you can use that against them.

Conceding to Evidence gives you 3 points. Evidence is the surest way of convincing your opponent but it also tends to be more work: you have to know about your opponent and know their situation.

Generally, this is signaled by placing a finger up in the air or pressing it against the table.

APPEALS

Appeals are appeals to a person‚Äôs emotions, their sense of self, or their Honor. Traits often (but not always) used this way. It can sometimes be a jab at someone‚Äôs character or an insinuation of skill, through bragging about how good of a climber you are or maybe displaying your weapon in a threatening manner. It can also be how famous your character is. Appeals vary in the number of points they get you.

When presented against Evidence, the Appeal gives them as many points as dice you put up but you're considered to have conceded the point, giving you 3 points. You also get a d4! Repercussion Dice.

If, however, you meet Appeal with Appeal, then each person rolls the dice assigned to it. The person with the lowest total gets points equal to the number of dice the other side put up and sets aside a d8! for Repercussions, while the other person gets nothing and sets aside a d6!. This makes this a sound move for very charismatic people but it is still risky.

This is signaled by placing the dice in front of you, or holding them up, though you‚Äôre not obliged to reveal how many or their sizes.

Tracking Points

Each side is responsible for their own points but to make it easier, you can use the E/A method.

Write an E and A on top of each other, and then draw a line between them. When you concede a point, put a tally next to the E. When someone loses a round with an Appeal, note how many dice are added by the A. When you're totaling your points at the end just multiple the tallies next to E by 3 and add that to the sum of the numbers by A. This should allow you to quickly jot things down and keep them straight during the Conflict.

Here‚Äôs some areas you can use to Appeal to your enemy and what their corresponding dice might be:

HONOR Your standing can carry you through the day, even if your arguments are weak. The character with the highest Honor gets to apply a free use of their Honor die to their pool.

STRESS When your opponent is overworked and they're at maximum whelm, it's much easier to get them to give in. You can redeem a d4! worth of Leverage for every Stress penalty your opponent has, however, you don't have to redeem all the d4!s all in one round and can spread them out over the course of the Conflict.

EQUIPMENT It may be the case that equipment can sway an argument. Often this is through imitation or torture, but gold can also persuade a hesitant sellsword or the right equipment might convince a thief to take on a risky job. A badge of authority may also win you some influence.

Equipment used for this purpose gains the character a base d8. Equipment that‚Äôs substandard or that‚Äôs being re-purposed from its original use (a knotted rope used to whip a victim or a broken glass bottle used to threaten someone) only nets a d4.

STAT SOCIAL DIE Having an exceptionally high Strength or Dexterity might be relevant to the situation. If that‚Äôs the case, add that Abilities Social Die to your pool.

APPLICABLE SKILLS You may roll an additional die for any applicable Advanced skill based on that Skill‚Äôs Rank. Take the Rank of that Skill and round up to the nearest dice size. So a Rank 5 Skill would net a d6 dice while a Rank 14 Skill would net a d20.

But that is by no means a comprehensive list and plays should be rewarded for out-of-the-box thinking. Appeals start at a d4!, but this can vary. For instance, a very skilled swordsman with a lot of Advanced Training in his weapon of choice makes a more powerful Appeal than a rookie.

You can use the same Appeal multiple times during a single Conflict.

Characteristic as Evidence vs Appeals

There‚Äôs no hard rule that can be given of when a Trait qualifies as one over the other, but there are some guidelines.

Generally, if the person whose Characteristic is being used is not directly involved and they‚Äôre known by both parties, it is Evidence.

If, however, one of the people is involved in the argument, especially if it is defense, or you‚Äôre trying to bring in the Characteristic of someone that the other person doesn‚Äôt know, then it is considered an Appeal.

Evidence as an Appeal

Truthfully, there may be a time where you have Evidence but it's in your best interest to reinforce the idea with emotion that makes it closer to an Appeal than Evidence.

In this case, you treat the Evidence as an Appeal with a base dice pool of your Intellect or Charisma's Social Die, a dice from your Persuasion Skill (if you don't have that skill, you don't get this dice), and a d12! for the argument itself. From there, you can add other Characteristics to the pool.

The GM can say no to a proposal if the counter argument doesn‚Äôt directly relate to the argument, so careful phrasing could limit what your opponent can do. This is usually the indication that the round isn‚Äôt finished yet and the person who was rebuffed can try a different tact or argument.

Bluffing, or in other words, Lying

As per normal, a character only needs to roll a Deception Check if the other person is suspicious of them, but even then, if one side accuses the other of something, trying to get them to reveal a secret, they can use Deception to try and bluff, hiding their actual reaction.

If the person is, in fact, telling the truth but someone is still suspicious of them, they‚Äôd roll a Persuasion Check to convince their accuser of their sincerity.

A strong willed or well disciplined person, when faced with insurmountable odds will often fight and/or argue long after the cowards have run, either gaining victory or assuring his death. Targeting these weaker willed people might also make or break a side. This is represented by a character‚Äôs Panic Threshold.

Check the character‚Äôs Honor to determine their starting Panic Threshold. Record this for your character by circling the Wound Category in your Vitality Section. When you reach that Wound Category, you become Panicked.

Panicked people only gain half the points from Evidence and they half their results from Appeals. When they become Panicked, they roll any Repercussion Dice they gained so far from Appeals and, should they gain more, they roll those immediately.

In combat, a Panicked person can't take aggressive actions: they'll still attack, but if the other side flees, they won't chase them and they'll try to fall back to more defensible positions, if they can.

If a person goes above their Stress Threshold, they immediately lose the Conflict. If they're in combat, they simply turn and flee, unable to face their own morality. Captains can try and rally them with a Check immediately.

If cornered and given no space to run, these routed fighters enter a frenzy - only using aggressive Tactics and becoming immune to Stress penalties.

FAST TALKING

üúÇ Deluxe Rule

There may be times when you don‚Äôt want to/can‚Äôt get into a protracted argument and just want to overwhelm your opponent into agreeing with you. For example, you need to fast talk your way past a guard or convince a henchmen to go first down a trap filled corridor before he remembers he doesn‚Äôt have insurance.

In those cases, you want to fast talk the person.

This is, essentially, a Conflict in a single round: either by showing a blatant, time-sensitive, piece of Evidence (‚ÄúLet me in! The orcs are RIGHT THERE‚Äù) or by making an Appeal that stuns the person with your Charisma.

If the person can‚Äôt match you or beat you, you won them over: for the next 30 minutes or so and then they‚Äôll come calling.

Fast Talking is not a way to permanently get a person on your side - it is a band-aid tactic to buy you a little extra time.

ENDING THE ROUND

Like I said, a round ends when an argument is countered or conceded.

Both sides putting up relevant Evidence is countering the argument and means that neither sides could gain points.

If one side conceded, that means they don‚Äôt really have anything to counter it (either through Evidence or an Appeal) and so they get points as noted above.

These points are kept secret until the end of the Conflict.

Multiple People in a Conflict

You can, of course, have people help out in a variety of support roles during a Conflict but they can often trip over each other, giving the opposition a chance to rebuttal.

While making an Appeal, everyone rolls separately, totaling their results. If this beats the opposition, however, they only gain points equal to the person that rolled the least amount of dice.

So, if you have 3 people, who roll 7, 5, and 1 dice, and they beat the opposition, they only get 1 point.

The opposition also adds one instance of their Charisma Social dice for each person opposing them.

If, however, the company is presenting Evidence, it pretty much works the same, however, if multiple players present multiple pieces of Evidence, the opposition is only obliged to counter one of them to get points and the company only gets the points for one Evidence.

So, if one of the players points out that the person was found at the scene of the crime with the bloody dagger in his hands (a point that the opposition has no counter for) and the other says that the person was also said that the person‚Äôs alibi was disapproved by an associate that ratted him out, the opposition only needs to reply that his associate was clearly lying to get a more forgiving sentence (which is technically a half-truth).

Both sides would only get 3 points from that exchange.

Players may take sidebars, of course, (See the Taking a Break Sidebar below) but they may also use the Warfare Advanced Skill to temporarily pause and discuss with each other.

If you‚Äôre facing multiple people, note that any Evidence has to apply to/be believed by everyone on the opposition to be effective, thus making Evidence less and less helpful in a situation where you have to convince a group or mob.

TRUMP CARDS

There are some arguments that are so good that they can counter multiple points. These are considered trump cards and should be played carefully because they can only counter arguments that your opponent has already put forward.

Trump cards give a number of points equal to their base times the number of points they counter. So, if you put forward Evidence that counters 3 arguments, it would give the other side 9 points, while having a 4 dice Appeal that counts the same number gives you 12 points.

It doesn‚Äôt matter if the arguments have already been counter, in fact, that‚Äôs when you get the most benefit out of trump cards.

ESCALATING A CONFLICT

üúÇ Deluxe Rule

A debate between friends is on a different level than two enemies mudslinging or threatening to cut each other‚Äôs throat if the other doesn‚Äôt listen. This is represented by levels of escalation.

At baseline, the Conflict is simply a battle of ideas, with each side having no person stake in the matter. It is the default and only inflicts Stress.

However, one or either side can make it PERSONAL, which means pride and personal image is starting to get in the way. This happens when one side uses more than 3 Appeals in a Conflict or when one side purposely makes an insulting comment.

When a Conflict becomes Personal, Appeals become more powerful, being worth 1 extra point on a success, however, people often dig their heels into the ground when you start questioning their character, making others things more dangerous.

The first is that you need to not just beat your opponent, but beat his total points by 5 plus a roll of his Honor Dice (which represents his mood).

But, it can be to your benefit: the winner gains Honor equal to how much he beat his opponent by, while the loser loses that much.

Above Personal Conflicts are INTIMIDATION Conflicts. This means that you‚Äôre now threatening personal harm to either the person, or someone the person cares about. You can now bring in that shiny blade or talk about what a shame it would be if that person‚Äôs house burned down.

This is similar to a Personal Conflict, except that, if you lose and you don‚Äôt then escalate to violence and follow through on your threats, you lose face, losing 5 Honor.

But, if you escalate to violence and also get your buttkicked, you lose 10 Honor so if you‚Äôre going to start threatening people, make sure you either win or that you‚Äôre as tough as you say you are.

You can jump up levels, going from a standard Conflict to intimidating them in a single round, however, you can‚Äôt de-escalate.

PUSHING THE POINT

When one side feels they may have the upper hand and that the other has run out of arguments, they can try to push the point, or end the Conflict. He simply tries to push the other side to make a decision.

If it turns out that the other person does still have more to say, the Conflict continues and the pusing person gains 1 point.

If, however, it turns out that the pushed person does, in fact, have no more to say, than the Conflict ends

Taking a Break

It may take days or weeks to sway someone, making sure you know them, their likes and dislikes, and that you‚Äôve got all your bases covered.

In this way, it is perfectly acceptable, should it make sense, that you suspend the Conflict, possibly indefinitely.

Folding is what happens when you want to try and give them a lie or are not planning on doing what the winner wants. In this case, you give in early.

If your opponent pushes, you can choose to Fold by saying that you have no more arguments, but if the other person suspects that you‚Äôre Folding they can try and call you on it, in which case it's an opposed Deception Check if you are and a Persuasion Check if you‚Äôre not.

The Persuasion Check is to make it harder to meta-game whether or not you're bluffing.

If you are Folding and you fail the Deception check, the Conflict continues.

Ending The Conflict

At the end of the Conflict, both sides reveal their points. The one with the least points gets their way.
