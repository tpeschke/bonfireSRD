Your character is a multi-faceted representation of a fictional persona. She has her hopes and her dreams, her strengths and her weakness. All of these add up to hopefully make someone that has a compelling story of victory over her flaws, or defeat from the same.

In game play terms, these are represented by 5 characteristics:

GOALS represent what your character is trying to achieve.

TRAITS represent what sets your character apart from other characters.

DEVOTIONS represent their relationships, either with people, organizations, or otherworldly powers.

REPUTATION represent how other people see them - what are they rumored to have done or what kind of people do others think they are.

FLAWS are also considered Characteristics so they're back!

These characteristics will be provided dice that will be used in Confrontations addressed below and represents how strong it is, with the bigger the dice, the better it is.

Goals

Characters can start with up to 3 Goals, but needs to have at least one at all times. They receive Experience and Honor, as well as increasing the other 3 Characteristics, as you progress along the path to completing them.

What does you character want? Why can’t they get it? These are the two fundamental questions that should be answered for each Goal.

Overlapping Characteristics

You may think you’re pretty smart by having a Trait and Devotion both be the same thing so that you always get to roll them at the same time. However, consider that you’re putting your eggs in one basket and it would be worth it to vary it a little to cover more bases, allowing you to use them more often and perhaps leveraging characteristics to bring in others.

Ideally, they should include other party members and the overarching plot of the campaign.

These goals can be anything from the incorporeal (“I want to find a home” or “I want to deal with this anger inside me”) to the very concrete (“I want to find my father’s sword” or “I want to stop this invasion”).

The best goals are those that are measurable and obtainable.

Being motivated by a Goal allows you to push yourself beyond what you thought was possible. When a Goal applies to a Confrontation, when you lose an Appeal, you take the second highest dice result in Stress, instead of the highest.

Starting Characteristics

If you’re bringing in a new character to a new campaign, it’s highly probable that you won’t know what the game is going to be about, especially if the GM wants to keep it a secret.

It’s perfectly acceptable to leave your Goals, Traits, and/or Devotions blank until you’ve got your feet under you but you should have at least a few Medium term goals and your strongest Trait filled out relating to your character’s personal arch.

Traits are things that stick out about your character. Things that people tend to notice about them or that the character defines his identity by. These can be something specific (“I have blonde hair”) or something general (“I have an intimidating demeanor”) or somewhere in between (“I’m an orc”).

Traits don’t have to be physical in nature. It could be how your character views himself (“I’m confident” or “I’m good at math”). They can also be something about your character that he just can’t explain (“I’ve always been good with dogs” or “I can read people easily”).

The sky's the limit when it comes to Traits, so go wild, though they can't have exceptions. So, "I'm an angry person" works but "I'm an angry person but I don't let it affect my work" doesn't.

You start off with 5 Traits of varying levels: 2 weak, 2 average, and 1 strong. These are represented by dice of varying size. How strong these Traits are depends on your Charisma. So, if you have a 13 Charisma, you gain 5 Traits - two at d4, one 2d6, one 2d8, and one at 3d8

While naturally Charismatic characters come out of the gate with strong personalities, as you adventure, your character will also grow and change, gaining and strengthening their Traits. This can be done by either increasing your Charisma, which also raises your Traits, or by buying Traits up directly.

Thinking of Traits

Thinking of Traits on the fly is hard. For new players, there's two ways of handling this: either picture a character from fiction you like or find interesting, and try to describe them. These are their Traits and you can crib off them to start with.

Or if you have no idea where to start or are just a risk taker, you can use the Trait Generator, which will give you a random Trait. These Traits may not always make sense but they can often serve as a jumping off point for your imagination.

INCREASING TRAITS WITH CrP

If you want to increase your Traits further, you can spend CrP to give them a boost. This represents your character spending time developing their personality rather than spending time learning other things.

Increasing a Trait costs 10 + 4 for each time you've increased the dice.

You can also buy new Traits for 10 CrP.

So, for example, if you want to increase a Medium Trait from 2d6 to 2d8, it would cost 14 CrP because you've only increased it once, from a d4 to a d6.

Please note that you can't buy a Trait past a d12

Racial Temperament

Your Racial Temperament also tends to come into play fairly regularly during social situations. Like a Trait, you can add its dice in an Appeal. But what is the dice size? Well, it's your Honor Dice.

So, basically, if your Racial Temperament applies, you get your Honor Dice that round. Pretty easy, pretty straightforward.

Devotions

The final aspect are the Devotions that your character has. What relationships does he have? What organizations does your character associate with? Is he religious and, if so, is his loyalty more to his cult or to his god? How does he feel about that thing?

Relationships between individuals don’t need to be reciprocal or even contemporary. A young fighter may be inspired by a girl who doesn't love him back or by a long dead king he greatly admires. These are still viable candidates for his Devotion.

Everyone receives two free Devotions: one to their character’s clan/family and another of their choice. Each Devotion comes with a "relationship verb" and the thing itself. So, his clan Devotion could be "Hate Clan" or "Have mixed feelings about Clan" or "Loyal to Clan"

These Devotions start a 2.

Devotions are a more powerful driving force than other Characteristics, meaning that, if a Devotion applies, you automatically subtract 1 point for every point you have in it. So, if you approach a blood enemy, trying to convince him to help you, even if you’re being entirely reasonable, it’ll be much harder to do so.

Reputation

Reputation, in a nutshell, is what your character is known for and how other people see them. So, if you want to convince someone that you’ll keep your word, having a Reputation for doing so will help you out.

Reputation takes the form of 3 incomplete sentences starting with “I’m known for...”. Each time you level up, your GM will finish or change out one or more of those sentences and assign points to them that represent their strength. She’ll choose how to finish them based on how your character has been acting or the results of their actions.

That’s right: just like in real life, you don’t really have control over your Reputation, though there is a way to influence it as you go.

So, for instance, Banwulf the fighter is a good hearted warrior who wants to protect the people of the town. After a raid, however, he finds himself in possession of a number of raider women and children.

The town’s leader, a highly influential person, tells him that he needs to put the captives to the sword since they’ll only grow up to continue the cycle of violence against the town but Banwulf demurs and, in the middle of the night, he releases them so they can escape.

Banwulf, of course, receives Honor for his decision, but at level up, the GM completes one of his Reputation sentences. He becomes known for betraying his friends. This is because the town, who he previously protected, feels betrayed at his actions.

In a Confrontation, Reputation counts as a form of Evidence, but instead of getting the standard 3 points, you instead get the Reputation’s strength in points. It can be combine with other Evidence or as a d8 if you’re using it to make an Appeal.

Flaws

Let's circle back to Flaws because they are considered part of your Confrontation Characteristics, after all, they make up who your character is as well. You start with your Flaws giving you 1d4! + your current total Stress you have in Confrontations should you bring them in.

Using your Flaws in such a way is extremely relaxing and so removes any Stress gained during that Confrontation.

When To Use These Rules

These rules may not be appropriate to break out every time two people talk to each other, or even disagree on something. They’re more of a framework that help you track how compelling a side is and, you’ll find, that you can quietly use these rules in the background as your players roleplay.

While there are other reasons to start a Confrontation, they’ll mainly be started by you for a specific purpose: things situations are covered by the Game Master’s Guide so what we’ll talk about here is when to know when a player-initiated Confrontation should happen and that’s fairly easy.

Think of your NPCs in terms of their GOALS and METHODS. Goals are pretty obvious and map one-to-one with the Goal Characteristics but Methods are how they get things done based on their Characteristics. Methods are kind of the NPC’s morality.

If the NPC feels like what the player is asking them to do aligns with their Goals and their Methods, no Confrontation happens: they don’t need to be convinced.

If, on the other hand, the Goal aligns but the Methods do or if the Methods align but the Goal doesn’t, a Confrontation is a great tool for determining whether the NPC agrees.

And if neither Goal nor Method aligns, no Confrontation happens: The NPC can’t be convinced.

Of course, if you think it’s reasonable, the players can enter into a Confrontation to convince the NPC that their Goals and Methods align, but that’s up to you and how much the players are trying to abuse it.

Playing Out The Confrontation

The Confrontation is done in a series of back and forths called “rounds”, in which each person is trying to get the other person to back down. This involves an argument-counterargument structure.

A round starts when someone puts forth an argument and ends when the other person feels they’ve countered it or they’ve had to concede, so a round can be multiple exchanges with each side talking back and forth, asking and answering questions.

Both sides begin with points equal to any applicable Devotions they have, with the side who didn’t starting with an extra 1 point.

Arguments come in two flavors: Evidence and Appeals.

EVIDENCE

Evidence is facts: things that are believed to be true, even if they’re not true in actuality.

These are flexes in power: I’m the king and so do you a favor if you do this for me.

These are also things you know about the other person, or subjects involved: if you know that a person is caring or has a temper, you can use that against them as Evidence.

If your opponent concedes to your Evidence, you get 3 points, otherwise no one gets anything. Note that the only thing your Evidence needs to be considered counted by your opponent is the fact that your opponent believes it, honestly.

So, while a normal person has to concede to the sky being blue, a color blind one could counter with the fact that the sky isn’t blue to them and that’s valid.

Evidence is the surest way of convincing your opponent but it also tends to be more work: you have to know about your opponent and know their situation.

Generally, this is signaled by placing a finger up in the air or pressing it against the table.

APPEALS

Appeals are appeals to a person’s emotions, their sense of self, or their Honor.

Traits are often (but not always) used this way. It can sometimes be a jab at someone’s character or an insinuation of skill, through bragging about how good of a climber you are or maybe displaying your weapon in a threatening manner. It can also be how famous your character is.

Appeals vary in the number of points they get you.

Versus Evidence, Appealing is considered to be talking past someone to go straight for the emotions. The Appeal gives them as many points as dice you put up but it also gives them 3 points. You'll still roll your dice, taking the lowest dice result as Stress, noting if you only took 1 Stress.

Versus another Appeal, each person rolls the dice assigned to the Characteristic they’re using.

The person who rolls the highest total gets that many points and Stress equal to their lowest dice result.

The other person gets nothing but the Stress from their highest dice result.

Either way, note if you only took 1 Stress this way. This makes this a sound move for very charismatic people but it is still risky.

This is signaled by placing the dice in front of you, or holding them up, though you’re not obliged to reveal how many or their sizes.

Tracking Points

Each side is responsible for their own points but to make it easier, you can use the E/A method.

Write an E and A on top of each other, and then draw a line between them. When you gain points through Evidence, put a single tally next to the E.

When you win an Appeal, note how many dice are added by the A.

When you're totaling your points at the end just multiple the tallies next to E by 3 and add that to the sum of the numbers by A. This should allow you to quickly jot things down and keep them straight during the Confrontation.

Here’s some areas you can use to Appeal to your enemy and what their corresponding dice might be:

HONOR Your standing can carry you through the day, even if your arguments are weak. The character with the highest Honor gets to apply a free use of their Honor die to their pool.

STRESS When your opponent is overworked and they're at maximum whelm, it's much easier to get them to give in. You can redeem a d4! worth of Leverage for every Stress penalty your opponent has, however, you don't have to redeem all the d4!s all in one round and can spread them out over the course of the Confrontation.

EQUIPMENT It may be the case that equipment can sway an argument. Often this is through imitation or torture, but gold can also persuade a hesitant sellsword or the right equipment might convince a thief to take on a risky job. A badge of authority may also win you some influence.

Equipment used for this purpose gains the character a base d8. Equipment that’s substandard or that’s being re-purposed from its original use (a knotted rope used to whip a victim or a broken glass bottle used to threaten someone) only nets a d4.

STAT CONFRONTATION DIE Having an exceptionally high Strength or Dexterity might be relevant to the situation. If that’s the case, add that Abilities Confrontation Die to your pool.

APPLICABLE SKILLS You may roll an additional die for any applicable Advanced skill based on that Skill’s Rank. Take the Rank of that Skill and round up to the nearest dice size. So a Rank 5 Skill would net a d6 dice while a Rank 14 Skill would net a d20.

But that is by no means a comprehensive list and plays should be rewarded for out-of-the-box thinking. Appeals start at a d4!, but this can vary. For instance, a very skilled swordsman with a lot of Advanced Training in his weapon of choice makes a more powerful Appeal than a rookie.

You can use the same Appeal multiple times during a single Confrontation.

Characteristic as Evidence vs Appeals

There’s no hard rule that can be given of when a Trait qualifies as one over the other, but there are some guidelines.

Generally, if the person whose Characteristic is being used is not directly involved and they’re known by both parties, it is Evidence.

If, however, one of the people is involved in the argument, especially if it is defense, or you’re trying to bring in the Characteristic of someone that the other person doesn’t know, then it is considered an Appeal.

Evidence as an Appeal

Truthfully, there may be a time where you have Evidence but it's in your best interest to reinforce the idea with emotion that makes it closer to an Appeal than Evidence.

In this case, you treat the Evidence as an Appeal with a base dice pool of your Intellect or Charisma's Confrontation Die, a dice from your Articulation Skill (if you don't have that skill, you don't get this dice), and your Honor Die for the argument itself. From there, you can add other Characteristics to the pool.

The GM can say no to a proposal if the counter argument doesn’t directly relate to the argument, so careful phrasing could limit what your opponent can do. This is usually the indication that the round isn’t finished yet and the person who was rebuffed can try a different tact or argument.

Bluffing, or in other words, Lying

As per normal, a character only needs to roll a Deception Check if the other person is suspicious of them, but even then, if one side accuses the other of something, trying to get them to reveal a secret, they can use Deception to try and bluff, hiding their actual reaction.

If the person is, in fact, telling the truth but someone is still suspicious of them, they’d roll a Articulation Check to convince their accuser of their sincerity.

ENDING THE ROUND

Like I said, a round ends when an argument is countered or conceded.

Quickly jot down the points gained that round and move on.

These points are kept secret until the end of the Confrontation.

Multiple People in a Confrontation

You can, of course, have people help out in a variety of support roles during a Confrontation but they can often trip over each other, giving the opposition a chance to rebuttal.

The big thing to note is that, if you’re not careful, you can step on each other’s toes as the opposition can choose which argument to focus on if many are provided, allowing them to peak the weakest one.

So, if multiple emotional appeals are presented, he can pick the lowest result.

This doesn’t mean that those arguments are wasted: they can be reused later in the Confrontation and, in fact, seeing which arguments the opposition chooses to ignore might give you some clues about where their argument is weakest.

Players may take sidebars, of course, (See the Taking a Break Sidebar below) but they may also use the Warfare Advanced Skill to temporarily pause and discuss with each other.

If you’re facing multiple people, note that any Evidence has to apply to/be believed by everyone on the opposition to be effective, thus making Evidence less and less helpful in a situation where you have to convince a group or mob.

TRUMP CARDS

There are some arguments that are so good that they can counter multiple points. These are considered trump cards and should be played carefully because they can only counter arguments that your opponent has already put forward.

Trump cards give a number of points equal to their base times the number of arguments they counter.

So, if you put forward Evidence that counters 3 arguments, it would give you 9 points, while having a 4 dice Appeal that counts against the same number gives you 12 points.

It doesn’t matter if the arguments have already been counter, in fact, that’s when you get the most benefit out of trump cards.

PUSHING THE POINT

When one side feels they may have the upper hand and that the other has run out of arguments, they can try to push the point, or end the Confrontation. He simply tries to push the other side to make a decision.

If it turns out that the other person does still have more to say, the Confrontation continues, gaining 1 point.

If, however, it turns out that the pushed person does, in fact, have no more to say, than the Confrontation ends

Taking a Break

It may take days or weeks to sway someone, making sure you know them, their likes and dislikes, and that you’ve got all your bases covered.

In this way, it is perfectly acceptable, should it make sense, that you suspend the Confrontation, possibly indefinitely.

Ending The Confrontation

At the end of the Confrontation, both sides reveal their points. The one with the most points gets their way.

Repercussions Of A Confrontation

If you gained less than your Wisdom score + your total level in Stress from Appeals, the character suffers only a short-term effect.

If the total is greater than that, the character suffers a long term effect. Choose one of the following:

🜂

Reduce the dice size of one of their Traits, Devotions by 1

🜂

Lose a dice from one of their Traits

🜂

Lose a Trait, or Devotion

If you rolled any 1s (which you should have noted), the character is also changed for the better. Most of the time, it doesn’t matter how many 1s you roll, you only choose one of the following:

🜂

Gain a Devotion at 1, or Trait at d4

🜂

Add a dice to one of their Traits

🜂

Receive a temporary d20! to your next conflict. You may gain an additional dice per extra 1 you rolled

🜂

Increase the dice size of 1 of their Characteristics by 1 for each time you rolled a 1 on your Appeal Stress dice, up to the number of dice associated with that Characteristic and up to a d12!.
