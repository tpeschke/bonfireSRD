Your character is a multi-faceted representation of a fictional persona. She has her hopes and her dreams, her strengths and her weakness. All of these add up to hopefully make someone that has a compelling story of victory over her flaws, or defeat from the same.

In game play terms, these are represented by 4 characteristics:

GOALS represent what your character is trying to achieve.

TRAITS represent what sets your character apart from other characters.

PASSIONS represent interests and things that get your character‚Äôs blood pumping.

DEVOTIONS represent their relationships, either with people, organizations, or otherworldly powers.

These characteristics will be provided dice that will be used in social conflicts addressed below and represents how strong it is, with the bigger the dice, the better it is.

GOALS

Characters start out with 6 Goals; 3 Short term, 2 Medium, and 1 Long. They receive Experience and Honor as well as increasing the other 3 social characteristics as you progress along the path to completing them.

What does you character want? Why can‚Äôt they get it? These are the two fundamental questions that should be answered for each Goal.

Traits are things that stick out about your character. Things that people tend to notice about them or that the character defines his identity by. These can be something specific (‚ÄúI have blonde hair‚Äù) or something general (‚ÄúI have an intimidating demeanor‚Äù) or somewhere in between (‚ÄúI‚Äôm an orc‚Äù).

The sky's the limit when it comes to Traits, so go wild.

You start off with 6 Traits of varying levels: 3 weak, 2 average, and 1 strong. These are represented by dice of varying size. How strong these Traits are depends on your Charisma. So, if you have a 13 Charisma, you gain 6 Traits - three at d4, one 3d6, one 3d8, and one at 4d8

While naturally Charismatic characters come out of the gate with strong personalities, as you adventure, your character will also grow and change, gaining and strengthening their Traits.

PASSIONS

What does your character do in their downtime? What‚Äôre their hobbies and what do they spend their time thinking about? These are their Passions.

Your number of Passions and their strength is based on your character's Intellect.

If your character selects a Passion related to one of their Adv Skills, bump up the dice size by 1 for ever 4 Ranks you have in that Skill (rounded up).

DEVOTIONS

The final aspect are the Devotions that your character has. What relationships does he have? What organizations does your character associate with? Is he religious and, if so, is his loyalty more to his cult or to his god?

Relationships between individuals don‚Äôt need to be reciprocal or even contemporary. A young fighter may be inspired by a girl who doesn't love him back or by a long dead king he greatly admires. These are still viable candidates for his Devotion.

Everyone receives two free Devotions: one to their character‚Äôs clan/family and another of their choice.

Servant Archetypes gain an additional Devotion which is to their god or cult.

All Devotions start at 1d6 but you can increase the dice size by 1 for 5 CrP. These dice can be increased up to a d12. You may also purchase additional Devotions for 10 CrP.

Facing the Powers that Be

There may come a time when you‚Äôre facing someone that‚Äôs related to your Devotions. This may be the leader of your family, a higher up in your cult, or the person that your Devotion is centered around trying to get you to do something.

In these case, your Devotion makes you more likely to follow them without question and so they can add your Devotion die to their social dice pool when in conflict with them.

WHEN TO USE THESE RULES

These rules may not be appropriate to break out everytime two people talk to each other, or even disagree on something. If you think your character can make a compelling argument that would convince an NPC to change his ways or give him a discount, don‚Äôt bother getting your dice out.

Additionally, if the GM thinks that what the player is asking isn‚Äôt probable, they can turn them away at the door. Sure, it‚Äôs possible that the blacksmith with give you that sword for free, but is it probable? Absolutely not.

However, it is probable that he‚Äôs at least willing to give you a discount. The other person must also see a possible benefit to engaging with you.

For the blacksmith giving away his sword, there really isn‚Äôt one. However, if you Conflict over a discount, that gives him a window to upsell you if you lose the Conflict. That might be worth it to him.

And so setting the stage for a Conflict is often helping the NPC see what they could possibly get from you should they win.

Other Reasons to Start a Conflict

There may be other reasons why you feel a Conflict is appropriate. This may be the case of a demon who forces his will on another‚Äôs demon or, in a more mundane case, in the case of someone who wants to tell the player a secret but also may not trust them entirely or doubt the wisdom of doing so.

There may also be certain circumstances where one person is forced into a Conflict, most often through magical means, but this is most often done as part of a plot point. More information is found in the Conflict as a Plot Point in Bonfire Advanced.

STARTING the CONFLICT

The characters have to decide what‚Äôs at stake; this is what they‚Äôre butting heads about and why they‚Äôre willing to put themselves on the line. So, for instance, a blacksmith and a fighter want to get in a conflict over the price of a sword. That‚Äôs what‚Äôs at stake. The fighter wants its a discount, the blacksmith wants to make a bigger profit.

Then each player rolls a single Trait‚Äôs worth of dice, representing their starting tactic, and any additional Situational Dice that are applicable. These are primarily from their Goals, Passions, and Devotions but can also be from their Flaws, Honor, equipment, or anything else the character might feel is applicable. Characters can know how many dice the other side is rolling but not the results of the role.

The player who initiated the conflict goes first, putting forward two dice and giving their total.

PLAYING OUT the CONFLICT

Each character goes back and forth, ponying up two die from their pre-rolled dice pool and roleplaying their arguments based on the die they use. The two dice‚Äôs rolled sum has to either match or exceed the rolled sum of the last dice put forward.

These dice represent their arguments and counterarguments and are coupled with roleplaying it out. What is said in-character should be reflective of the social characteristic that brought the dice in.

If the character can only match with two dice, he also puts 1 Honor Point on the table. His argument was weak and didn‚Äôt have the force behind it that it should have, and others noticed.

If a character can‚Äôt exceed the sum with just two dice, he can either pass (ending the conflict) or he has to put in additional dice forward to raise his total above his opponent‚Äôs. Once totalled, the number of extra dice that were needed are put aside for later.

A few things happen at the end of a conflict. The first is that the winner gets to decide what happens with the stakes. The winner receives an amount of Honor equal to the difference between what he put on the table and his opponent put on the table. This can end up being a negative sum. The loser deducts all the Honor they put on the table from his total. More on this is found in Chapter 6: Character Vitals under the Changing Honor Category section.
