STRESS

Working under pressure can get to you. When your crew mates are relying on you to pull out a win, it can mess with your head. Your character can become stressed under these circumstances and stress can later manifest in odd behaviors.

Characters can gain Stress in two ways in a lot of ways and those are discussed in their various sections - taking damage, stressing on checks, etc. - the only one we‚Äôll go over here is Stress can be gained (and healed) from acting out your character‚Äôs social Characteristics, especially when not playing them would be a huge benefit.

STRESS PENALTIES

Stress affects almost everything you do, making your life harder and your character becomes emotionally pent up.

Every 10 points of Stress gives you a penalty of 1 to Attack, Defense, Skill Check difficulties, Running and Sprinting time.

Stress affects Morale, lowering the character's Morale Threshold by 1 per 1 Stress.

HEALING STRESS

Stress heals slowly over time but there are things you can do to speed it up. You have to actively go out and do things that relax your character. There are several things you can do.

The first is to give into his nature. By role playing his Flaws in a detrimental way, a character can relieve 1 or more Stress (GM‚Äôs option). This means that when a character sees a situtation where his Stress could give him a disadvantage, he can bring it up the GM. If approved, the character heals Stress. The GM may also suggest this option as well if she things the situation warrents it.

The second way is through hobbies. Hobbies are based off your character's Social Characteristics, but besides that, they can be anything, from whoring it up to spending time with the family or working in their shop.

You heal 1 Stress for each hour spent on a hobby, though you can only spend up to the number of dice that Characteristic has in hours per day.

Finally, you can indulge in creature comforts. This can be done by by buying a nice bedroll or even hiring someone to set up your camp for you so you can relax at the end of the day. The rule of thumb is that each +1 heals you an additional Stress when applicable. So, a +3 bedroll will always do you good but a +3 tent will really only give you a bonus in inclement weather.

Also, as an aside here; the Musician and Cooking/Baking Advanced Skills also help heal Stress, so you can look into those.

MENTAL BREAKS

Rolling over 20 - 1 for every 10 full points of Stress you have on any skill check gives you a temporary Flaw, as your character starts to crack. This can happen whether or not the skill check succeeds or fails.

When this happens, you roll on the Mental or Behavioral Flaws table. You can modify this roll by adding an amount of Stress equal to up to half your current Stress level. Any Stress spent this way is subtracted from your total.

You can then choose either the Flaw you rolled, one that is lower, or one that is higher but if you don‚Äôt choose the one your rolled, it has to be one that your character has had before (as old habits resurface).

If you choose one lower than what you rolled, that Flaw becomes permanent and must be paid off but for half cost.

If you choose the one you rolled or one higher up on the list, it‚Äôs only temporary and lasts until all your Stress is healed.

It‚Äôs still pretty risky, however. Should your character drop to Bleeding Out, the near-death experience makes one temporary Flaw permanent. You never gain CrP from Flaws gotten this way.

VITALITY

Vitality represents how close you are to passing out and dying. It is both your health and fatigue. As you take damage, you will get closer to death and gain more penalties.

Vitality is divided into 5 categories, representing what percentage of damage a character has taken:

TIRED

To calculate how much Vitality you have for each category, add a roll of your Primary Archetype‚Äôs Vitality Die to your Constitution + their Vitality Size Modifier. That‚Äôs your total. Divide that by 4. If you have a remainder, alternate between rounding down on every category except the Wounded category.

So, if you‚Äôre a Human with and your Vitality Die is a d10 and you roll a 7 with a Constitution of 12, you have 29 Vitality. You‚Äôre Tired at 28, Hurt at 22, Bloodied at 15, and Wounded 8.

Record your Vitality Die roll on your character sheet.

DROPPING A VITALITY CATEGORY & ENCUMBRANCE

As stated, the more damage you take, the harder the fight becomes. When you drop a Vitality Category, you gain an amount of Stress equal to your Encumbrance.

Your Encumbrance is equal to your Con Encumb. + Wis Encumb. + Encumb. from armor and shields + however many times you‚Äôre over your carry limit.

CHANGING ENCUMBRANCE WHILE WOUNDED The Stress you gain from wounds isn't healed by normal means; it's only healed when either your Encumbrance is lowered or when your wounds heal enough to move you up a category.

That means, if you took 10 Stress dropping from a Vitality Category, taking a spa day wouldn't help. Instead, you should see a surgeon or a doctor. Now, what you could do is not carry so much gear around.

So, for example, let's say that you have a base Encumbrance of 10 from your Abilities but you're carrying around only a Light Shield, so your actual Encumbrance is 16. You drop from Fresh to Hurt, and you gain 32 Stress because of it. You're in the middle of combat, so you can't Take a Breather, so you drop your shield, your Encumbrance drops to 10 and you heal 12 Stress, which isn't bad. After the battle, you take a moment to catch your breathe and so you're now only Tired. Since your Encumbrance is now 10 so you heal 10 Stress.

Now, the flip side of this is that if you become MORE Encumbered while wounded, you gain that amount of Stress instantly. So, continuing the above example, if you were to then pick up your Light Shield again, your Encumbrance would raise by 6 and you'd gain 6 Stress.

PASSING OUT All characters pass out when they start Bleeding Out, but it is possible that they'll pass out before then. If your remaining Vitality is greater than or equal to your Stress Penalty, your character collapses, helpless but not actively bleeding out. In a sense, he's forced to Take a Breather. or remain helpless.

So, if you've got 30 Stress and 29 Vitality, when you've taken 25 damage, you're character collapses, forced to Take a Breather.

Wound Category Exception

Just as an aside - going from Fresh to Tired doesn‚Äôt count as changing a Wound Category for purposes of triggering a Trauma check.

TAKING A BREATHER

Pausing and not doing anything but move at a Stroll while not Threatened for 20 Seconds - Con, minimum 3 Seconds, heals you up to the bottom of the previous category and the amount of Stress you took the last time you dropped. Damage healed this way is taken from the smallest non-long term fatigue wound. You can‚Äôt take another breather until you drop another category.

If you become Threatened while taking a breather but continue to try and catch your breath, you take 1 Stress for every second you remain so.

If you‚Äôre attacked and still definitely rest, you reduce your defense dice by 1 size. If they miss, you‚Äôre breather is unaffected, though in either case, the attacker loses an Honor.

HEALING

Combat is dangerous. Even if you survive, you could end up with wounds that you carry with you for months on end.

Each time damage is done, it is tracked in a new wound.

Wounds heals at a slow rate. Each point takes a number of days equal to the damage done to heal it 1 point.

So, a 5 point wound takes 5 days to become a 4 point wound, which takes 4 days to becomes a 3 point wound, and so on. That means it takes 15 days to completely heal ( 5 + 4 + 3 + 2 + 1).

Thankfully, each wound heals simultaneously. So, if you have 3 separate 4 point wounds, you‚Äôll be completely healed by day 10.

CHANGING HONOR CATEGORY

The GM rewards or docks Honor points during the session based on a character's action: how true are they to their Social Characteristics? Was the player true to the stated core of their character or did they do something simply based on its expedency? If the infraction was particularly grievious, the GM may opt to just drop the player an Honor Categroy immediately, though the player should get a warning if that's the case.

But primarily, players should gain and lose Honor from Conflicts and completing Goals. Completing Goals give a character standing as they become known as someone that can get stuff done. You gain 1 Honor for each benchmark you complete or help complete during the session, with an additional bonus of 5 for the completion of a Short Term Goal, 10 for a Medium Term Goal, and jumping to the bottom of the next highest Honor Category for the completion of a Long Term Goal. If they abandon a Goal, they lose 1 Honor, regardless of the circumstances because, unfortunantely, context is the first thing that gets forgotten.

At the end of each session, each player totals the amount of Honor points they gained or lost during that session. If a character‚Äôs total is higher than their current level, they move up one category. If their total is negative at all, they move down one.

HONOR CATEGORIES & BONUSES

Honor has its benefits. Confidence allows you to push yourself a little bit more than you otherwise would. Compare your character‚Äôs Honor against the following chart to determine which category they fall under and what bonuses they receive.

DISHONOR The lowest category, this person is unsure that they have what it takes to be a hero and so every decision is laced with hesitation and uncertainty.

A character in this category takes a -1 penalty to each dice he rolls.

LOW HONOR This character hasn‚Äôt really proved anything, one way or another. Others and herself are waiting for her to make her mark on the world.

Being in Low Honor gives you a d4! Honor Dice.

AVERAGE HONOR Having reached this category, the character has shown that they have potential and some follow through.

Being in Average Honor gives you a d8! Honor Dice and 1 free reroll.

HIGH HONOR She has now separated herself from the common rabble and can stand a head above the crowd.

Being in High Honor gives you a d12! Honor Dice and 2 free reroll.

GREAT HONOR This rare sliver represents those who can perfectly balance being ahead of others but not so much so that they invoke the ire of those below them as well as being confident while not being cocky.

A character in this category gains a +1 to every dice roll they make for the rest of the session. Additionally, they gain a d20! + d4! for their Honor Dice and 3 free rerolls.

TOO MUCH HONOR There‚Äôs a certain point where people get too big for their britches. When this happen, people tend to go out of their way to take a chunk out of them. They‚Äôre labelled smart asses or show offs. This people also tend to be overconfident and get themselves into bad situations because of it.

Being Too Much Honor gives you a d20! Honor Dice and 1 free reroll.

REDEEMING YOUR BONUSES

There are 3 bonuses you gain from having Honor:

The first is your Honor Dice . When redeemed, a roll of the Honor Dice can be added (or subtracted) at anytime to another roll (such as damage, or attack), temporarily to a static stat (such as Vitality or DR), or a dice pool (such as a Conflict). Adding it to a ‚Äònat 1‚Äô on a defense, negates a fumble.

While Honor Dice can only be used once per session, you can also split it up. So, if you have a d10! Honor Dice, you can instead roll a d2 and have a d8! left over, which you can later roll as a d8! or a d2 and a d6! or a d3! and a d4!, or any combination, as long as the total sides adds up to less than the highest side of the original Dice.

The second is your free reroll , the number of which is determined by your Honor category. You may use a reroll after you‚Äôve made the roll and done your totals but not after you hear the result of the total. So, a character can reroll their defense, after realizing it only totalled to 5, but not after he hears that his attack actually misses because of that total. Additionally, rerolls can be used for rolls the GM has made, however they can‚Äôt be used to reroll Honor Dice.

The last is a Divine Intervention. The Divines look favorably on the Honorable and are willing to alter fate to save a favorite, though this isn't exactly the Honorable route. When used, you take -1 to your Honor but gain you gain your full Honor Dice to anything. So, if you have a d8 and call upon Divine Intervention, you just add 8 to any roll or temporarily to any static number, but you have a greater chance of falling an Honor Category at the end of the session. This counts as redeeming your Honor Dice, however.

You may also use this on any extra Honor Dice gained from your race or Archetype abilities

If you run out of rerolls and are in a bad way, you may drop an Honor Category for an additional one, down to the Dishonorable Category.

The bonus/penalty afforded by Great Honor or Dishonor is automatically redeemed at the beginning of the session and doesn‚Äôt drop you a category at all.

However, if you‚Äôre not in Dishonor and you take its penalty onto you willingly at the beginning of the session, you double all Honor gained at the end.

A good majority of people want to be associated with heroes and legends, even the bad ones. Charles Manson gets love letters everyday, after all. Famous people soon find themselves surrounded by people willing to help for their 15 minutes. Some of these people may actually end up being useful in the long term. So, it‚Äôs important to track you Fame or try to increase it, if you can.

GAINING & LOSING FAME

You gain Fame by doing things. This is primarily from completing Goals and Benchmarks but it can be from completing quests and simply bragging about your exploits in the local tavern.

The reason that Goals gain you more Fame than if you were just running around doing random fetch quests is because completing Goals creates a story that people want to talk about. Sure, they can talk about you slaying that dragon, but if it‚Äôs also the dragon that kidnapped your sister, that‚Äôs much more compelling and worth gossiping about at the local watering hole.

You lose Fame by not doing things. Every month your character doesn‚Äôt do anything new, people talk about her less and less. They lose 1 point of Fame for every month they don‚Äôt complete at least 1 Benchmark.

FAME & HONOR

Honor and Fame have a close tie as the more people talk about you, the more your reputation, whether good or ill, gets around.

At the end of each session, for every 10 points of Fame a character has, they gain an additional Honor point, but this is only added at the end of the session if they don‚Äôt end with a negative Honor pool. So, a character with 70 Fame that ends with -3 Honor, drops an Honor Category at the end of the session.

ATTRACTING NPCS

While a character may use the Recruiting skill to find hirelings at anytime, after a character reaches 50 Fame, they start having people seek them out. At that point, and at every 10 (or 25 for those with a Magical Archetype) Fame above that, the GM rolls on the Follower Attraction table.

These come in three varieties:

CAMP FOLLOWERS These are those that wish to swim in the wake of the big fish, often profiting off the other followers of the party and are often washerwoman, prostitutes, or errand boys. They don‚Äôt ask anything of the party but are happy to be hired on for specialty jobs.

They‚Äôre also the least loyal, abandoning the party should the party start to lose Retainers and they‚Äôll never put their lives on the line for the party.

RETAINERS Retainers make up the paid help. They often seek out the party directly and have a basic skill. The idea is that the party will help them further their craft and connections for a bit of pay. They‚Äôll make themselves as helpful as possible - such as repairing armor and weapons for the cost of room and board.

Retainers won‚Äôt break contract unless the captain of the party falls into Low Honor and will be more loyal than camp followers, however, they‚Äôll only risk their lives if it‚Äôs in their contract to do so.

SIDEKICKS Sidekicks are something special. A Sidekick is a person who is completely loyal to a character. They would lay down their lives for a character. A character has a limited number of Sidekicks that he can call upon over the course of his whole life time.

Sidekicks are childhood friends, people who deify the character, or other such unquestionably loyal followers.

A Sidekicks is essentially a second Player Character that the player gets to run.

Any new follower can be declared by the player to be a Sidekick, having sought out the party, he is recognized as an old friend or a new friend who swears undying loyalty to the character. Additionally, a follower can be elevated to the status of a Sidekick if he or the character saves the other‚Äôs life.

Even before 50 Fame, a character can search out his Sidekicks, however, they must be old friends from his past, in this case.

Sidekicks start with an automatic d6 Devotion to the character and the character gains an equivalent Devotion in return.

Only severe abuse can break the bond between the hero and his Sidekick but should this happen, the Sidekick becomes an enemy of equivalent zeal.
