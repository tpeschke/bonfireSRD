The purpose of this chapter is to condense all the rules for calculating things about your character so that it can keep the other chapters to just rules you’ll need at the spur of the moment during gameplay. To this end, these rules are missing why they’re important and what you’ll actually use them for but they do reference the chapter where more is to be found.

HONOR

Honor is a measure how not only how a person measures their own integrity but also how other people see them. Honor isn’t about doing the common sense thing; it’s about doing the heroic thing. People who run from a fight, even when outnumbered, lose Honor. People who let others talk bad about them and don’t get even lose Honor.

The question of Honor isn’t a question of good and evil; many evil men have been honorable. It’s more about living up to the ideals of your culture and being true to your inner self.

STARTING HONOR

The Honor category in which you start is determined by your “Start Honor”, listed under your Charisma.

CHANGING HONOR CATEGORY

At the end of each session, each player awards or docks points, with the approval of the GM to each other player for their character based on the following categories. If a character’s total is higher than their current level, they move up one category. If their total is negative at all, they move down one.

The GM also reserves the right to drop you additional Honor categories at the end of session if she feels like that you’re abusing the Honor system. For instance, knowing that you’re already going to drop an Honor Categories so you decided to go ahead with Plan G: Genocide the peaceful elf village.

MASTERY A character who can hold their own and be creative in a tight spot is an honorable one. Leveraging others and their knowledge in a unique way gains the esteem of their colleges. Honor earned this way must be from a creative use of a skill and not the roll itself. Simply succeeding a check is no indication of mastery but reapplying a skill to work is.

A character can gain Honor for mastery of a skill but using it in a way that makes sense but it’s covered or even hinted at by the rules.

COURAGE When the chips are down, does this character run? Courage is a universally applauded trait. Characters who put their lives at risk and take the dare gain Honor. Those that back down are docked Honor.

STRENGTH This category is unique and should be considered from the perspective of the culture that that the character is in. The Oedinkin respect cunning and being able to outwit your opponent, where the men of Iberese respect displays of wealth and generosity.

GOALS Completing Goals give a character standing as they become known as someone that can get stuff done. You gain 1 Honor for each benchmark you complete or help complete during the session, with an additional bonus of 5 for the completion of a Short Term Goal, 10 for a Medium Term Goal, and jumping to the bottom of the next highest Honor Category for the completion of a Long Term Goal.

HONOR CATEGORIES & BONUSES

Honor has its benefits. Confidence allows you to push yourself a little bit more than you otherwise would. Compare your character’s Honor against the following chart to determine which category they fall under and what bonuses they receive.

DISHONOR The lowest category, this person is unsure that they have what it takes to be a hero and so every decision is laced with hesitation and uncertainty.

A character in this category takes a -1 penalty to each dice he rolls.

LOW HONOR This character hasn’t really proved anything, one way or another. Others and herself are waiting for her to make her mark on the world.

Being in Low Honor gives you a d4! Honor Dice.

AVERAGE HONOR Having reached this category, the character has shown that they have potential and some follow through.

Being in Average Honor gives you a d8! Honor Dice and 1 free reroll.

HIGH HONOR She has now separated herself from the common rabble and can stand a head above the crowd.

Being in High Honor gives you a d12! Honor Dice and 2 free reroll.

GREAT HONOR This rare sliver represents those who can perfectly balance being ahead of others but not so much so that they invoke the ire of those below them as well as being confident while not being cocky.

A character in this category gains a +1 to every dice roll they make for the rest of the session. Additionally, they gain a d20! + d4! for their Honor Dice and 3 free rerolls.

TOO MUCH HONOR There’s a certain point where people get too big for their britches. When this happen, people tend to go out of their way to take a chunk out of them. They’re labelled smart asses or show offs. This people also tend to be overconfident and get themselves into bad situations because of it.

Being Too Much Honor gives you a d20! Honor Dice and 1 free reroll.

REDEEMING YOUR BONUSES

There are 3 bonuses you gain from having Honor:

The first is your Honor Dice . When redeemed, a roll of the Honor Dice can be added (or subtracted) at anytime to another roll (such as damage, or attack), temporarily to a static stat (such as Health or DR), or a dice pool (such as a Conflict). Adding it to a ‘nat 1’ on a defense, negates a fumble.

While Honor Dice can only be used once per session, you can also split it up. So, if you have a d10! Honor Dice, you can instead roll a d2 and have a d8! left over, which you can later roll as a d8! or a d2 and a d6! or a d3! and a d4!, or any combination, as long as the total sides adds up to less than the highest side of the original Dice.

The second is your free reroll , the number of which is determined by your Honor category. You may use a reroll after you’ve made the roll and done your totals but not after you hear the result of the total. So, a character can reroll their defense, after realizing it only totalled to 5, but not after he hears that his attack actually misses because of that total. Additionally, rerolls can be used for rolls the GM has made, however they can’t be used to reroll Honor Dice.

The last is a Divine Intervention . The Divines look favorably on the Honorable and are willing to alter fate to save a favorite. You can redeem this bonus to half or quarter the amount of damage you take from a single blow.

Redeeming your Honor Dice and free reroll(s) can be done at anytime but using Divine Intervention drops you 1 category for halving the damage or 2 for quartering it.

If you run out of rerolls and are in a bad way, you may drop an Honor Category for an additional one, down to the Dishonorable Category.

The bonus/penalty afforded by Great Honor or Dishonor is automatically redeemed at the beginning of the session and doesn’t drop you a category at all.

However, if you’re not in Dishonor and you take its penalty onto you willingly at the beginning of the session, you double all Honor gained at the end.

GAINING & LOSING FAME

You gain Fame by doing things. This is primarily from completing Goals and Benchmarks but it can be from completing quests and simply bragging about your exploits in the local tavern.

The reason that Goals gain you more Fame than if you were just running around doing random fetch quests is because completing Goals creates a story that people want to talk about. Sure, they can talk about you slaying that dragon, but if it’s also the dragon that kidnapped your sister, that’s much more compelling and worth gossiping about at the local watering hole.

You lose Fame by not doing things. Every month your character doesn’t do anything new, people talk about her less and less. They lose 1 point of Fame for every month they don’t complete at least 1 Benchmark.

FAME & HONOR

Honor and Fame have a close tie as the more people talk about you, the more your reputation, whether good or ill, gets around.

At the end of each session, for every 10 points of Fame a character has, they gain an additional Honor point, but this is only added at the end of the session if they don’t end with a negative Honor pool. So, a character with 70 Fame that ends with -3 Honor, drops an Honor Category at the end of the session.

FATIGUE

Doing things is tiring, obviously. Fatigue represents a character slowly running out of energy. Things become harder and jobs are done sloppier.

CALCULATING FATIGUE

For your base Fatigue, add the Fatigue Adjustment from your Con and Wis. Later, Encumbrance and your choice in shield and armor will also modify your base Fatigue so keep an eye for that. If you’re not wearing armor or using a shield, you instead gain a -2 for each one of those you’re missing. This is your Starting Fatigue.

A lower score is good.

EFFECTS of FATIGUE

As you do things, you’ll gain more Fatigue. Every time you roll under your Fatigue on Fatigue checks (like those made while running or trying to stay up late) or on any Defense roll, your Fatigue increase by 1. You receive a penalty to Attack, Damage, and the Difficulties of your Skill Checks for physical Skills (those with Str, Dex, or Con as Abilities) or a flat penalty to your Skill roll for academic skills (those with Int, Wis, or Cha as Abilities) equal to your Fatigue and an increase in your Channeling time if you're a Wizard or Mage, though you never gain a bonus from having a negative score - you just don't get winded as quickly.

NEGATIVE FATIGUE A character with a negative Fatigue always gains Fatigue on a roll of a natural 1 on their defense and Fatigue checks.

RECOVERING FATIGUE

After separating himself from the situation that caused him to Fatigue, a character halfs his Fatigue Level if given an hour of rest, however, that’s as far down as he can drop it that day. If he gains Fatigue later that day and then rests, that rest only gets rid of half the Fatigue he gains since his previous rest.

Rest doesn’t lower Fatigue caused by Wounds, only healing helps with that.

So, a character with -2 Starting Fatigue who gains 8 Fatigue in combat from just running around and 2 from Wounds has a total of 8 Fatigue. He then gets a rest, which drops his Fatigue to 5 ( (6 / 2) + 2 ). If he then gets into a race and gains another 3 Fatigue, resting after will drop him to 7 from 8 ( 5 from his Wounds and previous Fatigue + ( 3 / 2 ) ) Fatigue.

Assuming he’s gotten an adequate amount of sleep, he’ll return to his Starting Fatigue plus any Wounds he has left to heal. Taking from the above example, our character, after a good night sleep (assuming the day's healing hasn't dropped him a Wound Category) will have a Fatigue of 0, giving him no penalties, but not returning him to his Starting Fatigue of 0.

HEALTH

Where Fatigue is a measure of how tired you are, your character’s Health is a measure of how close to dying they are.

Health is divided into 5 categories, representing what percentage of damage a character has taken to their Health:

UNHARMED
0%
HURT
>1%
BLOODY
>25%
WOUNDED
>50%
DYING
>75%
BLEEDING OUT
>100%

To calculate how much Health you have for each category, add a roll of your Primary Archetype’s Health Die to your Constitution + their Health Size Modifier. That’s your total. Divide that by 4. If you have a remainder, alternate between rounding down on every category except the Dying category.

So, if you’re a Human with and your Health Die is a d10 and you roll a 7 with a Constitution of 12, you have 29 Health. You’re Hurt at 28, Bloody at 22, Wounded at 15, and Dying 8.

Record your Health Die roll on your character sheet.

Wound Category Exception

Just as an aside - going from Unharmed to Bloody doesn’t count as changing a Wound Category for purposes of triggering a Trauma check.

HEALING

Combat is dangerous. Even if you survive, you could end up with wounds that you carry with you for months on end.

Each time damage is done, it is tracked in a new wound.

Wounds heals at a slow rate. Each point takes a number of days equal to the damage done to heal it 1 point.

So, a 5 point wound takes 5 days to become a 4 point wound, which takes 4 days to becomes a 3 point wound, and so on. That means it takes 15 days to completely heal ( 5 + 4 + 3 + 2 + 1).

Thankfully, each wound heals simultaneously. So, if you have 3 separate 4 point wounds, you’ll be completely healed by day 10.

STRESS

Working under pressure can get to you. When your crew mates are relying on you to pull out a win, it can mess with your head. Your character can become stressed under these circumstances and stress can later manifest in odd behaviors.

Characters can gain Stress in two ways: the first is by suppressing their nature. Stress is given out when players don’t roleplay their Flaws, especially when not playing them gives them a huge benefit.

The second way is to take Stress on upon yourself. Why would you do this? Because by forcing your character to hyper-focus on what he’s doing, he can pull out a win, but the Stress will get to him.

Characters who stress about a skill check automatically succeed, however, they take 1 point of Stress for every point they would have otherwise failed by. Even if they do succeed, they still gain at least 1 Stress.

However, it gets a bit trickier when it comes to opposed checks. In this case, it becomes a bit like gamble. Each player may add an additional die to their Skill Check up to the size of the skill difficulty dice they're rolling. Regardless of whether or not they beat their opponent, you gain Stress equal to the bonus die's result.

For example, you're sneaking by a guard. You've prepared so you get to roll a d10 vs the guard's d8 to see if you can slip past him. It's a pretty important roll so you decided to Stress about it. You could add up to a d10 but you're pretty confident in yourself so you decided to just go with a d6. You get a 2 on the d10 and a 4 on the d6. The guard rolls a 7, meaning he spots you and you gain 4 Stress from the encounter.

Rolling over 20 - 1 for every 10 full points of Stress you have on any skill check gives you a temporary Flaw, as your character starts to crack. This can happen whether or not the skill check succeeds or fails.

When this happens, you roll on the Mental or Behavioral Flaws table. You can modify this roll by adding an amount of Stress equal to up to half your current Stress level. Any Stress spent this way is subtracted from your total.

You can then choose either the Flaw you rolled, one that is lower, or one that is higher but if you don’t choose the one your rolled, it has to be one that your character has had before (as old habits resurface).

If you choose one lower than what you rolled, that Flaw becomes permanent and must be paid off but for half cost.

If you choose the one you rolled or one higher up on the list, it’s only temporary and lasts until all your Stress is healed.

It’s still pretty risky, however. Should your character drop below 0 Healths, the near-death experience makes one temporary Flaw permanent. You never gain CrP from Flaws gotten this way.

Stress also affects three things: Morale, lowering the character's Morale Threshold by 1 per 1 Stress, and healing.

For every 10 points of Stress a character has, he adds 1 day of healing time to every wound at every level. So, a character with 5 severity wound would normally heal completely in 15 days, however if he was dealing with 23 points of Stress, he’d only heal completely after 25 days.

Additionally, every 10 points add 1 to the character’s Fatigue Score as well.

Stress heals slowly and the more you have, the slower it heals. Stress heals in a compound fashion, with 1 Stress healing after a number of days equal to the total Stress. So, 5 points of Stress takes 5 days to drop to 4 Stress, and a total of 15 days to heal completely. All Stress is added to the same total.

There is two ways for a character to decompress and speed up healing Stress.

The first is to give into his nature. By role playing his Flaws in a detrimental way, a character can relieve 1 or more Stress (GM’s option).

The second way is through hobbies. After spending leisure time, your character can check against the relevant social attribute (Traits/Passions/Devotions) that the hobby reflects. On a 4 or above on any dice, your character heals 1 Stress. Once you’ve failed on a check, you may not try that attribute again till you’ve moved through all your characteristics, but each try requires more down time spent.

Regardless of how you try, you can't heal Stress on days you've gained Stress, naturally or thorugh hobbies, though the Divine may be able to help out.
