Starting A Combat

Combat engages as soon as both sides are within eyesight, and both know that the other intends harm.

The CLOCK

The Clock begins at 1, and then progresses to 2, 3, 4 etc., ad infinitum. When the Clock matches a characterâ€™s Initiative, they go. Each of these numbers represent 1 second of time passing. What second a character's next action is referred to as their initiative (or "where they are on the Clock")

This can be a difficult when you first start so we recommend that you start with only running two or three enemies until you get the hang of it. We've also provided a tool that can help you keep track of the Clock and enemies easier. You can find it here.

STARTING INITIATIVE & SURPRISE

Each side rolls an initiative die. The lower the die, the more prepared they are for the attack, though typically the instigator goes on second 1. Those that know the battle is about to start (like being warned by a predetermined sign) use a d4, and the completely surprised receive a d20, while everyone else typically falls on a die between that.

Those whose initiative hasnâ€™t come up are surprised and continue to do what they were doing as if nothing is wrong. They can take no other actions: theyâ€™re completely unaware of whatâ€™s going on.

Should someone attack them during this time, they are completely helpless, defending on a d8!.

If the surprised person is hit, the pain quickly brings them into the reality of the situation, and they may act 2 seconds later (or before that if their initiative was the next second).

ACTIONS

Every action takes a certain amount of time. On a characterâ€™s Initiative, they declare their action and are told how long that action will take. After that time, the action is complete.

So, if a character wants to walk across a room, which will take 8 seconds, and his Initiative is 5, once the Clock reaches 5, he starts walking and once it reaches 14, heâ€™s gotten the other side of the room.

At any point, you may choose to forfeit your action and change your course. If, when halfway across the room (at second 9), the character sees that his buddy is in trouble, he can immediately decide that, no, heâ€™d rather be there, his character stops walking across the room and instead heads over to help his friend.

Keeping track of this can be tricky for new players unused to the system. To help players make the shift, we've created the Basic Combat Cheat Sheet that has a strip where you can keep track of when your next action is going to happen. It also includes handy references to other rules found in this chapter as well.

Tracking Initiative

Until you get the hang out it, it might be difficult to keep track of when exactly things are happening, which is why we've provided the combat strip on the Basic Combat Cheat Sheet, which is that numbered line on the left hand side going 1 to 20.

To use it, take a marker (dice or a penny works just fine) and place it on your initiative. When that number comes up on the Clock, you do your action and then add the seconds until your next action, looping back around to 0, if needed.

So, if you attack on second 17 with a weapon with a recovery of 8, place a marker on 17. When the Clock gets to second 17, you attack, and then move the marker to 5 ( since 17 + 8 is above 20, you subtract 20 from it).

You may also find it useful to place markers for other things you want to track, like movement and shooting for ranged fighters or when spells will go off or end.

ZONES of ENGAGEMENT: MEASURING YOUR OPPONENT

There are 3 positions that you can have relative to your enemy. These positions arenâ€™t reciprocal by nature: you can be threatened by someone and not have them be threatened by you.

NOT ENGAGED This is basically the default. You are too far away for them to reasonable do anything.

THREATENED This means that youâ€™re within 5 feet + your opponentâ€™s weaponâ€™s measure. From here, if you move faster than a Jog and if theyâ€™re within half their weapon Recovery, they can attack you.

ENGAGED Youâ€™ve closed the distance and can actually make an attack. The person with the highest weapon measure attacks first. If both weapons are exactly the same measure, the attacks happen simultaneously.

MELEE ATTACK & DEFENSE

Basic melee attacks always roll a base d20! + any attack modifiers, while defenders rolls a d20! + any defense modifiers

RANGED ATTACK & DEFENSE

Ranged attacks and defenses function identical to melee attacks and defenses (including Flanking), only get a few additional bonuses and penalties.

The first is that range (of course) and the second is movement. When defending against a ranged attack, you get a +2 for each Movement Category above standing still. So, if you're getting shot at, get the lead out.

DEALING DAMAGE

Once both sides have rolled and added all modifiers, compare the totals against each other.

If the defenderâ€™s is equal to or greater than the attacker, the attack misses.

PARRYING If a melee attack roll is within the Parry Threshold of the defenderâ€™s total, the defender may choose to deflect some of the incoming attack.

The attacker rolls their damage and subtracts the shield or weaponâ€™s DR and then their armorâ€™s DR. Any leftover is still done to the defender. For piercing weapons, the /d DR of the shield or weapon is tripled.

However, if itâ€™s not, the attack lands and the attacker rolls his weaponâ€™s damage die and adds any appropriate damage modifiers. The defender than subtracts their Damage Reduction and the remaining total is subtracted from their Vitality and added to their list of wounds.

DAMAGE REDUCTION Armor allows you to reduce the amount of damage taken. This is often times shorted to DR. There are two types: Static, noted by just a number, and Per Die, noted by a number /D.

Static simply subtracts that number from the damage and is done.

/D subtracts that number multiplied by the number of dice the attack is using. So, if someone is attacking you with a slashing weapon (2d6!), and you have armor that gives you 4/D, youâ€™d subtract 8 from the damage.

Either way, if the attacker chooses to remain in engaged, he then adds his weaponâ€™s Recovery to the current second on the Clock. Once the Clock reaches that new number, he may attack again.

These rules add another layer of strategy to combat and should be implemented as soon as you have the basics down.

ACTIVE DODGING This move relays on actively focusing on dodging against incoming attacks.

A defender commits to how much he wants to focus on dodging incoming attacks, both melee and ranged, choosing from a bonus to his defense in the range of +1 to +5, but netting an equal penalty to his attacks (-1 to -5).

Additionally, this lowers the range for Near Perfect Defenses by 1, so you score them on a base natural 18 - 19. This tactic needs to be committed to for at least your full weapon Recovery. After that you may continue using it and change tactics at any point.

But focusing on defense does slow you down, since you're zigzagging and trying to juke out your enemy, so your movement is considered 1 Category lower for how far you move. So, if you're Medium sized and Active Dodging while at a Run, you would move at 10 ft / sec, instead of 15. You would still be subject to the damage, however, since you're still technically running.

Active Dodging can be combined with Retreat and Step Back, adding only the attack penalties but gaining all auxiliary benefits from each (so no, the defenses don't stack).

BRACE YOURSELF Maybe you look at your opponent and say "No, you move". You root your stance and ready yourself, sacrificing some maneuverability to hold the line and get a better chance of striking first.

FEND OFF If your weapon's measure is at least 1 foot greater than your opponent's, you can try and fend them off. This means that you may attack them freely, while they can't do the same to you.

This basic training allows a character to more effectively utilize armor of different types based on their level of familiarity with them. Itâ€™s split into 3 categories:
