Starting A Combat

Combat engages as soon as both sides are within eyesight and both know they the other intends harm.

The CLOCK

The Clock begins at 1, and then progresses to 2, 3, 4 etc, ad infinitum. When the Clock matches a character’s Initiative, they go. Each of these numbers represent 1 second of time passing. What second a character's next action is referred to as their initiative (or "where they are on the Clock")

This can be a difficult when you first start so we recommend that you start with only running a two or three enemies until you get the hang off it. We've also provided a tool that can help you keep track of the Clock and enemies easier. You can find it here.

STARTING INITIATIVE & SURPRISE

Each side rolls an initiative die. The lower the die, the more prepared they are for the attack, though typically the instigator goes on second 1. Those that know the battle is about to start (like being warned by a predetermined sign) use a d4, and the completely surprised receive a d20, while everyone else typically falls on a dice between that.

Those whose initiative hasn’t come up are surprised and continue to do what they were doing as if nothing is wrong. They can take no other actions: they’re completely unaware of what’s going on.

Should someone attack them during this time, they are completely helpless, defending on a d8!.

If the surprised person is hit, the pain quickly brings them into the reality of the situation and they may act 2 seconds later (or before that if their initiative was the next second).

ACTIONS

Every action takes a certain amount of time. On a character’s Initiative, they declare their action and are told how long that action will take. After that time, the action is complete.

So, if a character wants to walk across a room, which will take 8 seconds, and his Initiative is 5, once the Clock reaches 5, he starts walking and once it reaches 14, he’s gotten the other side of the room.

At any point, you may choose to forfeit your action and change your course. If, when half way across the room (at second 9), the character sees that his buddy is in trouble, he can immediately decide that, no, he’d rather be there, his character stops walking across the room and instead heads over to help his friend.

Keeping track of this can be tricky for new players unused to the system. To help players make the shift, we've created the Basic Combat Cheat Sheet that has a strip where you can keep track of when your next action is going to happen. It also includes handy references to other rules found in this chapter as well.

MOVEMENT While, for the most part, you’ll be using your commonsense to determine how long things will take, to unify at least one thing, movement rates are given below.

A person may move up 1 movement category or down 2 in a second.

Running can be kept up for 5 Seconds + Endurance Rank. If you continue to Run after that you take d4 damage at the beginning of every 5 Seconds + Endurance Rank period.

Sprinting can be kept up for Endurance Rank - Stress Penalty in Seconds. If continue to Sprint you take d4! damage at the beginning of every Endurance Rank - Stress Penalty period.

You can push your character as hard as you'd like, continuing to take damage until they die from fatigue but if you take damage from Running or Sprinting and you stop, you can’t Run or Sprint again until you take a breather.

Also, moving from a Run to Sprint, or vice versa, doesn’t reset that count, so don’t think you can skip taking damage by moving from a Sprint to a Run, you cheeky devil.

While Running or Sprinting, you're unable to attack or defend yourself

Tracking Initiative

Until you get the hang out it, it might be difficult to keep track of when exactly things are happening, which is why we've provided the combat strip on the Basic Combat Cheat Sheet, which is that numbered line on the left hand side going 1 to 20.

To use it, take a marker (dice or a penny works just fine) and place it on your initiative. When that number comes up on the Clock, you do your action and then add the seconds until your next action, looping back around to 0, if needed.

So, if you attack on second 17 with a weapon with a recovery of 8, place a marker on 17. When the Clock gets to second 17, you attack, and then move the marker to 3 ( since 17 + 8 is above 20, you subtract 20 from it).

You may also find it useful to place markers for another things you want to track, like movement and shooting for ranged fighters or when spells will go off or end.

IMPROVISED WEAPONS

🜂 Deluxe Rule

It is entirely possible that you’ll find yourself unarmed and must defend yourself with a chair leg or broken bottle. In these cases, treat is as a club but penalize it for 3 seconds and drop its Parry to 0 for not being balanced properly.

Now, it’s also possible that you have superhuman strength and so you can rip a door off its hinges and start bashing people with want and abandon. In this case, it’s entirely possible to do more than a zweihander in sheer concussive force, however, you’ll find that because a door (or similar) isn’t properly weighted or balanced, you’ll find yourself taking a long time to swing and unable to defend yourself properly. In extreme cases like these, the GM can give you a Defense Modifier of somewhere around -8 and the Recovery should never be quicker than 20 seconds.

On top of this, these wide swings make an otherwise threatening object easy to dodge and so less of a danger, meaning you may still be counted as unarmed while trying to use it.

WEAPON STYLES

🜂 Deluxe Rule

The way your character wields a weapon can tell a lot about their priorities in a combat and a weapon can be versatile in the extreme if some thought is put into how it's held.

1 HANDED WEAPON WITH SHIELD This is the standard way your character is assumed to be wielding their weapon.

1 HANDED WEAPON WITH FREE SECOND HAND With one hand free, the character can focus a bit more on their defense and seizing openings. They gain a +2 to their Parry and -1 to their Recovery.

2 1 HANDED WEAPONS If attacking with both, your primary hand attacks with a d12! while your secondary attacks with a d8!. You only receive your main hand’s Parry, if any. The secondary attacks slightly slower; always attacking a number of seconds equal to its Recovery after the primary makes its attacks.

When defending with one hand, you defend against the first attack against you with a d20!, using the Parry value of the off-hand weapon.

You can only switch Tactics on your primary attack.

If the weapon off-hand weapon is as large as you, you can only attack with it.

2 HANDING 1 HANDED WEAPONS Using two hands where one is sufficient allows you to leverage more of your strength and deal a harsher blow but slows you down.

When using this style, you increase your damage by +4, however, you swap out the bonuses for a +3 to Recovery.

This style can’t be effectively used in combat on weapons smaller than your size.

DOUBLE SHIELDS While it gives no offensive benefit, the double shields style allows you to cover more facings to gain a greater defense. If two shields have overlapping faces, use the higher of the two's Parry.

WEAPON IN YOUR OFF HAND This may come about because your main hand is disabled or you just want to show off. But this implies the main hand is free. If not, see the 2 1 Handed Weapons style.

Non-Ambidextrous people attack with a d12! And gain a +5 Recovery penalty to off-handed attacks.

2 HANDS FREE (NO WEAPON) If you have no weapons in combat, you’re not really seen as a threat and so an attacker doesn’t really have to do the dance to try to avoid you blows while trying to strike you.

In this style, your opponent attacks at half their weapon Recovery and they get a free attack whenever you try and close in an attack them. That includes moving ranges.

Dropping Your Weapon to Grapple

Unarmed moves are more effective with two hands rather than one so you may decide to drop your weapon to try and grapple your opponent easier. If the unarmed attack is on the same second that you drop your weapon, your opponent doesn’t receive the free attack on you.

The Combat

It is inevitable that if you get into combat, you’ll end up engaged in a melee with your enemy.

ZONES of ENGAGEMENT: MEASURING YOUR OPPONENT

There are 3 positions that you can have relative to your enemy. These positions aren’t reciprocal by nature: you can be threatened by someone and not have them be threatened by you.

NOT ENGAGED This is basically the default. You are too far away for them to reasonable do anything.

THREATENED This means that you’re within 5 feet + your opponent’s weapon’s measure. From here, if you move faster than a Jog and if they’re within half their weapon Recovery, they can attack you.

If you go from Engaged to Threatened, your weapon initiative doesn't reset, so if you become Engaged again for any reason before your weapon's Recovery, you may not attack.

ENGAGED You’ve closed the distance and can actually make an attack. The person with the highest weapon measure attacks first. If both weapons are exactly the same measure, the attacks happen simultaneously.

MELEE ATTACK & DEFENSE

Basic melee attacks always roll a base d20! + any attack modifiers, while defenders either rolls a d20! + any defense modifiers

PERFECT ATTACKS & DEFENSES

🜂 Deluxe Rule

A Perfect Attack or Defense is one that puts you in a better position to win. A Perfect Attack occurs on an unmodified roll of 20 on your Attack roll, while a Perfect Defense is an unmodified 20 on your Defense roll, but only if your final totals are still greater than you opponent’s. If not, the attack still hits for Perfect Attacks and you still dodge the blow for Perfect Defenses but you don’t gain the auxiliary benefits.

If both those qualifiers are met you gain the following benefits on top of the normal ones:

PERFECT ATTACK You gain a free automatically successful Unarmed Combat attack. If you have Advanced Training in Unarmed Combat, you may roll for the additional benefit, but even on a failure, you gain the move’s base benefit.

You may also Shift with no penalty and change your Tactic.

If you’re firing a ranged weapon, you don’t receive the Unarmed attack, however you managed to hit a sensitive area, lowering their max movement speed by one category, minimum Crawl speed.

PERFECT DEFENSE You gain a free automatically successful Unarmed Combat attack. If you have Advanced Training in Unarmed Combat, you may roll for the additional benefit, but even on a failure, you gain the move’s benefit.

You may also Shift with no penalty and change your Tactic.

Additionally, you gain a chance to riposte, gaining a free full attack the next second if you’re wielding a melee weapon. This attack doesn’t affect your current count.

NEAR PERFECT ATTACKS AND DEFENSE An unmodified roll of 19 on either Attack or Defense results in a Near Perfect Attack or Defense, where you may Shift for no penalty, change your Tactic, or, for attacks, gain an extra d4! to damage, or, for defenses gained a free riposte with your off hand weapon if it’s smaller than your size.

RANGED ATTACK & DEFENSE

Ranged attacks and defenses function identical to melee attacks and defenses (including Flanking), only get a few additional bonuses and penalties.

The first is that range (of course) and the second is movement. When defending against a ranged attack, you get a +2 for each Movement Category above standing still. So, if you're getting shot at, get the lead out.

COVER

🜂 Deluxe Rule

Cover is anything that gets between you and the person trying to shoot at you.

For solid objects, such as walls, where it is easy to calculate how much of the person is covered, a flat bonus is appropriate. For every 10% covered, you get a +2, so 25% would be a +5, 50% would be a +10 and so on.

For people, tree, a moving object, or really anything that’s harder to calculate, you’ll use a dice backed on the difference in size, starting at a d20. Each difference in size shrinks or increases that cover die by 1 size.

Cover dice for most objects works the same as shields - if you’re behind cover, you add a roll of that dice to your defense. If you’re actively crouching behind it, you add the max it would give you to your defense plus another roll of the dice.

So, two medium size people standing one in front of the other would give the guy in the back d20 cover, same as two small sized people. If the person in the front is small sized and the guy in the back is still medium sized, the guy in the back only gains a d12 cover.

If the person is actively moving or trying not to be your cover, you can’t gain the bonus for actively crouching behind them.

If you don't move, however, cover becomes more and more useless as your shooter sights in. For each shot against you where you haven't moved, you either lower your effective cover percentage by 10 or shrink your cover dice by 1 size, depending on whether the cover is static or not.

Note this also applies to shields as well, so if you don't move while using a shield, it becomes less effective.

RANGED MISSES

🜂 Deluxe Rule

On a miss with a ranged weapon, the missile doesn’t magically disappear into oblivion. There’s a good chance it might hit someone else, especially if your target is locked in melee.

In the case of a miss, roll a d20! and a d4. The d4 determines to which side the missile strays - an odd result means it goes left, an even result means it goes right.

The d20! determines how many feet it strays from the target. Draw an imaginary line between the archer and that point. Everyone within 5ft of that line needs to roll a defense vs the original attack roll, starting with the person closest to the archer and ending with the furthest possible target.

Inanimate objects may provide enough cover to be considered targets in both Firing into Combat and Ranged Misses, such as a large boulder or tree. In which case, medium sized objects roll a d12!, with each size lower decreasing the dice size by 2, and each size larger increasing the dice size by 2.

FIRING INTO MELEE

🜂 Deluxe Rule

If there’s any one within 5 ft of a direct line between the origin of the missile and the target, they’ll have to roll the appropriate defense die vs the original attack roll to see if the missile hits them instead, starting with the person closest to the archer or gunner and ending with the intended target.

All the normal rolls apply so if the missile only misses because of cover, the missile has lodged itself in the cover and goes no further.

DEALING DAMAGE

Once both sides have rolled and added all modifiers, compare the totals against each other.

If the defender’s is equal to or greater than the attacker, the attack misses.

PARRYING If a melee attack roll is within the Parry Threshold of the defender’s total, the defender may choose to deflect some of the incoming attack.

The attacker rolls their damage and subtracts the shield or weapon’s DR and then their armor’s DR. Any left over is still done to the defender. For piercing weapons, the /d DR of the shield or weapon is tripled.

So, a defender with a Parry Threshold of 4, and a total of 10 for their defense roll vs an attack roll of 14, could choose to parry the blow, taking less damage.

However, if it’s not, the attack lands and the attacker rolls his weapon’s damage die and adds any appropriate damage modifiers. The defender than subtracts their Damage Reduction and the remaining total is subtracted from their Vitality and added to their list of wounds.

DAMAGE REDUCTION Armor allows you to reduce the amount of damage taken. This is often times shorted to DR. There are two types: Static, noted by just a number, and Per Die, noted by a number /D.

Static simply subtracts that number from the damage and is done.

/D subtracts that number multiplied by the number of dice the attack is using. So, if someone is attacking you with a slashing weapon (2d6!), and you have armor that gives you 4/D, you’d subtract 8 from the damage.

Either way, if the attacker chooses to remain in engaged, he then adds his weapon’s Recovery to the current second on the Clock. Once the Clock reaches that new number, he may attack again.

KNOCK-BACKS

If the pre-DR damage is higher than your enemy’s Vitality Size Modifier + 3 for every level of Encumbrance you have, the attack also moves them back 5 feet. If it’s twice the damage needed, they get knocked back 10 feet, and so on.

VITALITY & DAMAGE

Remember that if you drop a Vitality Category you gain Stress equal to your Encumbrance

TRAUMA

If someone does enough damage in one hit for you to skip a Wound Category (25% of their Vitality), you’ll have to roll a Trauma check, which is a Con check.

Failure means that your character has been overcome with pain to the point that he’s unable to act, leaving him completely defenseless for a number of seconds equal to how much he failed by times 3.

Double & Triple Trauma

A character that gets dealt 2 Wound Categories worth of damage in a single blow has suffered a grievous wound - the scar of which, if he survives, will be with him until the day he dies.

When this happens, the character rolls a 1d20. On any result other than 1 or 20, the character increases the number of scars they have by 1. On a 20, increase that number by 2.

But on a 1, the character has suffered such great trauma, that they gain a new Physical Flaw rolled randomly. They gain no CrP for it.

Battle scars are marks of pride and honor. Characters who become scared gain a d4 Trait “Battle Scars”. When their number of scars increases to more than the max value of that dice, increase that Trait by 1 dice size. So, a person who gains his 5th scar increases “Battle Scars” to a d6 while someone getting their 9th, increases it from a d8 to a d10.

Should the character suffer 3 Wound Categories worth of damage, the trauma is too much and they simply die where they stand if they fail their check. Even if they succeed, they’re still suffer Trauma for the next 30 seconds.

COUP de GRACE

A helpless enemy is one completely at your mercy. If a character is completely helpless, either from failing a Trauma check, being bound, or deep asleep, a character can kill the target instantly. This move takes 10 seconds to complete as it takes a bit of positioning to pull off. The character’s who throat is being slit gives out a cry of pain as they expire.

FACING & FIGHTING MULTIPLE PEOPLE

The likelihood is that at some point you’ll want to be flanking or someone with want to flank you, to try and gain an advantage.

Defending right in front of you is pretty easy and so brooks no penalties, however, every 45 degree off that the defender lowers their Defense dice by 1 size, with those being attacked from directly behind defend on a d8! since they take advantage of a huge blind spot.

This penalty can be mitigated through the use of shields. Small shields allow you to defend against an additional assailant, depriving them of that bonus, while Medium and Large ones allow you to defend against an extra 2 and 3, respectively.

A maximum of 8 people at a time can effectively attack person at once, assuming that they’re out in the open. After that, every assailant’s attack die is lowered by 1 size per extra person.

TERRAIN & SITUATIONAL FACTORS

No battlefield is absolutely flat and featureless and often these features can make a difference for those cunning enough to use them. This section won’t give you all the different situations you might find yourself in, but should give you a good basis to judge off of.

The numbers under the Attack and Defense columns are the change penalty or bonus. If there is a dice noted there, you replace your d20! with that when rolling. So, standing in quicksand gives you a -8 to Defense (amongst other things).

Note that defense penalties also affect ranged defenses.

TACTICS

🜂 Deluxe Rule

These rules add another layer of strategy to combat and should be implemented as soon as you have the basics down.

A combatant utilizing these moves can only commit to one at a time, unless otherwise stated and can only switch on their attack unless it says otherwise.

ACTIVE DODGING This move relays on actively focusing on dodging against incoming attacks.

A defender commits to how much he wants to focus on dodging incoming attacks, both melee and ranged, choosing from a bonus to his defense in the range of +1 to +5, but netting an equal penalty to his attacks (-1 to -5).

Additionally, this lowers the range for Near Perfect Defenses by 1, so you score them on a base natural 18 - 19. This tactic needs to be committed to for at least your full weapon Recovery. After that you may continue using it and change tactics at any point.

But focusing on defense does slow you down, since you're zigzagging and trying to juke out your enemy, so your movement is considered 1 Category lower for how far you move. So, if you're Medium sized and Active Dodging while at a Run, you would move at 10 ft / sec, instead of 15. You would still be subject to the damage, however, since you're still technically running.

Active Dodging can be combined with Retreat and Step Back, adding only the attack penalties but gaining all auxiliary benefits from each (so no, the defenses don't stack).

BRACE YOURSELF Maybe you look at your opponent and say "No, you move". You root your stance and ready yourself, sacrificing some maneuverability to hold the line and get a better chance of striking first.

While using this Tactic, a character adds +15 to their Knock-Back Threshold and gains a +1 to their Measure, however, they defend on a d12!.

CALLED SHOT By carefully aiming your shot, a character can reduce the effects of heavy armor by taking more time to target gaps. When employing this tactic, a character halves their effective number of damage dice (rounded up) for considering /D DR, however, it takes a bit longer to set up the strike and so they increase their Recovery by 3 seconds.

Crushing weapons are unable to employ this tactic.

CAREFUL AIMING By taking a moment or two, you can sight in with any ranged weapon, giving you a better chance to hit your target, however, there are diminishing returns after a bit.

For the first 3 seconds, you gain an increasing bonus of +1 to hit. So, the first second you’re aiming, you get a +1, the next a +2 (for a total of +3), and the third you get a +3 (for a total of +6).

After that, that bonus shrinks by 1 each second. So, the fourth second you get a +2 (for a total of +8) and the fifth second you get a +1 (for a total of +9).

Further delay gives you no bonuses.

CHARGE A character moving at least a Run can throw their full force behind a blow, knocking their opponent off their guard. If the Attacker has reach, the damage for their next attack is considered doubled for the purposes of determining Knock-Backs, though they defend with a d12! for half their weapon Recovery.

A charge is a terrifying tactic: if the blow lands, the defender takes an amount of Stress equal to how much they were hit by. If they're able to retaliate while the attacker is defending with that d12!, the attack also takes that much Stress.

CHOKING UP ON YOUR WEAPON When you’ve choked up on your weapon, your weapon’s Measure is halved, and you add that number, rounded down to its Recovery. Your halved Measure is considered your new Measure for determining who attacks first and what Tactics can be employed on you as well.

However, if an opponent is In Your Guard and you choke up on your weapon, you defend with a standard 1d20! and deal full weapon damage.

You’re unable to employ this tactic twice if someone again gets within your guard.

FEINT When performing this move, you (hopefully) get your enemy to commit his guard to a different location than you plan on hitting.

Before you roll, if you declare a Feint, you receive a +5 to that Attack but receive a -3 to Defense until your next attack.

FEND OFF If your weapon's measure is at least 1 foot greater than your opponent's, you can try and fend them off. This means that you may attack them freely, while they can't do the same to you.

You may only use this Tactic as someone is going from Threatened to Engaged, in which case they're automatically considered to be Fended Off. To full becoming engaged with you, they'll have to knock your weapon aside with a regular attack that deals no damage. Should they hit, they become fully engaged and can attack freely again.

IN THEIR GUARD If you're engaged with a person and have a weapon that is at least 1 foot shorter than your opponent, you may attempt to get in their guard. On a successful attack that deals no damage, the shorter weapon fighter has successfully knocked his foe's weapon aside and is no fighting at extreme close range, cause them huge penalties.

The longer weapon may only defend with a d12!. All damage dice is also reduced by one size (3d4! → 3d3!). They may, in turn, employ the Choke Up On Your Weapon Tactic.

FLURRY of BLOWS This tactic involves releasing a rain of quick attacks that rely more on speed than accuracy or power.

While employing this tactic, you attack 3 seconds quicker, however, you suffer a -3 to Attack, your damage doesn’t explode, and you gain Stress equal to your encumbrance for every 20 seconds you keep up your onslaught, though this is all healed if you Take a Breather.

JAB A strike that is meant to purposely to open your opponent’s guard and get them off their balance by making them shift their defense.

The Jab attack happens at half weapon Recovery (rounded up) for Piercing and Slashing weapons. Instead of a d20!, the defender uses a d12! but on a successful hit, no damage is done, and the defender nets a -1 to their next full defense against a non-jab attack. This penalty doubles with each jab, however, it only lasts until the defender’s next full defense.

Crushing weapons may use this tactic but it’s not any faster than their regular Recovery.

LONG GUARD By placing your weapon further out in front of you and trying to line the body of the weapon with the defender’s eye line, this may allow a person with a short weapon the first strike.

While using this tactic, you increase your weapon’s measure by 1/2 ft but you reduce your damage’s largest die by 1 size.

Crushing weapons are unable to employ this tactic.

POWER ATTACK This is the fighting equivalent of baseball batting your enemy, swinging with all your might.

With this attack, you receive a -3 to your Attack and next defense but your damage increases

Piercing weapons increase 1 die by 1 size. Slashing weapons increase all dice by 1 size and crushing weapons increase its highest die by 2 sizes.

RETREAT This is a defensive tactic that involves moving directly back at jog or at a 45 degree angle from your opponent’s blows at a walk.

After your defense roll, if you declare a Retreat, you receive a +5 to your next Defense roll but also a -5 to your next Attack and must continue to move back until your next attack, either at a jog, if moving directly back, or at a walk, if started to move at a 45 degree angle.

If your opponent is using the Flurry of Blows, Feint, or the Power Attack tactic, if they do hit, you only receive half damage

SHIFT You move at your walking speed to either side of your opponent, but suffer a -1 to your next Attack and Defense for every 5 ft you travel before your next Attack or Defense.

However, if you Shift with another tactic, you don’t receive this penalty and gain a +2 that can be either used to increase a bonus or decrease a penalty from that tactic. You may do this freely with any Tactic except Retreat and Step Back.

Shifting is also used to change Facings so if you’re engaged with an enemy and you Shift. And don’t forget that you get those tasty bonuses from flanking your enemy if they don’t also Shift to face you.

STEP BACK This is a defensive tactic that involves moving directly back from your opponent’s blows at your walking speed.

Before your defense roll, if you declare a Step Back, you receive a +5 to your next Defense roll but on your next attack roll shrink the damage of your dice based on its type and must continue to move back at a Walking speed until your next attack.

Piercing weapons lower 1 of their dice by 1 size. Slashing lower all dice by 1 size and crushing lowers their highest die by 2 sizes.

If your opponent is using the Flurry of Blows, Faint, or the Power Attack tactic, if they do hit, you only receive half damage.

Retreat v. Step Back

While they may seem similar on the surface, these two different tactics have some key differences I thought I’d point out so that you can utilize each better.

1. Step Back must be declared before you roll your defense while Retreat can be declared after, meaning that if you see you rolled low before employing them.

2. Step Back moves your character straight back, while Retreat allows you to move up to 45° to the left and right, meaning that if you’re going to be backed against a wall, it’s an option to try and skirt your opponent.

3. Retreat may be done at a jogging speed, while Step Back is always at a walk.

STRIKE THE WEAPON This has two purposes: to either knock an opponent's weapon aside to delay their attack or to try and shatter your opponent’s weapon.

The defender rolls a standard defense die vs the attacker’s regular attack roll.

Should the attack succeed, he delays the defender’s next attack by a number of seconds equal to 1 for every 3 full points he scored above the defender’s total.

If the difference between the attack and defense was greater than 15, the attacker has a chance of breaking the opponent’s weapon

The defender’s weapon is treated as a shield using the Shield Breakage rules, with the attacker dealing his damage to the weapon.

UNARMED COMBAT

🜂 Deluxe Rule

Unarmed Combat’s primary use is to either disable or control your foe, thus winning the battle by maneuvering, rather than raw damage output.

To use an unarmed attack, roll an attack on a d10!, adding your normal attack modifiers, but also adding your Damage Modifier. Your opponent rolls a regular defense (including shield unless otherwise stated) but also adds their Damage Modifier as well.

Size does count for something: the larger person, if there is one, gains a +6 for each Size Category they have on their opponent.

If you hit, you successfully pulled off the move and gain the benefit.

If the difference between the attack and defense totals were more than the target’s Vitality Size Modifier, you may gain additional benefits listed in the move. If the difference is double the Target’s Vitality Size Modifier, they may immediately make a second unarmed combat attack for free the same second.

If you’re doing the move or defending with two hands, roll your attack on a d20!.

GRAB This move is meant to disable or gain control of a limb or item, whether that’s their belt, their leg, or their sword.

The target must be declared before the roll. Attempting to grab a shield means the target subtracts their shield’s unmodified Encumbrance Adjustment from their roll as well since a larger shield is easier to grab. Additionally, attempting to grab most parts of the body involve grabbing armor, if they're wearing any. Add your target's armor's unmodified Encumbrance Adjustment as well as applicable.

The exact bonus will depend on the situation but here are some guidelines:

Weapon: The defender gets a free attack if the move isn’t successful but when grabbed it means the defender can no longer attack.

Shield: The defender no longer gains any bonus from their shield but still has the defense and Encumbrance penalties.

Arm: See Weapon if it’s the weapon’s arm and Shield if it’s the shield’s arm. The defender also defends on a d12 vs Pushes and can’t change their facing willingly.

Leg: Moving above a crawl is impossible and moving at all can only be done after a successful opposed Str check vs d20! + the grabber’s strength.

The grabber defends with a d8! unless they’re employing something to let them keep their distance (a lasso or mancatcher, for example). The grabber only defends with a d8! against Throws/Tackles. Their defense dice is lowered by 1 for all facings.

A second grab on a non-body part works as a disarm, wrenching the item free unless secured by something strong, though at the very least, the thing is no longer in hand. This is also done on an additional benefit from a well executed move if the attacker wishes.

PUSH By pushing you opponent in just the right way, you can move them or shift them in the direction you wish.

On a successful Push, your target changes one facing or moves 5 ft to the left, right, or directly back. You may immediately Shift to follow them without penalty.

You gain an additional facing change or 5 ft of movement on a well-executed Push.

THROW/TACKLE This move attempts to throw your opponent to the ground and is easier to pull of if you go prone with them.

For this move, you can increase the attack dice size by 1 if you’re at running speed when you Tackle them and another if you choose to go to the ground with them, however, if you lose the struggle, you still end up on the ground.

Getting forced to the ground for you opponent resets their Count and reduces their Attack and Defense dies to a d8! if they’re wielding a Medium sized or larger weapon.

The extra bonus for this move is being able to remain standing if you so choose and possibly disarming them if they fail a Str check vs d20! + your Str.

STRIKE A strike is a punch with any part of your body or shield. It’s not really about doing damage but more about throwing your opponent off.

On a successful Strike, you delay your opponent’s next attack by 1d4 seconds, though if you’re using a shield to strike, you subtract that shield’s Recovery Mod from that amount, delaying them further. Striking an already stunned opponent is less effective and so the number of second they would be further delayed is lowered by 1 for each time you've successfully struck them since their last attack, down to 0.

The extra bonus for this move is that it increases the time to a d6!.

BREAK FREE There will probably come a time when you want to break free from a grab, pin, or choke hold.

This is done through the normal unarmed combat resolution method with the following notes:

The first is that the defender rolls a d12! while you roll a full d20! as a base. Additionally, the defender adds their Rank in Escape Artist to the check.

The second is that you can only attempt this maneuver every 10 seconds, though this is modified by any Advanced Weapon Basic Training you have in unarmed attack.

MOUNTED COMBAT

🜂 Deluxe Rule

Whether you're playing a knight or a bounty hunter, you might find it's to your advantage to find yourself something to ride on and you'll probably want to know how you can use that mount in combat, so here are some rules to help you out.

Your mount will have a Turning, Acceleration, and Deceleration stat: this tells you how long, at base, it takes you to turn 180 degrees, speed up 1 Speed Category, or slowdown 1 Speed Category. You declare your intent, wait the few seconds, and, BAM, you've done.

Note, however, that you can't do more than one of these things at a time and once you've committed, you've committed because your mount has its own inertia that it has to deal with and there is a delay between you telling it something and it actually doing it, with the exception of Turning, which can be stopped at any time (so you don't have to always turn your mount 180 degrees), but you don't retroactively straighten out your mount to where it was.

So, for example, if you have a mount with a Turning of 8, that means it turns 22.5 degrees per second. If you declare you're turning and then 3 seconds later, you decide, never mind, you'll still have turned 67.5 degrees. A lot of this should be commonsense but I figure it's worth pointing out, just in case.

That being said, you can lower all of these base times by up to your Riding Skill, however, a mule will probably never be as quick on its hooves as a courser, so you can only lower it to half of what the unmodified value for that derived stat is. So, if your mount's Turning is 8, the lower is can go is 4.

Additionally, for Turning, your mount's Speed Category does prevent it from turning as quickly as you might like with each Speed Category above a Walk adding 2 Seconds to it.

So, if you have to think further ahead in combat so that you don't run into anything, what's the point of using a mount?

1) because it's awesome and 2) you get a lot of extra bonuses for doing so.

For starters, if you get that nice +2 to Defense and +d4! to damage if your opponent isn't mounted or on a rock or something, as well as additional damage dice if you're using a weapon specifically meant to be used from horse back (though you get this die even if you're on a camel, lizard, or what-have you).

BLEEDING OUT & DYING

A character falls into the Bleeding Out Wound Category is unconscious and is in danger of dying. This is played out like a Social Conflict, with the person fighting against Death for survival, Appealing to him to let him live.

However, this has a few changes from a standard Conflict, the first is that each round happens every 5 seconds that the character is in this state.

The second is that you won’t be counting points: it’ll only matter if you win or lose the round. Every time you lose, you bleed out more and take an additional point of damage; every time you win, you get closer to stabilizing.

Getting to half your Constitution in the negative (either from bleeding out or from additional wounds) results in the character's death but getting 10 points means that that stabilize, losing no more Vitality but still remaining unconscious until they're brought back up to Wounded.

The Conflict is mainly going to be Appeals, with Death rolling 5d4! to start with. Each successive round the player wins, Death increases the size of his dice. When the player fails a round, Death's dice drop back down to 5d4! again.

Other characters can add their Medicine Skill as an additional dice each round, but not First Aid, though First Aid can bring someone back up to Wounded and just stop the Conflict in its tracks anyway so there's that.

Morale

🜂 Deluxe Rule

Combat is not simply about who can do the most damage the quickest. Battles, at their heart, are manifestations of the fever of conflict going on between the two sides. This is why sometimes a small, untrained force of zealots can overcome a larger professional army.

This is partly represented by Morale, which is a metric for how a given individual is doing, but it’s also represented by a Social Conflict happening during the combat, or a Morale Conflict.

At the beginning of combat, if at least one side has a captain, a Conflict is also started with the two opposing sides are the two opposing forces (the players vs the goblins or the bandits).

This runs like a normal Conflict except the company captains and in charge and get to dictate pretty much everything. So as the battle progresses, the captains are going to run the Conflict in parallel with it, trying to swing the battlefield to their advantage.

Unlike a regular Conflict, however, it is not about gaining points but about who wins or loses that round.

During the combat, either captain can, at any time, state that they want a fighter to perform an action or behave in a certain way. For instance, they want him to switch his Tactic to be more aggressive, or swap who he’s aiming for. The only exception is they can’t make them run away (which can happen but we'll talk about that a bit later).

So, for example, you’re the company captain and you’re trying to convince the enemy goblin that he should try and get at your company’s ranger but currently he’s making a beeline for your fighter. In that case, you can use the fighter’s Characteristic “You Don’t Want to Mess With Me” to make an Appeal to get the goblin to second guess himself and go after your ranger.

If you win that round, the target behaves the way you want. This represents either them being too panicked or not having enough information to make a good decision, or effective leadership and a clear grasp of what’s going on.

A side without a captain can “defend” but can’t go on the attack, so to speak. Individual fighters can also engage in Morale Conflicts with other fighters they're currently threatening or with -2 range of but it's simply between the two of them: no outside arguments (such as one side out numbering the other) can be brought in.

Death of a Captain

If one side has a captain but they're removed from combat for any reason (including going unconscious), everyone on their side immediately has their Panic Threshold lowered by 1 Wound Category, so keep the company captain safe!

Captains can try to convince as many people as they’d like, but remember that, if they're use Evidence against them - it has to apply to every person they’re trying to persuade. As such, most of what they’ll be using is Appeals, which does still give Repercussion Dice.

ADDITIONAL ARGUMENTS

On top of that you’ll also have additional places you can pull from for your arguments that only really apply to Morale Conflicts, such as having a seasoned commander, outnumbering the opposing side, having a common Devotion to why they’re fighting and so on.

Here are common things you can draw from:

OUTNUMBERING THE ENEMY Each side can add their total numbers to an Appeal, thus favoring the side with the most people.

CATCHING AN ENEMY BY SURPRISE This nets you a d8 until their initiative comes up.

HAVING YOU BATTLE STANDARD UP AND VISIBLE Nets you a d8.

CAUSING AN ENEMY TO PANIC Nets you a d6.

CAUSING AN ENEMY TO FLEE Nets you a d10. This can increase the amount of dice you gain from outnumbering your enemy.

PARTY CAPTAIN’S CHARISMA You also gain your party captain’s Social Dice from Charisma in this case. Also, any abilities that the Party Captain has that effect Conflicts can also be used during this Conflict.

A PERFECT ATTACKS & DEFENSE this nets you a d6! until that person’s next Attack or Defense.

Note that if the argument is based on circumstance, like having an enemy Panic, it goes away when that circumstance does.

DEVOTIONS Normally, Devotions would add to your points automatically but since points don’t matter, it would make them useless in Morale Conflicts but, as we all know, why you fight does matter. If a Devotion applies to all the people being targeted that round, they add the lowest applicable Devotion to each dice. So with a Devotion of 1, if you're rolling 3d4, you'd get a +3.

Who Controls the Pool

It may be the case that the Party Leader, who should be responsible for the Morale Dice Pool is also your main line fighter and is already busy juggling an enemy or two.

You can have a player who runs a less combat intense character, such as the magic user or diplomat, be in charge of the Morale Dice Pool in that case and decide where to risk dice, however, only the actual Party Leader’s abilities affect the number or size of those dice applied.

Ending Combat

Combat ends when one side is incapacitated, reached a truce, or have fled the field. At that point the Clock stops and play moves back to narrative time.

If you engaged in a Morale Conflict, everyone on the side that won the combat gains the difference in winning pools to their Honor, regardless of whether or not their side has less. So, if the players has 3 in their winning pool and the goblins had 7, every player still gains 4 Honor.

The losing side subtracts that with the same caveats.

Trainings

ARMOR BASIC TRAINING

This basic training allows a character to more effectively utilize armor of different types based on their level of familiarity with them. It’s split into 3 categories:

LIGHT (15 CrP) This covers armor that tends to not to restrict the wearer so is often favored by skill centric characters.

MEDIUM (10 CrP) This armor is a bit heavier than Light armor and offers a tradeoff between protection and mobility.

HEAVY (10 CrP) The armor in this category is durable, thick, and heavy, able to withstand blows but at the cost of being hard to move in.

You have to buy armor training in sequence, starting at Light and moving all the way to Heavy.

You can always try and use armor without the baseline knowledge of how to properly wear and don it, but the Defense penalty and Encumbrance are both doubled if that’s the case.

You can further your training in armor to increase the benefits. See the Weapon, Shield, & Armor Advanced Training Sidebar for more details.

SHIELD BASIC TRAINING

For 10 CrP, this teaches a character the basic understanding of how to properly use and employ shields in combat. This training covers all types of shields.

You can always try to use a shield without the training; however, you gain a -4 to your defense when doing so.

You can further your training in shields to increase the benefits. See the Weapon, Shield, & Armor Advanced Training Sidebar for more details.

WEAPON BASIC TRAINING

By gaining the basic understanding of how a weapon is wielded, used and taken care of, you not only expand its longevity, but also your longevity on the battlefield.

Weapon training costs 10 CrP and is taken for individual weapons, however its benefits extend to weapons of that share similar features.

A person completely untrained in weapons both Attacks and Defends with that weapon using a d12!.

However, one trained uses a d20! Attack and Defense (depending on whether or not they use a shield).

Additionally, this is extended to any weapon that shares similar qualities.

For example, a character with training only in the Dusack extends that training to all melee weapons.

A character with training in both a Melee and Ranged weapons, gains that training to all weapons.

So, why would you take training in multiple weapons once you have one melee and one ranged? Because this training allows you to further master that weapon, increasing its usefulness.

You can further your training in weapons to increase the benefits. See the Weapon, Shield, & Armor Advanced Training Sidebar for more details.

Weapon, Shield, & Armor Advanced Training

Weapon, Shield, & Armor Advanced Trainings are further training in those particular fields, like the name implies. They can only be taken for things that you already have Basic Training in.

Each of these types of Training has two things: Tiers, which represent how broad your knowledge is (sort of like Suite Suites and Advanced Skills), and Ranks.

For instance, the Tiers of Armor training are All Armor, Armor Type, and Specific Armor. So, if you could gain a bonus for using all armors, or medium armors, or a single type of armor, like brigandine. Obviously, the broader the area of knowledge the more applications but the most expensive it’s going to be to increase your bonus with them.

Every type will also have areas you can specialize in. These are the same across the Tiers of that type of Advanced Training but you train in specific Tiers. So, Armor Advanced Training has Defense, Recovery Modifier, Encumbrance, and Initiative as its areas. Each purchase in that area gives them a Rank in one of the Tiers, which is a bonus that’s applied when applicable. This bonus can't bring a negative bonus above 0, however.

The price for increasing your Rank in an area is that Tier’s Cost + twice the current Rank.

You can’t buy up a Rank again for a given Tier until you’ve either filled out all the rest of the areas for that Tier or you have two areas in that Tier at the current highest Rank. If you want to get a Rank 5 in Encumbrance, you would have to buy up to a Rank 4 in a different category, Rank 3 in another, and Rank 2 in a final thing.

Ideally, that means that you bought Rank 4 in Init, Rank 3 in Recovery, and Rank 2 in Def. Notice that that additional 2 Ranks in Recovery and that Rank 1 in Def doesn’t give you anything besides allowing you to reach Rank 4 in Encumbrance.

Tiers don't stack with each other: you gain all the bonuses from a single Tier of your choice. If you have a +1 Attack and Damage in all melee weapons and a +1 to Defense and -1 to Recovery in polearms, while wielding a pike, you either gain the +1 Attack and Damage with it or +1 Defense and -1 Recovery, but not +1 Attack, Damage, Defense, and -1 Recovery.

Bonuses cascade down through tiers but only if they’re applicable.

UNARMED COMBAT ADVANCED TRAINING

While you can gain Advanced Trainings in Unarmed like other weapons, because it’s not treated exactly like a weapon, you’ll have different areas in which you can become specialized in it. It is also treated as Tier 2 with costs of 12 and 10 respectively.

AREAS Attack, Defense, Recovery, Effectiveness

Effectiveness is applied to the difference between the Attack and Defense for additional benefits if the attack hits. Every purchase adds +3 times the Tier to the total. So, Tier 3 in Effectiveness gives you a +9 to your total.

Additionally, regardless of the number of Advanced Trainings in Unarmed Combat, you’re never treated as if you have a weapon.

Ranged Weapon Firing Break Down

If you’re wondering what you’re doing at any given second when firing a ranged weapon: here’s a breakdown for you.

So, if you already loading and cranked (drawing) your crossbow, it would only take 5 seconds to fire it.

MISSILE RETRIEVAL This is drawing the missile from its quiver or pouch. For firearms, that means drawing out the powder and bullets.

FUSE This is the process of lighting the fuse. This can be dropped to 1 second but increases the chance of backfiring from 1 to 5.

LOADING This is knocking the arrow or loading the bullet and powder in the barrel.

DRAW This is drawing back the bow, cranking the crossbow, or cocking your arm for the throw. This can be prepped ahead of time for the belly- and crossbows but not the composite, long, or war bows

AIMING This is actually sighting your target and lining up the shot. This can be skipped, making it so you fire faster, but you roll attack on a d12! instead of the standard d20!. This can't bring a weapon below its minimum weapon Recovery.

FIRING This is pulling the trigger or loosing the arrow.

Advanced Training that lowers Recovery take from Missile Retrieval, Fuse, Loading, Drawing, and Aiming evenly but they can never reduce a single category to below 1 second. So, the minimum firing Recovery for a Composite Bow is 5 seconds.
