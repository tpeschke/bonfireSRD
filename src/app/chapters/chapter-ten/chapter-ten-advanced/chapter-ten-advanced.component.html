STARTING a COMBAT

Combat engages as soon as both sides are within eyesight and both know they the other intends harm.

The CLOCK

The Clock begins at 1, and then progresses to 2, 3, 4 etc, ad infinitum. When the Clock matches a character‚Äôs Initiative, they go. Each of these numbers represent 1 second of time passing. What second a character's next action is referred to as their initiative (or "where they are on the Clock")

This can be a difficult when you first start so we recommend that you start with only running a two or three enemies until you get the hang off it. We've also provided a tool that can help you keep track of the Clock and enemies easier. You can find it here.

STARTING INITIATIVE

Each side rolls an initiative die. The lower the die, the more prepared they are for the attack, though typically the instigator goes on second 1. Those that know the battle is about to start (like being warned by a predetermined sign) use a d4, and the completely surprised receive a d20, while everyone else typically falls on a dice between that.

ACTIONS

Every action takes a certain amount of time. On a character‚Äôs Initiative, they declare their action and are told how long that action will take. After that time, the action is complete.

So, if a character wants to walk across a room, which will take 8 seconds, and his Initiative is 5, once the Clock reaches 5, he starts walking and once it reaches 14, he‚Äôs gotten the other side of the room.

At any point, you may choose to forfeit your action and change your course. If, when half way across the room (at second 9), the character sees that his buddy is in trouble, he can immediately decide that, no, he‚Äôd rather be there, his character stops walking across the room and instead heads over to help his friend.

Keeping track of this can be tricky for new players unused to the system. To help players make the shift, we've created the Basic Combat Cheat Sheet that has a strip where you can keep track of when your next action is going to happen. It also includes handy references to other rules found in this chapter as well.

MOVEMENT While, for the most part, you‚Äôll be using your common sense to determine how long things will take, to unify at least one thing, movement rates are given below.

A person may move up 1 movement category or down 2 in a second.

When a person Runs or Sprints they must make a Fatigue check on a d20 if they're Running, or a d10 if they‚Äôre Sprinting.

This happens a number of seconds equal to their Endurance Rank after they start to at least Run. So, a character with an Endurance Rank of 3, who starts Sprinting on 2, needs to make a check on second 5, 8, 11, and so on.

A result equal to or under their Fatigue Score means they need to stop and catch their breath. They need to Walk for twice the amount of time they were Running or Sprinting for before Running or Sprinting again.

Every roll increases the person's Fatigue by 1 regardless of the results.

While Running or Sprinting, you're unable to attack or defend yourself

IMPROVISED WEAPONS

üúÇ Advanced Rule

It is entirely possible that you‚Äôll find yourself unarmed and must defend yourself with a chair leg or broken bottle. In these cases, treat is as a club but penalize it for 3 seconds and drop its Parry to 0 for not being balanced properly.

Now, it‚Äôs also possible that you have superhuman strength and so you can rip a door off its hinges and start bashing people with want and abandon. In this case, it‚Äôs entirely possible to do more than a zweihander in sheer concussive force, however, you‚Äôll find that because a door (or similar) isn‚Äôt properly weighted or balanced, you‚Äôll find yourself taking a long time to swing and unable to defend yourself properly. In extreme cases like these, the GM can give you a Defense Modifier of somewhere around -8 and the speed should never be quicker than 20 seconds.

On top of this, these wide swings make an otherwise threatening object easy to dodge and so less of a danger, meaning you may still be counted as unarmed while trying to use it.

WEAPON STYLES

üúÇ Advanced Rule

The way your character wields a weapon can tell a lot about their priorities in a combat and a weapon can be versatile in the extreme if some thought is put into how its held.

1 HANDED WEAPON WITH SHIELD This is the standard way your character is assumed to be wielding their weapon.

1 HANDED WEAPON WITH FREE SECOND HAND With one hand free, the character can focus a bit more on their defense and seizing openings. They gain a +2 to their Parry and -1 to their Speed.

2 1 HANDED WEAPONS Using a 1 handed weapon in both hands is a good tactic against a single opponent as it can give a small defense bonus and allow the character to make a few extra strikes.

First, if the off-hand weapon is smaller than your size, add its Parry to your main weapon but only against 1 non-flanking foe.

Dual wielding in such a manner also allows you to gain an extra attack, if you so choose. The secondary weapon has a speed of its unmodified weapon speed + 3 but doing so causes you to lose the extra Parry from the off hand until it can attack again.

You can only switch Tactics on your primary attack.

Non-Ambidextrous people receive a +3 Speed penalty to their primary weapon and the secondary attacks with a d12!.

If the weapon off-hand weapon is as large as you, you can only attack with it.

2 HANDING 1 HANDED WEAPONS Using two hands where one is sufficient allows you to leverage more of your strength and deal a harsher blow but leaves you open.

When doing this move on a weapon, you increase the damage dice as if you were Power Attacking with it, however, you swap out the bonuses for a +3 to Speed and Max Strength.

This style can‚Äôt be effectively used in combat on weapons smaller than your size.

DOUBLE SHIELDS While it gives no offensive benefit, the double shields style allows you to cover more facings to gain a greater defense. If two shields have overlapping faces, use the higher of the two's Parry.

WEAPON IN YOUR OFF HAND This may come about because your main hand is disabled or you just want to show off. But this implies the main hand is free. If not, see the 2 1 Handed Weapons style.

Non-Ambidextrous people attack with a d12! And gain a +5 speed penalty to off-handed attacks.

2 HANDS FREE (NO WEAPON) If you have no weapons in combat, you‚Äôre not really seen as a threat and so an attacker doesn‚Äôt really have to do the dance to try to avoid you blows while trying to strike you.

In this style, your opponent attacks at half their weapon speed and they get a free attack whenever you try and close in an attack them. That includes moving ranges.

Dropping Your Weapon to Grapple

Unarmed moves are more effective with two hands rather than one so you may decide to drop your weapon to try and grapple your opponent easier. If the unarmed attack is on the same second that you drop your weapon, your opponent doesn‚Äôt receive the free attack on you.

The COMBAT

It is inevitable that if you get into combat, you‚Äôll end up engaged in a melee with your enemy.

ZONES of ENGAGEMENT: MEASURING YOUR OPPONENT

There are 4 positions that you can have relative to your enemy. These positions aren‚Äôt reciprocal by nature: you can be threatened by someone and not have them be threatened by you.

NOT ENGAGED This is basically the default. You are too far away for them to reasonable do anything.

THREATENED This means that you‚Äôre within 5 feet + your opponent‚Äôs weapon‚Äôs measure. From here, if you move faster than a Jog and if they‚Äôre within half their weapon speed, they can attack you.

If you go from Engaged to Threatened, your weapon initiative doesn't reset, so if you become Engaged again for any reason before you weapon's speed, you may not attack.

ENGAGED You‚Äôve closed the distance and are now actually at their weapon‚Äôs measure. If your weapons‚Äô measures are within 1 foot of each other, you may attack freely, with the person with the longer measure going first.

If both weapons are exactly the same measure, the attacks happen simultaneously.

If one weapon is longer than the other by more than a foot, the longer weapon may attack his enemy, but the shorter weapon has to get in their guard to attack. He can do so by knocking aside his opponent‚Äôs weapon, who defends as normal vs his standard attack roll.

IN THEIR GUARD If a person with a short measure weapon successfully knocks the longer weapon aside, he can move into their guard.

Assuming that the shorter weapon fighter is engaging his opponent and not simply threatening him (which may very well be the case if it‚Äôs a knife vs pike), he can attack freely.

However, the longer weapon is now at a disadvantage and may only defend with a d12!. All damage dice is also reduced by one size (3d4! ‚Üí 3d3!).

Changing Position

Note that your Position has to do with relative distance from your opponent and their weapon so can change by any tactic that forces moves a fighter. For instance, a dagger fighter in a spearman‚Äôs guard who is forced to give ground, change the spearman to Engaged and the dagger fighter to simply Threatened.

MELEE ATTACK & DEFENSE

Basic melee attacks always roll a base d20! + any attack modifiers, while defenders either rolls a d20! + any defense modifiers

PERFECT ATTACKS & DEFENSES

üúÇ Advanced Rule

A Perfect Attack or Defense is one that puts you in a better position to win. A Perfect Attack occurs on an unmodified roll of 20 on your Attack roll, while a Perfect Defense is an unmodified 20 on your Defense roll, but only if your final totals are still greater than you opponent‚Äôs. If not, the attack still hits for Perfect Attacks and you still dodge the blow for Perfect Defenses but you don‚Äôt gain the auxiliary benefits.

If both those qualifiers are met you gain the following benefits on top of the normal ones:

PERFECT ATTACK You gain a free automatically successful Unarmed Combat attack. If you have Masteries in Unarmed Combat, you may roll for the additional benefit, but even on a failure, you gain the move‚Äôs base benefit.

You may also Shift with no penalty and change your Tactic.

If you‚Äôre firing a ranged weapon, you don‚Äôt receive the Unarmed attack, however you managed to hit a sensitive area, lowering their max movement speed by one category, minimum Crawl speed.

PERFECT DEFENSE You gain a free automatically successful Unarmed Combat attack. If you have Masteries in Unarmed Combat, you may roll for the additional benefit, but even on a failure, you gain the move‚Äôs benefit.

You may also Shift with no penalty and change your Tactic.

Additionally, you gain a chance to riposte, gaining a free full attack the next second if you‚Äôre wielding a melee weapon. This attack doesn‚Äôt affect your current count.

NEAR PERFECT ATTACKS AND DEFENCE An unmodified roll of 19 on either Attack or Defense results in a Near Perfect Attack or Defense, where you may Shift for no penalty, change your Tactic, or, for attacks, gain an extra d4! to damage, or, for defenses gained a free riposte with your off hand weapon if it‚Äôs smaller than your size.

RANGED ATTACK & DEFENSE

Ranged attacks function identical to melee attacks, only the range also gets factored in.

Defense rolls, however, are a d20! if you‚Äôre sprinting, a flat d12! if you‚Äôre at least moving, or a flat d4! if standing still. Defenders receive no defense bonus other than the Cover from their shield.

COVER

üúÇ Advanced Rule

It‚Äôs a matter of the difference in size between what you‚Äôre trying to hit and what‚Äôs behind it and if the thing is moving or not.

Cover dice for other objects works the same as shields - if you‚Äôre behind cover, you add a roll of that dice to your defense. If you‚Äôre actively crouching behind it, you add that max it would give you to your defense.

To find out what‚Äôs the appropriate dice, you look at the difference in size and what it is. A 6ft wall, for example, will be better cover than a 6ft person.

For solid objects, such as walls, where it is easier to calculate how much of the person is covered, a flat bonus is appropriate, which each 5% of coverage adding a cumulative +1 for every 5% before it. So standing behind a brick that only gives 5% cover will result in a +1, but standing behind a low wall that gives 25% cover will give you a +15 (1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5).

For people, tree, a moving object, or really anything that‚Äôs harder to calculate, you‚Äôll use a dice backed on the difference in size, starting at a d20. Each difference in size shrinks or increases that cover die by 1 size.

So, two medium size people standing one in front of the other would give the guy in the back d20 cover, same as two small sized people. If the person in the front is small sized and the guy in the back is still medium sized, the guy in the back only gains a d12 cover.

If the person is actively moving or trying not to be your cover, you can‚Äôt gain the bonus for actively crouching behind them.

RANGED MISSES

üúÇ Advanced Rule

On a miss with a ranged weapon, the missile doesn‚Äôt magically disappear into oblivion. There‚Äôs a good chance it might hit someone else, especially if your target is locked in melee.

In the case of a miss, roll a d20! and a d4. The d4 determines to which side the missile strays - an odd result means it goes left, an even result means it goes right.

The d20! determines how many feet it strays from the target. Draw an imaginary line between the archer and that point. Everyone within 5ft of that line needs to roll a defense vs the original attack roll, starting with the person closest to the archer and ending with the furthest possible target.

Inanimate objects may provide enough cover to be considered targets in both Firing into Combat and Ranged Misses, such as a large boulder or tree. In which case, medium sized objects roll a d12!, with each size lower decreasing the dice size by 2, and each size larger increasing the dice size by 2.

FIRING INTO MELEE

üúÇ Advanced Rule

If there‚Äôs any one within 5 ft of a direct line between the origin of the missile and the target, they‚Äôll have to roll the appropriate defense die vs the original attack roll to see if the missile hits them instead, starting with the person closest to the archer or gunner and ending with the intended target.

All the normal rolls apply so if the missile only misses because of cover, the missile has lodged itself in the cover and goes no further.

DEALING DAMAGE

Once both sides have rolled and added all modifiers, compare the totals against each other.

If the defender‚Äôs is equal to or greater than the attacker, the attack misses.

However, if it‚Äôs not, the attack lands and the attacker rolls his weapon‚Äôs damage die and adds any appropriate damage modifiers. The defender than subtracts their Damage Reduction and the remaining total is subtracted from their Health and added to their list of wounds.

PARRYING If the attack roll is within the Parry Threshold of the defender‚Äôs total, the defender may choose to deflect some of the incoming attack.

For non-piercing weapons, The attacker rolls their regular damage for their weapon (if applicable) and subtracts the shield or weapon‚Äôs DR and then their armor‚Äôs DR. Any left over is still done to the defender.

So, a defender with a Parry Threshold of 4, and total of 10 for their defense roll vs a attack roll of 14, could choose to parry the blow, taking less damage.

Either way, if the attacker chooses to remain in engaged, he then adds his weapon‚Äôs Speed to the current second on the Clock. Once the Clock reaches that new number, he may attack again.

KNOCK-BACKS

üúÇ Advanced Rule

If the pre-DR damage is higher than your enemy‚Äôs Health Size Modifier + 3 for every level of Encumbrance you have, the attack also moves them back 5 feet. If it‚Äôs twice the damage needed, they get knocked back 10 feet, and so on.

GAINING FATIGUE IN COMBAT

As the battle drags on, character‚Äôs body and spirit become more and more fatigued. Their swings become slower and have less impact, and they don‚Äôt dodge as quickly as before. This is represented by Fatigue.

If a character rolls under his Fatigue Score on his defense, the character gains a level of Fatigue.

HEALTH & DAMAGE

Your character‚Äôs Health will probably slowly drain over the course of combat and as that happens, he‚Äôll be a less effective fighter.

At each wound category above Unharmed, a character automatically receives 1 level of Fatigue. So a character who‚Äôs Wounded is fighting with a penalty of 4 to his Speed, Defense, Damage, Attack, Fatigue Score and Skill Checks (assuming he isn‚Äôt Fatigued from doing anything else in the fight).

TRAUMA

If someone does enough damage in one hit for you to skip a Wound Category (25% of their Health), you‚Äôll have to roll a Trauma check, which is a Con check.

Failure means that your character has been overcome with pain to the point that he‚Äôs unable to act, leaving him completely defenseless for a number of seconds equal to how much he failed by times 3.

Double & Triple Trauma

A character that gets dealt 2 Wound Categories worth of damage in a single blow has suffered a grievous wound - the scar of which, if he survives, will be with him until the day he dies.

When this happens, the character rolls a 1d20. On any result other than 1 or 20, the character increases the number of scars they have by 1. On a 20, increase that number by 2.

But on a 1, the character has suffered such great trauma, that they gain a new Physical Flaw rolled randomly. They gain no CrP for it.

Battle scars are marks of pride and honor. Characters who become scared gain a d4 Trait ‚ÄúBattle Scars‚Äù. When their number of scars increases to more than the max value of that dice, increase that Trait by 1 dice size. So, a person who gains his 5th scar increases ‚ÄúBattle Scars‚Äù to a d6 while someone getting their 9th, increases it from a d8 to a d10.

Should the character suffer 3 Wound Categories worth of damage, the trauma is too much and they simply die where they stand if they fail their check. Even if they succeed, they‚Äôre still suffer Trauma for the next 30 seconds.

GRIT

üúÇ Advanced Rule

There are times when you have to reach deep down inside and find the hero within. Pushing through the pain to line up that perfect shot. This is what it means to have Grit.

On Trauma and Attack rolls, characters may choose to reach deep down and push through. If they do so, they automatically succeed at their check, but gain an amount of Fatigue equal to how much they failed by, though they do gain at least 1 even if they succeed. This needs to be declared before the roll.

Note: if you're using Grit for an Attack and you gain enough Fatigue to have your character pass out, he does so after the rest of the Attack is resolved.

COUP de GRACE

A helpless enemy is one completely at your mercy. If a character is completely helpless, either from failing a Trauma check, being bound, or deep asleep, a character can kill the target instantly. This move takes 10 seconds to complete as it takes a bit of positioning to pull off. The character‚Äôs who throat is being slit gives out a cry of pain as they expire.

FACING & FIGHTING MULTIPLE PEOPLE

The likelihood is that at some point you‚Äôll want to be flanking or someone with want to flank you, to try and gain an advantage.

You have one primary front at your basic defense (d20!). This front is directly in front of you. Every additional person after the first that engages you, however, can take advantage of your split attention, forcing you to roll a d12! for your defense verses their attacks.

This penalty can be mitigated through the use of shields. Small shields allow you to defend against an additional assailant with your standard defense, while Medium and Large ones allow you to defend against an extra 2 and 3, respectively.

If a person is coming up straight behind you, and taking advantage of that blindspot, you only defend with a d8!, however, only one person can really fit in that space.

A maximum of 8 people at a time can effectively attack person at once, assuming that they‚Äôre out in the open. After that, every assailant‚Äôs attack die is lowered by 1 size per extra person.

TACTICS

üúÇ Advanced Rule

These rules add another layer of strategy to combat and should be implemented as soon as you have the basics down.

A combatant utilizing these moves can only commit to one at a time, unless otherwise stated and can only switch on their attack unless it says otherwise.

ACTIVE DODGING This move relays on actively focusing on dodging against incoming attacks.

A defender commits to how much he wants to focus on dodging incoming attacks, choosing from a bonus to his defense in the range of +1 to +5, but netting an equal penalty to his attacks (-1 to -5).

Additionally, this lowers the range for Near Perfect Defenses by 1, so you score them on a base natural 18 - 19. This tactic needs to be committed to for at least your full weapon speed. After that you may continue using it and change tactics at any point.

Active Dodging can be combined with Retreat and Step Back, adding their defense and attack penalties on top of each other.

CALLED SHOT By carefully aiming your shot, a character can reduce the effects of heavy armor by taking more time to target gaps. When employing this tactic, a character halves their effective number of damage dice (rounded up) for considering /D DR, however, it takes a bit longer to set up the strike and so they increase their speed by 3 seconds.

Crushing weapons are unable to employ this tactic.

CHARGE A character moving at least a Run can throw their full force behind a blow, knocking their opponent off their guard. If the Attacker has reach, the damage for their next attack is considered doubled for the purposes of determining Knock-Backs, though they defend with a d12! for half their weapon Speed.

A charge is a terrifying tactic: should the blow land, the target‚Äôs Morale drops by 1 for every point the attacker hits by.

CHOKING UP ON YOUR WEAPON When you‚Äôve choked up on your weapon, your weapon‚Äôs Measure is halved, and you add that number, rounded down to its speed.

However, if an opponent is in your guard and you choke up on your weapon, you defend with a standard 1d20! and deal full weapon damage.

You‚Äôre unable to employ this tactic twice if someone again gets within your guard.

FEINT When performing this move, you (hopefully) get your enemy to commit his guard to a different location than you plan on hitting.

Before you roll, if you declare a Feint, you receive a +5 to that Attack but receive a -3 to Defense until your next attack.

FLURRY of BLOWS This tactic involves releasing a rain of quick attacks that rely more on speed than accuracy or power.

While employing this tactic, you attack 3 seconds quicker, however, you suffer a -3 to Attack, your damage doesn‚Äôt explode, and attacks inflict a level of Fatigue if the attack die rolls under your Fatigue Score.

JAB A strike that is meant to purposely to open your opponent‚Äôs guard and get them off their balance by making them shift their defense.

The Jab attack happens at half weapon speed (rounded up) for Piercing and Slashing weapons. Instead of a d20!, the defender uses a d12! but on a successful hit, no damage is done, and the defender nets a -1 to their next full defense against a non-jab attack. This penalty doubles with each jab, however, it only lasts until the defender‚Äôs next full defense.

Crushing weapons may use this tactic but it‚Äôs not any faster than their regular speed.

LONG GUARD By placing your weapon further out in front of you and trying to line the body of the weapon with the defender‚Äôs eye line, this may allow a person with a short weapon the first strike.

While using this tactic, you increase your weapon‚Äôs measure by 1/2 ft but you reduce your damage‚Äôs largest die by 1 size.

Crushing weapons are unable to employ this tactic.

POWER ATTACK This is the fighting equivalent of baseball batting your enemy, swinging with all your might.

With this attack, you receive a -3 to your Attack and next defense but your damage increases

Piercing weapons increase 1 die by 1 size. Slashing weapons increase all dice by 1 size and crushing weapons increase its highest die by 2 sizes.

RETREAT This is a defensive tactic that involves moving directly back at jog or at a 45 degree angle from your opponent‚Äôs blows at a walk.

After your defense roll, if you declare a Retreat, you receive a +5 to your next Defense roll but also a -5 to your next Attack and must continue to move back until your next attack, either at a jog, if moving directly back, or at a walk, if started to move at a 45 degree angle.

If your opponent is using the Flurry of Blows, Feint, or the Power Attack tactic, if they do hit, you only receive half damage

SHIFT You move at your walking speed to either side of your opponent, but suffer a -1 to your next Attack and Defense for every 5 ft you travel before your next Attack or Defense.

However, if you Shift with another tactic, you don‚Äôt receive this penalty and gain a +1 that can be either used to increase a bonus or decrease a penalty from that tactic. You may do this freely with any tactic except Retreat and Step Back.

Shifting is also used to changed Facings.

STEP BACK This is a defensive tactic that involves moving directly back from your opponent‚Äôs blows at your walking speed.

Before your defense roll, if you declare a Step Back, you receive a +5 to your next Defense roll but also a -2 to your next Attack and must continue to move back at a walking speed until your next attack.

If your opponent is using the Flurry of Blows, Faint, or the Power Attack tactic, if they do hit, you only receive half damage.

Retreat v. Step Back

While they may seem similar on the surface, these two different tactics have some key differences I thought I‚Äôd point out so that you can utilize each better.

1. Step Back must be declared before you roll your defense while Retreat can be declared after, meaning that if you see you rolled low before employing them.

2. Step Back moves your character straight back, while Retreat allows you to move up to 45¬∞ to the left and right, meaning that if you‚Äôre going to be backed against a wall, it‚Äôs an option to try and skirt your opponent.

3. Retreat may be done at a jogging speed, while Step Back is always at a walk.

STRIKE THE WEAPON This has two purposes: to either knock an opponent's weapon aside to delay their attack or to try and shatter your opponent‚Äôs weapon.

The defender rolls a standard defense die vs the attacker‚Äôs regular attack roll.

Should the attack succeed, he delays the defender‚Äôs next attack by a number of seconds equal to 1 for every 3 full points he scored above the defender‚Äôs total.

If the difference between the attack and defense was greater than 15, the attacker has a chance of breaking the opponent‚Äôs weapon

The defender‚Äôs weapon is treated as a shield using the Shield Breakage rules, with the attacker dealing his damage to the weapon.

UNARMED COMBAT

üúÇ Advanced Rule

Unarmed Combat‚Äôs primary use is to either disable or control your foe, thus winning the battle by maneuvering, rather than raw damage output.

To use an unarmed attack, roll an attack on a d10! but also add your Damage Modifier. Your opponent rolls a regular defense (including shield unless otherwise stated) but also adds their Damage Modifier as well.

If you hit, you successfully pulled off the move and gain the benefit.

If the difference between the attack and defense totals were more than the target‚Äôs Health Size Modifier, you may gain additional benefits listed in the move. If the difference is double the Target‚Äôs Health Size Modifier, they may immediately make a second unarmed combat attack for free the same second.

If you‚Äôre doing the move or defending with two hands, roll your attack on a d20!.

GRAB This move is meant to disable or gain control of a limb or item, whether that‚Äôs their belt, their leg, or their sword.

The target must be declared before the roll. Attempting to grab a shield means the target subtracts their shield‚Äôs Fatigue Adjustment from their roll as well since a larger shield is easier to grab.

The exact bonus will depend on the situation but here are some guidelines:

Weapon: The defender gets a free attack if the move isn‚Äôt successful but when grabbed it means the defender can no longer attack.

Shield: The defender no longer gains any bonus from their shield but still has the defense and Fatigue penalties.

Arm: See Weapon if it‚Äôs the weapon‚Äôs arm and Shield if it‚Äôs the shield‚Äôs arm. The defender also defends on a d12 vs Pushes and can‚Äôt change their facing willingly.

Leg: Moving above a crawl is impossible and moving at all can only be done after a successful opposed Str check vs d20! + the grabber‚Äôs strength.

The grabber defends with a d8! unless they‚Äôre employing something to let them keep their distance (a lasso or mancatch, for example). The grabber only defends with a d8! against Throws/Tackles. Their defense dice is lowered by 1 for all facings.

A second grab on a non-body part works as a disarm, wrenching the item free unless secured by something strong, though at the very least, the thing is no longer in hand. This is also done on an additional benefit from a well executed move if the attacker wishes.

PUSH By pushing you opponent in just the right way, you can move them or shift them in the direction you wish.

On a successful Push, your target changes one facing or moves 5 ft to the left, right, or directly back. You may immediately Shift to follow them without penalty.

You gain an additional facing change or 5 ft of movement on a well executed Push.

THROW/TACKLE This move attempts to throw your opponent to the ground and is easier to pull of if you go prone with them.

For this move, you can increase the attack dice size by 1 if you‚Äôre at running speed when you Tackle them and another if you choose to go to the ground with them, however, if you lose the struggle, you still end up on the ground.

Getting forced to the ground for you opponent resets their Count and reduces their Attack and Defense dies to a d8! if they‚Äôre wielding a Medium sized or larger weapon.

The extra bonus for this move is being able to remain standing if you so choose and possibly disarming them if they fail a Str check vs d20! + your Str.

STRIKE A strike is a punch with any part of your body or shield. It‚Äôs not really about doing damage but more about throwing your opponent off.

On a successful Strike, you delay your opponent‚Äôs next attack by 1d4 seconds, though if you‚Äôre using a shield to strike, you subtract that shield‚Äôs Speed Defense to that amount, delaying them further.

The extra bonus for this move is that it increases the time to a d8!.

BLEEDING OUT & DYING

A character whose Health is below 0 is bleeding out and is danger of dying. He can still move, attack, and basically function if he's still above his Fatigue.

Every 5 seconds that character is in this state, he rolls a Con check + his current Health vs d20! + 10. If he fails, he takes another point of damage. If he succeeds, he doesn‚Äôt lose any Health that second.

If he beats the roll by more than 10, he stabilizes: he no longer has to roll for bleeding out.

Should his Health reach half his Con in the negative (whether from bleeding out or additional Wounds), he dies. So, a character with 12 Con dies at -6, while one with 8 dies at -4.

MORALE

üúÇ Advanced Rule

CALCULATING MORALE THRESHOLDS

A strong willed or well disciplined person, when faced with insurmountable odds will often fighter long after the cowards have run, either gaining victory or assuring his death. This is represented by a character‚Äôs Morale.

Morale is represented by 2 thresholds: Panicked and Broken, which correlate to Wound Categories. Which Wound Categories are dictated by that character‚Äôs Honor. The more Honorable a foe is, the less likely they are to flee or act hastily.

PANICKED is when a character starts to make mistakes more often based fear, rather than logic. They can only put up 1 dice without Repercussions in a Morale Conflict, instead of 2.

BROKEN is when a character has completely succumbed to fear. They can‚Äôt put up any dice without Repercussions in a Morale Conflict, instead of 2. They also have a chance of making a break for it.

From there, events and actions may increase or decrease these thresholds by 1 Health. This can be from Stress or what‚Äôs going on on the battlefield currently.

Record this for your character by circling the Wound Category in your Health Section.

For example, let's say there‚Äôs a fighter with Average Honor and 26 Health, which means, baseline, he Panics when he‚Äôs taken a total of 13 damage and Routes when he‚Äôs taken 19 damage.

Now let‚Äôs say that he‚Äôs fighting goblins on the top of a fortified hill. Because he‚Äôs in a really good position (he has the high ground), he feels pretty confident. This raises his Morale by 3 Health. He now Panics at 16 damage and won‚Äôt Route until he‚Äôs taken 22 damage.

Additionally, a character can make a Rally check to keep a fighter from Panicking or Routeing, temporarily raising their Morale thresholds by 1 Health per point they succeeded by. If the enemy has a leader, that Rally check is opposed by them.

MORALE CONFLICTS

Combat is not simply about who can do the most damage the quickest. Battles, at their heart, are manifestations of the fever of conflict going on between the two sides. This is why sometimes a small, untrained force of zealots can overcome a larger professional army sometimes.

This is partly represented by Morale, which is a metric for how a given individual is doing, but it‚Äôs also represented by a Social Conflict happening during the combat, or a Morale Conflict.

At the beginning of combat, a Conflict is also started, but unlike a regular Conflict, the two opposing sides are the two opposing forces (the players and the goblins, or the bandits, etc.).

Like a normal Conflict, you‚Äôll have a dice pool but you‚Äôll also have additional places you can get Leverage from such as having a seasoned commander, outnumbering the opposing side, having a common Devotion to why they‚Äôre fighting and so on.

Here are common things that can give you Leverage in a Morale Conflict:

OUTNUMBERING THE ENEMY You gain a d6 every time you can double their number. So, you gain a d6 for 2 vs 1, 2d6 for 3 vs 1 and nothing for 3 vs 2, 2d6 for 4 vs 2, 3d6 for 6 vs 2 and so on.

CATCHING AN ENEMY BY SURPRISE This nets you a d8.

HAVING YOU BATTLE STANDARD UP AND VISIBLE Nets you a d8.

CAUSING AN ENEMY TO PANIC Nets you a d6.

CAUSING AN ENEMY TO BECOME BROKEN Nets you a d8. This can increase the amount of dice you gain from outnumbering your enemy.

CAUSING AN ENEMY TO FLEE Nets you a d12. This can increase the amount of dice you gain from outnumbering your enemy.

PARTY CAPTAIN‚ÄôS CHARISMA You also gain your party captain‚Äôs Social Dice from Charisma in this case. Also, any abilities that the Party Captain has that effect Conflicts can also be used during this Conflict.

A PERFECT ATTACKS & DEFENSE This nets you a d6.

FUMBLING This shrinks one of your dice by 1 size. If the dice is a d4, it removes it completely. If it‚Äôs larger than that, the dice are rerolled at the lower size. Fumbler‚Äôs choice.

As the battle progresses, you‚Äôre going to run mini-Conflicts across the board to try and swing the battle to your advantage.

Drawing from the pool, you can state that you want a fighter to perform an action or behave in a certain way. So, for instance, you want him to switch his Tactic to be more aggressive, or swap who he‚Äôs aiming for. The only exception is you can‚Äôt make them run away.

You ante up two dice from your Morale dice pool and your opponent does the same. This is just like a normal Conflict except that it‚Äôs only one exchange, instead of an escalating one.

If the fighter you‚Äôre trying to swap is Panicked, they can only put up 1 dice without suffering Repercussions, and if they‚Äôre Broken, they can‚Äôt put up any without Repercussions.

If you win, the fighter behaves the way you want. This represents either them being too panicked or not having enough information to make a good decision, or effective leadership and a clear grasp of what‚Äôs going on.

If you have no dice left in your pool, you can‚Äôt use Morale offensively but you do still have a chance of not making a dumb decision, so each character gets an endless pool of d4s that represent their Inner Resolve that they can roll and they can pull from them, regardless of how full the Morale pool is. The commander can also use the Leverage from his Rally Skill to substitute for one of these d4s, should there be a clear line of sight or hearing.

Instead of being rolled ahead of time, however, they‚Äôre rolled on the spot and ponied up in that moment.

If the character is below his Broken Threshold and has to rely on this inner resolve but ends up with half of what the other side anted up, he routes and flees for his life. If cornered and given to space to run, he enters a frenzy - only using aggressive Tactics and becoming immune to Fatigue penalties, though gaining 10 Stress per second he‚Äôs in this state.

ADDING DICE TO YOUR POOL You can gain dice to your pool as the battle progresses, such as when you score a Perfect Attack or when an enemy becomes Panicked, but also from anything you can think of, such as surrounding your enemy or trying to intimidate a foe that one of your party members is locked in combat with.

Any time this happens, this is counted as an escalation, but, each party member has to put 1 Honor on the table. Any Honor on the table at the end of Combat is divided evenly between all the members on the winning side. Players can keep any dice they could bring in aside and bring them in all at once, thus counting as only one escalation.

DICE POOL EXCEPTIONS

Generally things won‚Äôt cancel each other out. So, having two people with 15 Str on each side of the combat means that both add a d6 to their respective pool.

Additionally, having two people with 15 Str on the same side means you‚Äôd add 2d6 to your pool.

This is a general rule for adding dice to the collective pool but there many be exceptions but your GM may rule on that case by case.

Who Controls the Pool

It may be the case that the Party Leader, who should be responsible for the Morale Dice Pool is also your main line fighter and is already busy juggling an enemy or two.

You can have a player who runs a less combat intense character, such as the magic user or diplomat, be in charge of the Morale Dice Pool in that case and decide where to risk dice, however, only the actually Party Leader‚Äôs abilities affect the number or size of those dice are applied.

ENDING COMBAT

Combat ends when one side is incapacitated, reached a truce, or have fled the field. At that point the Clock stops and play moves back to narrative time.
