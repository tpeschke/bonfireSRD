STARTING a COMBAT

Combat engages as soon as both sides are within eyesight and both know they the other intends harm.

The CLOCK

The Clock begins at 1, and then progresses to 2, 3, 4 etc, ad infinitum. When the Clock matches a character‚Äôs Initiative, they go. Each of these numbers represent 1 second of time passing. What second a character's next action is referred to as their initiative (or "where they are on the Clock")

This can be a difficult when you first start so we recommend that you start with only running a two or three enemies until you get the hang off it. We've also provided a tool that can help you keep track of the Clock and enemies easier. You can find it here.

STARTING INITIATIVE & SURPRISE

Each side rolls an initiative die. The lower the die, the more prepared they are for the attack, though typically the instigator goes on second 1. Those that know the battle is about to start (like being warned by a predetermined sign) use a d4, and the completely surprised receive a d20, while everyone else typically falls on a dice between that.

Those whose initiative hasn‚Äôt come up are surprised and continue to do what they were doing as if nothing is wrong. They can take no other actions: they‚Äôre completely unaware of what‚Äôs going on.

Should someone attack them during this time, they are completely helpless, defending on a d8!.

If the surprised person is hit, the pain quickly brings them into the reality of the situation and they may act 2 seconds later (or before that if their initiative was the next second).

ACTIONS

Every action takes a certain amount of time. On a character‚Äôs Initiative, they declare their action and are told how long that action will take. After that time, the action is complete.

So, if a character wants to walk across a room, which will take 8 seconds, and his Initiative is 5, once the Clock reaches 5, he starts walking and once it reaches 14, he‚Äôs gotten the other side of the room.

At any point, you may choose to forfeit your action and change your course. If, when half way across the room (at second 9), the character sees that his buddy is in trouble, he can immediately decide that, no, he‚Äôd rather be there, his character stops walking across the room and instead heads over to help his friend.

Keeping track of this can be tricky for new players unused to the system. To help players make the shift, we've created the Basic Combat Cheat Sheet that has a strip where you can keep track of when your next action is going to happen. It also includes handy references to other rules found in this chapter as well.

MOVEMENT While, for the most part, you‚Äôll be using your common sense to determine how long things will take, to unify at least one thing, movement rates are given below.

A person may move up 1 movement category or down 2 in a second.

Running can be kept up for 5 Seconds + Endurance Rank. If you continue to Run after that you take d4 damage at the beginning of every 5 Seconds + Endurance Rank period.

Sprinting can be kept up for Endurance Rank - Stress Penalty in Seconds. If continue to Sprint you take d4! damage at the beginning of every Endurance Rank - Stress Penalty period.

You can push your character as hard as you'd like, continuing to take damage until they die from fatigue but if you take damage from Running or Sprinting and you stop, you can‚Äôt Run or Sprint again until you take a breather.

Also, moving from a Run to Sprint, or vice versa, doesn‚Äôt reset that count, so don‚Äôt think you can skip taking damage by moving from a Sprint to a Run, you cheeky devil.

While Running or Sprinting, you're unable to attack or defend yourself

IMPROVISED WEAPONS

üúÇ Advanced Rule

It is entirely possible that you‚Äôll find yourself unarmed and must defend yourself with a chair leg or broken bottle. In these cases, treat is as a club but penalize it for 3 seconds and drop its Parry to 0 for not being balanced properly.

Now, it‚Äôs also possible that you have superhuman strength and so you can rip a door off its hinges and start bashing people with want and abandon. In this case, it‚Äôs entirely possible to do more than a zweihander in sheer concussive force, however, you‚Äôll find that because a door (or similar) isn‚Äôt properly weighted or balanced, you‚Äôll find yourself taking a long time to swing and unable to defend yourself properly. In extreme cases like these, the GM can give you a Defense Modifier of somewhere around -8 and the Recovery should never be quicker than 20 seconds.

On top of this, these wide swings make an otherwise threatening object easy to dodge and so less of a danger, meaning you may still be counted as unarmed while trying to use it.

WEAPON STYLES

üúÇ Advanced Rule

The way your character wields a weapon can tell a lot about their priorities in a combat and a weapon can be versatile in the extreme if some thought is put into how its held.

1 HANDED WEAPON WITH SHIELD This is the standard way your character is assumed to be wielding their weapon.

1 HANDED WEAPON WITH FREE SECOND HAND With one hand free, the character can focus a bit more on their defense and seizing openings. They gain a +2 to their Parry and -1 to their Recovery.

2 1 HANDED WEAPONS (without Ambidextrous Mastery) If attacking with both, your primary hand attacks with a d12! while your secondary attacks with a d8!. You only receive your main hand‚Äôs Parry, if any. The secondary attacks slightly slower; always attacking a number of seconds equal to its Recovery after the primary makes its attacks.

When defending with one hand, you defend against the first attack against you with a d20!, using the Parry value of the off-hand weapon.

You can only switch Tactics on your primary attack.

If the weapon off-hand weapon is as large as you, you can only attack with it.

2 1 HANDED WEAPONS (with Ambidextrous Mastery) f attacking with both, both attack with a d20!. You only receive your main hand‚Äôs Parry, if any. The secondary attacks slightly slower; always attacking a number of seconds equal to its Recovery after the primary makes its attacks.

When defending with one, you defend against the first attack against you with a d20!, using the full Parry of one and half the Parry of the other, added together (the combination is your choice).

You can only switch Tactics and Weapon Styles on your primary attack.

If the weapon off-hand is as large as you, you can only attack with it.

2 HANDING 1 HANDED WEAPONS Using two hands where one is sufficient allows you to leverage more of your strength and deal a harsher blow but slows you down.

When using this style, you increase your damage by +4, however, you swap out the bonuses for a +3 to Recovery.

This style can‚Äôt be effectively used in combat on weapons smaller than your size.

DOUBLE SHIELDS While it gives no offensive benefit, the double shields style allows you to cover more facings to gain a greater defense. If two shields have overlapping faces, use the higher of the two's Parry.

WEAPON IN YOUR OFF HAND This may come about because your main hand is disabled or you just want to show off. But this implies the main hand is free. If not, see the 2 1 Handed Weapons style.

Non-Ambidextrous people attack with a d12! And gain a +5 Recovery penalty to off-handed attacks.

2 HANDS FREE (NO WEAPON) If you have no weapons in combat, you‚Äôre not really seen as a threat and so an attacker doesn‚Äôt really have to do the dance to try to avoid you blows while trying to strike you.

In this style, your opponent attacks at half their weapon Recovery and they get a free attack whenever you try and close in an attack them. That includes moving ranges.

Dropping Your Weapon to Grapple

Unarmed moves are more effective with two hands rather than one so you may decide to drop your weapon to try and grapple your opponent easier. If the unarmed attack is on the same second that you drop your weapon, your opponent doesn‚Äôt receive the free attack on you.

The COMBAT

It is inevitable that if you get into combat, you‚Äôll end up engaged in a melee with your enemy.

ZONES of ENGAGEMENT: MEASURING YOUR OPPONENT

There are 3 positions that you can have relative to your enemy. These positions aren‚Äôt reciprocal by nature: you can be threatened by someone and not have them be threatened by you.

NOT ENGAGED This is basically the default. You are too far away for them to reasonable do anything.

THREATENED This means that you‚Äôre within 5 feet + your opponent‚Äôs weapon‚Äôs measure. From here, if you move faster than a Jog and if they‚Äôre within half their weapon Recovery, they can attack you.

If you go from Engaged to Threatened, your weapon initiative doesn't reset, so if you become Engaged again for any reason before your weapon's Recovery, you may not attack.

ENGAGED You‚Äôve closed the distance and can actually make an attack. The person with the highest weapon measure attacks first. If both weapons are exactly the same measure, the attacks happen simultaneously.

MELEE ATTACK & DEFENSE

Basic melee attacks always roll a base d20! + any attack modifiers, while defenders either rolls a d20! + any defense modifiers

PERFECT ATTACKS & DEFENSES

üúÇ Advanced Rule

A Perfect Attack or Defense is one that puts you in a better position to win. A Perfect Attack occurs on an unmodified roll of 20 on your Attack roll, while a Perfect Defense is an unmodified 20 on your Defense roll, but only if your final totals are still greater than you opponent‚Äôs. If not, the attack still hits for Perfect Attacks and you still dodge the blow for Perfect Defenses but you don‚Äôt gain the auxiliary benefits.

If both those qualifiers are met you gain the following benefits on top of the normal ones:

PERFECT ATTACK You gain a free automatically successful Unarmed Combat attack. If you have Masteries in Unarmed Combat, you may roll for the additional benefit, but even on a failure, you gain the move‚Äôs base benefit.

You may also Shift with no penalty and change your Tactic.

If you‚Äôre firing a ranged weapon, you don‚Äôt receive the Unarmed attack, however you managed to hit a sensitive area, lowering their max movement speed by one category, minimum Crawl speed.

PERFECT DEFENSE You gain a free automatically successful Unarmed Combat attack. If you have Masteries in Unarmed Combat, you may roll for the additional benefit, but even on a failure, you gain the move‚Äôs benefit.

You may also Shift with no penalty and change your Tactic.

Additionally, you gain a chance to riposte, gaining a free full attack the next second if you‚Äôre wielding a melee weapon. This attack doesn‚Äôt affect your current count.

NEAR PERFECT ATTACKS AND DEFENCE An unmodified roll of 19 on either Attack or Defense results in a Near Perfect Attack or Defense, where you may Shift for no penalty, change your Tactic, or, for attacks, gain an extra d4! to damage, or, for defenses gained a free riposte with your off hand weapon if it‚Äôs smaller than your size.

RANGED ATTACK & DEFENSE

Ranged attacks function identical to melee attacks, only the range also gets factored in.

Defense rolls, however, are a d20! if you‚Äôre sprinting, a flat d12! if you‚Äôre at least moving, or a flat d4! if standing still. Defenders receive no defense bonus other than the Cover from their shield.

COVER

üúÇ Advanced Rule

It‚Äôs a matter of the difference in size between what you‚Äôre trying to hit and what‚Äôs behind it and if the thing is moving or not.

Cover dice for other objects works the same as shields - if you‚Äôre behind cover, you add a roll of that dice to your defense. If you‚Äôre actively crouching behind it, you add the max it would give you to your defense plus another roll of the dice.

To find out what‚Äôs the appropriate dice, you look at the difference in size and what it is. A 6ft wall, for example, will be better cover than a 6ft person.

For solid objects, such as walls, where it is easier to calculate how much of the person is covered, a flat bonus is appropriate, which each 5% of coverage adding a cumulative +1 for every 5% before it. So standing behind a brick that only gives 5% cover will result in a +1, but standing behind a low wall that gives 25% cover will give you a +15 (1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5).

For people, tree, a moving object, or really anything that‚Äôs harder to calculate, you‚Äôll use a dice backed on the difference in size, starting at a d20. Each difference in size shrinks or increases that cover die by 1 size.

So, two medium size people standing one in front of the other would give the guy in the back d20 cover, same as two small sized people. If the person in the front is small sized and the guy in the back is still medium sized, the guy in the back only gains a d12 cover.

If the person is actively moving or trying not to be your cover, you can‚Äôt gain the bonus for actively crouching behind them.

RANGED MISSES

üúÇ Advanced Rule

On a miss with a ranged weapon, the missile doesn‚Äôt magically disappear into oblivion. There‚Äôs a good chance it might hit someone else, especially if your target is locked in melee.

In the case of a miss, roll a d20! and a d4. The d4 determines to which side the missile strays - an odd result means it goes left, an even result means it goes right.

The d20! determines how many feet it strays from the target. Draw an imaginary line between the archer and that point. Everyone within 5ft of that line needs to roll a defense vs the original attack roll, starting with the person closest to the archer and ending with the furthest possible target.

Inanimate objects may provide enough cover to be considered targets in both Firing into Combat and Ranged Misses, such as a large boulder or tree. In which case, medium sized objects roll a d12!, with each size lower decreasing the dice size by 2, and each size larger increasing the dice size by 2.

FIRING INTO MELEE

üúÇ Advanced Rule

If there‚Äôs any one within 5 ft of a direct line between the origin of the missile and the target, they‚Äôll have to roll the appropriate defense die vs the original attack roll to see if the missile hits them instead, starting with the person closest to the archer or gunner and ending with the intended target.

All the normal rolls apply so if the missile only misses because of cover, the missile has lodged itself in the cover and goes no further.

DEALING DAMAGE

Once both sides have rolled and added all modifiers, compare the totals against each other.

If the defender‚Äôs is equal to or greater than the attacker, the attack misses.

PARRYING If the attack roll is within the Parry Threshold of the defender‚Äôs total, the defender may choose to deflect some of the incoming attack.

The attacker rolls their damage and subtracts the shield or weapon‚Äôs DR and then their armor‚Äôs DR. Any left over is still done to the defender. For piercing weapons, the /d DR of the shield or weapon is tripled.

So, a defender with a Parry Threshold of 4, and a total of 10 for their defense roll vs an attack roll of 14, could choose to parry the blow, taking less damage.

However, if it‚Äôs not, the attack lands and the attacker rolls his weapon‚Äôs damage die and adds any appropriate damage modifiers. The defender than subtracts their Damage Reduction and the remaining total is subtracted from their Vitality and added to their list of wounds.

DAMAGE REDUCTION Armor and one Mastery allows you to reduce the amount of damage taken. This is often times shorted to DR. There are two types: Static, noted by just a number, and Per Die, noted by a number /D.

Static simply subtracts that number from the damage and is done.

/D subtracts that number multiplied by the number of dice the attack is using. So, if someone is attacking you with a slashing weapon (2d6!), and you have armor that gives you 4/D, you‚Äôd subtract 8 from the damage.

Either way, if the attacker chooses to remain in engaged, he then adds his weapon‚Äôs Recovery to the current second on the Clock. Once the Clock reaches that new number, he may attack again.

KNOCK-BACKS

If the pre-DR damage is higher than your enemy‚Äôs Vitality Size Modifier + 3 for every level of Encumbrance you have, the attack also moves them back 5 feet. If it‚Äôs twice the damage needed, they get knocked back 10 feet, and so on.

VITALITY & DAMAGE

Remember that if you drop a Vitality Category you gain Stress equal to your Encumbrance

TRAUMA

If someone does enough damage in one hit for you to skip a Wound Category (25% of their Vitality), you‚Äôll have to roll a Trauma check, which is a Con check.

Failure means that your character has been overcome with pain to the point that he‚Äôs unable to act, leaving him completely defenseless for a number of seconds equal to how much he failed by times 3.

Double & Triple Trauma

A character that gets dealt 2 Wound Categories worth of damage in a single blow has suffered a grievous wound - the scar of which, if he survives, will be with him until the day he dies.

When this happens, the character rolls a 1d20. On any result other than 1 or 20, the character increases the number of scars they have by 1. On a 20, increase that number by 2.

But on a 1, the character has suffered such great trauma, that they gain a new Physical Flaw rolled randomly. They gain no CrP for it.

Battle scars are marks of pride and honor. Characters who become scared gain a d4 Trait ‚ÄúBattle Scars‚Äù. When their number of scars increases to more than the max value of that dice, increase that Trait by 1 dice size. So, a person who gains his 5th scar increases ‚ÄúBattle Scars‚Äù to a d6 while someone getting their 9th, increases it from a d8 to a d10.

Should the character suffer 3 Wound Categories worth of damage, the trauma is too much and they simply die where they stand if they fail their check. Even if they succeed, they‚Äôre still suffer Trauma for the next 30 seconds.

GRIT

üúÇ Advanced Rule

There are times when you have to reach deep down inside and find the hero within. Pushing through the pain to line up that perfect shot. This is what it means to have Grit.

You may spend Vitality to increase or decrease any dice roll on a 1 for 1 with it all going on 1 wound. The following are exceptions:

The first Vitality spent also zero outs any Stress Penalties, so spending 1 Vitality while Bloodied, gives you a net +1.

Only one person may use Grit at a time. If two people declare they‚Äôre using Grit, it goes to the person in the Vitality Category closest to Bleeding Out. Ties go to the person who declared first.

You may declare your use of Grit after you know the totals of the rolls but before the next roll is made.

COUP de GRACE

A helpless enemy is one completely at your mercy. If a character is completely helpless, either from failing a Trauma check, being bound, or deep asleep, a character can kill the target instantly. This move takes 10 seconds to complete as it takes a bit of positioning to pull off. The character‚Äôs who throat is being slit gives out a cry of pain as they expire.

FACING & FIGHTING MULTIPLE PEOPLE

The likelihood is that at some point you‚Äôll want to be flanking or someone with want to flank you, to try and gain an advantage.

Defending right in front of you is pretty easy and so brooks no penalties, however, every 45 degree off that the defender lowers their Defense dice by 1 size, with those being attacked from directly behind defend on a d8! since they take advantage of a huge blindspot.

This penalty can be mitigated through the use of shields. Small shields allow you to defend against an additional assailant, depriving them of that bonus, while Medium and Large ones allow you to defend against an extra 2 and 3, respectively.

A maximum of 8 people at a time can effectively attack person at once, assuming that they‚Äôre out in the open. After that, every assailant‚Äôs attack die is lowered by 1 size per extra person.

TACTICS

üúÇ Advanced Rule

These rules add another layer of strategy to combat and should be implemented as soon as you have the basics down.

A combatant utilizing these moves can only commit to one at a time, unless otherwise stated and can only switch on their attack unless it says otherwise.

ACTIVE DODGING This move relays on actively focusing on dodging against incoming attacks.

A defender commits to how much he wants to focus on dodging incoming attacks, both melee and ranged, choosing from a bonus to his defense in the range of +1 to +5, but netting an equal penalty to his attacks (-1 to -5).

Additionally, this lowers the range for Near Perfect Defenses by 1, so you score them on a base natural 18 - 19. This tactic needs to be committed to for at least your full weapon Recovery. After that you may continue using it and change tactics at any point.

Active Dodging can be combined with Retreat and Step Back, adding only the attack penalties but gaining all auxiliary benefits from each (so no, the defenses don't stack).

BRACE YOURSELF Maybe you look at your opponent and say "No, you move". You root your stance and ready yourself, sacrificing some maneuverability to hold the line and get a better chance of striking first.

While using this Tactic, a character adds +15 to their Knock-Back Treshold and gains a +1 to their Measure, however, they defend on a d12!.

CALLED SHOT By carefully aiming your shot, a character can reduce the effects of heavy armor by taking more time to target gaps. When employing this tactic, a character halves their effective number of damage dice (rounded up) for considering /D DR, however, it takes a bit longer to set up the strike and so they increase their Recovery by 3 seconds.

Crushing weapons are unable to employ this tactic.

CAREFUL AIMING By taking a moment or two, you can sight in with any ranged weapon, giving you a better chance to hit your target, however, there are diminishing returns after a bit.

For the first 3 seconds, you gain an increasing bonus of +1 to hit. So, the first second you‚Äôre aiming, you get a +1, the next a +2 (for a total of +3), and the third you get a +3 (for a total of +6).

After that, that bonus shrinks by 1 each second. So, the fourth second you get a +2 (for a total of +8) and the fifth second you get a +1 (for a total of +9).

Further delay gives you no bonuses.

CHARGE A character moving at least a Run can throw their full force behind a blow, knocking their opponent off their guard. If the Attacker has reach, the damage for their next attack is considered doubled for the purposes of determining Knock-Backs, though they defend with a d12! for half their weapon Recovery.

A charge is a terrifying tactic: should the blow land, they may add a d6! Leverage to their side‚Äôs Morale for the rest of the combat.

CHOKING UP ON YOUR WEAPON When you‚Äôve choked up on your weapon, your weapon‚Äôs Measure is halved, and you add that number, rounded down to its Recovery. Your halved Measure is considered your new Measure for determining who attacks first and what Tactics can be employed on you as well.

However, if an opponent is In Your Guard and you choke up on your weapon, you defend with a standard 1d20! and deal full weapon damage.

You‚Äôre unable to employ this tactic twice if someone again gets within your guard.

FEINT When performing this move, you (hopefully) get your enemy to commit his guard to a different location than you plan on hitting.

Before you roll, if you declare a Feint, you receive a +5 to that Attack but receive a -3 to Defense until your next attack.

FEND OFF If your weapon's measure is at least 1 foot greater than your opponent's, you can try and fend them off. This means that you may attack them freely, while they can't do the same to you.

You may only use this Tactic as someone is going from Threathened to Engaged, in which case they're automatically considered to be Fended Off. To full becoming engaged with you, they'll have to knock your weapon aside with a regular attack that deals no damage. Should they hit, they become fully engaged and can attack freely again.

IN THEIR GUARD If you're engaged with a person and have a weapon that is at least 1 foot shorter than your opponent, you may attempt to get in their guard. On a successful attack that deals no damage, the shorter weapon fighter has successfully knocked his foe's weapon aside and is no fighting at extreme close range, cause them huge penalties.

The longer weapon may only defend with a d12!. All damage dice is also reduced by one size (3d4! ‚Üí 3d3!). They may, in turn, employ the Choke Up On Your Weapon Tactic.

FLURRY of BLOWS This tactic involves releasing a rain of quick attacks that rely more on speed than accuracy or power.

While employing this tactic, you attack 3 seconds quicker, however, you suffer a -3 to Attack, your damage doesn‚Äôt explode, and you gain Stress equal to your encumbrance for every 20 seconds you keep up your onslaught, though this is all healed if you Take a Breather.

JAB A strike that is meant to purposely to open your opponent‚Äôs guard and get them off their balance by making them shift their defense.

The Jab attack happens at half weapon Recovery (rounded up) for Piercing and Slashing weapons. Instead of a d20!, the defender uses a d12! but on a successful hit, no damage is done, and the defender nets a -1 to their next full defense against a non-jab attack. This penalty doubles with each jab, however, it only lasts until the defender‚Äôs next full defense.

Crushing weapons may use this tactic but it‚Äôs not any faster than their regular Recovery.

LONG GUARD By placing your weapon further out in front of you and trying to line the body of the weapon with the defender‚Äôs eye line, this may allow a person with a short weapon the first strike.

While using this tactic, you increase your weapon‚Äôs measure by 1/2 ft but you reduce your damage‚Äôs largest die by 1 size.

Crushing weapons are unable to employ this tactic.

POWER ATTACK This is the fighting equivalent of baseball batting your enemy, swinging with all your might.

With this attack, you receive a -3 to your Attack and next defense but your damage increases

Piercing weapons increase 1 die by 1 size. Slashing weapons increase all dice by 1 size and crushing weapons increase its highest die by 2 sizes.

RETREAT This is a defensive tactic that involves moving directly back at jog or at a 45 degree angle from your opponent‚Äôs blows at a walk.

After your defense roll, if you declare a Retreat, you receive a +5 to your next Defense roll but also a -5 to your next Attack and must continue to move back until your next attack, either at a jog, if moving directly back, or at a walk, if started to move at a 45 degree angle.

If your opponent is using the Flurry of Blows, Feint, or the Power Attack tactic, if they do hit, you only receive half damage

SHIFT You move at your walking speed to either side of your opponent, but suffer a -1 to your next Attack and Defense for every 5 ft you travel before your next Attack or Defense.

However, if you Shift with another tactic, you don‚Äôt receive this penalty and gain a +1 that can be either used to increase a bonus or decrease a penalty from that tactic. You may do this freely with any Tactic except Retreat and Step Back.

Shifting is also used to change Facings so if you‚Äôre engaged with an enemy and you Shift. And don‚Äôt forget that you get those tasty bonuses from flanking your enemy if they don‚Äôt also Shift to face you.

STEP BACK This is a defensive tactic that involves moving directly back from your opponent‚Äôs blows at your walking speed.

Before your defense roll, if you declare a Step Back, you receive a +5 to your next Defense roll but on your next attack roll shrink the damage of your dice based on its type and must continue to move back at a Walking speed until your next attack.

Piercing weapons lower 1 of their dice by 1 size. Slashing lower all dice by 1 size and crushing lowers their highest die by 2 sizes.

If your opponent is using the Flurry of Blows, Faint, or the Power Attack tactic, if they do hit, you only receive half damage.

Retreat v. Step Back

While they may seem similar on the surface, these two different tactics have some key differences I thought I‚Äôd point out so that you can utilize each better.

1. Step Back must be declared before you roll your defense while Retreat can be declared after, meaning that if you see you rolled low before employing them.

2. Step Back moves your character straight back, while Retreat allows you to move up to 45¬∞ to the left and right, meaning that if you‚Äôre going to be backed against a wall, it‚Äôs an option to try and skirt your opponent.

3. Retreat may be done at a jogging speed, while Step Back is always at a walk.

STRIKE THE WEAPON This has two purposes: to either knock an opponent's weapon aside to delay their attack or to try and shatter your opponent‚Äôs weapon.

The defender rolls a standard defense die vs the attacker‚Äôs regular attack roll.

Should the attack succeed, he delays the defender‚Äôs next attack by a number of seconds equal to 1 for every 3 full points he scored above the defender‚Äôs total.

If the difference between the attack and defense was greater than 15, the attacker has a chance of breaking the opponent‚Äôs weapon

The defender‚Äôs weapon is treated as a shield using the Shield Breakage rules, with the attacker dealing his damage to the weapon.

UNARMED COMBAT

üúÇ Advanced Rule

Unarmed Combat‚Äôs primary use is to either disable or control your foe, thus winning the battle by maneuvering, rather than raw damage output.

To use an unarmed attack, roll an attack on a d10!, adding your normal attack modifiers, but also adding your Damage Modifier. Your opponent rolls a regular defense (including shield unless otherwise stated) but also adds their Damage Modifier as well.

Size does count for something: the larger person, if there is one, gains a +6 for each Size Category they have on their opponent.

If you hit, you successfully pulled off the move and gain the benefit.

If the difference between the attack and defense totals were more than the target‚Äôs Vitality Size Modifier, you may gain additional benefits listed in the move. If the difference is double the Target‚Äôs Vitality Size Modifier, they may immediately make a second unarmed combat attack for free the same second.

If you‚Äôre doing the move or defending with two hands, roll your attack on a d20!.

GRAB This move is meant to disable or gain control of a limb or item, whether that‚Äôs their belt, their leg, or their sword.

The target must be declared before the roll. Attempting to grab a shield means the target subtracts their shield‚Äôs unmodified Encumbrance Adjustment from their roll as well since a larger shield is easier to grab. Additionally, attempting to grab most parts of the body involve grabbing armor, if they're wearing any. Add your target's armor's unmodified Encumbrance Adjustment as well as applicable.

The exact bonus will depend on the situation but here are some guidelines:

Weapon: The defender gets a free attack if the move isn‚Äôt successful but when grabbed it means the defender can no longer attack.

Shield: The defender no longer gains any bonus from their shield but still has the defense and Encumbrance penalties.

Arm: See Weapon if it‚Äôs the weapon‚Äôs arm and Shield if it‚Äôs the shield‚Äôs arm. The defender also defends on a d12 vs Pushes and can‚Äôt change their facing willingly.

Leg: Moving above a crawl is impossible and moving at all can only be done after a successful opposed Str check vs d20! + the grabber‚Äôs strength.

The grabber defends with a d8! unless they‚Äôre employing something to let them keep their distance (a lasso or mancatch, for example). The grabber only defends with a d8! against Throws/Tackles. Their defense dice is lowered by 1 for all facings.

A second grab on a non-body part works as a disarm, wrenching the item free unless secured by something strong, though at the very least, the thing is no longer in hand. This is also done on an additional benefit from a well executed move if the attacker wishes.

PUSH By pushing you opponent in just the right way, you can move them or shift them in the direction you wish.

On a successful Push, your target changes one facing or moves 5 ft to the left, right, or directly back. You may immediately Shift to follow them without penalty.

You gain an additional facing change or 5 ft of movement on a well executed Push.

THROW/TACKLE This move attempts to throw your opponent to the ground and is easier to pull of if you go prone with them.

For this move, you can increase the attack dice size by 1 if you‚Äôre at running speed when you Tackle them and another if you choose to go to the ground with them, however, if you lose the struggle, you still end up on the ground.

Getting forced to the ground for you opponent resets their Count and reduces their Attack and Defense dies to a d8! if they‚Äôre wielding a Medium sized or larger weapon.

The extra bonus for this move is being able to remain standing if you so choose and possibly disarming them if they fail a Str check vs d20! + your Str.

STRIKE A strike is a punch with any part of your body or shield. It‚Äôs not really about doing damage but more about throwing your opponent off.

On a successful Strike, you delay your opponent‚Äôs next attack by 1d4 seconds, though if you‚Äôre using a shield to strike, you subtract that shield‚Äôs Recovery Mod from that amount, delaying them further. Striking an already stunned opponent is less effective and so the number of second they would be further delayed is lowered by 1 for each time you've successfully struck them since their last attack, down to 0.

The extra bonus for this move is that it increases the time to a d6!.

BREAK FREE There will probably come a time when you want to break free from a grab, pin, or choke hold.

This is done through the normal unarmed combat resolution method with the following notes:

The first is that the defender rolls a d12! while you roll a full d20! as a base. Additionally, the defender adds their Rank in Escape Artist to the check.

The second is that you can only attempt this manuever every 10 seconds, though this is modified by any Advanced Weapon Basic Training you have in unarmed attack.

MOUNTED COMBAT

üúÇ Advanced Rule

Whether you're playing a knight or a bounty hunter, you might find it's to your advantage to find yourself something to ride on and you'll probably want to know how you can use that mount in combat, so here are some rules to help you out.

Your mount will have a Turning, Acceleration, and Deceleration stat: this tells you how long, at base, it takes you to turn 180 degrees, speed up 1 Speed Catergory, or slow down 1 Speed Category. You declare your intent, wait the few seconds, and, BAM, you've done.

Note, however, that you can't do more than one of these things at a time and once you've committed, you've committed because your mount has its own inertia that it has to deal with and there is a delay between you telling it something and it actually doing it, with the exception of Turning, which can be stopped at any time (so you don't have to always turn your mount 180 degrees), but you don't retroactively straighten out your mount to where it was.

So, for example, if you have a mount with a Turning of 8, that means it turns 22.5 degrees per second. If you declare you're turning and then 3 seconds later, you decide, nevermind, you'll still have turned 67.5 degrees. A lot of this should be common sense but I figure it's worth pointing out, just in case.

That being said, you can lower all of these base times by up to your Riding Skill, however, a mule will probably never be as quick on its hooves as a courser, so you can only lower it to half of what the unmodified value for that derived stat is. So, if your mount's Turning is 8, the lower is can go is 4.

Additionally, for Turning, your mount's Speed Category does prevent it from tunring as quickly as you might like with each Speed Category above a Walk adding 2 Seconds to it.

So, if you have to think further ahead in combat so that you don't run into anything, what's the point of using a mount?

1) because it's awesome and 2) you get a lot of extra bonuses for doing so.

For starters, if you get that nice +2 to Defense and +d4! to damage if your opponent isn't mounted or on a rock or something, as well as additional damage dice if you're using a weapon specifically meant to be used from horse back (though you get this die even if you're on a camel, lizard, or what-have you).

This can bring you below a d4! and into the negative so lowering 1 size down from a d4! means you roll a d0 and stepping 2 sizes down gives you a -d4! to your attack. A handy table has been provided. Your mount might also be smarter than you and decide that it really doesn't want to put itself in danger. To get your mount to do something that could cause it damage, you roll a Riding Check + the Mount‚Äôs Bravery vs d20! + the amount of damage involved without the mount‚Äôs DR factored in. The exception to this is when presented with a crushing or slashing weapon, in which case you half the bonus the enemy d20! gets, rounded down.

So, charging into a pike (3d6! Damage) would mean rolling vs a d20!+3d6!, while charging a man brandishing a schiavona would be a d20!+d6! (or 2d3!, either is valid and up to you). Trying to get it to leap a chasm with a 40 ft drop would also cause you to make a roll vs d20!+3d12!.

BLEEDING OUT & DYING

A character falls into the Bleeding Out Wound Category is unconscious and is danger of dying. This is played out like a Social Conflict, with the person fighting against Death for survival.

However, this has a few changes from a standard Conflict, the first being that there is no Advantage Dice. The second is that each round happens every 5 seconds that character is in this state and losing a round means you take another point of damage while success in a round means he doesn't take the damage and gets closer to stabilizing.

Getting to half your Constitution in the negative (either from bleeding out or from additional wounds) results in the character's death and getting 10 successes means that that stabilize, losing no more Vitality but still remaining unconscious until they're brough back up to Wounded.

Each round, the player will draw on their will to live from their Characteristics, while Death will start by rolling 5d4!. Each successive round the player wins, Death increases the size of his dice. When the player fails a round, Death's dice drop back down to 5d4! again.

Other character's can add their Medicine Skill as Leverage each round, but not First Aid, though First Aid can bring someone back up to Wounded and just stop the Conflict in its tracks anyway so there's that.

MORALETHRESHOLDS

A strong willed or well disciplined person, when faced with insurmountable odds will often fight long after the cowards have run, either gaining victory or assuring his death. This is represented by a character‚Äôs Morale.

Morale is represented by when a person Panics, which correlates to Wound Categories. Which Wound Category is dictated by that character‚Äôs Honor. The more Honorable a person is, the less likely they are to panic and act hastily.

If you target a Panicked person, you can also target an additional Panicked person for every Rank of difference between your Warfare Skill and the enemy's, minimum of at least the original person.

MORALE CONFLICTS

Combat is not simply about who can do the most damage the quickest. Battles, at their heart, are manifestations of the fever of conflict going on between the two sides. This is why sometimes a small, untrained force of zealots can overcome a larger professional army.

This is partly represented by Morale, which is a metric for how a given individual is doing, but it‚Äôs also represented by a Social Conflict happening during the combat, or a Morale Conflict.

At the beginning of combat, if at least one side has a captain, a Conflict is also started but, unlike a regular Conflict, the two opposing sides are the two opposing forces (the players vs the gobins or the bandits).

This runs like a normal Conflict except the company captains, those in charge, are the ones that decide when a round occurs.

As the battle progresses, the captains are going to run the Conflict in parallel with it, trying to swing the battlefield to their advantage.

Drawing from available Characteristics and Leverage, one of them can state that they want a fighter to perform an action or behave in a certain way. For instance, they want him to switch his Tactic to be more aggressive, or swap who he‚Äôs aiming for. The only exception to is they can‚Äôt make them run away (which can happen but we'll talk about that a bit later).

A round can be initiated at any time by either captain, regardless of who has the Advantage Dice. A side without a captain can ‚Äúdefend‚Äù but can‚Äôt go on the attack.

Captains can draw dice from 3 places: their Characteristics, Leverage (including the additional Leverage noted below), and/or any person‚Äôs Characteristics that might be involved.

So, for example, you‚Äôre the company captain and you‚Äôre trying to convince the enemy goblin that he should try and get at your company‚Äôs ranger but currently he‚Äôs making a beeline for your fighter. In that case, you can use the fighter‚Äôs Characteristic ‚ÄúYou Don‚Äôt Want to Mess With Me‚Äù to get the goblin to second guess himself.

If you win, the target behaves the way you want. This represents either them being too panicked or not having enough information to make a good decision, or effective leadership and a clear grasp of what‚Äôs going on.

ADDITIONAL LEVERAGE PLACES

On top of that you‚Äôll also have additional places you can get Characteristics and Leverage from, such as having a seasoned commander, outnumbering the opposing side, having a common Devotion to why they‚Äôre fighting and so on.

Here are common things that can give you Leverage in a Morale Conflict:

OUTNUMBERING THE ENEMY You gain a d6 every time you can double their number. So, you gain a d6 for 2 vs 1, 2d6 for 3 vs 1 and nothing for 3 vs 2, 2d6 for 4 vs 2, 3d6 for 6 vs 2 and so on.

CATCHING AN ENEMY BY SURPRISE This nets you a d8 until their initiative comes up.

HAVING YOU BATTLE STANDARD UP AND VISIBLE Nets you a d8.

CAUSING AN ENEMY TO PANIC Nets you a d6.

CAUSING AN ENEMY TO FLEE Nets you a d10. This can increase the amount of dice you gain from outnumbering your enemy.

PARTY CAPTAIN‚ÄôS CHARISMA You also gain your party captain‚Äôs Social Dice from Charisma in this case. Also, any abilities that the Party Captain has that effect Conflicts can also be used during this Conflict.

A PERFECT ATTACKS & DEFENSE this nets you a d6! until that person‚Äôs next Attack or Defense.

Note that if the Leverage comes from circumstances, like having an enemy Panic, it goes away when that circumstance does.

EFFECTS of MORALE THRESHOLD

While you may target any fighter, remember that those that are Panicked have additional penalties that make them easier to target.

Also, winning a round agaisnt a Panicked person gives them (and all Panicked people targeted) a number of Stress equal to the difference in results.

If a person goes above their Stress Threshold, they simply turn and flee, unable to face their own morality. Captains can try and rally them with a Check immediately.

If cornered and given no space to run, these routed fighters enter a frenzy - only using aggressive Tactics and becoming immune to Stress penalties.

Who Controls the Pool

It may be the case that the Party Leader, who should be responsible for the Morale Dice Pool is also your main line fighter and is already busy juggling an enemy or two.

You can have a player who runs a less combat intense character, such as the magic user or diplomat, be in charge of the Morale Dice Pool in that case and decide where to risk dice, however, only the actually Party Leader‚Äôs abilities affect the number or size of those dice are applied.

ENDING COMBAT

Combat ends when one side is incapacitated, reached a truce, or have fled the field. At that point the Clock stops and play moves back to narrative time.

If you engaged in a Morale Conflict, everyone on the side that won the combat gains the difference in winning pools to their Honor, regardless of whether or not their side has less. So, if the players has 3 in their winning pool and the goblins had 7, every player still gains 4 Honor.

The losing side subtracts that with the same caveats.
