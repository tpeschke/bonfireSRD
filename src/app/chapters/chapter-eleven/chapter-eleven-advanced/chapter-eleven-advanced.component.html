Now it’s time to kit out your character and get them suited up to hit the road.

Most things won’t warrant an explanation but some have been provided for further clarification.

ENCUMBRANCE

You’ve probably noticed that Carry under your Strength Stat is just a string of numbers and letters and you probably wondered: what does that mean? Well, I’m about to tell you.

Instead of each item having a weight in pounds or ounces, each item is assigned a size that represents, not only how much it weighs, but also its general bulky and awkwardness. These sizes correspond to the naming conventions set up in Chapter 15: Misc Rules however, just because something is Medium sized here, doesn’t mean it’s the size of a human - again, item sizes are based on their weights, bukliness, and awkwardness to carry.

Your Carry matches up with that, so if you have 2M as your Carry, you can carry 2 Medium items - or 2M items.

You can also swap out 1 for 3 of the size lower. So instead of carrying 2M items, you could carry 1M item and 3S items.

Each individual instance of an item counts towards this: carrying two daggers, both S items, would count as 2S for Encumbrance.

WEARING CLOTHES, ARMOR, AND PERSONAL CONTAINERS

Normally what is written under the Carry column for each item is it’s Encumbrance. The only exception is clothes, armor, and personal containers. If worn on your person, not carried or stuffed in a backpack, these become less encumbering since their weight is distributed across basically the entire body.

In this case, shrink them 1 size for Carry purposes. Small items become negligible. Wearing boots, a Small item, then makes them effectively weightless, while any Heavy Armor makes them only M.

PERSONAL CONTAINERS

Personal Containers, such as backpacks or sacks, go one step further. Since it is assumed that your Carry weight is how much you can hold effectively, it does take into account that some things are too awkward to effectively carry despite being fairly light.

These containers increase your Carry by the noted amount.

The exceptions, sort of, to this are coin purses, which make coins negligible (otherwise they’re 10 = 1S), quivers which do the same thing but with arrows, and scabbards and sheaths allow you to wear weapons - shrinking them 1 size.

GOING OVER CARRY

You can go over your Carry, of course, but each M item worth over your limit increases your Encumbrance by +1, which is fairly forgiving for S and smaller items but not for L and larger items.

Unless proven otherwise, the GM should assume the characters are carrying their max Carry if they pick up something new while out adventuring.

CURRENCY

The denominations of coinage is fairly simple and is done in divisions of 10.

10 brass coins (bc) = 1 copper coin (cc)

10 cc = 1 silver coin (sc)

10 sc = 1 gold coin (gc)

10 gc = 1 platinum coin (pc)

So, 100 cc is equal to 1 gc.

The silver coin is the base of the economy and is what most day to day interactions use. If a denomination isn’t noted, it’s assumed to be sc.

WAR GEAR

🜂 Advanced Rule

It’s generally in poor taste to walk around town or go about your daily business armed like your going to battle. Even nobles don’t make a habit of breaking out their plate when surveying their fief as it signals a general distrust of the smallfolk.

Wearing armor heavier than light, and weapons and armor larger than small while in town or another similarly inappropriate setting docks a person 1 Honor for each infringement at the end of session.

If they enter a Conflict with someone while they’re clearly being aggressive, the person receives an additional 4d4! per escalation to resist their charms. This applies in the case of an armed man trying to haggle with the shop keeper but not, for instance, in the case of two nobles on the battlefield meeting in the middle to exchange terms and verbal blows with one another.

This penalty can be overcome by simply storing your war equipment on your cart, horse, or arming squire.

Appropriate Attire

These rules can really be applied to most situations. A character showing up to a noble’s banquet in rags is going to be just out of place as one in armor, if not more so. Players should be warned if their characters would know they’re walking into a situation where they might earn the derision of others by their attire.

SUPERIOR EQUIPEMENT

🜂 Advanced Rule

Not all equipment is built the same and, if you have some extra coin, it might benefit you to invest in some good quality gear. On the flip side, if you’re strapped for cash, you might save a few silver buying less than optimate gear.

Superior gear is always unique and even two +1 longswords can be different. This is done through Traits and they're like the Traits that your character has: they're used to describe the gear.

During a Conflict, Combat, or Challenge, if that Trait applies, you can gain an extra d4! to what is going on. This is in additional to the normal d6! Leverage for gear you get in a Conflict and the +d6! you get to your Skill roll in a Challenge.

So, for instance, let's say that you get some better armor. You ask to make it extra padded and that becomes it's Trait: Extra Padded. A little while later, you find yourself in combat and you take a particularly nasty blow and you don't want to die and the Trait applies here so you add a temporary d4! to your armor's DR.

This, however, comes at a cost: when used this way in Combat or a Skill Challenge, your gear becomes a little more worn and less impressive, temporarily shrinking that Trait's dice size (down to nothing) until it is mended or repaired.

You can have additional Traits on a single weapon and increase the power of Traits you already have. Increasing the power simply increases the size of the bonus dice you get, which means you can use it more often between mends and repairs.

Every time you do so adds an extra 50% the cost of the item.

MOUNT STATS

Like people, each mount is different though they do have ranges based on the breed and this is represented by their unique set of stats that are rolled for each mount you look at while shopping. These can usually be decerned by a Handle Animal Check that's, of course, appropriate to the type. So, nice try, but Handle Animal (horse) won't help you figuring out which riding lizard is best. Higher stats are always better.

These stats are then used to determine the specifics of each mount: how fast can they get up and go? How long can they run for? Each of the main stats has a positive effect on some of these derived stats and a negative effect on others so figuring out the right balance for what you want out of a mount can be tricky. Averages for each breed is found under that breed's profile in the Bonfire Bestiary.

ARMOR TABLE HEADER EXPLANATION

DR This number is subtracted from the damage dealt by a weapon.

Def All armor weighs you down and makes it harder to dodge incoming attacks. This number is added to your Defense roll.

Init Mod Armor is restricting, even at the best of times. This is added to your Initiative roll at the start of combat.

Spd Mod Again, armor is restricting. This is added to your weapon’s speed.

Type This donates what category your armor falls in. You should note that should you purchase Medium or Heavy armor, this comes with a Gambeson that’s worn underneath for comfort. This is already factored into the other stats of the armor.

Weight This is how heavy your armor is for encumbrance when your armor is carried on your person. Worn armor doesn’t count towards your encumbrance.

Encumb This number is added to your Encumbrance as doing things with a weight on you is tiring.

Skill Adj Armor, regardless of how fitted, is restricting and heavy, making it harder to perform certain skills in certain gear. The first number is what you apply to Str based skills, while the second is how many for Dex based skills.

In the case of a skill being both Str and Dex based, use the larger of the two numbers.

DR This number is subtracted from the damage dealt by a weapon on a successful parry with a shield.

Def This is the number that you subtract from your character’s defense. Lugging a huge piece of material on the end of your arm makes dodging a bit difficult. Additionally, you’re often moving a shield into harm’s way, meaning, overall, you get hit more while wielding one.

Parry Some shields are balanced better than others and that allows you to deflect blows more effectively. This number is the shield’s Parry Threshold. This is overrides by your weapon’s Parry. Parrying with a shield subjects it to the Shield Breakage rules.

Cover This dice is added to your Defense roll against ranged weapons. If you’re actively crouching behind it, you instead add the maximum value of any dice and roll of that dice (i.e., a d8, you add d8 + 8, for d12 + d8, you add d12 + d8 + 20, etc.).

Flanks This is the number of additional attackers from the side that the shield can defend against. More on that is found in Chapter 10: Combat

Weight This is how heavy your shield is for encumbrance.

Encumb This number is added to your Encumbrance as doing things with a weight on you is tiring.

Bonus Some shields have additional unique bonuses. If there is a ‘yes’ in this column, look up the shield in the following section to find out more details.

If the attacker manages to score enough damage after the shield or weapons DR to get over the appropriate Shield & Weapon Breakage threshold, a saving throw is made with the attack rolling a d20! and the defender rolling the corresponding dice.

SHIELD BONUSES

Buckler The buckler is a lightweight shield which relies more on parrying blows than outright stopping them. The buckler also has the added benefit of being rather small with almost no weight, allowing it to be worn but not used while wielding two handed weapons. You receive no bonuses when doing this but it means that if you do switch over to a one handed weapon, you instantly get the buckler’s bonus.

Figure Eight This shield is designed specifically for phalanx or shield wall tactics. This gives a +2 Parry and a Cover of d4 to anyone fighting on either side of you. This stacks with any Parry or Cover that they have from either their weapon or shields.

Pavise This shield is less a shield and more portable cover employed by gunners and crossbowman, rather than fighters.

However, the Pavise can be set up using a kick stand in the back, giving anyone crouching behind it basically complete cover and allowing that person the ability to steady their weapon on it.

Round This shield was meant for hitting your opponent, trying to throw them off guard. On a natural roll of 20 on Defense or an Unarmed Strike, you delay your opponent’s next attack by 5 seconds.
