Encumbrance

Youâ€™ve probably noticed that Carry under your Strength Stat is just a string of numbers and letters and you probably wondered: what does that mean? Well, Iâ€™m about to tell you.

Instead of each item having a weight in pounds or ounces, each item is assigned a size that represents, not only how much it weighs, but also its general bulky and awkwardness. These sizes correspond to the naming conventions set up in Chapter 14: Misc Rules however, just because something is Medium sized here, doesnâ€™t mean itâ€™s the size of a human - again, item sizes are based on their weights, bulkiness, and awkwardness to carry.

Your Carry matches up with that, so if you have 2M as your Carry, you can carry 2 Medium items - or 2M items.

You can also swap out 1 for 3 of the size lower. So instead of carrying 2M items, you could carry 1M item and 3S items.

Each individual instance of an item counts towards this: carrying two daggers, both S items, would count as 2S for Encumbrance.

WEARING CLOTHES, ARMOR, AND PERSONAL CONTAINERS

Normally what is written under the Carry column for each item is its Encumbrance. The only exception is clothes, armor, and personal containers. If worn on your person, not carried or stuffed in a backpack, these become less encumbering since their weight is distributed across basically the entire body.

In this case, shrink them 1 size for Carry purposes. Small items become negligible. Wearing boots, a Small item, then makes them effectively weightless, while any Heavy Armor makes them only M.

PERSONAL CONTAINERS

Personal Containers, such as backpacks or sacks, go one step further. Since it is assumed that your Carry weight is how much you can hold effectively, it does take into account that some things are too awkward to effectively carry despite being fairly light.

These containers increase your Carry by the noted amount.

The exceptions, sort of, to this are coin purses, which make coins negligible (otherwise theyâ€™re 10 = 1S), quivers which do the same thing but with arrows, and scabbards and sheaths allow you to wear weapons - shrinking them 1 size.

GOING OVER CARRY

You can go over your Carry, of course, but each M item worth over your limit increases your Encumbrance by +1, which is fairly forgiving for S and smaller items but not for L and larger items.

Unless proven otherwise, the GM should assume the characters are carrying their max Carry if they pick up something new while out adventuring.

Currency

The denominations of coinage is fairly simple and is done in divisions of 10.

10 cc = 1 silver coin (sc)

10 sc = 1 gold coin (gc)

10 gc = 1 platinum coin (pc)

So, 100 cc is equal to 1 gc.

The silver coin is the base of the economy and is what most day to day interactions use. If a denomination isnâ€™t noted, itâ€™s assumed to be sc.

Wear

Wear represents how close your weapon is to breaking.

Simply put: if, on a Skill Check, Attack roll (for weapons), or Defense roll (for shields and armor), you roll equal to or below the Wear of that item on the d20, that item breaks and becomes functionality unusable until itâ€™s repaired.

Note that this retroactively applied to rolls in which a piece of equipment gains Wear. So, if someone rolls a 3 and then, as a result of that roll, gains their 3rd point of Wear, that item breaks.

There are some specific cases that should be noted:

1) You still maintain any penalties (Encumbrance, Defense Modifiers from armor and shields, for example) that item gives you unless you discard it.

2) In the case of weapons, you can still use the broken weapon - you just half all the damage it does.

3) And finally, instead of having the item break, if itâ€™s a Superior quality item, you can instead reduce one of its Traits by 1 dice size as long as you have at least Trait at a d4!.

Superior Equipement

ðŸœ‚ Deluxe Rule

Not all equipment is built the same and, if you have some extra coin, it might benefit you to invest in some good quality gear.

Superior gear is always unique and even two +1 longswords can be different. This is done through Traits and they're like the Traits that your character has: they're used to describe the gear.

During a Confrontation, Combat, or Challenge, if that Trait applies, you can gain an extra d4! to what is going on. This is in addition to the normal d6! for gear you get in a Confrontation and the reroll that you would normal get in a Skill Challenge.

So, for instance, let's say that you get some better armor. You ask to make it extra padded and that becomes its Trait: Extra Padded. A little while later, you find yourself in combat and you take a particularly nasty blow and decide that you don't want to die and the Trait applies here so you add a temporary d4! to your armor's DR.

This, however, comes at a cost: when used this way in Combat or a Skill Challenge, your gear becomes a little more worn, gaining 1 Wear.

You can have additional Traits on a single weapon and/or increase the dice size of Traits you already have. Increasing the power simply increases the size of the bonus dice you get.

Every time you do so adds an extra 50% the cost of the item.

War Gear

ðŸœ‚ Deluxe Rule

Itâ€™s generally in poor taste to walk around town or go about your daily business armed like you're going to battle. Even nobles donâ€™t make a habit of breaking out their plate when surveying their fief as it signals a general distrust of the smallfolk.

Wearing armor heavier than light, and weapons and armor larger than small while in town or another similarly inappropriate setting docks a person 1 Honor for each infringement at the end of session.

If they enter a Confrontation with someone while theyâ€™re clearly being aggressive, the person receives an additional 4d4! per escalation to resist their charms. This applies in the case of an armed man trying to haggle with the shop keeper but not, for instance, in the case of two nobles on the battlefield meeting in the middle to exchange terms and verbal blows with one another.

This penalty can be overcome by simply storing your war equipment on your cart, horse, or arming squire.

Appropriate Attire

These rules can really be applied to most situations. A character showing up to a nobleâ€™s banquet in rags is going to be just out of place as one in armor, if not more so. Players should be warned if their characters would know theyâ€™re walking into a situation where they might earn the derision of others by their attire.
