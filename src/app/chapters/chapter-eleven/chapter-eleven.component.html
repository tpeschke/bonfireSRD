Most things won’t warrant an explanation but some have been provided for further clarification.

CURRENCY

The denominations of coinage is fairly simple and is done in divisions of 10.

10 brass coins (bc) = 1 copper coin (cc)

10 cc = 1 silver coin (sc)

10 sc = 1 gold coin (gc)

10 gc = 1 platinum coin (pc)

So, 100 cc is equal to 1 gc.

The silver coin is the base of the economy and is what most day to day interactions use. If a denomination isn’t noted, it’s assumed to be sc.

BUYING AT 1st LEVEL

A character starts with one of the following kits. After doing so, he rolls the kit's Extra Cash dice to determine any extra starting money to buy additional gear. When selecting an item, use the Source price.

Some items have a sc cost listed next to the item. That's because players should feel free to sell back items they don't want or don't think they'll need for the source price so they can purchase different equipment and customize their load out. For items where you get to pick from a range of items (such as weapons), there is often a huge spread in prices so use this price as the sell back price.

TABLE HEADER EXPLANATIONS

The following is a break down of how to read the following tables. Note that the prices here are not absolute and are actually the lowest price you’ll find for a give item based on the area. War, tariffs, what relationship the buyer has with the seller, and even the political climate can cause these numbers to fluctuate wildly.

Name This is the common name for the item.

Weight This is the weight of the object in pounds.

Source This is the wholesale sc cost of producing the item.

Local Market This is the sc price of purchasing this item within a day’s travel of the place where it was manufactured.

Nearby City This is the sc price of picking up this item within about a week’s travel from where the thing was crafted.

Distance City This is the sc price of the the item in far away markets, either across a political border or beyond a month’s travel away. This price increases the further away the city is, obviously.

ENCUMBRANCE

Unsurprisingly, carrying more things tires you out quicker.

How much a character can carry before they tucker out is a product of their Strength score, specifically, their Carry.

When a person hauls more than their Carry they gain 1 to their Fatigue Score for every time they go over it. So, a person with a Carry of 20 lbs, who is lugging around 60 lbs worth of loot increases their Fatigue Score by 2.

It’s not all bad: carrying more than your fair share of gear is looked upon well by a group. The character carrying the most receives an amount of Honor at the end of each session equal to the adjustment to their Fatigue Score.

This is the unadjusted weight, so it does count if someone is technically carrying more thanks for the Quarter Mastering skill.

WEAPON TABLE HEADER EXPLANATION

Size A weapon’s size indicates the smallest size the wielder can be to use it 1 handed. Weapons larger in size must be wielded with 2 hands.

Damage This is the damage inflicted when a successful hit is scored.

Speed This is the number of seconds a character locked in melee must wait before attacking again with this weapon.

Dam Type This tells you whether the weapon is Slashing (S), Piercing (P), or Crushing (C).

Parry Some weapons are balanced better than others and that allows you to deflect some blows. This is the weapon’s Parry Threshold. This is overridden by your shield’s Parry if you’re using one. A successful parry with a weapon has a DR of 2/D + 2. Parrying with a weapon subjects it to the Shield Breakage rules.

Measure This is a measure in feet of how long a weapon is.

Max Str This is the max strength bonus and penalty that can be applied to this weapon. So, if you have a +6 Str Bonus and are wielding a Dusack, the most you get is a +4. On the flip side, if you're especially weak, say you have a -6 Str Bonus and are wielding the same weapon, you only apply a -4, so that's nice.

Bonus Some weapons have additional unique bonuses. If there is a ‘yes’ in this column, look up the weapon in the following section to find out more details.

Speed Masteries for this weapon remove 3 seconds off their Speed per bonus instead of 1.

Dagger This weapon can be used as either a Slashing or Piercing weapon. You only need one Weapon Training and any Weapon Masteries (except for Tier 3 - in which case use the highest) count for both.

Handaxe This weapon can be used either in melee or as the Throwing Axe. Masteries must be purchased separately.

Javelin If this weapon strikes a shield and its damage is greater than the shield’s DR, the javelin has gotten lodged in the shield’s surface.

The Defender must either spend 2d6! seconds pulling it out or defend with a base 1d12! for as long as he continues to use the shield.

Unlike other ranged weapons, you add your Damage Modifier from Strength to your damage.

Knife This weapon can be used either in melee or as the Throwing Knife. Masteries must be purchased separately.

Koncerz This weapon is designed for use on horseback. Double it’s damage dice while mounted.

Throwing Axe If this weapon strikes a shield and it’s damage is greater than the shield’s DR, the axe has gotten lodged in the shield’s surface.

The Defender must either spend 2d6! seconds pulling it out or defend with a base 1d12!.

Unlike other ranged weapons, you add your Damage Modifier from Strength to your damage.

This weapon can be used either as a ranged weapon or as a handaxe. Masteries must be purchased separately.

Throwing Knife This weapon can be used either as a ranged weapon or as a knife. Masteries must be purchased separately.

Unlike other ranged weapons, you add your Damage Modifier from Strength to your damage.

Unarmed This is the character’s bare knuckles and so they pose less of a threat to those attacking with a weapon, meaning attackers double their weapon’s speed against unarmed opponents.

On explosions, the additional dice are at a -2, instead of the standard -1.

War Flail This weapon can get around guards more effectively. While attacking someone, reduce their Parry by 8.

Zweihander This weapon is built for breaking and chopping. When this weapon strikes a weapon or a shield, reduce its effective number of dice to 3. More on this is found in the Weapon & Shield Breakage section below.

ARMOR TABLE HEADER EXPLANATION

DR This number is subtracted from the damage dealt by a weapon. There are two types: Static, noted by just a number, and Per Die, noted by a number /D.

Static simply subtracts that number from the damage and is done.

/D subtracts that number multiplied by the number of dice the attack is using. So, if someone is attacking you with a slashing weapon (2d6!), and you have armor that gives you 4/D, you’d subtract 8 from the damage.

Def All armor weighs you down and makes it harder to dodge incoming attacks. This number is added to your Defense roll.

Init Mod Armor is restricting, even at the best of times. This is added to your Initiative roll at the start of combat.

Spd Mod Again, armor is restricting. This is added to your weapon’s speed.

Type This donates what category your armor falls in. You should note that should you purchase Medium or Heavy armor, this comes with a Gambeson that’s worn underneath for comfort. This is already factored into the other stats of the armor.

Weight This is how heavy your armor is for encumbrance when your armor is carried on your person. Worn armor doesn’t count towards your encumbrance.

Fatigue Adj This number is added to your Fatigue Score as doing things with a weight on you is tiring.

Skill Adj Armor, regardless of how fitted, is restricting and heavy, making it harder to perform certain skills in certain gear. The first number is how many dice skills you raise the difficulty by for Str based skills, while the second is how many for Dex based skills.

In the case of a skill being both Str and Dex based, use the larger of the two numbers.

ARMOR BONUSES

Bone This armor is especially effective when channelling magic. It only increases your channelling time by 5 if the legs and arms are uncovered and 10 if you wear the gauntlets and boots.

SHIELD TABLE HEADER EXPLANATION

Size This indicates the general size of a shield. It also determines how tough your shield is.

DR This number is subtracted from the damage dealt by a weapon on a successful parry with a shield.

Def This is the number that you subtract from your character’s defense. Lugging a huge piece of material on the end of your arm makes dodging a bit difficult. Additionally, you’re often moving a shield into harm’s way, meaning, overall, you get hit more while wielding one.

Parry Some shields are balanced better than others and that allows you to deflect blows more effectively. This number is the shield’s Parry Threshold. This is overrides by your weapon’s Parry. Parrying with a shield subjects it to the Shield Breakage rules.

Cover This dice is added to your Defense roll against ranged weapons. If you’re actively crouching behind it, you instead add the maximum value of any dice (i.e., a d8, you add 8, for d12 + d8, you add 20, etc.).

Weight This is how heavy your shield is for encumbrance.

Fatigue Adj This number is added to your Fatigue Score as doing things with a weight on you is tiring.

Bonus Some shields have additional unique bonuses. If there is a ‘yes’ in this column, look up the shield in the following section to find out more details.

If the attacker manages to score enough damage after the shield or weapons DR to get over the appropriate Shield & Weapon Breakage threshold, a saving throw is made with the attack rolling a d20! and the defender rolling the corresponding dice.

SHIELD BONUSES

Buckler The buckler is a lightweight shield which relies more on parrying blows than outright stopping them. The buckler also has the added benefit of being rather small with almost no weight, allowing it to be worn but not used while wielding two handed weapons. You receive no bonuses when doing this but it means that if you do switch over to a one handed weapon, you instantly get the buckler’s bonus.

Round This shield was meant for hitting your opponent, trying to throw them off guard. On a natural roll of 20 on Defense or an Unarmed Strike, you delay your opponent’s next attack by 5 seconds.
