Now it’s time to kit out your character and get them suited up to hit the road.

Most things won’t warrant an explanation but some have been provided for further clarification.

Encumbrance

You’ve probably noticed that Carry under your Strength Stat is just a string of numbers and letters and you probably wondered: what does that mean? Well, I’m about to tell you.

Instead of each item having a weight in pounds or ounces, each item is assigned a size that represents, not only how much it weighs, but also its general bulky and awkwardness. These sizes correspond to the naming conventions set up in Chapter 14: Misc Rules however, just because something is Medium sized here, doesn’t mean it’s the size of a human - again, item sizes are based on their weights, bulkiness, and awkwardness to carry.

Your Carry matches up with that, so if you have 2M as your Carry, you can carry 2 Medium items - or 2M items.

You can also swap out 1 for 3 of the size lower. So instead of carrying 2M items, you could carry 1M item and 3S items.

Each individual instance of an item counts towards this: carrying two daggers, both S items, would count as 2S for Encumbrance.

WEARING CLOTHES, ARMOR, & PERSONAL CONTAINERS

Normally what is written under the Carry column for each item is its Encumbrance. The only exception is clothes, armor, and personal containers. If worn on your person, not carried or stuffed in a backpack, these become less encumbering since their weight is distributed across basically the entire body.

In this case, shrink them 1 size for Carry purposes. Small items become negligible. Wearing boots, a Small item, then makes them effectively weightless, while any Heavy Armor makes them only M.

PERSONAL CONTAINERS

Personal Containers, such as backpacks or sacks, go one step further. Since it is assumed that your Carry weight is how much you can hold effectively, it does take into account that some things are too awkward to effectively carry despite being fairly light.

These containers increase your Carry by the noted amount.

The exceptions, sort of, to this are coin purses, which make coins negligible (otherwise they’re 10 = 1S), quivers which do the same thing but with arrows, and scabbards and sheaths allow you to wear weapons - shrinking them 1 size.

GOING OVER CARRY

You can go over your Carry, of course, but each M item worth over your limit increases your Encumbrance by +1, which is fairly forgiving for S and smaller items but not for L and larger items.

Unless proven otherwise, the GM should assume the characters are carrying their max Carry if they pick up something new while out adventuring.

Currency

The denominations of coinage is fairly simple and is done in divisions of 10.

10 brass coins (bc) = 1 copper coin (cc)

10 cc = 1 silver coin (sc)

10 sc = 1 gold coin (gc)

10 gc = 1 platinum coin (pc)

So, 100 cc is equal to 1 gc.

The silver coin is the base of the economy and is what most day to day interactions use. If a denomination isn’t noted, it’s assumed to be sc.

Buying At 1st Level

A character starts with one of the following kits. After doing so, he rolls the kit's Extra Cash dice to determine any extra starting money to buy additional gear. When buying new items, use the Source price.

The individual sizes are notes next to the item. Note that anything with * at the end denotes that the item is worn and so the size has been adjusted accordingly. You're also given the sellback price (in sc) in case you don't want the item. For weapons, you'll notice that you're giving a generic size of weapon you can select from - the sellback price is unaffected by which weapon you would have picked up.

Table Header Explanations

The following is a breakdown of how to read the following tables. Note that the prices here are not absolute and are actually the lowest price you’ll find for a give item based on the area. War, tariffs, what relationship the buyer has with the seller, and even the political climate can cause these numbers to fluctuate wildly.

Name This is the common name for the item.

Weight This is the weight of the object in pounds.

Source This is the wholesale sc cost of producing the item.

Local Market This is the sc price of purchasing this item within a day’s travel of the place where it was manufactured.

Nearby City This is the sc price of picking up this item within about a week’s travel from where the thing was crafted.

Distance City This is the sc price of the item in far away markets, either across a political border or beyond a month’s travel away. This price increases the further away the city is, obviously.

Weapon Table Header Explanation

Size A weapon’s size indicates the smallest size the wielder can be to use it 1 handed. Weapons 1 size larger must be wielded with 2 hands and those 2 sizes greater must be wielded with 2 hands and the wielder gains a +3 to their Encumbrance.

Damage This is the damage inflicted when a successful hit is scored.

Recovery This is the number of seconds a character locked in melee must wait before attacking again with this weapon.

Dam Type This tells you whether the weapon is Slashing (S), Piercing (P), or Crushing (C).

Parry Some weapons are balanced better than others and that allows you to deflect some blows. This is the weapon’s Parry Threshold. This is overridden by your shield’s Parry if you’re using one. A successful parry with a weapon has a DR of 2/D. Parrying with a weapon subjects it to the Shield Breakage rules.

Measure This is a measure in feet of how long a weapon is.

Bonus Some weapons have additional unique bonuses. If there is a ‘yes’ in this column, look up the weapon in the following section to find out more details.

Armor Table Header Explanation

DR This number is subtracted from the damage dealt by a weapon.

Def All armor weighs you down and makes it harder to dodge incoming attacks. This number is added to your Defense roll.

Init Mod Armor is restricting, even at the best of times. This is added to your Initiative roll at the start of combat.

Rec Mod Again, armor is restricting. This is added to your weapon’s Recovery.

Type This donates what category your armor falls in. You should note that should you purchase Medium or Heavy armor, this comes with a Gambeson that’s worn underneath for comfort. This is already factored into the other stats of the armor.

Weight This is how heavy your armor is for encumbrance when your armor is carried on your person. Worn armor doesn’t count towards your encumbrance.

Encumb This number is added to your Encumbrance as doing things with a weight on you is tiring.

Skill Adj Armor, regardless of how fitted, is restricting and heavy, making it harder to perform certain skills in certain gear. This is the number applied to physical Skill Checks.

Armor Bonuses

Bone This armor is especially effective when channeling magic. It only increases your channeling time by 5 if the legs and arms are uncovered and 10 if you wear the gauntlets and boots.

Shield Table Header Explanation

Size This indicates the general size of a shield. It also determines how tough your shield is.

DR This number is subtracted from the damage dealt by a weapon on a successful parry with a shield.

Def This is the number that you subtract from your character’s defense. Lugging a huge piece of material on the end of your arm makes dodging a bit difficult. Additionally, you’re often moving a shield into harm’s way, meaning, overall, you get hit more while wielding one.

Parry Some shields are balanced better than others and that allows you to deflect blows more effectively. This number is the shield’s Parry Threshold. This is overrides by your weapon’s Parry. Parrying with a shield subjects it to the Shield Breakage rules.

Cover This dice is added to your Defense roll against ranged weapons. If you’re actively crouching behind it, you instead add the maximum value of any dice and roll of that dice (i.e., a d8, you add d8 + 8, for d12 + d8, you add d12 + d8 + 20, etc.).

Flanks This is the number of additional attackers from the side that the shield can defend against. More on that is found in Chapter 9: Combat

Weight This is how heavy your shield is for encumbrance.

Encumb This number is added to your Encumbrance as doing things with a weight on you is tiring.

Bonus Some shields have additional unique bonuses. If there is a ‘yes’ in this column, look up the shield in the following section to find out more details.

For This is a recommendation for who would benefit most from using this type of shield. "S" stands for Specialist, meaning that if you're a primarily a shield fighter, this shield will get drastically better as you take Advanced Trainings in it but it will not serve your average person well.

"W" stands for warrior: this is a solid shield for frontline fighters who are going to be specializing in other things and only lightly deeping into Shield Advanced Trainings.

"A" stands for anyone: these shields serve as cheap replacements for armor and anyone can pick one of these up and get an instant benefit from the.

SHIELD & WEAPON BREAKAGE When you parry with a shield or weapon, there’s a chance of it breaking from the blow.

If the attacker manages to score enough damage after the shield or weapons DR to get over the appropriate Shield & Weapon Breakage threshold, a saving throw is made with the attack rolling a d20! and the defender rolling the corresponding dice.

A failed check on a weapon nets a cumulative -1 to Attack and Damage for a weapon until it can be repaired.

A failed check for a shield nets a cumulative -1 to Defense, Parry Threshold, and DR until it can be repaired.

On a success, the weapon or shield has managed to withstand the blow and gains no more penalties.

Shield Bonuses

Buckler The buckler is a lightweight shield which relies more on parrying blows than outright stopping them. The buckler also has the added benefit of being rather small with almost no weight, allowing it to be worn but not used while wielding two handed weapons. You receive no bonuses when doing this but it means that if you do switch over to a one handed weapon, you instantly get the buckler’s bonus.

Round This shield was meant for hitting your opponent, trying to throw them off guard. On a natural roll of 20 on Defense or an Unarmed Strike, you delay your opponent’s next attack by 5 seconds.
