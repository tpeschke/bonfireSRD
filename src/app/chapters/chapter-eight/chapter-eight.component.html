Skills are used as a way to determine the success or failure of a given action, based on what your character knows about the given subject or about how to perform the given task.

You don’t have to use these rules all the time, i.e., you don’t have to roll to see if your character successfully walks across a room, but at times they’re a good way to semi-randomly determine the outcome of a tricky task, i.e., walking across a room full of traps.

Skill Introduction

There are two types of skills that you’re going to be pulling from: Skill Suites and Advanced Skills.

Skill Suites represent broad swathes of knowledge that cover a variety of subjects. Suites are hard to increase because there’s so much ground to cover but can be used if you don’t have the specific Advanced Skill. An example of this is the Survival and Lore Skill Suites.

Advanced Skills are more tightly focused, covering one intellectual discipline. They’re easier to master but may not be applicable in every case. An example of this would be the First Aid, Fire Building, and Foraging skills in the Survival Skill Suite and the Law, History, and Medicine skills in the Lore Skill Suite.

ACQUIRING SKILLS

Throughout your character’s career, she’ll acquire and improve her skills. Both types of skills are purchased with CrP, that is spent either at Character Creation or on leveling up. All skills have a unique cost that represents how easy or difficult they are to master. You pay the price as noted in the section below, you improve the skill.

CALCULATING RANK

SKILL SUITES For all your Skill Suites, your starting Rank is 0, unless noted in the following example:

  • Your Primary Generalist's Main Skill Suite starts at Rank 3.
    • If you get two (noted with an "and", not "or"), one starts at Rank 3, the other starts at Rank 2, otherwise pick one to start at 3.
    • When you Specialize, you gain Rank 2 in its Main Skill Suite.
      • If you get two (noted with an "and", not "or"), one starts at Rank 2, the other starts at Rank 1, otherwise pick one to start at 2.
  • Your Secondary Generalist's Main Skill Suite starts at Rank 2.
    • If you would normally get two, you only get 1.
    • When you Specialize, you gain Rank 1 in its Main Skill Suite.
      • If you would normally get two, you only get 1.
  • Finally, you may pick 1 Skill Suite to start at Rank 2.
  • If, for any reason, a Skill Suite doubles up, you only get a +1 to its Rank.

ADVANCED SKILLS Your starting Rank for Advanced Skills is the Rank for the mother Skill Suite.

PURCHASING A SKILL Advanced Skills cost their Base Cost + (Current Rank * 2), while Skill Suites cost their Base Cost Base Cost + (Current Rank * 5). If you have Skill Adept, both have a minimum of 1.

Every purchase increases your Rank in that Skill by 1. Purchases of Skill Suites also raises the Rank of any child Advanced Skills you’ve already purchased, so that’s nice.

The Basics (Skill Checks)

Before we go off talking about Skill Challenges, time, equipment, and all those other things, it’s first important to understand the very basic mechanic all of those things will hang off of: Skill Checks.

Skill Checks are any checks made that move the Skill Challenge along and/or determine a success or failure.

At their very basic, Skill Checks have both sides rolling a d20!, adding any applicable Skill’s Rank, the Skill Modifier based on the Skill’s relevant stat, and any modifiers for difficulty. The person with the highest total wins and you successfully bypass or eliminate the Obstacle, depending on the situation.

If a Skill has more than one relevant stat, use the lowest modifier. A space on your character sheet has been provided to record this to make referencing during play quicker.

It is assumed that your character is trying their hardest to be successful in whatever they attempt and so any Check you make inflicts d4! Stress if they don't succeed by more than 5.

Skill Challenges

Skill Checks refer to a single roll against a single Skill, while a Skill Challenge refers to a host of Skill Checks. To use a metaphor: a Skill Challenge would be breaking into the castle. The Skill Checks would be climbing the walls, hiding in the shadows, sneaking past the guards, listening at doors, lockpicking any locks, etc.

OBJECTIVES AND OBSTACLES

A Skill Challenge doesn’t exist without something that your character wants and at least one thing that stands in their way.

This could be something straightforward: I want to get out of this jail cell, but a locked door is in my way, or it could be something more complicated like I want to capture this outlaw but he’s several days’ ride ahead of me.

These are your Objective and Obstacles, respectively.

Achieving your Objective is a matter of beating or getting past all the Obstacles in your way.

Obstacles are beaten through successfully rolling Skill Checks, which will be discussed more in the next section. Note that an Obstacle can continue to be a factor that needs to be considered, for example, a guard patrol, or it can be something that only needs to be taken care of once, for example, unlocking a door.

Obstacles also have Failure Threshold, which represents badly you have to fail against them to actually fail. More on this in the Complications & Hard Failures section below.

The Single Check

Before we dive in, I think it's worth saying that there may be times when you have something that might call for a Skill Check but not a Skill Challenge. Most often you'll do this when there is danger but not a huge time crunch, like, for instance, players leaping over a cliff or when there’s a chance of failure, like the player not knowing a monster weakness.

In that case, it would be perfectly reasonable to just call for a single Jumping (or Monster Craft) Check. If the players want to do some Preparation or use any of the other tools at their disposal, they can.

Or, if there is a time crunch but it is so narrow players really wouldn't have the time to prepare, like having to jump the chasm while a horde of brigands with bows are closing it, you might also just default to the single roll: no need to break out these rules in that case.

In these cases, the Failure Threshold of the Check would be 0, meaning a failure is a failure, but you can spice it up by having the jump almost make it but now the player is just barely hanging out: however, much time you want to spend on it.

DIFFICULTY

Difficulty is a dice that is added to the Skill Check based on things that might be going on at the time to either help or hinder your success. This difficulty is noted just as a single dice (+d6!, for example) but you'll add a d20! to it by default.

So, for example, finding food to eat while living off the land is much easier in a forest than it is in, say, a desert. In the forest, the players would receive a +d8! to their roll while in the desert the other side, the Obstacle, would get a +d20!. This is in addition to the base d20!, by the way.

Obstacles can have static, variable, shrinking, circular, growing, or universal difficulties.

STATIC means that the dice pretty much stays the same, outside of player interaction. This is often used for one off checks and not really used as the main part of a Skill Challenge because, let’s face it, doing the same Check over and over again isn’t that interesting.

What you'll mainly see is Static Difficulties linked together in an Obstacle. So, for instance, a rock wall that is slippery at the base, then has a section with a lot of handholds, and finishes with an overhang, would be three separate Checks, the first with a d12! difficulty for the wall, the next with a d4! for the players, and the final with a d10!, again for the wall.

SHRINKING means that it starts high but then the dice shrinks as time passes, often becoming static at a certain point. This is when the threat becomes less and less severe, and I would use this sparingly and with other types of Obstacles, but it can represent the fact that leaving a place is often easier than getting in.

GROWING is the opposite of shrinking: it starts low and then increases as time passes, again, sometimes becoming static at a certain point. This is used to represent threats that continue to grow, often times when there is time pressure.

UNIVERSAL This is a term for an Obstacle that will go off at some time but won't be directly faced by the players. It represents a chance encounter, such as running into someone randomly, or a stroke of bad luck, like bad weather. Unlike other Obstacles, players won't make Checks directly against it but any Failure (or Success) against another Obstacle in that Challenge adds a like amount of Failure (or Success) to any Universal Obstacles.

This can also be used for timed Challenges, such as a chance or encroaching deadline. In that case you not only count both Soft Successes and Complications, as they represent time passing, but also questions your players ask count as 1 Complication, representing them taking time to get stock of their surroundings, which you oftentimes don’t have as much time to do under pressure.

CIRCULAR means it starts at a difficulty, grows to a certain difficulty, and then shrinks down to its original difficulty, rinse and repeat, ad nauseam. It can also start shrinking, if you’d like. This is often used as patrols, like guards and such, but also a monster stalking an area would be represented by this type of Obstacle though you would have it be a bit more random to represent that unpredictability.

LINKED Obstacles are Obstacles that whose outcomes affect one another. So, for instance, if the players are in a wagon chase down a rocky road, with one player at the helm, stirring, and the others in the back firing crossbows, how well the player of the reigns does affect the attack rolls of those in the back. In this case, add or subtract the number of Complications or Soft Successes to the Check of any linked Obstacles.

Note that Linked Obstacles will also be paired with another Difficulty type.

So, for instance, a guard patrol that makes a loop around the house would be a good example of a Circular Obstacle. When the patrol is furthest away from where the players are breaking in, the difficulty to not be detected by them is 0 so they add nothing to their roll, but when the guards are right on top of the players breaking in, the difficulty is +d20!. If it takes them 7 minutes to get from that furthest point to the players, that means every minute, the difficulty for the players increases by 1 dice size.

But, as the guards finish their circuit, heading back to the furthest point away from the players, the difficulty shrinks by 1 size per minute.

Multi-Layer Checks

Let's say that you're trying to deduce facts about bears before you go to fight one. The thing is, there is a lot to know about bears and your character probably isn't going to know all of it, so making a single Check is too binary while making multiple Checks is too tedious.

In steps the Mutli-Layer Check.

In this case, the GM would assign Difficulty Dice to the different facts about bears based on how esoteric the knowledge is. He would then roll 1 d20! and all of those Difficulty Dice. You would only roll a single Check and the compare that to the difference totals to see if you recall those facts.

So, for instance, the knowledge that they hibernate is no common but it's not uncommon, do the GM assigns that d6!. The knowledge that they're afraid of fire is common, so he rolls d4! for that and the knowledge that they're sacred animals to the local indigenous tribe is pretty rare because said tribe is extremely secretive so that would be a d20!.

You roll your Monster Craft and get a total of 16. The GM gets a 13 for the d20, a 4 for the d4!, a 5 for the d6! and a 1 for the second d20!. That means the totals are 17, 18, and 14 respectively. This means your character hasn't really been around bears to he doesn't know that they hibernate or that you can scare them with fire, however, he does recall over hearing some of the wanderers in the local tavern talk about how the animal was sacred to the local tribe and killed one of them after he wounded one of the creatures.

"Pausing" the Skill Challenge

The players don’t always have to be making Skill Checks during a Skill Challenge - they should only do so when it makes sense.

So, (using the above example) if the patrols aren’t very good and the guards get too far away from the players to reasonably hear them, no Check should be required.

But that doesn’t mean that time stops: if the players wait long enough, the guards will return, and Checks will need to start up again.

COMPLICATIONS & HARD FAILURES

Failing a Check doesn’t automatically mean that you’re instantly screwed: Failing a single Move Silently Check doesn’t result in the guards catching you. In Bonfire, this represented by Complications and Hard Failures.

Every Obstacle will have a number of Complications it can gain before it hard fails. This is called its Failure Threshold. You gain Complications by failing Skill Checks: every 5 points below what you needed to get gives the Obstacle 1 Complication, rounded up (so a failure by 1 - 5 is still considered a Complication).

Complications represent complications, such as the guard hearing something, or you leaving a clue behind that might give someone a heads up that you're there. An Obstacle gets +2 for every Complication is has on it.

When an Obstacle gets more Complications than its Failure Threshold, it hard fails. Hard failures are when something bad actually happens. In this case, it does result in you getting caught by the guards, or it results in your falling off the wall and taking damage. Even after a hard failure, you can sometimes try again, like in the case of falling off the wall.

Failure doesn’t diminish during the Skill Challenge, but it does reset for a new one.

Complication in the Narrative

Complications don’t mean that the Skill Challenge grinds to a halt while the players keep reattempting that Skill: instead, it should represent the players coming closer to danger.

For a quick example, let’s say that there’s an adventurer, we’ll call him New York Smith, York for short, who is attempting to jump a chasm, which has a Failure Threshold of 1.

He rolls his Check but fails by 7, giving the Obstacle 1 Complication. In this case, he obviously jumped but also didn’t make it. What does that mean? Well, York didn’t quite make it but managed to grab the ledge as he fell, so he didn’t fall to his death, but he isn’t safe either.

York is grabbing hold of the ledge so he can't make another Jumping Check, but he does reason that he can use Climbing. Makes sense to the GM so they roll again. If York fails this time, he falls, but if he succeeds, he manages to scramble up the cliff, safe on the other side, but he is working at a disadvantage: the GM gets +2 to her roll this time around so he better hope he put Ranks in Climbing.

Strategies

ASSETS

These represent static pools of knowledge or information that you can pull from if you have time. So, for instance, a book on the subject, or a library are examples of an Asset but also an old friend who used to be a ranger and who could give you a few pointers could also be considered one.

Anything you can think of that might apply might be an Asset you can draw on.

Assets have two stats: their KNOWLEDGE and their ORGANIZATION. Basically, how much they know and how easy that knowledge is to get.

Each of these is given a qualifier of either POOR, AVERAGE, or GOOD.

For Knowledge, Poor represents a +3, Average +6, and Good +9 while for Organization Poor is Days, Average is Hours, and Good is Minutes.

Spending 1 unit of an Asset’s Organization gives you +1 bonus up to their Knowledge. So, if you’re searching through a poorly taken care of but extensive library (Knowledge: Good, Organization: Poor), spending 5 days there gives you a +5, while spending two weeks there only gives you a +9.

That bonus can then be redeemed on a Skill Check later on. Although it can only be redeemed once, it does apply to any rerolls made against the same Obstacle.

You don’t automatically know the Rank or the Time Investment of an Asset. This can also be applied to Weapon, Armor, and Shield Trainings through manuals and sparring.

Another benefit of Assets is that it is often multifaceted and can be used to get different bonuses. That old ranger friend can help you with navigation but also probably knows a thing or two about botany.

Assets can also be increased and expanded upon as well, increasing its qualifiers. A good relationship with a contact might give you information quicker, donating to the local library may increase its selection, or hiring excavators to dig up a ruin may unearth more artifacts that help you decipher that ancient script.

There may be some special cases where an Asset will take care of itself, but you may have to check back on it, such as Snaring in the forest. You set your trap and then a day or two later, you can go back and check it to see if you caught anything - in the meantime, you can get other work done.

REDEEMING CONTACTS Characters get a number of starting contacts based on their Charisma, with the expectation they'll gain more as they adventure, but these starting contacts can prove useful as they can be redeemed at any time and defined on the spot.

So, if the players need, for example, access to a monestary's library, one of the players can remember that one of his buddies is actually a monk there and might be able to help, redeeming one of his contacts.

After that, of course, contacts can be used time and time again: they're part of the world.

How a contact helps is largely determined by the circumstances of why they were redeemed but the GM should, for the most part, consider them Assets starting with Poor Knowledge and Organization. A player can increase that when they redeem a contact by sacrificing additional contacts, raising one of their stats on a one-for-one basis.

So a character with 3 unredeemed contacts can bring one in as a smuggler buddy who knows a lot about the local underworld, which would normally only cost him 1 of his contacts, but, instead, he sacrifices his 2 remaining ones to increase his smuggler buddy's Knowledge to Good. He can't redeem any more contacts but at least his smuggler buddy knows what he's talking about.

ROLEPLAYING OUT A SCENE VS USING ASSETS Assets are not meant to replace specific roleplaying areas where you need specific pieces of information: they’re meant to reward players for remembering to do otherwise boring and mundane things that might still help their character. You can also combine the two.

For instance, if the players are hunting a griffon, they might consider talking to a local monster hunter, who, though a scene, tells them specifics of how the griffon hunts and fights. During this time, the players learn that griffons tend to nest in mountains, so, after the talk is finished, one of the players hits up an old friend who knows a lot about climbing and spends a few hours training and getting points with him.

The first scene is roleplayed in detail while the second is handwaved because there’s nothing specific that the player will learn that will help them. Additionally, the first scene could also give the players a bonus to their Monstercraft roll for remembering additional things about the beast.

Using Other Players as Assets

I think it's completely reasonable that players would want to use each other's characters as Assets and why shouldn't they? So, in this case the player’s Knowledge is instead based around the Rank they have in the applicable Skill with their Organization being based on their Characteristics.

If they have 3 or fewer Characteristics related to teaching or communication, they’re considered Poor; 4 to 6 they’re considered Average; greater than 6, Good.

GMs should also feel free to reward players who sit there and talk things out and plan by awarding the Asset bonus as well, even if they don't spend a full hour+ planning.

EQUIPMENT

Your kit can really pull you through in a pinch and these are catchall rules for the use of equipment that doesn’t already come with auxiliary benefits, such as weapons (damage) and armor (DR).

Players are assumed to be using all equipment that would help them with a Check if they have time so they shouldn’t feel the need to go through their list of climbing gear every time they scale a wall, although, if time is short and they need to rush, that may be appropriate.

On a Skill Check, the first piece of equipment you use gives you a free reroll, taking the higher of the two results. After that, each additional piece gives you a +1, although if this additional gear has Traits that apply, you can use that instead of the +1. More on equipment Traits is found in the Deluxe version of Chapter 10: Equipment.

Redeeming this bonus causes the tool degrades, giving it (and any other equipment you use) 1 Wear, whether you succeed or not. More on Wear can be found in Chapter 10: Equipment.

Assets & Equipment Based Skills

Some skills are only going to be useful if you have the proper equipment or the right Asset. For instance, if you’re not out in the wilderness, you can’t really use Snaring or if you don’t have the proper tools or a woodshop, Maintenance won’t do you any good. In cases, being able to make the Check in the first place is considered an auxiliary benefit and so this bonus doesn't apply. This will need to be handled on a case-to-case basis.

HELPER SKILLS

Let’s say that you’re tracking someone in the wilderness. You have Hunting at Rank 4 and Endurance at Rank 3. Seems like being able to move quickly over a longer period of time, should help you catch the guys, and it does.

If you have another Advanced Skill that you think might help you, you can add it as a bonus, however doing so costs you d4! Stress for every Skill you apply. This can't be reduced by Not Sweating It.

This may seem like it is nearly identical to Preparation, and they are very close. The difference is that Preparation is a general thing that applies to a Skill Check you’re going to make in the future, while using a helper Skill is in the moment and is specific to the scenario. You can use both at the same time, since the help Skill gives a bonus and Preparation gives a reroll.

Skill Mobbing

For GMs, note that Helper Skills are how you handle Skill Mobbing, where everyone just overpowers a roll by all rolling against it. Instead, only one person rolls and only those with an applicable Advanced Skill and willing to take that Stress add their Rank to help out.

MULTI-TASKING

Let’s be realistic here: you’re probably going to end up trying to do two things at once, like climbing a wall and doing so quietly.

If that’s the case, you’re going to have to “split” your d20! into smaller dice and assign those to each of the Checks you’re trying to make, because your attention is divided. The total max value of the dice can never exceed 20 but it can be lower than it. The other side will still be rolling full d20!s against you.

So, if you’re climbing a wall and trying to do so quietly, you’d have to make a Climbing and a Move Silently Check. You could split it like so: d12! for Climbing and d8! for Move Silently, or d10! for Climbing and d10! for Move Silently, or d12!+d4! for Move Silently and d4! for Climbing.

Or you could just say “screw being quiet” and roll a d20! for Climbing and take a 0 for Move Silently. In this case, the GM would just roll against your Skill Rank. So, if you choose to ignore Move Silently but have a Rank 4 in it, the GM would roll their dice, and you can hope they don’t roll higher than 4.

Either way, besides this, it works like a normal Check, except that, for the Stress gain, you're going to roll 1d4! for each Skill they're attempting but only use the highest if applicable. They need to succeed all Checks by 5 to completely remove the Stress gain.

Not Paying Attention

Multi-Tasking is also used when someone isn't paying attention, such as a guard who's on watch but he's been there for several hours at this point so he's not doing it so closely. In this case, start at a d10!, lowering it further as needs be.

Helping Others

It feels good to help people and Bonfire is all about group cooperation. The fighter helps the skill monkey survive combat and the skill monkey helps the fighter not look like an idiot while doing detective work.

And how you help other characters will really depend on the situation. Perhaps your Preparation can be used to help another person hide or perhaps you have a bit of knowledge that they don’t.

Really, this will be done to what feels right in the moment, but this note is here to draw attention to someone helping you as an Asset and someone adding their Rank to yours with a Helper Skill, and how they’re different.

And the difference is time. If you have plenty of time to coach someone while they’re climbing a cliff face, you’d be an Asset and you wouldn't take Stress.

But if you’re being chased by the guards and it’s a matter of seconds or minutes, you’d add a Helper Skill and both of you would need to take the Stress.

Note that if the skill monkey is also trying to climb the cliff face and coach as they go, that would be considered a d10! worth of effort for Multi-Tasking, though you don’t have to actually roll to see if you help.

NOT SWEATING IT

While the default is to assume that characters actually care about the outcome of the Check, that's not always the case since not everything is equally important. In the cases of lesser importance, the character can simply not sweat it.

In this case, they take no Stress from the Check but take a -4 penalty.

PREPARATION

Preparation is anything your character does to help a specific Skill Check that will be made in the future (even if that future is 2 seconds from now). Note that this doesn't have to be a Check you make: it can also apply to an ally's Check (if it makes sense).

Usually, these only take a couple of seconds but can vary widely depending on the circumstances. Throwing a rock is a matter of moments while constructing a gilly suit will take a lot longer.

As long as there is time, there is no chance of failure or, if the players have plenty of time, Preparation just happens. Mark what you did for later in the Skill Challenge.

However, if there is a time crunch or a chance of failure, Preparation works like a normal Skill Check with success means the preparation is actually useful.

Either way, you only get one Prep Check per Advanced Skill that applies for each upcoming Check, unless you have Assets but remember that they do take time.

That means that if you know you're going to have to make a jump, you can use Acrobatics to stretch, Endurance to do a few practice sprints to the edge, and Sports (long jumping) to remember techniques all to prep for the same Jump check.

When you redeem Preparation that you’ve readied, you can reroll Obstacle's d20, taking the worse of the two results.

Planning & Action Phases

While it isn’t entirely black and white, it’s helpful to think of Challenges as composing two phases: planning and action; the laying out the plan and then the heist.

Planning is gathering intel, getting your team and equipment together, and planning your route of attack. This is where you’ll use Preparation and Assets mostly.

Action phase is when you’ll be doing a lot of the Checks and Stress, as well as equipment and companions - using what you learned in the Prep Phase to help you get through the sticky parts.

But don’t get too hung up on separating these ideas though. Even during the heist, you might find it worth your time to sit on your heels and take your time while doing some Preparation as there will almost certainly be problems that crop up that you need to adjust to.

RUSHING IT

If you’re in a hurry to get the job down, you can just rush it. This lowers the time a Check actually makes but gives you a -5.

The bonus for this is that Complications you would get from the Check don't get added to Universal or Linked Obstacles in the Challenge, though Hard Failures do add 2 Complications to them.

ACROBATICS This covers your character's ability to stretch their body, do flips, and turn cartwheels. This allows the character to vault fences without slowing down or roll to avoid falling damage.

Reference the Check to Not Slow Down table to calculate the base Difficulty to, well, not slow down when you encounter an obstacle.

To reduce falling damage, remove 10 ft from the effective fall distance for every soft success you get.

CLIMBING This Skill covers climbing and repelling up and down walls of various heights and textures.

Difficulty should be based on height, with about every 40 ft increasing the Difficulty by 1 dice size.

Textures should also play a major factor in not just how tough the Check is but also how many Checks need to be made for a given wall.

So, if there’s a wall, where the base is stone at a 45% slant for 20 ft, then it moves to close knit stones for another 40 ft, and then finally to a wood overhang for just 5 ft, and then to a wooden wall for 10 ft, there would be 4 Climbing Checks in this order: +d4!, +d10! (d8! for base but because it’s 40 ft, it gets bumped up 1), d10!, and +0.

ENDURANCE Through training, a character can push his body past the limits of his base physical ability. The Rank of this Skill serves as DR against the damage you take while Running or Sprinting, although specifics are given in the Actions section of Chapter: 9 Combat.

ESCAPE ARTIST This allows the character to escape bonds and restrictions and is always rolled opposed, either against the Rope Use Skill of the person who tied them or against the quality of the manacles or binds the person was put in.

The character can make a straight +0 Check, not to break the bounds or get free, but to instead move his body to an advantageous position.

JUMPING When you need to cover a distance without touching the ground, jump.

Typically, a character can jump 16 inches vertically or 5 ft horizontally without a Check, however, if they wish to go further, they’ll need to roll.

Each soft success adds 2 inches to the vertical jump and 6 inches to the horizontally. Starting at a run doubles this.

Small creatures half these numbers while Large creatures double them.

SPORT (SPECIFIC) This covers the actual playing of a specific sport as well as an in-depth knowledge of the game’s rules as well as famous players and techniques.

If it becomes a plot point, the GM should feel free to make this Skill apply to only certain positions, like pitching or shortstop but this a fantasy roleplaying game so I doubt that’ll happen.

LORE SKILL SUITE

This suite covers general knowledge, which is mostly academic in nature. Most of it is pretty heavy stuff but the basics are second nature or stuff that’s been picked up here and there.

ADMINISTRATION While they’ll never inspire the awe or respect that other leaders do, people who oversee the day to day functions of an organization keep the lights on so that others can make the speeches.

Administrators can make a Check at +0 to gain a clue about an item the party might need on their adventure, or one to retroactively remember that they did buy an item back at the market for just such an occasion.

Each of these Checks they make increases the Difficulty of the next one by 1 step.

HISTORY Everyone knows certain large events, but few can tie those events into an overarching narrative and understand their effects on later events.

The History Skill allows a character to not just recall events but also to link them to the present day, to certain traditions and rituals of a culture, or the behavior of certain members of a culture, giving insight on how to proceed.

LANGUAGE (SPECIFIC) A character receives this Skill for free in their native tongue with a Rank equal to their Int and it allows them to speak and be understood by others with Skill in that language.

Checks are only made when timing is critical, such as communication in a battle, or when understanding is key, such as when debating foreign policy.

If one of the characters is in a Confrontation with another in a tongue that’s not their native one, that person can only bring in as many dice per round as he has Ranks in that Language.

Additionally, when two native speakers are in a Confrontation, the one with the higher Language Rank gains an additional dice (see Applicable Skills in Chapter 8: Confrontation ). In case of tie, nothing happens.

The Lore Skill Suite can’t be used in place of a Language Check for tongues you don’t know unless you have the Language Skill in a related Language. Using a real-world example, someone with the Language (Swedish) Advanced Skill couldn’t use the Lore Skill Suite to understand a person with Language (French) but could use it when talking to someone with Language (Norwegian) or Language (Danish).

LAW (CULTURE) This Skill covers the intricacies of the legal system, including landmark rulings and common punishments for common crimes. A character can make their case or find loopholes to exploit the legal system.

A character’s Lore Skill can only be used to substitute for their native culture but not others’. If the character does wish to branch out, his mastery of Law for that new culture starts at 0, as he must learn everything from the ground up. Diving more into his own culture’s laws (buying this Advanced Skill for his culture) uses the Lore Skill as base, as per usual.

LITERACY (ALPHABET) This Skill allows a character to read and write in a language’s alphabet, giving them the power to pen books and poems, or passive aggressive notes. A character who's taken the Lore Skill Suite is considered to be literate in their native language's alphabet.

This Skill can be taken multiple times, each instance representing mastery of a new writing system. The scribe can then use that alphabet to pen any language they know, in the same way that we could use the Latin Alphabet to pen Arabic or the Korean Alphabet to write English.

To decipher a text, the person needs to know both the alphabet it's written in and the language it's using, which most of the time won't matter but can be used as a cheap way to do a cypher in a pinch.

MATHEMATICS While this may seem like just a Skill that allows you to be able to juggle figures and add totals and calculate the area of a circle like a huge nerd, mathematics has a more practical use: correctly guessing distances.

While you can try to eyeball how far away something is, a Mathematics Check vs +0 allows you to do rough calculations, giving you the ability to know exactly what penalty you’re going to get for using a ranged weapon or who's going to be affected by the Area of Effect of a spell you’re going to cast.

MEDICINE With proper treatment and nursing, a wound that would lay you out for weeks, may only keep you down for a couple of days. This Skill involves the knowledge of long-term care, when bandages should be changed, which poultices will have the greatest effect, and what minor surgeries need to be performed.

A character with this Skill can make a Check for a wounded person, adding the Severity of his largest Wound to the opposing roll.

So, if a person has a 5, 3, and 8 Severity Wound, he can make a Medicine Check vs d20+8.

Success means that the person can take 1 day off the healing time for every Wound every time it drops a Severity.

So, a success against a 5 Severity Wound means that it was treated properly and takes 10 days to completely heal ( 4 + 3 + 2 + 1 + 0 ) instead of the normal 15.

You may also increase the Difficulty modifier by half again as much - if you succeed, you take 2 days off per Severity. So, if you're against a d20!+9, you can make it a d20!+14 ( 9 + ( 9 / 2 ) ) for that bonus.

Additionally, when resisting Poison, Toxin, or Diseases, you can add your Medicine Rank, rounded up, to each round of the Confrontation.

MONSTER CRAFT This Advanced Skill is under both the Lore and Survival Skill Suite but still counts as 1 Skill, so you don’t have to buy it twice - they share their Rank. It simply represents the different ways that each Skill Suite approaches the craft.

Monster Craft gives you insight into the creatures that roam the Middle Realm. Simply put, each soft success gives you a (truthful) answer to a question.

Difficulty is based on how common the monster is. Common is +0, uncommon +d6!, rare is d12!, and legendary is +d20!.

RELIGION (CULT) This Skill acquaints the character with a knowledge of cults, their common Covenants and Miracles, as well as their hierarchies, means of advancement, and important members.

It also allows a character to gain a knowledge of their rituals and sacrifices, as well as the taboos of their clergy and how to appease their heavenly messengers and avenging angels.

The Religion Skill is taken for a single Covenant, but can be purchased independently for different Covenants.

A character adds the tens digit of this Skill to their chance of gaining Favor when making sacrifices to the god of that cult.

WORLD CRAFT This area of study covers the topography of the Middle Realm. It’s forests and rock formations and biomes. It helps predict whether or not it’s going to rain or whether the next summer is going to be a hot one.

Predicting what’s coming in the next hour is a Check vs +0, with the Difficulty increasing by 1 dice size for each time you double the prediction window. So, detecting the weather in the next 2 hours is vs +d4!, while doing so in the next 16 hours is vs +d10!.

STREETWISE SKILL SUITE

This suite covers knowledge that a character might pick on the street or in the school of hard knocks. While Lore is book smart, Streetwise is street smart.

CURRENT AFFAIRS A character with this Skill is well informed about the day to day affairs of regional powers and men and women of importance, their dealings and alliances, as well as their hatreds. He can pick out celebrities and know how and why someone got rich and famous.

They can also use this Skill to guess the Honor category of someone by watching how people react to them and letting them know about certain NPCs' Reputations.

DECEPTION This covers all sorts of deception, where you want the person to believe the opposite of the truth, or, to put it simply, when you want to lie to them.

The level of Difficulty to pull off a lie is based on how much of what you’re saying isn’t the truth, strictly speaking. This is always opposed by either the other character’s Deception or Perception but only if the character has reason to suspect that you’re lying to them.

Even if you succeed, this isn’t a permanent way of changing their mind. New evidence presented to that person may cause them to rethink their opinion on what you tried to sell them so be careful about what falsehoods you spin.

FORGERY Mostly commonly used for creating fake currency, forgery is a Skill that allows a character to reproduce something effectively and fool others. This Skill acquaints the character with common forging techniques and knowledge with what possible agents will be looking for.

The forger must also have the appropriate corresponding Craft or Trade Skill, so he can’t forge coins if he doesn’t have the Metalworking Skill, though that’s within reason: a character forging an official passport with one at hand doesn’t need to have Literacy as he can just copy the letters, though he may also need to have Artistry (wood cutting) to create the heraldry on it, but someone trying to forge an official declaration from scratch does need to have the Literacy Skill in that language plus Artistry (wood cutting).

Forgery is opposed by Perception, the applicable Skill, or Forgery but only if the character looking has reason to suspect there’s something else. A lazy guard probably won’t Check your passport too closely right before lunch, but a particularly astute one who's on the lookout for a promotion will.

GAMBLING/GAMING This Skill doesn’t give the character a gift for winning games, just that he knows the rules and knows the odds, which could in turn lead to him winning the game.

INTUITION The ability to understand people and get where they're coming from can help you figure out what will and won't work with them better.

A Check vs +0 allows the character to get a feel for how open a person would be to a Confrontation over something or what would be definite deal breakers for them. It doesn't reveal any of their Characteristics or their strengths as it's more of gut feeling than anything.

Each Check on the same person increases the Difficulty by 1 dice size.

They can also use it to confirm their theories about what Characteristics a character has. The player needs to make a reasonable guess about the Characteristic first - so players who’re simply throwing out guesses automatically fail.

This is done at a Check vs +0 to start with but increases by 1 for every dice size per guess, whether the character succeeded at the last ones or not.

LISTENING While Perception handles the other senses, Listening has to deal with your character’s auditory senses. This goes beyond simply hearing something: listening allows the character to know when an otherwise innocuous noise is actually something to pay attention to though it might not always be apparent why at first.

PERCEPTION This covers four of the five senses that a character has: sight, touch, taste, and smell. SLEIGHT OF HAND This Skill covers all things that require you to do quick, delicate movements with your fingers. This may be slipping a card up your sleeve, picking a pocket, or doing cat’s cradle while bored.

SURVIVAL SKILL SUITE

This suite covers everything that a character might need to survive in the wild; Knowledge of what plants to eat and the Skills to hunt and breed animals.

BOTANY This gives a knowledge of what the herbs used in cooking, alchemy, and medicine look like in their natural environment and it allows you to identify their common uses. It also helps find food in the wilderness.

If halving their overland travel speed, a person with Botany can make a Check every travel period: if they succeed, they can consider themselves 1 step up on the Long-Term Exhaustion table. So, if they had nothing, they could consider themselves Rationing.

If they choose to stay put, a success nets them 2 steps up.

4 or more soft successes results in doubling their output, meaning they possibly found themselves enough food for the next day.

Mostly, this is used for detecting poisonous plants, which is Check vs +d4! but is a common Helper Skill for both Medicine and Cooking/Baking.

FIRE BUILDING Against the cold, lonely nights out in the wild, there is only the fertility symbols you carve for yourself and the roar of the fire. This Skill allows you to build one of those things: the fire.

Under normal conditions, it takes about 30 minutes and a Check vs +0 to set up and light a fire, assuming you don’t have to go far out of your way to find the wood.

FIRST AID This gives the character a crude understanding of battlefield medicine: making splints and staunching wounds.

A character with this Skill can make a Check for a person wounded in that last 30 minutes, adding the Severity of any Wounds that he's treating to the opposing roll.

So, if a person has a 5, 3, and 8 Severity Wound, he can make a First Aid Check vs d20+16 or he can choose not to treat the 8 Severity Wound and make a vs d20!+8. Success means that the person can take 1 level of Severity off those Wounds. So, a 5 Severity Wound drops immediately to a 4 Severity Wound. You cannot go back and do a Check for a Wound that you skipped.

You have 30 minutes to perform First Aid Checks for a person, with each Wound taking 3 minutes, if applicable.

You may also increase the Difficulty modifier by half again as much - if you succeed, you hit 2 points of damage. So, if you're against a d20!+9, you can make it a d20!+14 ( 9 + ( 9 / 2 ) ) to heal 2 points of damage.

HANDLE ANIMAL (SPECIFIC) This Skill deals with the handling and care of a specific animal, from birth to the slaughterhouse. It also deals with training and common illnesses to make sure the stock is healthy and virile.

While most Checks are handled like normal knowledge Checks or on a case-by-case basis (such as calming down your animal during a storm), using a question-and-answer style, this Skill is also used for training, with the base number of tricks you can teach an animal equal to their Int (note that carrying things and handling a bridle are two different tricks).

The animal needs to have both the basic temperament and physical ability to learn a trick (no sheep becoming pack animals and no dogs learning to pick locks). The GM may increase or decrease this for particularly smart/dumb and obedient/obstinate animals.

After a month of training, a roll is made vs +0. If successfully, the animal learned the trick. If not, another Check can be made in a month, although every failure increases the Difficulty to learn that trick by 1 dice size.

The base price of this Skill is calculated towards domesticated animals. If the player wishes to learn how to deal with wild animals, this Skill costs thrice as much to purchase.

HIDING This allows a character to make the most of a situation or environment to cloak themselves against prying eyes. This is always opposed by Perception, with the Difficulty determined by the area. Characters can also use this to instantly get an idea of how well they can hide in the given space.

MONSTER CRAFT This Skill is the same as the Monster Craft under the Lore Skill Suite. Look there for details on it.

NAVIGATION Either using the stars or the position of the moons and sun, this character can track their way even through the most untamed wilderness without getting lost. It also allows them to tell the time.

Without proper equipment, a character can only give rough estimations of where they are, anchored on larger geographic features (like mountains or rivers but not towns or roads) to the nearest 10 miles.

SCAVENGING There’s a gift in high-speed appraisal. This is often used by those picking over battlefields for anything they could sell, but it’s also found a niche with adventurers who use it to find food and sustenance while traveling away from civilization.

A Check vs +0 tells a character which of two things considered is worth more. This can’t be used for coinage, but can be any other type of good. It also gives the character an idea of whether broken equipment can be reasonably salvaged.

This doesn’t give even a ballpark of what anything is worth, but it only takes a few seconds of consideration.

If using this Skill to scavenge for food, use the rules presented in the Botany section, however, two or more Complications results in you eating something poisonous.

SNARING This teaches the character how to properly create and place snares to trap smaller wildlife. It also gives some cursory knowledge on the best places to put them.

Snares have a chance of giving you food once a week, although you’ll only actually roll when you Check them. Every soft success you get gives you half a day’s worth of food.

HUNTING Most of the time, you’re not going to down your query in one go, and so the hunt is on as your watch for subtle signs of their passing. While this Skill was originally used to hunt wildlife, it has been repurposed by wardens and bounty hunters trying to capture the most dangerous game.

The Hunting Skill can be used to track your prey, get a sense of their movements, and predict where they’re going. It can also give insight into common and uncommon natural creatures.

If you’re using it for food, hunting is a boom or bust strategy: at the end of each full day you spend hunting, roll a Check.

For each soft success you gain 2 days’ worth of food, but either way, you gain 1 damage from exhaustion and possibly more if you suffer a Complication.

USE ROPE Covering all kinds of knots and bindings, the Use Rope Skill is pretty self-explanatory, though it should be noted that you can also use this Skill to get a sense of the tensile strength of a rope or how much you’re going to need for a certain job.

To keep your players on their toes, I suggest you make the Check, not when they tie the knot, but when it’s tested.

TACTICS SKILL SUITE

Combat isn’t just about raw physical power, but more about how it’s directed and used. This Skill Suite deals with logistics and making the most out of what you have.

COMBAT STYLE (STYLE) By studying the best tactics and how to fight in certain circumstances, a warrior can take advantage of his surroundings and combat styles emerge. Combat Styles can be anything but involved in parts: a situation and a metric.

This situation is when the Combat Style is most effective. For instance, it can be “in the forest”, “fighting on rooftops”, “being outnumbered”, “being in a duel”, “fighting while prone”, etc. Really anything (as long as your GM approves).

The second part of the Combat Style is the metric applied to. This can be anything you could normally specialize in.

Both the situation and metric are selected at the time of purchase and cannot be changed.

If the situation is applicable, the character receives a +1 to that metric for every 2 Ranks of that Skill he’s obtained.

So, a character with the Combat Skill (unarmed fighting, attack) at 5 who gets into a tavern brawl gets a +2 to attack as long as he doesn’t draw his weapon and simply uses fisticuffs.

Having a high Rank in Tactics doesn't automatically give you Combat Style bonuses under all circumstances and buying this Skill for the first time starts it off at Rank 0, instead of the Tactic Suite's Rank.

DISTRACTION More than simply yelling “hey, look” at someone, the art of distraction is the art of holding people’s attention.

The amount of time that someone is delayed is based on the Difficulty of the Check and how bent on their current activity they are.

On a success, the person (or persons) is distracted for the amount of time according to the Difficulty * half the number of soft successes, rounded up.

LEADERSHIP The difference between a leader and an administrator is that the latter gets stuff done and the former gets others to get stuff done but hooking into what they believe and how they view themselves.

Assuming you have a willing audience, you may make a Leadership Skill Check vs +0 pointed at a Characteristic you think the person or group might have.

If you succeed the Check and they have the Characteristic you’re looking for (or close enough - you don’t have to be exact), you increase their Panic threshold by 1 step for the next d4! hours. Gaining 4 or more soft success increases it by 2 steps.

You can increase the number of Characteristics you’re aiming for by increasing the Difficulty dice size of the Check by a similar number.

QUARTER MASTERING There’s an art to packing and making sure that everyone is well fed and prepared. If the quartermaster is using a personal container of any kind, he adds 2S for each Rank he has in this Skill. Otherwise, he adds 1S per Rank. This also applies to any person he packs for.

RALLY When manure hits the windmill, no speeches or grand narratives will keep a person from bolting. Only the cold will of a competent commander can keep a battle from becoming a route.

A captain who successfully makes a Check vs +) for someone who is currently over their Stress Threshold, heals that person a number of Stress equal to 3 * his number of soft successes. This is opposed by the enemy captain, if applicable, and each Check against the same target increases the Difficulty by 1 dice size, regardless of whether it was successful or not.

WARFARE The Warfare Skill hands directing troops on the battlefield, weighing options, and devising plans on the fly.

TRADES SKILL SUITE

This suite covers the crafting and creating Skills. While there are a wide array and breadth of professions, this list is pared down to those areas of knowledge where the common man and the adventurer overlap in their needs.

APPRAISAL (SPECIFIC) It’s wise to know what something is worth before selling and buying it. This Skill handles that and is taken for a specific type of good, such as books & scrolls, livestock, jewelry, arms & armor, and so on.

If a character has the appropriate Skill covering the item he’s looking to sell/purchase, a Check will tell them what kind of deal they're getting on the item - how much they’re being ripped off or how much of a steal it actually is.

Additionally, the character should get a bonus if he has any Skill that’s applicable to the thing he’s purchasing.

ARTICULATION You may think that the truth is powerful enough to carry your argument, but you can be beat out by a master of deception. Articulation is the Skill of rhetoric and formulating the truth in such a way that makes it the most convincing.

The level of Difficulty to convince someone is how much evidence you can supply to back up your arguments. This is opposed by Deception or Articulation but only if someone is actively trying to deceive or convince the same person as you.

Note that the Difficulty of their Deception Check may add bonus dice to your Articulation Check.

Even if you succeed, this isn’t a permanent way of changing a person’s mind. Others may lie more effectively than you can tell the truth and that may cause them to rethink their opinion on what you tried to sell them.

ARTISTRY (SPECIFIC) While some may not find the Fine Arts a worthy way to spend time, others may disagree. Artistry covers all artistic endeavors, including painting, sculpture, embroidery and anything else that is done for art’s sake.

COOKING/BAKING On the road, a hot meal can count for a lot. A character who has a hot meal gains 1 Relaxation for every soft success the cook had on their Check.

On top of this, a character can prepare otherwise unpalatable or poisonous food in such a way as to make the most of it, though it is a Check vs d12! to do so.

Unless the character is trying to prepare a feast worthy of His Imperial Majesty, basic daily cooking only requires a Check vs +0.

CRAFT (SPECIFIC) This Skill covers any Skill that makes something that isn’t noted here, such as carpentry, tailoring, basket weaving, pottery, etc. The GM may adjust the price for a particularly difficult to master study.

LEATHERWORKING From the moment it leaves the beast to the moment the final touches are finished, leatherworking handles the process of making and crafting works from the hides of animals, including skinning and tanning.

MAINTENANCE The Skill of tinkers and adventurers, this gives the person a general knowledge of easy and ad hoc fixes that can be done on the fly.

A successful Check lowers the Wear on the item by 1. The level of Difficulty in repairing something is equal to the Wear on the item, rounded up to the nearest dice size.

So repairing a spear with 1 Wear is a d4!, while repairing one with a 5 Wear is a d6!.

Another Check can't be made until the item gains more Wear.

METALWORKING This covers the process of changing crude ore into a beautiful weapon, armor, or decorative piece. It also covers the construction and repair of more mundane things, like plows, but if you’re picking this Skill up, I doubt you’re doing it to corner the farmer’s market.

MUSICIAN (SPECIFIC) This covers the mastery of specific instruments, including singing. At the end of the day, the dulcet tones of a neatly played mandolin can ease the ache of a long day.

A character who hears someone play an instrument gains 1 Relaxation for every soft success the player had on his Check. This doesn’t stack with others playing; however, you will take the highest roll and use that to determine Relaxation. It also does no good if you’re in a stressful situation, like being hunted.

Unless the character is trying to serenade a queen, basic daily ditties only require a Check vs +0.

PERFORMANCE This Skill covers everything from making your case before a court or a stirring speech before charging into battle.

On a success, anyone listening gains a temporary Devotion to what the performer wants: whether that’s themselves or an organization or a case. If the Devotion already exists, the size is temporarily increased.

The Devotions strength is equal to the number of soft successes the performer had.

WEIRDCRAFT SKILL SUITE

The science of the Weird is an oft studied but little understood branch of knowledge that requires a deep knowledge of the nature of the Painted World.

With this Skill Suite, someone can identify a spell, point out needed regents and processes for ritual shapings, detect if something is touched by the Weird and what its properties are, or even detect if a spell has been cast in an area, and have a chance of finding out what it was.

ALL ADVANCED WEIRDCRAFT SKILLS Unlike other Advanced Skilled, the Weirdcraft Advanced Skills pretty much work the same and so don't need their own section.

The first bonus that having an Advanced Weirdcraft Skill gives you is that it gives you further insight into a particular Weird Tradition, allowing you to detect it better. For more information on what each Tradition specializes in, head over to Chapter 11: The Weird.

The other thing it does is allow you to shape cantrip versions of spells from that Tradition, regardless of Archetype. The ability to shape is inherit in all living things though it takes decades of practice, a strong family bloodline, and/or a natural gift to do what Weird-Adept Archetypes do, a form of practical "folkweird" is often passed down as part of the tools of the trade.

This doesn't, and will never, allow someone to shape the full version of a spell, but it can give them a boost or an edge. The specific details should be left up to the GM and player but they should always be appropriate to the Tradition and never be more powerful than a spell that does the same thing. Most commonly this will mean it will get used as a Helper Skill.

Shaping a cantrip is still shaping and is stressful and so that anytime any of these Skills are used to shape, the shaper gets 2d6! Stress.
