Skills are used as a way to determine the success or failure of a given action, based on what your character knows about the given subject or about how to perform the given task.

You don’t have to use these rules all the time, i.e., you don’t have to roll to see if your character successfully walks across a room, but at times they’re a good way to semi-randomly determine the outcome of a tricky task, i.e., walking across a room full of traps.

SKILL INTRODUCTION

There are two types of skills that you’re going to be pulling from: Skill Suites and Advanced Skills.

Skill Suites represent broad swathes of knowledge that cover a variety of subjects. Suites are hard to increase because there’s so much ground to cover but can be used if you don’t have the specific Advanced Skill. An example of this is the Survival and Lore Skill Suites.

Advanced Skills are more tightly focused, covering one intellectual discipline. They’re easier to master but may not be applicable in every case. An example of this would be the First Aid, Fire Building, and Foraging skills in the Survival Skill Suite and the Arcana, History, and Medicine skills in the Lore Skill Suite.

ACQUIRING SKILLS

Throughout your character’s career, she’ll acquire and improve her skills. Both types of skills are purchased with CrP, that is spent either at Character Creation or on leveling up. All skills have a unique cost that represents how easy or difficult they are to master. You pay the price, you improve the skill.

CALCULATING RANK

SKILL SUITES Your Skill Suites, your starting Rank is the Skill Bonus listed under the corresponding Stat. For Suites with multiple Stats, average the Skill Bonus, rounded down.

ADVANCED SUITES Your starting Rank for Advanced Skills is the Rank for the mother Skill Suite.

PURCHASING A SKILL Skills cost their Base Cost - Skill Bonus + (Current Rank * 2), minimum 1. This is true for both Skill Suites and Advanced Skill. Note that, like Skills Suites above, if there are multiple Stats attached to a Skill, average the Skill Bonus, rounded down.

Every purchase increases your Rank in that Skill by 1. Purchases of Skill Suites also raises the Rank of any child Advanced Skills you’ve already purchased, so that’s nice.

SKILL SPECIALIZATIONS

🜂 Advanced Rule

It often pays to specialize. After all, many dissertations cover extremely minute subjects with incredible depth and drawing on that well of knowledge can be a huge benefit.

Skill Specializations represent an area of knowledge within an Advanced Skill that your character spends extra time versing themselves in. As such, they gain a bonus.

Skill specializations come in layers, modifiers that narrow down the field. Each layer gives a +1 to your effective Rank when it comes into play.

For example, a monster hunter studying predators, would gain a+1 Rank, while another that studies flying predators would gain a +2 Rank bonus to their checks, and a monster hunter that studies the flying predators of the Uchka Mountains would gain a bonus of +3 to their Rank.

There may come a time when a specialization only kind of applies. In this case, it may be kind of helpful, as the max bonus that can be gained from it is +1.

So, when encountering a land predator in the Uchka Mountains, all three monster hunters would only gain +1 to their Rank.

That sort of super-focused study takes a lot of time and effort and man only has so much time and energy to give. The total number of specializations per Advanced Skill that a character can have is equal to half his Intelligence score.

A character can gain a specialization like any other skill; having his character go out of the way to learn more about his craft. The price is the base cost of the Advanced Skill times the number of layers in the specialization plus one.

A character can go back and increase the number of layers in his Skill Specialization after his initial purchase of it, but it costs additional amount of CrPs equal to twice the base cost of the Advanced Skill.

THE BASICS (SKILL CHECKS)

Before we go off talking about Skill Challenges, time, equipment, and all those other things, it’s first important to understand the very basic mechanic all of those things will hang off of: Skill Checks.

Skill Checks are any checks made that move the Skill Challenge along and determines a success or failure.

These are made by rolling the Difficulty Dice (defaulting as a d20!), adding your Rank and comparing it to your opponent’s total, who will be rolling similar dice.

ORDER OF OPERATIONS OF A CHECK

These will be gone into greater detail later on but here is a quick overview on how to resolve a Skill Check:

1. Decide to Stress or Not

2. Figure Out Factors

3. Roll Dice

4. Decide If You Want Any Equipment to Apply

5. Resolve Fallout

SKILL CHALLENGES

Skill Checks refer to a single roll against a single Skill, while a Skill Challenge refers to a host of Skill Checks. To use a metaphor: a Skill Challenge would be breaking into the castle. The Skill Checks would be climbing the walls, hiding in the shadows, sneaking past the guards, listening at doors, lockpicking any locks, etc.

PACE & BASE TIME

Before we begin, we need to talk about the scale, or Pace of Skill Checks, just so you understand it. Different Skill Challenges take different amounts of time and cover different areas and this contributes to the feel of that specific Challenge.

For instance, a person tracking an outlaw over in the hinterlands has a very different Pace than one who is chasing that outlaw down in a city. In the first case, the Challenge can stretch for month, so the Pace could be days or weeks while in the second case, it's a matter of minutes, so the Pace is in seconds.

Essentially, the Pace is how much time a Skill Check takes up. You'll notice that all Skills have a Base Time, which is either 1/2 Pace, 1 Pace, or Per Asset. So, if the Pace is 1 day, 1/2 Pace Skills take 6 hours, 1 Pace Skills take 1 day, and per Asset Skills are per Asset, which will be discussed further in the Tools at your Disposal section.

CLOCKS, FAILURES & SUCCESSES

Like a warrior has Vitality, experts have Clocks. These Clocks represent how your character could fail or succeed at their Skill Challenge and are defined by the how of how you tackle the Challenge. These are known as Fail State and Objective Clocks.

For instance, let’s say that you’re breaking into the house to steal the royal jewels. You have 2 ways you can fail, running into a patrol or running into the family, and 1 way to succeed, finding those jewels. If you just go in blind, your GM may say that it will take 3 minutes for the guards to find you, 5 minutes to run into a member of the family, and 15 minutes to find the jewels, though your character won’t know this starting out.

You can modify, remove, discover, or get to know Clocks by gathering information. Continuing the above example: by getting to know how the patrols move around the mansion, your character can always be where they’re not so not there’s no chance of running to a patrol and that Clock doesn’t exist any longer.

Maybe, through some friends who work there, he discovers that the family randomly moves around the house so it’s impossible to know their movements at any given time but he can guess how likely he is to run into them. Now, he knows that there is 5 minutes on the family Clock.

And, finally, he uses some reasoning to decide that the jewels are probably in the private quarters of the mansion, vastly narrowing his searching and reducing that Clock from 15 to 7 minutes. Though, if he’s wrong, that Clock also disappears because it won’t matter how long he searches: what he’s looking for isn’t there.

For the most part, players shouldn’t know what Clocks they’re up against or how long they have on them starting out and need to use their brains, Skills, and other party members to discover the information. Knowledge is power.

Clock of Damocles

GMs should run the Challenge off of the most urgent Fail State Clocks, though what this Clock is counting down to should be kept from the players. This keeps the threat of failure forward in their mind, even when that actual threat is out of view. Updates should be given based on 0 for that Clock.

So, for instance, the players are scouting an enemy base. There are patrols nearby but the players don't know that. The patrol takes 2 hours to make their rounds and the Pace of the Challenge is set to 30 minutes.

The GM states that the players have 2 hours left: what're they going to do with that time? That's all they know: something is going to happen in 2 hours and they need to make the best use of that time.

Should the players figure out what the Clock of Damocles represents it should become the next big unknown threat. It should also change if the players are able to modifiy or remove it.

Note that Fail State Clocks will continue to tick along with in-game time, while Object Clocks will only progress when the players make checks. So, if you're on the lam, the Fail State Clock that represents you being found continues to count down to 0, while the Object Clock that represents you escaping will stop if your character decides to sleep through the night.

Everything in a Skill Challenge is considered in time. You don’t say “the wall is 200 feet high,” instead you say “it’ll take you 3 minutes to scale the wall.” This represents the minimum amount of time to get there and the base line around which you’ll plan your trip. This can also help you set the Pace of a Skill Challenge and build the Clocks around that.

THE HOW, SKILL CHALLENGES, & YOU It is important to note that all these things factor into the Skill Challenge itself and you may slide from one Skill Challenge to another by changing the how.

For instance, the objective is to find the enemies base. You start by simply wandering around their territory trying to find it, but part way through, you run into a patrol, when that Clock runs out. You don’t get caught so you think: this patrol will probably head back to base eventually so I should tail them. The objective is the same but suddenly the How is different and so, essentially, a new Skill Challenge is starting with different Clocks to deal with.

CLOCKS STATES & OPPOSITION

Clocks can have two states: Passive and Active. These represent what the opposition is going to roll. You can also go into Dire Straits on a Clock, which I’ll get to in a bit.

PASSIVE means that whomever you’re up against doesn’t realize they’re up against you. This is the, for example, the watchman doing his nightly rounds or the river you have to cross.

By default they roll a d20! + a dice that represents their Rank or Difficulty. For the watchman, it’s based on his Perception Rank and for the river, it’s based on white water it is.

To figure out what to roll, take the Rank and turn it into the nearest dice size, rounded down. So, if the watchman’s Perception is 7, he’s going to roll a d6!, if a 4, a d4!, or if a 3 or less, he gets nothing.

ACTIVE means that whomever you’re up against realizing something is amiss and is now actively working against you. This doesn’t mean your caught: it just means the person is on high alert.

In this case, they roll a d20! + their full Rank. They can additionally make use of Factors more offensively.

On top of this, any failed checks subtracts your Base Time from any applicable Clocks again: so when Stealthing, instead of eating up 1 minute of your time, 2 minutes are eaten up instead.

GOING FROM PASSIVE TO ACTIVE When you fail a check, any applicable Clocks go Active. Additionally, a new Clock may start.

So, returning to the original example; you’re in the house and know the patrols but, as you’re sneaking around, you stubble and make a noise. This failure doesn’t affect your Objective Clock, nor does it affect the families Clock, but it does alert the guards a room or two over. This means that a new Clock is started since the guards are now on the hunt for something suspicious.

Generally, these new Clock start as Passive and when they run out, as long as they also didn’t go Active, they simply disappear: in this case, the guards go back to their patrols and dismiss the noise.

Even Fail State Clocks don’t have to become Active if it makes no sense for them to do so.

Staying Active

Now, most NPCs will stay Active for 1d4! hours unless they have a Characteristic that effects how diligent they are, but what about players? A person, NPC or not, gains 3 Stress for every hour they remain Active, so yeah, your players can remain ever viligent but they'll be mental wrecks in a week or so.

DIRE STRAITS If one of your Fail State Clocks run out, that isn’t the end: you don’t automatically fail but you do enter Dire Straits, which means your next fail on a Skill Check does result in bad things happening but, if by some miracle, you manage to keep passing your Checks, you can possibly seize victory from defeat’s jaws.

What state the Clock is in (and which Clocks have put you in Dire Straits) depends on how ready the opposition is to take you down.

For instance, a Passive Clock for patrols means that, on your next failure, the guard runs into you but they’re completely surprised while if they were Active, they would have figured out exactly where you are.

This can also be used to determine other outcomes. For instance, a Passive River might buffet you around, dealing some damage and causing some Stress, but ultimately throw you on the right side, while an Active one does the same but forces you back to where you came, forcing you to start the Challenge over again.

GM Note For Setting Up Some Clocks

There may be Clocks that may or may not be a fail state. For instance, bad weather when traveling. In these cases, the GM can boil them down to a single Check that represents that it might be an issue. Failure on the Check means it is, success, don’t worry about it.

TOOLS AT YOUR DISPOSAL

Okay, now it’s time to talk about what you can do to make success more likely. As a player, it is on your shoulders to divvy up the time that you’ve been given and figure out how to approach things, so here are some things you should consider using.

FACTOR DICE

The most basic roll assumes that everything is on a level playing field, which it rarely is. Different tasks may be harder than others, like trying to stealth in broad daylight, or easier, like trying to spot someone stealthing in broad daylight. These are considered as Factors.

Factors add a dice to one side’s roll, with the size of that dice and who it gets added to based on how many advantages or disadvantages that side has. Generally, each Factor adds or subtracts 1 to the dice size but really good advantages or really bad disadvantages may add or subtract more.

Let’s return to the above example, since our sneakthief is trying to sneak in broad daylight, that means the watcher has a slight advantage, giving him an extra d4! to his Skill Check. Now, let’s also say that the designer of the castle specifically put in squeaky floor boards to better hear thieves. This is also a Factor and now the watcher increases his Factor dice to a d6! instead.

Only one person can have a Factor dice working in their favor at once so if the thief had 3 things going in his favor at this point, the watcher would get no extra dice and he would get a d4!.

Note that there are EXTENDED CHECKS or checks where your character is making a single check made over a longer period of time than just the Base Time listed by the Skill against a Fail State Clock.

So, this would be sneaking for a 3 or 4 minutes if the Pace is set at 1 minute, for instance. In reality, these would be a bunch of smaller checks of the same kind in a row, but, let's be honest, that's super boring and no one wants to deal with that so we've made guidelines for rolling it into one.

In these cases, the opposition would increase the size of their Factor Dice by 1 size for everytime the Base Time is doubled. So, if a Skill has a Base Time of 1 and the Pace is 1 minute but the circumstances for you to do it for 2 minutes, you would increase it by 1 size. If instead it takes 4 minutes, you increase it by 2, 8 minutes, you increase it by 3 and so on.

How much you fail or succeed by is multiplied by half the Skill's Base Time and then added or subtracted from any applicable Clocks. For example, you're forced to sneak across a room that will take you 2 minutes to cross. You succeed by 2 on your check. Turns out, as you were crossing, you realized there was some soft carpeting so you were able to move faster over that area, saving you a minute of time.

TAKING YOUR TIME

If you have time to spare, you definitely up your chances of getting the job done. Every increment of the standard time for a Skill you add to your Objective Clock, you gain 1 Factor in your favor, but this increment doubles for each time you use it. Note that this additional time eats away at any applicable Fail State Clocks but not your Objective Clock.

So, since Perception takes 30 seconds, if you take 2 and a half minutes to take a look around, you would gain +3 Factor (30 for the first, 1 minute for the second, 2 minutes for the third).

PREP CHECKS

Prep Checks are special checks made to influence a specific Skill Check that will be made in the future (even if that future is 2 seconds from now). Note that this doesn't have to be a Check you make: it can also apply to an ally's Check (if it makes sense).

These are made like normal Checks only they’re against a straight d20! + Factors and, generally, you’ll know if you failed right away. Also, they always have a Base Time of 1/2 Pace and failing them doesn't count towards Failure if in Dire Straits and doesn't eat away additional time when your opposition is Active (but, again, it still take 1/2 Pace's worth of time).

On a success, you gain a reroll or +1 Factor on 1 future Skill Check: this only affects one of the dice rolled (either the d20!, the Factor Dice, or the Rank Dice, if applicable) but this reroll can affect either you or your opponent’s.

Either way, you only get one Prep Check per Skill that applies for each upcoming Check, unless you have Assets but remember that they do take time!

That means that if you know you're going to have to make a jump, you can use Acrobatics to stretch, Endurance to do a few practice sprints to the edge, and Sports (long jumping) to remember techniques all to prep for the same Jump check.

One last thing they can be used for, which is not a mechanical benefit but still useful; finding out more information. So, for instance, if you want to know whether there’s a river you could follow between a mountain range and a lake, you could use a Prep Check to find out.

ASSETS

These represent static pools of knowledge or information that you can pull from with a Prep Check. So, for instance, a book on the subject, or a library is an example of an Asset but also an old friend who used to be a ranger could give you a few pointers so a Gather Information Prep Check could give you a bonus to the Navigation Check you’re going to have to make to find those ruins.

Basically, anything you can think of that might apply might be an Asset you can draw on.

Assets have a Time Investment and a Usefulness, which is how long you have to spend to get an extra roll on your Prep Check and what they add to their Check, respectively. Note, that taking extra time with an Asset doesn’t scale exponentially like Taking Your Time does, but instead scales linearly.

So, you go to your old ranger friend, who happens to be a talker, so you know it’ll take you at least 2 hours to get anything useful out of him so you decide to spend the day with him, spending about 8 hours there.

Now, your friend doesn’t remember as much as he used to so you get a d10! Factor ( 8 / 2 = 4 ) for spending so much time with him but his Usefulness is a d12!, so that’s what he adds to the opposition dice for your Prep Check.

You don’t automatically know the Usefulness of an Asset.

Another benefit of Assets is that they allow you to do multiple Prep Checks for a single Skill Check and the same Asset can be used to Prep for multiple different checks. A library, for example, could allow you to Prep for both a Monster Craft and History check.

Assets can also be increased and expanded upon as well, increasing its Usefulness. A good relationship with a contact might give you more information, donating to the local library may increase its selection, or hiring excavators to dig up a ruin may unearth more artifacts that help you decipher that ancient script.

There may be some special cases where an Asset will take care of itself but you have to check back on it, such as Snaring in the forest. You set your trap and then a day or two later, you can go back and check it to see if you caught anything - in the meantime, you can get other work done..

ASSET & EQUIPMENT BASED SKILLS

Some skills are only going to be useful if you have the proper equipment or the right Asset. For instance, if you’re not out in the wilderness, you can’t really use Snaring or if you don’t have the proper tools or a woodshop, Maintenance won’t do you any good. This will need to be handled on a case to case basis.

STRESSING

When you’re about to make a Skill Check, you can choose to Stress about it. Why would you do this? Because by forcing your character to hyper-focus on what he’s doing, he can pull out a win, but it comes at a cost.

When you Stress, you can +6 to your roll, or +8 if the Skill Check directly relates to a Goal. At the end of the Skill Challenge, roll 1d6! for each time you did this and gain that much Stress.

EQUIPMENT

Your kit can really pull you through in a pinch.

Players are assumed to be using any and all equipment that would help them with a check and during Step 4, you can choose to have one of these pieces of equipment to give you, in that moment, a reroll one of one your dice (taking the better of the two) or a +d6! to your roll. You can have multiple pieces of equipment apply but the reroll can only be redeemed once per Check.

For the first redemption of this bonus between Maintenance Prep Checks this comes with no penalty but every time after that, the tool degrades, giving it a -1 regardless of whether you succeed or not.

You can use faulty equipment (ones with a negative modifier), since something is better than nothing. This still gives you a reroll but it’s on 1 dice lower (again, keeping the better of the two) or on a d4!.

Superior equipment also gives you that reroll but 1 size larger instead or gives you a +d8!.

COMPANIONS

Your friends can help by making their own Checks. For example, they can research in the library while you try and get information from the local monster hunters guild.

They do become a drawback. While Passive, their failed checks also count towards whether a Clock becomes Active and on failed Checks against Active Clocks, you multiple the time lost by the number of companions you have helping.

So, when the guards are looking for you and your two mates, and any one fails a Stealth Check, you lose 3 minutes off your time, instead of 1.

PREP & ACTION PHASE

While it isn’t entirely black and white, it’s helpful to think of Challenges as composing two phases: prep and action; the planning and then the heist.

Prepping is gathering intel, getting your team and equipment together, and planning your route of attack. This is where you’ll use Prep Checks, Taking Your Time, and Assets mostly.

Action phase is when you’ll be doing a lot of the Action Checks and Stress, as well as equipment and companions - using what you learned in the Prep Phase to help you get through the sticky parts.

But don’t get too hung up on separating these ideas though. Even during the heist, you might find it worth your time to sit on your heels and take your time while doing some Prep Checks as there will almost certainly be problems that crop up that you need to adjust to.

CLIMBING This skill covers climbing and repelling up and down walls of various heights and textures.

Difficulty should be based on height, with about every 40 ft increasing the difficulty by 1. Textures should also play a major factor in not just how tough the check is but also how many checks need to be made for a given wall.

So, if there’s a wall, where the base is stone at a 45% slant for 20 ft, then it moves to close knit stones for another 40 ft, and then finally to a wood overhang for just 5 ft, and then to a wooden wall for 10 ft, there would be 4 Climbing checks in this order: Trivial, Challenging (Average for base but because it’s 40 ft, it gets bumped up 1), Challenging, and Easy.

DISARM TRAP It takes nerves of steel to be able to cut the right wire or trip the trap in such a way that the least bad thing happens

Disarming a trap takes a base of 30 minutes if you don’t want to just set it off. If you’re in a hurry, you can half that time but you increase the difficult by 1 each time you do so. So, reducing the time to 7 minutes, would bump a Trivial trap up to Average in difficulty.

A failure indicates that you set the trap off accidentally.

ENDURANCE Through training, a character can push his body past the limits of his base physical ability. The Rank of this Skill is the number of seconds between taking damage while Running or Sprinting.

If a character is not trained specifically in this Advanced Skill, default to their Athletics Skill Suite Rank.

ESCAPE ARTIST This allows the character to escape bonds and restrictions and is always rolled opposed, either against the Rope Use skill of the person who tied them or against the quality of the manacles or binds the person was put in.

The character can make a straight Average check, not to break the bounds or get free, but to instead move his body to an advantageous position.

JUMPING You need to cover a distance without touching the ground: jump.

For every point you scored under what you needed, you travel either 2 ft horizontally or 1 ft vertically.

Jumping from a stand still is an Average check, while doing so with a running start is an Easy check. Add 1 to your roll for every Level of Fatigue your character has.

SPORT (specific) This covers the actually playing of a specific sport as well as an in-depth knowledge of the game’s rules and famous players and techniques. If it becomes a plot point, the GM should feel free to make this skill apply to only certain positions, like pitching or shortstop.

STEALTH When you want to move silently, you’ll want to use this skill. This involves being able to feel your way along and compensate for creaks.

LORE SKILL SUITE

This suite covers general knowledge, which is mostly academic in nature. Most of it is pretty heavy stuff but the basics are second nature or stuff that’s been picked up here and there.

ADMINISTRATION While they’ll never inspire the awe or respect that other leaders do, people who oversee the day to day functions of an organization keep the lights on so that others can make the speeches.

Administrators can make an Average skill check to gain a clue about an item the party might need on their adventure, or a Challenging one to retroactively remember that they did buy an item back at the market for just such an occasion.

Each of these checks they make increases the difficulty of the next one by 1 step.

ARCANA The science of magic is an oft studied but little understood branch of Lore that requires a deep knowledge of the nature of the Painted World.

With this skill, someone can identify a spell, point out needed regents and processes for ritual castings, detect if something is magical, or even detect if a spell has been cast in an area, and have a chance of finding out what it was.

All of these, except for the last two, are Average checks, with detecting whether or not a spell was cast in an area is Trivial + 1 for every day since the casting, or to detect what specific spell it was, Challenging + 1 for every day since the casting.

HISTORY Everyone knows certain large events, but few can tie those events into an overarching narrative and understand their effects on later events.

The History skill allows a character to not just recall events but also to link them to the present day, to certain traditions and rituals of a culture, or the behavior of certain members of a culture, giving insight on how to proceed.

LANGUAGE (specific) A character receives this skill for free in their native tongue at double their Rank in Lore and it allows them to speak and be understood by others with skill in that language.

Checks are only made when timing is critical, such as communication in a battle, or when understanding is key, such as when debating foreign policy.

If one of the characters is in a Social Conflict with another in a tongue that’s not their native one, that person can only bring in as many Characteristic Die per round as he has Ranks in that Language.

Additionally, when two native speakers are in a Conflict, the one with the higher Language Rank gains Leverage (see Applicable Skills under the Leverage Advance Rule in Chapter 9: Social ). In case of tie, nothing happens.

The Lore Skill Suite can’t be used in place of a Language Check for tongues you don’t know unless you have the Language Skill in a related Language. Using a real world example, someone with the Language (Swedish) Advanced Skill couldn’t use the Lore Skill Suite to understand a person with Language (French) but could use it when talking to someone with Language (Norwegian) or Language (Danish).

LAW (culture) This skill covers the intricacies of the legal system, including landmark rules and common punishments for common crimes. A character can make their case or find loopholes to exploit the legal system.

A character’s Lore skill can only be used to substitute in for their native culture but not others. If the character does wish to branch out, his mastery of Law for that culture starts at 0, as he must learn everything from the ground up. Diving more into his own culture’s laws (buying this Advanced Skill for his culture) uses the Lore skill as base, as per usual.

LITERACY (language) Unlike other skills, this skill can’t be attempted unless you’ve specifically bought this Advanced Skill and it’s mastery starts at 0. That’s right; every character starts illiterate.

This skill allows a character to read and write in a language’s alphabet, giving them the power to pen books and poems, or passive aggressive notes.

MATHEMATICS While this may seem like just a skill that allows you to be able to juggle figures and add totals and calculate the area of a circle like a huge nerd, Mathematics has a more practical use: correctly guessing distances.

While you can try to eyeball how far away something is, an Average Mathematics check allows you to do rough calculations, giving you the ability to know exactly what penalty you’re going to get for using a ranged weapon or who’s going to be affected by the Area of Effect of a spell you’re going to cast.

MEDICINE With proper treatment and nursing, a wound that would lay you out for weeks, may only keep you down for a couple of days. This skill involves the knowledge of long term care, when bandages should be changed, which poultices will have the greatest effect, and what minor surgeries need to be performed.

A character with this skill can make a check for each Wound sustained within the last hour with the difficulty dice equal to the Severity of the Wound rounded up to the closest dice size. So, a Severity 5 Wound has a d6! difficulty. Success means that the person can take 1 day off the healing time for that Wound every time it drops a Severity.

So, a 5 Severity Wound would be an Easy (d6!) Check. A success means that is was treated properly and takes 10 days to completely heal ( 4 + 3 + 2 + 1 + 0 ) instead of the normal 15.

Additionally, an Average check allows character to reroll their save vs Toxins, Poisons, or Diseases.

MONSTER CRAFT This Advanced Skill is under both the Lore and Survival Skill Suite but still counts as 1 skill, so you don’t have to buy it twice - they share their Rank. It simply represents the different ways that each Skill Suite approaches the craft.

Monster Craft gives you insight into the creatures that roam the Middle Realm. Starting with something simple, such as their name, and the progressively learning more and more about them, such as they’re are fundamentally two different types of Incorporeal Undead or that you can convince Demons to do things if you win a Conflict with them.

The knowledge should be fairly general - weaknesses or where they draw their power from, so it will never reveal anything meta such as what the monster’s attack or Vitality is but it can let you know if the creature is known for having a lot of Vitality or is good at hitting.

Each Monster Craft check starts as an Average Skill Check and answers one question the player has on a success but each successive check on the same monster increases the difficulty by 2. On top of this, for rare creatures or hard questions, the GM may up the Difficulty as she feels is appropriate.

NATURE This area of study covers the topography of the Middle Realm. It’s forests and rock formations and biomes. It helps predict whether or not it’s going to rain or whether the next summer is going to be a hot one.

Predicting what’s coming in the next hour is a Trivial check, with the difficulty increasing by 1 for each time you double the prediction window. So, detecting the weather in the next 2 hours is Easy, while doing so in the next 16 hours is a Hard check.

RELIGION (cult) This skill acquaints the character with a knowledge of cults, their common Covenants and Miracles, as well as their hierarchies, means of advancement and important members.

It also allows a character to gain a knowledge of their rituals and sacrifices, as well as the taboos of their clergy and how to appease their heavenly messengers and avenging angels.

The Religion Skill is taken for a single Covenant, but can purchased independently for different Covenants.

A character adds the tens digit of this skill to their chance of gaining Favor when making sacrifices to the god of that cult.

STREETWISE SKILL SUITE

This suite covers knowledge that a character might pick on the street or in the school of hard knocks. While Lore is book smart, Streetwise is street smart.

CURRENT AFFAIRS A character with this skill is well informed about the day to day affairs of regional powers and men and woman of importance, their dealings and alliances, as well as their hatreds. He can pick out celebrities and know how and why someone got rich and famous. They can also use this skill to guess the Honor category of someone by watching how people react to them.

DECEPTION This covers all sort of deception, where you want the person to believe opposite the truth, or, to put it simply, when you want to lie to them.

The level of difficulty to pull off a lie is based on how much of what you’re saying isn’t the truth, strictly speaking. This is always opposed by either the other character’s Deception or Perception but only if the character has reason to suspect that you’re lying to them.

Even if you succeed, this isn’t a permanent way of changing their mind. New evidence presented to that person may cause them to rethink their opinion on what you tried to sell them so be careful about what falsehoods you spin.

FORGERY Mostly commonly used for creating fake currency, forgery is a skill that allows a character to reproduce something effectively and fool others. This skill acquaints the character with common forging techniques and knowledge with what possible agents will being looked for.

The forger must also have the appropriate corresponding Craft or Trade skill, so he can’t forge coins if he doesn’t have the Metalworking skill, though that’s within reason: a character forging an official passport with one at hand doesn’t need to have Literacy as he can just copy the letters, though he may also need to have Artistry (wood cutting) to create the heraldry on it, but someone trying to forge an official declaration from skratch does need to have the Literacy skill in that language plus Artistry (wood cutting).

Forgery is opposed by Perception, the applicable skill, or Forgery but only if the character looking has reason to suspect there’s something else. A lazy guard probably won’t check your passport too closely right before lunch, but a particularly astute one who’s on the lookout for a promotion will, thus granting him a check.

GAMBLING/GAMING This skill doesn’t give the character a gift for winning games, just that he knows the rules and knows the odds, which could in turn lead to him winning the game.

INTUITION The ability to understand people and get where they're coming form can help you figure out what will and won't work with them better. A Trivial Intuition check allows the character to get a feel for how open a person would be to a Conflict over something or what would be definite dealbreakers. It doesn't reveal any of their Characteristics or their strengths as it's more of gut feeling than anything.

Each check on the same person increases the Difficulty by 1. To gain this insight, they must either watch them for a total of 3 hours + 1 hour for every additional thing they wish to learn, or talk to them for 10 minutes + 5 minutes for every additional. You can speed up the learning process, but 1 hour or 5 minutes, respectively, but doing so also increases the difficulty by 1. This time doesn't have to be in one sitting or done purposeful and more represents the fact that the person making the check needs to be familiar with the person on some leve.

LISTENING While Perception handles the other senses, Listening has to deal with your character’s auditory senses. This goes beyond simply hearing something: listening allows the character to know when an otherwise innocuous noise is actually something to pay attention to though it might not always be apparent why at first.

LOCK PICKING It takes a lot more know how then you might think to pick a lock. The amount of time it takes to pick any lock is based on your skill level and the quality of the lock.

Subtract the Time Off x how much you succeeded your check by from the Base Time.

PERCEPTION This covers four of the five senses that a character has: sight, touch, taste, and smell. Technically, your character is always receiving inputs from these senses but Perception helps filter out the most important ones. Can he detect if there are orcs in the nearby bush? Can she taste the hidden poison in her drink before she swallows? These are all Perception checks

Like Listening, sometimes Perception will help the character notice something that may be important, even if it’s not obvious why it’s grabbing your attention.

SLEIGHT OF HAND This skill covers all things that require you to do quick, delicate movements with your fingers. This may be slipping a card up your sleeve, picking a pocket, or doing cat’s cradle while bored.

TRAP FINDING This actually covers a lot more than simple detecting trip wires or arrow holes in a dungeon. This can detect pits and snares, as well as buttons disguised as bricks or switches hidden as sconces.

A character can sweep an entire room with a Hard check but a character may be awarded a modifier secretly by the GM if they point out 2 or 3 spots they want to pay extra close attention to if one or more of those places actually houses the trap. Obviously, you don’t earn that modifier if you just name everything in the room and the more specific you are with what you’re searching, the higher that modifier should be.

SURVIVAL SKILL SUITE

This suite covers everything that a character might need to survive in the wild; Knowledge of what plants to eat and the skills to hunt and breed animals.

BOTANY This gives a knowledge of what the herbs used in cooking, alchemy, and medicine look like in their natural environment and it allows you to identify their common uses.

Mostly, this is used for detecting poisonous plants, which is a Trivial check, but someone with the Medicine skill can also find any local plants that might help him in his cures with an Average check, lowering his Medicine check by 1 if succeeds.

CAMOUFLAGE This involves more than just ducking behind a crate or sliding into the shadow of a doorway; the skill of camouflage represents a character’s ability to blend into their surroundings, given enough time and patience. It also always them to help others hide more effectively as well.

Assuming that the character has time and equipment to do so, a character gains his skill percentage in Camouflage to his Hiding temporary. Additionally, under the same conditions, he can also give this bonus to other’s Hiding checks.

When used to hide something in plain sight, like a cart, building, or weapon, he may roll a check based on its size.

FIRE BUILDING Against the cold, lonely nights out in the wild, there is only the fertility symbols you carve for yourself and the roar of the fire. This skill allows you to build one of those things: the fire.

Under normal conditions, it takes about 30 minutes and a Trivial check to setup and light a fire, assuming you don’t have to go far out of your way to find the wood.

FIRST AID This gives the character a crude understanding of battlefield medicine: making splints and staunching wounds.

A character with this skill can make a check for each Wound sustained within the last 30 minutes with the difficulty dice equal to the Severity of the Wound rounded up to the closest dice size. So, a Severity 5 Wound has a d6! difficulty. Success means that the person can take 1 level of Severity off that Wound. So a 5 Severity Wound drops immediately to a 4 Severity Wound.

Each Wound takes about 3 minutes to deal with but he can lower that by 30 seconds for each level of difficulty he’s willing to increase his check by, down to 10 seconds. Additionally, you can lower the Wound by an another Severity if you increase the Difficulty of the check by 2.

HANDLE ANIMAL (specific) This skill deals with the handling and care of a specific animal, from birth to the slaughter house. It also deals with training and common illnesses to make sure the stock is healthy and viril.

The base price of this skill is calculated towards domesticated animals. If the player wishes to learn how to deal with wild animals, this skill costs thrice as much to purchase.

HIDING This allows a character to make the most of a situation or environment to cloak themselves against prying eyes. This is always opposed by Perception, with the difficulty determined by the area. Character can also use this to instantly get an idea of how well they can hide in the given space.

MONSTER CRAFT This skill is the same as the Monster Craft under the Lore Skill Suite. Look there for details on it.

NAVIGATION Either using the stars or the position of the moons and sun, this character can track their way even through the most untamed wilderness without getting lost. It also allows them to tell the time.

Without proper equipment, a character can only give rough estimations of where they are, anchored on larger geographic feature (like mountains or rivers but not towns or roads) to the nearest 10 miles.

SCAVENGING There’s a gift in high speed appraisal. This is often used by those picking over battlefields for anything they could sell, but it’s also found a niche with adventurers who use it to find food and sustainance while traveling away from civilization.

A Trivial check tells a character which of two things considered is worth more. This can’t be used for coinage, but can be any other type of good. It also gives the character an idea of whether broken equipment can be reasonably salvaged.

This doesn’t give even a ballpark of what anything is worth but it only takes a few seconds of consideration.

SNARING This teaches the character how to properly create and place snares to trap smaller wildlife. It also gives some cursory knowledge on the best places to put them.

A successful Trivial check nets 1 lbs of usable game meat at the end of the day, with the amount you can catch increasing by 1 for each additional difficulty.

HUNTING Most of the time, you’re not going to down your query in one go, and so the hunt is on as your watch for subtle signs of their passing. While this skill was originally used to hunt wildlife, it has been repurposed by wardens and bounty hunters trying to capture the most dangerous game.

The base for a Hunting check is Average difficulty, however, a lot of different factors play into a hunt. Each condition either raises or lowers the difficulty by the noted amount. These are not all the conditions that come into play but it should give you a good base line.

On a failure, the Tracker loses the trail for a number of days equal to the amount they failed by. They must double back and can try a second check after the time elapsed to see if they’ve found the trail again. Should they fail the second time, they’ve completely lost the trail.

If your query is actively trying to hide their tracks, it becomes opposed by the query’s Hunting skill.

USE ROPE Covering all kinds of knots and bindings, the Use Rope skill is pretty self explanatory, though it should be noted that you can also use this skill to get a sense of the tensile strength of a rope or how much you’re going to need for a certain job.

To keep your players on their toes, I suggest you make the check, not when they tie the knot, but when it’s tested.

TACTIC SKILL SUITE

Combat isn’t just about raw physical power, but more about how it’s directed and used. This Skill Suite deals with logistics and making the most out of what you have.

COMBAT STYLE (style) By studying the best tactics and how to fight in certain circumstances, a warrior can take advantage of his surroundings and combat styles emerge. Combat Styles can be anything but involve to parts: a situation and a metric. This situation is when the Combat Style is most effective. For instance, it can be “in the forest”, “fighting on rooftops”, “being outnumbered”, “being in a duel”, “fighting while prone”, etc.. Really anything (as long as your GM approves). The second part of the Combat Style is the metric is applied to. This can be anything you could normally specialize in. Both the situation and metric are selected at the time of purchase and cannot be changed.

If the situation is applicable, the character receives a +1 to that metric for every 2 Ranks of that skill he’s obtained.

So a character with the Combat Skill (unarmed fighting, attack) at 5 who gets into a tavern brawl gets a +2 to attack as long as he doesn’t draw his weapon and simply uses fisticuffs.

Having a high Rank in Tactics doesn't automatically give you Combat Style bonuses under all circumstances and buying this skill for the first time starts it off at Rank 0, instead of the Tactic Suite's Rank.

DISTRACTION More than simply yelling “hey, look” at someone, the art of distraction is the art of holding people’s attention.

The amount of time that someone is delayed is based on the difficulty of the check and how bent on their current activity they are.

The time given below is the time between checks, however, each successive check gets 1 difficulty harder, with the same result. So, distracting someone for 1d3 minutes the first time is Average, but the next 1d3 minutes you’re trying to hold their attention is Challenging.

This skill is less effective when the person knows they’re in immediate danger. In this case, as long as they’re not actively engaged in melee with someone, the time is reduced to equivalent amount of seconds. So, a successful Challenging check only delays someone for 1d4 seconds instead. Each successive check gets 2 difficulties harder in these cases.

LEADERSHIP The difference between a leader and an administrator is that the latter gets stuff done and the former gets others to get stuff done but hooking into what they believe and how they view themselves. Assuming you have a willing audience, you may make an Average Leadership skill check vs a social characteristic you think the person or group might have. If you succeed the check and they have the characteristic you’re looking for (or close enough - you don’t have to be exact), you increase their Morale by a number of points equal to the maximum value of one of the dice of that characteristic. So, a 3d4 Trait will only net 4 points of Morale.

You can increase the number of characteristics you’re aiming for by increasing the difficulty of the check by a like number, but the audience only gains the bonus from the largest of the characteristics.

QUARTER MASTERING There’s an art to packing and making sure that everyone is well fed and prepared. A Quarter Master reduces the total carry weight of person who’s gear he’s helped pack by 1% for every Rank he’s achieved in this skill.

RALLY When manure hits the windmill, no speeches or grand narratives will keep a person from bolting. Only the cold will of a competent commander can keep a battle from becoming a route. This skill is rolled to rally fleeing allies and to force them to fight on or to get the enemy to break. This is always rolled opposed against the enemy’s Rally skill. More information is found in the Morale Advanced Rules of Combat (which are not in the basic edition).

RECRUITING Knowing where to find the right person is a crucial skill for any long term expedition. A character with this skill can find a random person willing to hire on with a Trivial check in a number of days equal to their roll.

Now, finding specialists or someone with a certain skill is another matter. Every additional criteria adds 1 level of difficulty to the search.

Where you’re looking also makes a difference, as well.

This doesn’t guarantee that the person found will take the job or doesn’t have some serious issues, like a crippling fear of dogs: it only guarantees that they have the skills you’re looking for and that they’re somewhat interested in being hired on. Pay and comfort make a lot of difference to a prospective hire.

RIDING This skill teaches a character how to maintain their stability while on horseback and making them capable of feats of equine prowess.

A character without this skill Attacks and Defends with a d8! on horseback but is only able to do so while the horse is relatively still. Each speed category above a stand still, lowers that dice by 1 size, down to simply not being able to Attack or Defend at all.

For each difficulty they can pass without rolling, they increase that dice size by 1, back up to their full Attack and Defense. Flanking and other modifiers may lower this again, in the normal manner.

SIGNALLING This is the knowledge of how to communicate over long distances, either using semaphore, smoke signals, signal lamps, or horn blowing.

When using this means of communication, each side rolls a check based on how complicated the message is and how quickly they need to send it. Signalling is an ad hoc language with no grammatical rules or complicated tenses and so even at the best of times, only simple messages can be sent.

WARFARE The Warfare skill hands directing troops on the battlefield, weighing options and devising plans on the fly. Those with this skill can roll it to temporarily pause combat to ask one question and receive one answer from a specific member of the party, or to simply tell them something. On top of this, you can also use this, out of combat, to confer with players who’re characters are not present in the scene.

Checks start at Trivial but increase by 1 difficulty each time they’ve been rolled that combat.

Warfare also has additional benefits and uses that will be outlined in Crowns of Fire, the kindgom management and mass combat supplement for Bonfire Advanced.

TRADES SKILL SUITE

This suite covers the crafting and creating skills. While there are a wide array and breathe of professions, this list is pared down to those areas of knowledge where the common man and the adventurer overlap in their needs.

APPRAISAL (specific) It’s wise to know what something is worth before selling and buying it. This skill handles that and is taken for a specific type of good, such as books & scrolls, livestock, jewelry, armors & armor, and so on.

If a character has the appropriate skill covering the item he’s looking to sell/purchase, he can get within her roll, expressed at a percentage, of what the item is currently worth, with the Check based on how common the item is and how closely it relates to his skill’s focus.

Additionally, the character should get a bonus if he has any skill that’s applicable to the thing he’s purchasing.

ARTISTRY (specific) While some many not find the Fine Arts a worthy way to spend time, others may disagree. Artistry covers all artistic endeavors, including painting, sculpture, embroidery and anything else that is done for art’s sake.

COOKING/BAKING On the road, a hot meal can count for a lot. A character who has a hot meal subtracts 1 day off the amount of time it takes to heal their Stress per Stress, so, they’re able to heal 15 Stress in 120 days, instead of 105.

On top of this, a character can prep otherwise unpalatable or poisonous food in such a way as to make the most of it, though it is a Challenging check to do so, meaning they can stretch rations for longer.

Unless the character is trying to prep a feast worthy of His Imperial Majesty, basic daily cooking only requires a Trivial check.

CRAFT (specific) This skill covers any skill that makes something that isn’t noted here, such as carpentry, tailoring, basket weaving, pottery, etc.. The GM may adjust the price for a particularly difficult to master study.

LEATHERWORKING From the moment it leaves the beast to the moment the final touches are finished, leatherworking handles the process of making and crafting works from the hides of animals, including skinning and tanning.

MAINTAINCE The skill of tinkers and adventurers, this gives the person a general knowledge of easy and ad hoc fixes that can be done on the fly. The level of difficulty in repairing something is equal to the total penalty it gives, rounded up to the nearest dice size.

So repairing a spear with a -1 to Attack and Damage is Trivial (d4!), while repairing one with a -3 to Attack and Damage is Easy (d6!).

METALWORKING This covers the process of changing crude ore into a beautiful weapon, armor, or decorative piece. It also covers the construction and repair of more mundane things, like plows, but if you’re picking this skill up, I doubt you’re doing it to corner the farmer’s market.

MUSICIAN (specific) This covers the mastery of specific instrument, including singing. At the end of the day, the dulcet tones of a neatly played mandolin can ease the ache of a long day. A character who hears someone play an instrument subtracts 1 day off the amount of time it takes to heal their Stress per Stress, so, they’re able to heal 15 Stress in 10 days, instead of 15.

This doesn’t stack with others playing, however, and does no good if you’re in a stressful situation, like being hunted.

Unless the character is trying to serenade a queen, basic daily ditties only require a Trivial check.

PERFORMANCE This skill covers everything from making your case before a court or a stirring speech before charging into battle. On a success, anyone listening gains a temporary d4 Devotion to what the performer wants: whether that’s themselves or an organization or a case. If the Devotion already exists, the size is temporarily increased. Additionally, the Devotion dice size can be increased 1 for every extra level of difficulty the orator passes their check by.

PERSUASION You may think that the truth is powerful enough to carry your argument but you can be beat out by a master of deception. Persuasion is the skill of rhetoric and formulating the truth in such a way that make it the most convincing.

The level of difficulty to convince someone is how much evidence you can supply to back up your arguments. This is opposed by Deception or Persuasion but only if someone is trying to actively trying to deceive or convince the same person as you.

Even if you succeed, this isn’t a permanent way of changing a person’s mind. Others may lie more effectively than you can tell the truth and that may cause them to rethink their opinion on what you tried to sell them.
