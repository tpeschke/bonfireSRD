Skills are used as a way to determine the success or failure of a given action, based on what your character knows about the given subject or about how to perform the given task.

You don‚Äôt have to use these rules all the time, i.e., you don‚Äôt have to roll to see if your character successfully walks across a room, but at times they‚Äôre a good way to semi-randomly determine the outcome of a tricky task, i.e., walking across a room full of traps.

Skill Introduction

There are two types of skills that you‚Äôre going to be pulling from: Skill Suites and Advanced Skills.

Skill Suites represent broad swathes of knowledge that cover a variety of subjects. Suites are hard to increase because there‚Äôs so much ground to cover but can be used if you don‚Äôt have the specific Advanced Skill. An example of this is the Survival and Lore Skill Suites.

Advanced Skills are more tightly focused, covering one intellectual discipline. They‚Äôre easier to master but may not be applicable in every case. An example of this would be the First Aid, Fire Building, and Foraging skills in the Survival Skill Suite and the Law, History, and Medicine skills in the Lore Skill Suite.

ACQUIRING SKILLS

Throughout your character‚Äôs career, she‚Äôll acquire and improve her skills. Both types of skills are purchased with CrP, that is spent either at Character Creation or on leveling up. All skills have a unique cost that represents how easy or difficult they are to master. You pay the price as noted in the section below, you improve the skill.

CALCULATING RANK

SKILL SUITES Your Skill Suites, your starting Rank is the Skill Bonus listed under the corresponding Stat. For Suites with multiple Stats, average the Skill Bonus, rounded down.

ADVANCED SUITES Your starting Rank for Advanced Skills is the Rank for the mother Skill Suite.

PURCHASING A SKILL Advanced Skills cost their Base Cost - Skill Bonus + (Current Rank * 2), while Skill Suites cost their Base Cost Base Cost - Skill Bonus + (Current Rank * 5), both have a minimum of 1. Note that, like Skills Suites above, if there are multiple Stats attached to a Skill, average the Skill Bonus, rounded down.

The exception to this is the Weirdcraft Advanced Skills, that more expensive as you purchase other Weirdcraft Advanced Skills. The price is the Base Cost + 2 * (Current Ranks + number of previous Weirdcraft Advanced Skills you've got Ranks in). Going back and purchasing more Ranks in a Weirdcraft Advanced Skills you‚Äôre already got Ranks in doesn‚Äôt ‚Äúshift‚Äù it around for purposes of ‚Äúnumber of previous Traditions trained in‚Äù.

Every purchase increases your Rank in that Skill by 1. Purchases of Skill Suites also raises the Rank of any child Advanced Skills you‚Äôve already purchased, so that‚Äôs nice.

SKILL SPECIALIZATIONS

üúÇ Advanced Rule

It often pays to specialize. After all, many dissertations cover extremely minute subjects with incredible depth and drawing on that well of knowledge can be a huge benefit.

Skill Specializations represent an area of knowledge within an Advanced Skill that your character spends extra time versing themselves in. As such, they gain a bonus.

Skill specializations come in layers, modifiers that narrow down the field. Each layer gives a +1 to your effective Rank when it comes into play.

For example, a monster hunter studying predators, would gain a+1 Rank, while another that studies flying predators would gain a +2 Rank bonus to their checks, and a monster hunter that studies the flying predators of the Uchka Mountains would gain a bonus of +3 to their Rank.

There may come a time when a specialization only kind of applies. In this case, it may be kind of helpful, as the max bonus that can be gained from it is +1.

So, when encountering a land predator in the Uchka Mountains, all three monster hunters would only gain +1 to their Rank.

That sort of super-focused study takes a lot of time and effort and man only has so much time and energy to give. The total number of specializations per Advanced Skill that a character can have is equal to half his Intelligence score.

A character can gain a specialization like any other skill; having his character go out of the way to learn more about his craft. The price is the base cost of the Advanced Skill times the number of layers in the specialization plus one.

A character can go back and increase the number of layers in his Skill Specialization after his initial purchase of it, but it costs additional amount of CrPs equal to twice the base cost of the Advanced Skill.

The Basics (skill Checks)

Before we go off talking about Skill Challenges, time, equipment, and all those other things, it‚Äôs first important to understand the very basic mechanic all of those things will hang off of: Skill Checks.

Skill Checks are any checks made that move the Skill Challenge along and determines a success or failure.

At their very basic, Skill Checks have both sides rolling a d20! and adding any applicable Skill‚Äôs Rank and any modifiers for difficulty. The person with the highest total wins and you successfully bypass or eliminate the Obstacle, depending on the situation.

It is assumed that your character is trying their hardest to be successfully in whatever they attempt and so any Check you make inflicts 5 Stress. However, you can buy down that Stress with points off your total result on a 1 to 1 basis. You declare this before you get the results of the Check, however.

So, let's you're making a Check to see if a pillar you're inspecting is load bearing. You have Rank 5 in Craft (Carpentry) and you roll an 18. Your total is 23 ( 18 + 5 ) but you're confident that you'll succeed due to the high roll so you tell the GM that you got a 21 total and take 3 Stress, instead of 5.

Answering a Question

Another use of a single use Skill Check is answering a question that you don‚Äôt know the answer to like ‚Äúout of these two walls, which looks easier to climb?‚Äù Things that would be reasonable for them to be there but you don‚Äôt know because you didn‚Äôt prepare for that question.

Skill Challenges

Skill Checks refer to a single roll against a single Skill, while a Skill Challenge refers to a host of Skill Checks. To use a metaphor: a Skill Challenge would be breaking into the castle. The Skill Checks would be climbing the walls, hiding in the shadows, sneaking past the guards, listening at doors, lockpicking any locks, etc.

OBJECTIVES AND OBSTACLES

A Skill Challenge doesn‚Äôt exist without something that your character wants and at least one thing that stands in their way.

This could be something straightforward: I want to get out of this jail cell but a locked door is in my way, or it could be something more complicated like I want to capture this outlaw but he‚Äôs several days‚Äô ride ahead of me.

These are your Objective and Obstacles, respectively.

Achieving your Objective is a matter of beating or getting past all the Obstacles in your way.

Obstacles are beaten through successfully rolling Skill Checks, which will be discussed more in the next section. Note that an Obstacle can continue to be a factor that needs to be considered, for example, a guard patrol, or it can be something that only needs to be taken care of once, for example, unlocking a door.

Obstacles also have Failure Threshold, which represents badly you have to fail against them to actually fail. More on this in the Failures, Hard & Soft section below.

The Single Check

Before we dive in, I think it's worth saying that there may be times when you have something that might call for a Skill Check but not a Skill Challenge. Most often you'll do this when there is danger but not a huge time crunch, like, for instance, players leaping over a cliff or when there‚Äôs a chance of failure, like the player not knowing a monster weakness.

In that case, it would be perfectly reasonable to just call for a single Jumping (or Monster Craft) Check. If the players want to do some Preparation or use any of the other tools at their disposal, they can.

Or, if there is a time crunch but it is so narrow players really wouldn't have the time to prepare, like having to jump the chasm while a horde of brigands with bows are closing it, you might also just default to the single roll: no need to break out these rules in that case.

In these cases, the Failure Threshold of the Check would be 1, meaning a failure is a failure, but you can spice it up by having the jump almost make it but now the player is just barely hanging out: however much time you want to spend on it.

DIFFICULTY

Difficulty is a dice that is added to the Skill Check based on things that might be going on at the time to either help or hinder your success.

So, for example, finding food to eat while living off the land is much easier in a forest than it is in, say, a desert. In the forest, the players would receive a +d8! to their roll while in the desert the other side, the Obstacle, would get a +d20!. This is in addition to the base d20!, by the way.

Obstacles can have static, variable, shrinking, circular, growing, or universal difficulties. They can also be linked together.

STATIC means that the dice pretty much stays the same, outside of player interaction. This is often used for one off checks and not really used as the main part of a Skill Challenge because, let‚Äôs face it, doing the same Check over and over again isn‚Äôt that interesting.

What you'll mainly see is Static Difficulties linked together in an Obstacle. So, for instance, a rock wall that is slippery at the base, then has a section with a lot of handholds, and finishes with an overhang, would be three separate Checks, the first with a d12! difficulty for the wall, the next with a d4! for the players, and the final with a d10!, again for the wall.

SHRINKING means that it starts high but then the dice shrinks as time passes, often becoming static at a certain point. This is when the threat becomes less and less severe, and I would use this sparingly and with other types of Obstacles, but it can represent the fact that leaving a place is often easier than getting in.

GROWING is the opposite of shrinking: it starts low and then increases as time passes, again, sometimes becoming static at a certain point. This is used to represent threats that continue to grow, often times when there is time pressure.

UNIVERSAL This is a term for an Obstacle that will go off at sometime but won't be directly faced by the players. It represents a chance encounter, such as running into someone randomly, or a stroke of bad luck, like bad weather. Unlike other Obstacles, players won't make Checks directly against it but any Failure (or Success) against another Obstacle in that Challenge adds a like amount of Failure (or Success) to any Universal Obstacles.

This can also be used for timed Challenges, such as a chance or encroaching deadline. In that case you not only count both Soft Successes and Failures, as they represent time passing, but also questions your players ask count as 1 Soft Failure, representing them taking time to get stock of their surroundings, which you oftentimes don‚Äôt have as much time to do under pressure.

CIRCULAR means it starts at a difficulty, grows to a certain difficulty, and then shrinks down to its original difficulty, rinse and repeat, ad nauseam. It can also start shrinking, if you‚Äôd like. This is often used as patrols, like guards and such, but also a monster stalking an area would be represented by this type of Obstacle though you would have it be a bit more random to represent that unpredictability.

LINKED Obstacles are Obstacles that whose outcomes affect one another. So, for instance, if the players are in a wagon chase down a rocky road, with one player at the helm, stirring, and the others in the back firing crossbows, how well the player of the reigns does affects the attack rolls of those in the back. In this case, add or subtract the number of Soft Failures or Soft Successes to the Check of any linked Obstacles.

Note that Linked Obstacles will also be paired with another Difficulty type.

So, for instance, a guard patrol that makes a loop around the house would be a good example of a Circular Obstacle. When the patrol is furthest away from where the players are breaking in, the difficulty to not be detected by them is 0 so they add nothing to their roll, but when the guards are right on top of the players breaking in, the difficulty is +d20!. If it takes them 7 minutes to get from that furthest point to the players, that means every minute, the difficulty for the players increases by 1 dice size.

But, as the guards finish their circuit, heading back to the furthest point away from the players, the difficulty shrinks by 1 size per minute.

Multi-Layer Checks

Let's say that you're trying to deduce facts about bears before you go to fight one. The thing is, there is a lot to know about bears and your character probably isn't going to know all of it, so making a single Check is too binary while making multiple Checks is too tedious.

In steps the Multi-Layer Check.

In this case, the GM would assign Difficulty Dice to the different facts about bears based on how esoteric the knowledge is. He would then roll 1 d20! and all of those Difficulty Dice. You would only roll a single Check and the compare that to the difference totals to see if you recall those facts.

So, for instance, the knowledge that they hibernate is no common but it's not uncommon, do the GM assigns that d6!. The knowledge that they're afraid of fire is common, so he rolls d4! for that and the knowledge that they're sacred animals to the local indigenous tribe is pretty rare because said tribe is extremely secretive so that would be a d20!.

You roll your Monster Craft and get a total of 16. The GM gets a 13 for the d20, a 4 for the d4!, a 5 for the d6! and a 1 for the second d20!. That means the totals are 17, 18, and 14 respectively. This means your character hasn't really been around bears to he doesn't know that they hibernate or that you can scare them with fire, however, he does recall over hearing some of the wanderers in the local tavern talk about how the animal was sacred to the local tribe and killed one of them after he wounded one of the creatures.

"Pausing" the Skill Challenge

The players don‚Äôt always have to be making Skill Checks during a Skill Challenge - they should only do so when it makes sense.

So, (using the above example) if the patrols aren‚Äôt very good and the guards get too far away from the players to reasonably hear them, no Check should be required.

But that doesn‚Äôt mean that time stops: if the players wait long enough, the guards will return and Checks will need to start up again.

FAILURES, HARD & SOFT

Failing a Check doesn‚Äôt automatically mean that you‚Äôre instantly screwed: Failing a single Move Silently Check doesn‚Äôt result in the guards catching you. In Bonfire, there are soft failures and hard failures.

Every Obstacle will have a number of Soft Failures it can gain before it hard fails. This is called its Failure Threshold. You gain Soft Failures by failing Skill Checks: every 5 points below what you needed to get gives the Obstacle 1 Soft Failure.

Soft Failures represent complications, such as the guard hearing something, or you leaving a clue behind that might give someone a heads up that you're there. An Obstacle gets +2 for every Soft Failure is has on it.

When an Obstacle gets more Soft Failures than its Failure Threshold, it hard fails. Hard failures are when something bad actually happens. In this case, it does result in you getting caught by the guards, or it results in your falling off the wall and taking damage. Even after a hard failure, you can sometimes try again, like in the case of falling off the wall.

Failure doesn‚Äôt diminish during the Skill Challenge but it does reset for a new one.

Soft Failure in the Narrative

Soft Failures don‚Äôt mean that the Skill Challenge grinds to a halt while the players keep reattempting that Skill: instead it should represent the players coming closer to danger.

For a quick example, let‚Äôs say that there‚Äôs an adventurer, we‚Äôll call him New York Smith, York for short, who is attempting to jump a chasm, which has a Failure Threshold of 1.

He rolls his Check but fails by 7, giving the Obstacle 1 Soft Failure. In this case, he obviously jumped but also didn‚Äôt make it. What does that mean? Well, York didn‚Äôt quite make it but managed to grab the ledge as he fell, so he didn‚Äôt fall to his death but he isn‚Äôt safe either.

York is grabbing hold of the ledge so he can't make another Jumping Check, but he does reason that he can use Climbing. Makes sense to the GM so they roll again. If York fails this time, he falls, but if he succeeds, he manages to scramble up the cliff, safe on the other side, but he is working at a disadvantage: the GM gets +2 to her roll this time around so he better hope he put Ranks in Climbing.

Strategies

ASSETS

These represent static pools of knowledge or information that you can pull from. So, for instance, a book on the subject, or a library are examples of an Asset but also an old friend who used to be a ranger could give you a few pointers so could give you a bonus to the Navigation Check you‚Äôre going to have to make to find those ruins.

Basically, anything you can think of that might apply might be an Asset you can draw on.

Assets add their Skill Rank to your Check, however this happens slowly based on that Asset's Time Investment.

For each period of the Time Investment you spend with the Asset, you gain a +1 to one of your future Checks, up until the Asset's Rank.

So, you go to your old ranger friend to get information about the local area. You know he happens to be a talker, so you know it‚Äôll take you at least 2 hours (his Time Investment) to start to get anything useful out of him so you decide to spend the day with him, spending about 8 hours there.

Now, your friend doesn‚Äôt remember as much as he used to so his Navigation is Rank 3. After spending 8 hours with him, you get a +3 to your Navigation Prep Check. If you have stayed 6 or 7 hours, you would have still gotten that +3, while if you had stayed for 2 hours, you would have only gotten only a +1.

You don‚Äôt automatically know the Rank or the Time Investment of an Asset.

Another benefit of Assets is that they allow you to do multiple Preparation for a single Skill Check and the same Asset can be used to Prepare for multiple different checks. A library, for example, could allow you to Prepare for both a Monster Craft and History check.

Assets can also be increased and expanded upon as well, increasing its Rank. A good relationship with a contact might give you information quicker, donating to the local library may increase its selection, or hiring excavators to dig up a ruin may unearth more artifacts that help you decipher that ancient script.

There may be some special cases where an Asset will take care of itself but you may have to check back on it, such as Snaring in the forest. You set your trap and then a day or two later, you can go back and check it to see if you caught anything - in the meantime, you can get other work done.

Using Other Players as Assets

I think it's completely reasonable that players would want to use each other's characters as Assets and why shouldn't they? So, in this case, the Time Investment is set at 250 minutes, with each point of Intellect that Asset has taking 10 minutes off. This means that a character with an Intellect of 13 has a Time Investment of 120 minutes, or 2 hours.

GMs should also feel free to reward players who sit there and talk things out and plan by awarding the Asset bonus as well, even if they don't spend a full hour+ planning.

EQUIPMENT

Your kit can really pull you through in a pinch.

Players are assumed to be using any and all equipment that would help them with a check and you can choose to have one or more of these pieces of equipment to give you, in that moment, a reroll one of one your dice (taking the better of the two). You can have multiple pieces of equipment apply but the reroll can only be redeemed once per Check.

On top of this, if the gear you‚Äôre using has Traits that applies, you add any of those applicable bonuses to whichever roll you keep.

For the first redemption of this bonus between Maintenance Prep Checks this comes with no penalty but every time after that, the tool degrades, giving it a -1 regardless of whether you succeed or not. While players are assumed to be using all equipment that might be useful without having to specifically state as such, if a player did point out how a piece of equipment is being used to help them, or if they use equipment in an unorthodox but clever way, they gain 1 Honor.

You can use faulty equipment (ones with a negative modifier), since something is better than nothing. This still gives you a reroll but it‚Äôs on 1 dice lower (again, keeping the better of the two).

Assets & Equipment Based Skills

Some skills are only going to be useful if you have the proper equipment or the right Asset. For instance, if you‚Äôre not out in the wilderness, you can‚Äôt really use Snaring or if you don‚Äôt have the proper tools or a woodshop, Maintenance won‚Äôt do you any good. This will need to be handled on a case to case basis.

HELPER SKILLS

Let‚Äôs say that you‚Äôre tracking someone in the wilderness. You have Hunting at Rank 4 and Endurance at Rank 3. Seems like being able to move quickly over a longer period of time, should help you catch the guys and it does.

If you have another Advanced Skill that you think might help you, you can add it for the normal Stress Dice penalty and roll a Stress Dice.

Stress Dice

Much like in a Social Conflict, your character is probably going to get Stress Dice. Note that, unless it says otherwise, that dice is a d6!.

When any of these tools note that you roll a Stress Dice, if you don‚Äôt have one, you simply gain one that then waits in the wings to be rolled the next time you roll a tool. You can gain multiple Dice this way without rolling them.

At the end of Challenge, you roll all Stress Dice and add it to your Character‚Äôs Stress.

This may seem like it is nearly identical to Preparation, and they are very close. The difference is that Preparation is a general thing that applies to a Skill Check you‚Äôre going to make in the future, while using a helper Skill is in the moment and is specific to the scenario. You can use both at the same time, since the help Skill gives a bonus and Preparation gives a reroll.

Skill Mobbing

For GMs, note that Helper Skills are how you handle Skill Mobbing, where everyone just overpowers a roll by all rolling against it. Instead, only one person rolls and only those with an applicable Advanced Skill and willing to take that Stress Dice add their Rank to help out.

MULTI-TASKING

Let‚Äôs be realistic here: you‚Äôre probably going to end up trying to do two things at once, like climbing a wall and doing so quietly.

If that‚Äôs the case, you‚Äôre going to have to ‚Äúsplit‚Äù your d20! into smaller dice and assign those to each of the Checks you‚Äôre trying to make, because your attention is divided. The total max value of the dice can never exceed 20 but it can be lower than it. The other side will still be rolling full d20!s against you.

So, if you‚Äôre climbing a wall and trying to do so quietly, you‚Äôd have to make a Climbing and a Move Silently Check. You could split it like so: d12! for Climbing and d8! for Move Silently, or d10! for Climbing and d10! for Move Silently, or d12!+d4! for Move Silently and d4! for Climbing.

Or you could just say ‚Äúscrew being quiet‚Äù and roll a d20! for Climbing and take a 0 for Move Silently. In this case, the GM would just roll against your Skill Rank. So, if you choose to ignore Move Silently but have a Rank 4 in it, the GM would roll their dice, and you can hope they don‚Äôt roll higher than 4.

Either way, besides this, it works like a normal Check

Not Paying Attention

Multi-Tasking is also used when someone isn't paying attention, such as a guard whose on watch but he's been there for several hours at this point so he's not doing it so closely. In this case, start at a d10!, lowering it further as needs be.

Helping Others

It feels good to help people and Bonfire is all about group cooperation. The fighter helps the skill monkey survive combat and the skill monkey helps the fighter not look like an idiot while doing detective work.

And how you help other characters will really depend on the situation. Perhaps your Preparation can be used to help another person hide or perhaps you have a bit of knowledge that they don‚Äôt.

Really, this will be done to what feels right in the moment but this note is here to draw attention to someone helping you as an Asset and someone adding their Rank to yours with a Helper Skill, and how they‚Äôre different.

And the difference is time. If you have plenty of time to coach someone while they‚Äôre climbing a cliff face, you‚Äôd be an Asset and nobody would take a Stress Die.

But if you‚Äôre being chased by the guards and it‚Äôs a matter of seconds or minutes, you‚Äôd add a Helper Skill and both of you would need to take the Stress Die.

Note that if the skill monkey is also trying to climb the cliff face and coach as they go, that would be considered a d10! worth of effort for Multi-Tasking, though you don‚Äôt have to actually roll to see if you help.

PREPARATION

Preparation is anything your character does to help a specific Skill Check that will be made in the future (even if that future is 2 seconds from now). Note that this doesn't have to be a Check you make: it can also apply to an ally's Check (if it makes sense).

Usually, these only take a couple of seconds but can vary widely depending on the circumstances. Throwing a rock is a matter of moments while constructing a gilly suit will take a lot longer.

As long as there is time, there is no chance of failure or, if the players have plenty of time, Preparation just happens. Mark what you did for later in the Skill Challenge.

However, if there is a time crunch or a chance of failure, Preparation works like a normal Skill Check.

Either way, you only get one Prep Check per Advanced Skill that applies for each upcoming Check, unless you have Assets but remember that they do take time.

That means that if you know you're going to have to make a jump, you can use Acrobatics to stretch, Endurance to do a few practice sprints to the edge, and Sports (long jumping) to remember techniques all to prep for the same Jump check.

When you redeem Preparation that you‚Äôve readied, you can reroll one of the dice that was rolled for the Check. This means either the base dice or the difficulty dice for either you or the Obstacle.

Planning & Action Phases

While it isn‚Äôt entirely black and white, it‚Äôs helpful to think of Challenges as composing two phases: planning and action; the laying out the plan and then the heist.

Planning is gathering intel, getting your team and equipment together, and planning your route of attack. This is where you‚Äôll use Preparation, Taking Your Time, and Assets mostly.

Action phase is when you‚Äôll be doing a lot of the Checks and Stress, as well as equipment and companions - using what you learned in the Prep Phase to help you get through the sticky parts.

But don‚Äôt get too hung up on separating these ideas though. Even during the heist, you might find it worth your time to sit on your heels and take your time while doing some Preparation as there will almost certainly be problems that crop up that you need to adjust to.

RUSHING IT

On the flipside, if you‚Äôre in a hurry to get the job down, you can just rush it. This subtracts 50% from the estimated time but gives you a -5. Like Taking Your Time, this can be done multiple times in a row to half the time again but it also increases the penalty by 1.

Failures don‚Äôt consider this -3 while determining the extent of your failure and success means you bought yourself some more time: removing the amount you succeed by, with the -3, from the Failure on one of the Obstacles.

TAKING YOUR TIME

The other thing that you can do is take your time and really try and do the job well. Doubling the time for your Check gives you a +6. This can be done multiple times in a row to gain a further bonus but each time lowers the bonus by 1, down to +0. Regardless of how long you take your time, you always only roll 1 Stress Dice.

So, if it would take you 15 minutes to search a room, you could take 30 minutes instead and get a +6, or 60 minutes for a total of +11 ( 6 + 5 ).

WAITING IT OUT

By doing nothing, you can calm your nerves and let who you‚Äôre facing do the same. This can be by either finding a shadow to hunker down in or finding a spot in the wall where you can rest while climbing.

Using this tool allows you to half the current Soft Failures. You do roll 1 Stress Dice either way.

This may or may not work on an inanimate Obstacle but you need to use your best judgement.

LORE SKILL SUITE

This suite covers general knowledge, which is mostly academic in nature. Most of it is pretty heavy stuff but the basics are second nature or stuff that‚Äôs been picked up here and there.

ADMINISTRATION While they‚Äôll never inspire the awe or respect that other leaders do, people who oversee the day to day functions of an organization keep the lights on so that others can make the speeches.

Administrators can make an Average skill check to gain a clue about an item the party might need on their adventure, or a Challenging one to retroactively remember that they did buy an item back at the market for just such an occasion.

Each of these checks they make increases the difficulty of the next one by 1 step.

Checks are only made when timing is critical, such as communication in a battle, or when understanding is key, such as when debating foreign policy.

If one of the characters is in a Social Conflict with another in a tongue that‚Äôs not their native one, that person can only bring in as many Characteristic Die per round as he has Ranks in that Language.

Additionally, when two native speakers are in a Conflict, the one with the higher Language Rank gains Leverage (see Applicable Skills in Chapter 8: Social ). In case of tie, nothing happens.

The Lore Skill Suite can‚Äôt be used in place of a Language Check for tongues you don‚Äôt know unless you have the Language Skill in a related Language. Using a real-world example, someone with the Language (Swedish) Advanced Skill couldn‚Äôt use the Lore Skill Suite to understand a person with Language (French) but could use it when talking to someone with Language (Norwegian) or Language (Danish).

A character‚Äôs Lore skill can only be used to substitute in for their native culture but not others. If the character does wish to branch out, his mastery of Law for that culture starts at 0, as he must learn everything from the ground up. Diving more into his own culture‚Äôs laws (buying this Advanced Skill for his culture) uses the Lore skill as base, as per usual.

LITERACY (language) Unlike other skills, this skill can‚Äôt be attempted unless you‚Äôve specifically bought this Advanced Skill and its mastery starts at 0. That‚Äôs right; every character starts illiterate.

This skill allows a character to read and write in a language‚Äôs alphabet, giving them the power to pen books and poems, or passive aggressive notes.

This skill involves the knowledge of long-term care, when bandages should be changed, which poultices will have the greatest effect, and what minor surgeries need to be performed.

A character with this skill can make a Check for a wounded person, added the Severity of any Wounds that he's treating to the opposing roll. So, if a person has a 5, 3, and 8 Severity Wound, he can make a Medicine Check vs d20+16 or he can choose not to treat the 8 Severity Wound and make a vs d20!+8. Success means that the person can take 1 day off the healing time for that Wound every time it drops a Severity.

So, a success against a 5 Severity Wound means that is was treated properly and takes 10 days to completely heal ( 4 + 3 + 2 + 1 + 0 ) instead of the normal 15.

You may also increase the Difficulty modifier by half again as much - if you succeed, you take 2 days off per Severity. So, if you're against a d20!+9, you can make it a d20!+14 ( 9 + ( 9 / 2 ) ) for that bonus.

Each Monster Craft check starts as an Average Skill Check and answers one question the player has on a success but each successive check on the same monster increases the difficulty by 2. WEIRDCRAFT SKILL SUITE

The science of the Weird is an oft studied but little understood branch of knowledge that requires a deep knowledge of the nature of the Painted World.

With this Skill Suite, someone can identify a spell, point out needed regents and processes for ritual shapings, detect if something is touched by the Weird and what its properties are, or even detect if a spell has been cast in an area, and have a chance of finding out what it was.

ALL ADVANCED WEIRDCRAFT SKILLS Unlike other Advanced Skilled, the Weirdcraft Advanced Skills pretty much work the same and so don't need their own section.

The first bonus that having an Advanced Weirdcraft Skill gives you is that it gives you further insight into a particular Weird Tradition, allowing you do detect it better. For more information in what each Tradition specializes in, head over to Chapter 11: The Weird.

The other thing it does is allow you to shape cantrip versions of spells from that Tradition, regardless of Archetype. The ability to shape is inherit in all living things though it takes decades of practice, a strong familiy bloodline, and/or a natural gift to do what Weird-Adept Archetypes do, a form of practical "folkweird" is often passed down as part of the tools of the trade.

This doesn't, and will never, allow someone to shape the full version of a spell but it can give them a boost or an edge. The specific details should be left up to the GM and player but they should always be appropriate to the Tradition and never be more powerful than a spell that does the same thing. Most commonly this will mean it will get used as a Helper Skill.

Shaping a cantrip is still shaping and is stressful and so that anytime any of these Skills are used to shape, the shaper gets 2d6! Stress dice.
