Skills are used as a way to determine the success or failure of a given action, based on what your character knows about the given subject or about how to perform the given task.

You donâ€™t have to use these rules all the time, i.e., you donâ€™t have to roll to see if your character successfully walks across a room, but at times theyâ€™re a good way to semi-randomly determine the outcome of a tricky task, i.e., walking across a room full of traps.

TYPES of SKILLS

There are two types of skills that youâ€™re going to be pulling from: Skill Suites and Advanced Skills.

Skill Suites represent broad swathes of knowledge that cover a variety of subjects. Suites are hard to increase because thereâ€™s so much ground to cover but can be used if you donâ€™t have the specific Advanced Skill. An example of this is the Survival and Lore Skill Suites.

Advanced Skills are more tightly focused, covering one intellectual discipline. Theyâ€™re easier to master but may not be applicable in every case. An example of this would be the First Aid, Fire Building, and Foraging skills in the Survival Skill Suite and the Arcana, History, and Medicine skills in the Lore Skill Suite.

ACQUIRING SKILLS

Throughout your characterâ€™s career, sheâ€™ll acquire and improve her skills. Both types of skills are purchased with CrP, that is spent either at Character Creation or on leveling up. All skills have a unique cost that represents how easy or difficult they are to master. You pay the price, you improve the skill.

CALCULATING RANK

For Skill Suites, your starting Rank is the 10s digit of the relevant score, the 10s digit of the average of the listed scores. For Advanced Skill, unless it says otherwise (I'm looking at you Literacy and Combat Style), it is the Rank of the mother Skill Suite.

So, if you have 15 Wis and 18 Cha, your starting Rank in Streetwise is 1. If you didn't purchase Perception, your Rank in that is 1 as well since it's mother Skill Suite is Streetwise.

It is possible to have a Rank 0.

When you purchase a Skill, the cost is Bbase Price - the average of the relevant scores, minimum 1 + the previous Rank of that Skill. Every purchase increases your Rank in that Skill by 1. Should you purchase a Skill Suite, it also increases all of its Advanced Skills by 1 as well.

So, let's go back to the above example and say that you purchased Perception twice. You'd have to pay 4 CrP. The first time you bought it it cost 1 ( 15 - 15, minimum 1 + 1 ). Remember that you're already considered to have Rank 1 in it because you have Rank 1 in Streetwise. The second time costs 3 ( 15 - 15, minimum 1 + 2 ).

SKILL SPECIALIZATIONS

It often pays to specialize. After all, many dissertations cover extremely minute subjects with incredible depth and drawing on that well of knowledge can be a huge benefit.

Skill Specializations represent an area of knowledge within an Advanced Skill that your character spends extra time versing themselves in. As such, they gain a bonus.

Skill specializations come in layers, modifiers that narrow down the field. Each layer gives a +1 to your effective Rank when it comes into play.

For example, a monster hunter studying predators, would gain a+1 Rank, while another that studies flying predators would gain a +2 Rank bonus to their checks, and a monster hunter that studies the flying predators of the Uchka Mountains would gain a bonus of +3 to their Rank.

There may come a time when a specialization only kind of applies. In this case, it may be kind of helpful, as the max bonus that can be gained from it is +1.

So, when encountering a land predator in the Uchka Mountains, all three monster hunters would only gain +1 to their Rank.

That sort of super-focused study takes a lot of time and effort and man only has so much time and energy to give. The total number of specializations per Advanced Skill that a character can have is equal to half his Intelligence score.

A character can gain a specialization like any other skill; having his character go out of the way to learn more about his craft. The price is the base cost of the Advanced Skill times the number of layers in the specialization plus one.

A character can go back and increase the number of layers in his Skill Specialization after his initial purchase of it, but it costs additional amount of CrPs equal to twice the base cost of the Advanced Skill.

Because not all tasks are the same in terms of how challenging they are (building a house is harder than building a simple table), Skills Checks are given variable difficulties that are represented by different sized dice. The bigger the dice, the harder the task.

This difficulty dice is an abstraction, of course, and represents a number of things, and so, therefore, can be changed based on planning and equipment used, or the amount of time granted to complete it. Following the previous example, if someone was trying to pick a lock underwater while blindfolded and having to do so under a minute, he might roll a d20! + d4!, hoping that that total is under his Skill Rank.

Note on notation: whenever something says it increases the difficulty by 1, that means it increases it by 1 step. So, a Challenging check increased by 2 becomes a Heroic check.

SKILL CHECKS v SKILL CHALLENGES

You may see the terms "Skill Checks" and "Skill Challenges" thrown around here and assume they're synonymous: They're not. Skill Checks refer to a single roll against a single Skill, while a Skill Challenge refers to a host of Skill Checks. To use a metaphor: a Skill Challenge would be breaking into the castle. The Skill Checks would be climbing the walls, hiding in the shadows, sneaking past the guards, listening at doors, lockpicking any locks, etc.

OPPOSED CHECKS

Now, some checks wonâ€™t be your character battling against the elements or an object, sheâ€™ll be facing off against another character, who has their own set of skills and can think and act for themselves.

This case, you roll an opposed check. Both sides roll a d8! + their Skill Rank and the side with the higher total wins, whatever that may mean for the situation.

One character may also have an advantage over the other. Increase or lower the dice size of what each person rolls as appropriate.

The RIGHT EQUIPMENT for the RIGHT JOB

If the person attempting the Skill Check has the equipment that could make his job easier, they have a chance of turning a fail into a success. If they fail by only one and have previously stated they were using equipment, they can take a drawback and pass the check.

So, for instance, a person with Rank 2 in Climbing rolls a 3 on his check - a fail - but stated before that he's using a rope. He pipes up and says that even though he made it to the top, the knot of his rope came undone and it is now on the ground far below. Or, if the player is using crampons, he may state that during the climb, the iron spikes got stuck in the cliff face and bent, making them useless until he gets them repaired.

This does stack for every piece of equipment he's using, and he only needs to find drawbacks for the amount of +1s he needs. So, expanding the above example: if our climber is using both rope and crampons, and fails by 1, he only needs to lose his rope or break his crampons. If he fails by 2, he needs both.

You can also use this to reduce the Stress you'd get from failing by a like amount. Final note: the drawbacks doesn't neccessarily have to relate to the check or make it easier directly but it is something that the players just can't recover easily.

COORDINATED EFFORT

Others may offer to help you out with a Skill Check if theyâ€™re trained in the specific Advanced Skill youâ€™re rolling for, but not for a general Skill Suite check. Doing so follows the same rules as equipment with the following clarifications:

The first is that one person will take the lead and actually do the rolling. Everyone else will only be worth a +1 regardless of their Rank in that Skill.

The second is that once a person states a drawback, they're committed to that drawback, whether or not the check ultimately fails.

So, for example, a party of three, Kiara, Damien, Amelia, Ryan, and John, are searching a room using Perception. Their Ranks go Kiara 1, Damien 3, Amelia 4, Ryan none, and John 1. They decide that Amelia is going to be doing the searching so she's going to roll a d10!. She rolls a 7; 3 off from what she needed. She can still make it, because she has help but everyone needs to think of a drawback, except Ryan who can't help at all.

Kiara decides that her character is so focused on searching that if she gets jumped, her character will increase her initiative die: that's a +1. Damien decides that his character, while searching, comes gets in a small scuffle with the owner of the room because he got into something private and so he lowers his Honor by 1 and, should they get into a Conflict with the owner, the owner gets a d6 against him. That's also another +1 despite Damien having Rank 3 in Perception.

John, like always, screws the pooch, and can't think of anything and so Ameila still fails her check. Kiara and Damien are still saddled with their drawbacks so when the zombie bursts out of the closet, Kiara is so focused on searching, she fails to notice as quickly as she might have.

RETRYING CHECKS

There may be times where a character has enough time to try a Skill Check again, however, he must change the circumstances in a unique way before he proceeds.

For example, a character climbing a wall fails his first attempt. Heâ€™s got rope and crampons on his shoes that made the attempt a little easier but he just didnâ€™t make it. He canâ€™t simply roll his check again.

Parts of a Skill

Itâ€™s important to think about Skills in Bonfire having three parts: Mechanics, Knowledge, and Creativity.

Mechanics are the â€˜solidâ€™ part of the Skill and give that Skill a purpose that can always be utilized. This gives every Skill a definite thing it can do and reason why youâ€™d spend your precious CrPs on it.

Knowledge is a step beyond the mechanic of the Skill. This is most evident in the Advanced Lore Skills but it applies to every Skill. A climber can use her Climbing Skill to deduce how hard an ascent is going to be or a person with Use Rope can get a sense for how strong a length of rope is.

Creativity is the final and perhaps the most important part of a Bonfire Skill. This is applying and repurposing the Skill in creative ways that arenâ€™t layouted specifically by the rules. For example, using your Quarter Mastering Skill to stow illegal items in your pack in such a way as to not be detected by the searching guards.
